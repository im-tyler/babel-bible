## Intuition [Beginner]

Suppose you have a quantity spread out over space — think of rainfall measured across a wide region. Most of the region gets a gentle, manageable amount, but a few small patches catch a downpour. If you want to study the whole rainfall pattern, it helps to split it into two clean pieces: a smooth background that never exceeds some manageable level, plus a collection of isolated storm cells where all the heavy stuff is concentrated. The background you can handle with crude tools because it is bounded; the storm cells you handle one at a time because each is small and you know exactly where it sits.

The Calderón-Zygmund decomposition does precisely this for an integrable function. You pick a height — call it $\lambda$ — and you ask: where is the function, on average over small boxes, bigger than $\lambda$? Those boxes are the storm cells. Everywhere else the local averages stay below $\lambda$, and you keep that part as the smooth background. The clever bookkeeping is that the storm cells cannot be too plentiful: the total area they occupy is controlled by how much total mass the function has, divided by the height $\lambda$. Raise the threshold and the storm cells shrink away; lower it and they spread.

Why bother carving a function up this way? Because many of the deepest tools in analysis only work on functions that are either bounded or supported on a small set with a special averaging property. A general integrable function is neither. The decomposition manufactures both situations at once: a bounded background and a list of localized bumps, each averaging to zero so its oscillation does the work. Almost every estimate that controls a wild operator at the borderline — where the function is merely integrable and nothing stronger — runs through this single split.

The one-sentence takeaway: cut an integrable function at a height $\lambda$ into a bounded good part and a sum of small, mean-zero bad bumps, with the bumps occupying a total area no larger than the mass divided by $\lambda$ — and you have the universal engine for borderline estimates.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the graph of a positive function on a line, with a horizontal dashed line drawn across it at height $\lambda$. Now imagine sliding boxes along the line and computing the average height of the function inside each box. Where those box-averages climb above $\lambda$, mark the box. The marked boxes cluster around the tall spikes of the function. Keep growing or shrinking the boxes by a stopping rule until you have a tidy, non-overlapping collection of marked boxes that together capture every place the function runs hot.

The marked boxes never overlap, so adding up their lengths is honest bookkeeping, and that total length is what the mass-over-$\lambda$ rule controls.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We split a simple function at a chosen height and read off the two parts by hand.

Step 1. Work on the line and let $f$ be the function equal to $6$ on the interval from $0$ to $1$, and $0$ everywhere else. Its total mass — the area under it — is $6$ times $1$, which is $6$. Choose the height $\lambda = 2$.

Step 2. Find where the local averages exceed $\lambda = 2$. Over the interval from $0$ to $1$ the average of $f$ is $6$, which is above $2$. Over a very large window the average is tiny, below $2$. The interval from $0$ to $1$ is the one box whose average sits above the threshold, so it becomes our single storm cell $Q_1 = [0, 1]$.

Step 3. Build the good part $g$. Outside $Q_1$ the function is already $0$, which is below $\lambda$, so we keep it. Inside $Q_1$ we replace $f$ by its average over $Q_1$, namely $6$. So $g$ equals $6$ on $[0,1]$ and $0$ outside. Notice the cap: here the average $6$ exceeds $\lambda = 2$, because in one dimension the bound on $g$ is the height times a fixed factor, not exactly $\lambda$ — the average over a stopping box stays within a bounded multiple of $\lambda$.

Step 4. Build the bad part $b$. By definition $b = f - g$. On $[0,1]$ that is $6 - 6 = 0$, and outside it is $0 - 0 = 0$. In this clean example $b$ vanishes because $f$ was already constant on its box. The total length of storm cells is the length of $[0,1]$, which is $1$; the rule says this is at most the mass $6$ divided by $\lambda = 2$, that is $3$, and indeed $1$ is at most $3$.

What this tells us: the decomposition replaces a function by its box-average on the heavy boxes (the good part) and records the leftover oscillation as mean-zero bumps (the bad part). When the function is already flat on a box the bump is empty; when it wiggles, the bump captures exactly the wiggle. The mass-over-$\lambda$ bound on the total length of boxes is the quantitative heart, and it always holds with room to spare.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $\mathcal{D}$ denotes the family of dyadic cubes of $\mathbb{R}^n$: cubes of the form $2^{-k}(m + [0,1)^n)$ with $k \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $m \in \mathbb{Z}^n$. Distinct dyadic cubes are either nested or disjoint, and each dyadic cube $Q$ has a unique dyadic parent $\widehat{Q}$ of twice the side length and $2^n$ times the volume. For a cube $Q$ write $\fint_Q f = \frac{1}{|Q|}\int_Q f$ for the average of $f$ over $Q$.

**Definition (Calderón-Zygmund decomposition).** Let $f \in L^1(\mathbb{R}^n)$ and $\lambda > 0$. A *Calderón-Zygmund decomposition of $f$ at height $\lambda$* is a representation
$$f = g + b, \qquad b = \sum_j b_j,$$
together with a countable collection of pairwise disjoint dyadic cubes $\{Q_j\}$, such that:

1. (*Good part bounded.*) $\|g\|_{L^\infty} \le 2^n \lambda$ and $\|g\|_{L^1} \le \|f\|_{L^1}$.
2. (*Bad part localized.*) Each $b_j$ is supported in $Q_j$, and $b = \sum_j b_j$ is supported in $\Omega = \bigcup_j Q_j$.
3. (*Mean zero.*) $\int_{Q_j} b_j \, dx = 0$ for every $j$.
4. (*Controlled mass.*) $\|b_j\|_{L^1} \le 2^{n+1}\lambda\, |Q_j|$, equivalently $\fint_{Q_j}|b_j| \le 2^{n+1}\lambda$, and $\lambda < \fint_{Q_j}|f| \le 2^n \lambda$.
5. (*Total measure bound.*) $\sum_j |Q_j| = |\Omega| \le \lambda^{-1}\|f\|_{L^1}$.

The cubes $\{Q_j\}$ are obtained by the *dyadic stopping-time selection*: $Q_j$ ranges over the maximal dyadic cubes $Q$ with $\fint_Q |f| > \lambda$. On each such cube one sets $g = \fint_{Q_j} f$ and $b_j = (f - \fint_{Q_j} f)\chi_{Q_j}$; off $\Omega$ one sets $g = f$ and $b = 0$.

**Definition (good-$\lambda$ / bad-part splitting).** The induced splitting of any sublinear operator estimate at height $\lambda$ into a *good-part contribution* $\{|Tg| > \lambda/2\}$ and a *bad-part contribution* $\{|Tb| > \lambda/2\}$ is the *good-$\lambda$/bad-part splitting*. The good part is controlled by an $L^2$ (or $L^\infty$) estimate; the bad part is controlled by its mean-zero cancellation against the smoothness of the operator kernel away from $\Omega$.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *The good part is bounded by $2^n\lambda$, not by $\lambda$.* On a stopping cube $Q_j$ the value of $g$ is the average $\fint_{Q_j}f$, which exceeds $\lambda$ by selection. The bound $\fint_{Q_j}|f| \le 2^n\lambda$ comes from the parent $\widehat{Q_j}$, which was *not* selected, so $\fint_{\widehat{Q_j}}|f| \le \lambda$ and $\fint_{Q_j}|f| \le 2^n \fint_{\widehat{Q_j}}|f| \le 2^n\lambda$. Forgetting the parent step gives the wrong constant.

- *Selection must take maximal cubes, not all cubes.* The set $\{Q : \fint_Q|f| > \lambda\}$ is closed upward under nothing in particular, but each such $Q$ is contained in a *largest* such cube because $|Q| < \lambda^{-1}\|f\|_{L^1}$ bounds the side length. Choosing maximal cubes makes the $\{Q_j\}$ disjoint; choosing all of them double-counts mass.

- *Mean zero is a property of $b_j$, not of $b$ globally in a useful sense.* Each $b_j$ integrates to zero over its own cube, which is what the kernel-cancellation argument needs cube by cube. The global statement $\int b = 0$ is true but too weak; the per-cube cancellation is the load-bearing fact.

- *The decomposition is for $L^1$ functions and a fixed $\lambda$, not a single canonical object.* Different heights $\lambda$ produce genuinely different cube families and different good/bad splits of the *same* $f$. There is no decomposition independent of $\lambda$; the $\lambda$-dependence is the point, since estimates integrate over $\lambda$ via the distribution function.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Calderón-Zygmund decomposition; Calderón-Zygmund 1952 *Acta Math.* 88, 85; dyadic form Stein 1970 *Singular Integrals* Ch. I §3).** Let $f \in L^1(\mathbb{R}^n)$ and $\lambda > 0$. Then there exist a function $g$, functions $b_j$, and pairwise disjoint dyadic cubes $\{Q_j\}$ satisfying properties (1)–(5) of the Formal definition. In particular $f = g + b$ with $b = \sum_j b_j$, $\|g\|_\infty \le 2^n\lambda$, $\|g\|_1 \le \|f\|_1$, each $b_j$ supported on $Q_j$ with $\int b_j = 0$, and $\sum_j|Q_j| \le \lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1$.

*Proof.* Set $\Omega = \{ M_{\mathcal{D}}f > \lambda\}$ where $M_{\mathcal{D}}$ is the dyadic maximal operator of [02.19.01].

**Step 1 (stopping-time selection of maximal cubes).** For each $x$ with $M_{\mathcal{D}}f(x) > \lambda$ there is a dyadic cube $Q \ni x$ with $\fint_Q|f| > \lambda$. Since $f \in L^1$, any such cube satisfies $|Q| < \lambda^{-1}\int_Q|f| \le \lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1$, so its side length is bounded; hence among the dyadic cubes through $x$ with average exceeding $\lambda$ there is a *maximal* one. Let $\{Q_j\}$ be the collection of all cubes that are maximal with respect to inclusion among dyadic cubes with $\fint_Q|f| > \lambda$. By the nested-or-disjoint dichotomy of dyadic cubes together with maximality, the $\{Q_j\}$ are pairwise disjoint, and $\Omega = \bigcup_j Q_j$.

**Step 2 (average control on each selected cube).** Fix a selected cube $Q_j$ and let $\widehat{Q_j}$ be its dyadic parent. By maximality of $Q_j$, the strictly larger cube $\widehat{Q_j}$ does *not* satisfy the selection inequality, so $\fint_{\widehat{Q_j}}|f| \le \lambda$. Since $|\widehat{Q_j}| = 2^n|Q_j|$,
$$\lambda < \fint_{Q_j}|f| = \frac{1}{|Q_j|}\int_{Q_j}|f| \le \frac{2^n}{|\widehat{Q_j}|}\int_{\widehat{Q_j}}|f| = 2^n\fint_{\widehat{Q_j}}|f| \le 2^n\lambda.$$
This is property (4): the average of $|f|$ over each selected cube lies in $(\lambda, 2^n\lambda]$.

**Step 3 (definition of $g$ and $b$).** Set
$$g(x) = \begin{cases} \fint_{Q_j} f & x \in Q_j, \\ f(x) & x \notin \Omega, \end{cases} \qquad b_j(x) = \bigl(f(x) - \fint_{Q_j}f\bigr)\chi_{Q_j}(x), \quad b = \sum_j b_j.$$
On $\Omega$ each $x$ lies in exactly one $Q_j$ by disjointness, so $g$ and $b$ are well defined and $f = g + b$ everywhere: off $\Omega$, $b = 0$ and $g = f$; on $Q_j$, $g + b_j = \fint_{Q_j}f + (f - \fint_{Q_j}f) = f$.

**Step 4 (good part is bounded).** For $x \notin \Omega$, the Lebesgue differentiation theorem [02.19.01] gives $|f(x)| = \lim_{r\to 0}\fint_{B(x,r)}|f| \le \lambda$ for a.e. such $x$, since every small dyadic cube through $x$ has average at most $\lambda$ (otherwise $x \in \Omega$); thus $|g(x)| \le \lambda$ a.e. off $\Omega$. For $x \in Q_j$, $|g(x)| = |\fint_{Q_j}f| \le \fint_{Q_j}|f| \le 2^n\lambda$ by Step 2. Hence $\|g\|_\infty \le 2^n\lambda$. Moreover $\int|g| \le \int|f|$: off $\Omega$ they agree, and on each $Q_j$, $\int_{Q_j}|g| = |Q_j|\,|\fint_{Q_j}f| \le \int_{Q_j}|f|$ by Jensen. So $\|g\|_1 \le \|f\|_1$.

**Step 5 (bad part: support, mean zero, mass).** By construction $b_j$ is supported in $Q_j$ and
$$\int_{Q_j} b_j = \int_{Q_j} f - |Q_j|\fint_{Q_j}f = \int_{Q_j}f - \int_{Q_j}f = 0,$$
which is property (3). For the mass, the triangle inequality and Step 2 give
$$\int_{Q_j}|b_j| \le \int_{Q_j}|f| + |Q_j|\,\bigl|\fint_{Q_j}f\bigr| \le 2\int_{Q_j}|f| = 2|Q_j|\fint_{Q_j}|f| \le 2^{n+1}\lambda\,|Q_j|,$$
property (4).

**Step 6 (total measure bound).** Each selected cube satisfies $|Q_j| < \lambda^{-1}\int_{Q_j}|f|$ from $\fint_{Q_j}|f| > \lambda$. Summing over the disjoint family,
$$\sum_j|Q_j| < \frac{1}{\lambda}\sum_j\int_{Q_j}|f| = \frac{1}{\lambda}\int_{\Omega}|f| \le \frac{1}{\lambda}\|f\|_1,$$
property (5). All five properties hold. $\square$

**Bridge.** The decomposition builds toward the weak-type $(1,1)$ boundedness of every Calderón-Zygmund singular integral operator, and the same stopping-time selection appears again in the dyadic-cube proofs of the $T(1)$ theorem, the John-Nirenberg inequality, and the modern theory of sparse domination. The central insight is that the maximal-function level set $\{M_{\mathcal{D}}f > \lambda\}$ *is* the bad set: selecting the maximal cubes where the average first crosses $\lambda$ is exactly the act of producing the disjoint family, so this is the foundational reason a single $L^1$ hypothesis splits into a bounded $L^2$-controllable good part and a mean-zero $L^1$-controllable bad part. Putting these together, the weak-$(1,1)$ proof of [02.19.01] for the maximal operator and the decomposition here are dual faces of one stopping-time argument: the bound $\sum_j|Q_j| \le \lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1$ that controlled the maximal level set now controls the support of the bad part, and the bridge is that the good-$\lambda$/bad-part splitting generalises the layer-cake estimate to operators that see cancellation, not just size.

In the dyadic Calderón-Zygmund decomposition, the average of $|f|$ over each selected cube $Q_j$ satisfies $\lambda < \fint_{Q_j}|f| \le 2^n\lambda$.

Give the value of $\fint_{Q_1}|f|$ as a single number.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The dyadic interval $[0,1)$ has average $8 > 3$; its dyadic parent $[0,2)$ has average $4 > 3$ as well, so $[0,1)$ is not maximal — climb until the parent's average drops to $\le 3$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the good part satisfies the interpolation estimate $\|g\|_{L^2}^2 \le 2^n\lambda\,\|f\|_{L^1}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $\|g\|_\infty \le 2^n\lambda$ and $\|g\|_1 \le \|f\|_1$ together in $\int|g|^2 \le \|g\|_\infty\int|g|$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Estimate $|g|^2 = |g|\cdot|g| \le \|g\|_\infty\,|g|$ pointwise. Integrating,
$$\|g\|_{L^2}^2 = \int|g|^2 \le \|g\|_\infty\int|g| = \|g\|_\infty\|g\|_{L^1} \le (2^n\lambda)\,\|f\|_{L^1}.$$
The first factor is property (1) ($\|g\|_\infty \le 2^n\lambda$) and the second is $\|g\|_1 \le \|f\|_1$, also property (1). This $L^2$ bound is precisely what feeds the Chebyshev step $|\{|Tg| > \lambda/2\}| \le 4\lambda^{-2}\|Tg\|_2^2 \le C\lambda^{-2}\|g\|_2^2 \le C\lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1$ in the weak-$(1,1)$ argument for a singular integral $T$ bounded on $L^2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that $\|b\|_{L^1} \le 2^{n+1}\,\|f\|_{L^1}$, where $b = \sum_j b_j$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The $b_j$ have disjoint supports; sum the per-cube mass bound and use the total measure bound, or sum $\int_{Q_j}|b_j| \le 2\int_{Q_j}|f|$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The supports $Q_j$ are pairwise disjoint, so $\|b\|_1 = \sum_j\|b_j\|_1$. From Step 5 of the proof, $\int_{Q_j}|b_j| \le 2\int_{Q_j}|f|$. Summing over the disjoint family,
$$\|b\|_{L^1} = \sum_j\int_{Q_j}|b_j| \le 2\sum_j\int_{Q_j}|f| = 2\int_{\Omega}|f| \le 2\|f\|_{L^1}.$$
This already gives the cleaner bound $\|b\|_1 \le 2\|f\|_1$. The weaker $2^{n+1}\|f\|_1$ follows the same way through property (4): $\int_{Q_j}|b_j| \le 2^{n+1}\lambda|Q_j|$, so $\|b\|_1 \le 2^{n+1}\lambda\sum_j|Q_j| \le 2^{n+1}\lambda\cdot\lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1 = 2^{n+1}\|f\|_1$. Either route shows the bad part carries no more $L^1$ mass than a fixed multiple of $f$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Using the decomposition, prove the weak-type $(1,1)$ bound for the dyadic maximal operator: $|\{M_{\mathcal{D}}f > \lambda\}| \le \lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The set $\{M_{\mathcal{D}}f > \lambda\}$ is exactly the union $\Omega = \bigcup_j Q_j$ of the selected cubes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the stopping-time selection (Step 1 of the proof), $\{M_{\mathcal{D}}f > \lambda\} = \Omega = \bigcup_j Q_j$, since $M_{\mathcal{D}}f(x) > \lambda$ holds exactly when some dyadic cube through $x$ has average $> \lambda$, equivalently when $x$ lies in a maximal such cube $Q_j$. The selected cubes are disjoint and each obeys $|Q_j| < \lambda^{-1}\int_{Q_j}|f|$. Therefore
$$|\{M_{\mathcal{D}}f > \lambda\}| = \sum_j|Q_j| < \frac{1}{\lambda}\sum_j\int_{Q_j}|f| = \frac{1}{\lambda}\int_\Omega|f| \le \frac{1}{\lambda}\|f\|_1.$$
The constant is $1$, matching the direct argument of [02.19.01]: the decomposition's measure bound (property 5) *is* the dyadic weak-$(1,1)$ inequality.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $T$ be a linear operator bounded on $L^2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ with $\|Tf\|_2 \le A\|f\|_2$, given by integration against a kernel $K(x,y)$ off the diagonal that satisfies the Hörmander condition
$$\int_{|x - y| > 2|y - y'|}|K(x,y) - K(x,y')|\,dx \le B \quad \text{for all } y, y'.$$
Prove $T$ is of weak type $(1,1)$: $|\{|Tf| > \lambda\}| \le C\lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1$ with $C = C(n, A, B)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Decompose $f = g + b$ at height $\lambda$. Bound $\{|Tg| > \lambda/2\}$ by Chebyshev and the $L^2$ estimate of Exercise 4. For the bad part, integrate $Tb_j$ over the complement of the doubled cube $2Q_j$, using $\int b_j = 0$ to insert $K(x, c_j)$ and the Hörmander condition; control the doubled cubes' total measure directly.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Form the Calderón-Zygmund decomposition $f = g + b$ at height $\lambda$, with cubes $\{Q_j\}$ and centres $c_j$. By sublinearity, $\{|Tf| > \lambda\} \subseteq \{|Tg| > \lambda/2\} \cup \{|Tb| > \lambda/2\}$, so it suffices to bound each piece by $C\lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1$.

*Good part.* By Chebyshev, the $L^2$-boundedness of $T$, and Exercise 4,
$$|\{|Tg| > \lambda/2\}| \le \frac{4}{\lambda^2}\|Tg\|_2^2 \le \frac{4A^2}{\lambda^2}\|g\|_2^2 \le \frac{4A^2}{\lambda^2}\cdot 2^n\lambda\|f\|_1 = \frac{2^{n+2}A^2}{\lambda}\|f\|_1.$$

*Bad part.* Let $\Omega^* = \bigcup_j 2Q_j$ be the union of the cubes doubled about their centres. Then $|\Omega^*| \le 2^n\sum_j|Q_j| \le 2^n\lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1$, so it remains to bound $|\{x \notin \Omega^* : |Tb(x)| > \lambda/2\}|$. By Chebyshev with the $L^1$-norm of $Tb$ off $\Omega^*$,
$$|\{x\notin\Omega^* : |Tb| > \lambda/2\}| \le \frac{2}{\lambda}\int_{(\Omega^*)^c}|Tb|\,dx \le \frac{2}{\lambda}\sum_j\int_{(2Q_j)^c}|Tb_j|\,dx.$$
For $x \notin 2Q_j$ use $\int b_j = 0$ to write, with $y_j' = c_j$ the centre,
$$Tb_j(x) = \int_{Q_j}K(x,y)b_j(y)\,dy = \int_{Q_j}\bigl(K(x,y) - K(x,c_j)\bigr)b_j(y)\,dy.$$
Hence, by Tonelli [02.07.06] and the Hörmander condition (for $y \in Q_j$, $x \notin 2Q_j$ one has $|x - y| > 2|y - c_j|$),
$$\int_{(2Q_j)^c}|Tb_j|\,dx \le \int_{Q_j}|b_j(y)|\int_{|x-y| > 2|y - c_j|}|K(x,y) - K(x,c_j)|\,dx\,dy \le B\|b_j\|_1.$$
Summing and using $\sum_j\|b_j\|_1 \le 2\|f\|_1$ (Exercise 5),
$$|\{x\notin\Omega^* : |Tb| > \lambda/2\}| \le \frac{2B}{\lambda}\sum_j\|b_j\|_1 \le \frac{4B}{\lambda}\|f\|_1.$$
Combining the good part, the doubled-cube measure, and the bad part off $\Omega^*$,
$$|\{|Tf| > \lambda\}| \le \frac{2^{n+2}A^2 + 2^n + 4B}{\lambda}\|f\|_1,$$
which is the weak-type $(1,1)$ bound with $C = 2^{n+2}A^2 + 2^n + 4B$. This is the central application: $L^2$-boundedness plus a kernel-regularity (Hörmander) hypothesis upgrades to the $L^1$ endpoint through the decomposition.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Deduce from Exercise 7 (together with $L^2$-boundedness) that $T$ is bounded on $L^p(\mathbb{R}^n)$ for all $1 < p < 2$, and explain how to obtain $2 < p < \infty$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Interpolate the weak-$(1,1)$ bound with the strong-$(2,2)$ bound by Marcinkiewicz. For $p > 2$ pass to the adjoint $T^*$, whose kernel $K^*(x,y) = \overline{K(y,x)}$ also satisfies a Hörmander condition.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The operator $T$ is of weak type $(1,1)$ (Exercise 7) and of strong type $(2,2)$ (hypothesis), hence of weak type $(2,2)$. The Marcinkiewicz interpolation theorem [02.07.06], applied to a (sub)linear operator with weak endpoints at $(1,1)$ and $(2,2)$, yields strong type $(p,p)$ for every $p \in (1,2)$:
$$\|Tf\|_{L^p} \le C_p\|f\|_{L^p}, \qquad C_p \le C(n,A,B)\Bigl(\frac{1}{p-1}\Bigr),$$
the constant blowing up like $(p-1)^{-1}$ as $p \to 1^+$, reflecting the failure of strong type $(1,1)$.

For $2 < p < \infty$, consider the adjoint $T^*$, given by the kernel $K^*(x,y) = \overline{K(y,x)}$. The Hörmander condition for $K$ in the first variable is the Hörmander condition for $K^*$ in its first variable after swapping roles, so $T^*$ also satisfies the hypotheses of Exercise 7 and is therefore bounded on $L^{p'}$ for $1 < p' < 2$. By duality $\|T\|_{L^p \to L^p} = \|T^*\|_{L^{p'} \to L^{p'}}$ with $1/p + 1/p' = 1$; as $p$ ranges over $(2,\infty)$, $p'$ ranges over $(1,2)$, so $T$ is bounded on $L^p$ there as well. Together with the $L^2$ hypothesis this gives boundedness on the full range $1 < p < \infty$ — the Calderón-Zygmund theorem for singular integrals.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (weak-type $(1,1)$ for Calderón-Zygmund operators; Calderón-Zygmund 1952 *Acta Math.* 88, 85).** Let $T$ be bounded on $L^2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ and given off-diagonal by a kernel satisfying the Hörmander integral condition. Then $T$ extends to a bounded operator of weak type $(1,1)$, with bound $C(n,A,B)$ as in Exercise 7, and consequently is bounded on $L^p$ for $1 < p < \infty$. The decomposition is the only substantive ingredient: the good part is handled by the $L^2$ bound and Chebyshev, the bad part by the mean-zero cancellation against the kernel's regularity away from the doubled cubes [ref: TODO_REF Calderón-Zygmund 1952].

**Theorem 2 (Hörmander's refinement; Hörmander 1960 *Acta Math.* 104, 93).** The smoothness hypothesis on the kernel can be weakened from the pointwise gradient bound $|\nabla_y K(x,y)| \le C|x-y|^{-n-1}$ of Calderón-Zygmund to the integral (now-called *Hörmander*) condition used in Exercise 7. This is the minimal regularity under which the good-$\lambda$/bad-part argument closes, and it is the form in which the theorem propagates to operators whose kernels are merely Dini-continuous rather than $C^1$ [ref: TODO_REF Hörmander 1960].

**Theorem 3 (Fefferman-Stein sharp maximal characterization; Fefferman-Stein 1972 *Acta Math.* 129, 137).** The decomposition at a continuum of heights $\lambda$ is the engine behind the sharp maximal function $M^\#f(x) = \sup_{Q \ni x}\fint_Q|f - \fint_Q f|$ and the identity $\|f\|_{L^p} \approx \|M^\# f\|_{L^p}$ for $1 < p < \infty$ on functions with suitable decay. Iterating the stopping-time selection — each bad cube re-decomposed at a higher height — produces the good-$\lambda$ inequalities $|\{Mf > 2\lambda, M^\#f \le \gamma\lambda\}| \le C\gamma|\{Mf > \lambda\}|$ that prove the equivalence [ref: TODO_REF Fefferman-Stein 1972].

**Theorem 4 (vector-valued Calderón-Zygmund decomposition).** For $f \in L^1(\mathbb{R}^n; H)$ with values in a Hilbert space $H$, the identical stopping-time selection (now of cubes where $\fint_Q\|f\|_H > \lambda$) yields $f = g + b$ with $\|g(x)\|_H \le 2^n\lambda$, each $\int_{Q_j}b_j = 0$ in $H$, and $\sum_j|Q_j| \le \lambda^{-1}\|f\|_{L^1(H)}$. This Banach-valued form drives the Littlewood-Paley and Fourier-multiplier theory, where $f$ carries a sequence of frequency-localized pieces and $H = \ell^2$ tracks the square function [ref: TODO_REF Stein 1970].

**Theorem 5 (decomposition relative to a doubling measure).** If $\mu$ is a doubling Borel measure on $\mathbb{R}^n$ (so $\mu(2Q) \le C_\mu\mu(Q)$), the dyadic stopping-time selection at height $\lambda$ produces cubes with $\lambda < \fint_{Q_j}^\mu|f| \le C_\mu\lambda$ and $\sum_j\mu(Q_j) \le \lambda^{-1}\|f\|_{L^1(\mu)}$, with $2^n$ replaced by the doubling constant $C_\mu$. This is the form that underlies singular-integral theory on spaces of homogeneous type, where dyadic cubes are replaced by the Christ-David dyadic systems [ref: TODO_REF Stein 1993].

**Theorem 6 ($H^1$-atomic connection; Coifman-Weiss).** The bad bumps $b_j$, after normalization, are precisely *$(1,2)$-atoms* of the Hardy space $H^1$: each $a_j = b_j/(2^{n+1}\lambda|Q_j|)$ is supported in $Q_j$, has mean zero, and satisfies $\|a_j\|_2 \le |Q_j|^{-1/2}$. The Calderón-Zygmund decomposition is therefore the constructive bridge from $L^1$ to the atomic decomposition of $H^1$, and the weak-$(1,1)$ bound for $T$ is the shadow on $L^1$ of the *strong* $H^1 \to L^1$ bound that holds because $T$ maps atoms to integrable functions with uniformly controlled $L^1$-norm [ref: TODO_REF Fefferman-Stein 1972].

**Synthesis.** The Calderón-Zygmund decomposition is the foundational reason that a single quantitative hypothesis — $L^2$-boundedness plus kernel regularity — propagates to the entire scale $L^p$, $1 < p < \infty$, and to the $L^1$ endpoint in weak form, and this is exactly the structural device that converts a size hypothesis into a cancellation hypothesis. The central insight is a dictionary: the stopping-time selection of maximal dyadic cubes is dual to the maximal-function level set of [02.19.01], so the bound $\sum_j|Q_j| \le \lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1$ that proved weak-$(1,1)$ for the maximal operator is the *same* bound that controls the support of the bad part here. Putting these together, the good-$\lambda$/bad-part splitting generalises in three directions that recur throughout harmonic analysis: vertically, from Lebesgue measure to arbitrary doubling and homogeneous-type measures via the Christ-David cubes (Theorem 5); horizontally, from scalar to Hilbert-valued functions via the vector-valued decomposition that powers Littlewood-Paley theory (Theorem 4); and structurally, from $L^1$ to the Hardy space $H^1$, where the bad bumps are revealed to be atoms (Theorem 6) and the weak-$(1,1)$ bound is the trace of a strong $H^1 \to L^1$ estimate. The bridge is that every borderline boundedness theorem in the subject — singular integrals, the $T(1)$ theorem, John-Nirenberg, sparse domination — routes through this one act of cutting $f$ at height $\lambda$, and the central insight unifying them is that selecting the heavy cubes is simultaneously a statement about the maximal function and a statement about cancellation.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (disjointness of the selected cubes).** The maximal dyadic cubes $\{Q_j\}$ with $\fint_{Q}|f| > \lambda$ are pairwise disjoint, and $\bigcup_j Q_j = \{M_{\mathcal{D}}f > \lambda\}$.

*Proof.* Two dyadic cubes are nested or disjoint. If $Q_j \cap Q_k \neq \emptyset$ with $j \neq k$, one contains the other, say $Q_j \subsetneq Q_k$; then $Q_j$ is not maximal among cubes with average $> \lambda$ (it sits inside the larger $Q_k$, which also has average $> \lambda$), contradicting selection. So the $Q_j$ are disjoint. For the union: $x \in \{M_{\mathcal{D}}f > \lambda\}$ iff some dyadic $Q \ni x$ has $\fint_Q|f| > \lambda$, iff (by the side-length bound $|Q| < \lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1$ giving a maximal element) $x$ lies in a maximal such cube, iff $x \in \bigcup_j Q_j$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (the cap $\|g\|_\infty \le 2^n\lambda$ is sharp in order).** There is $f \in L^1(\mathbb{R})$ and $\lambda > 0$ for which the good part attains $\|g\|_\infty$ comparable to $2^n\lambda$ (here $n = 1$, so $2\lambda$).

*Proof.* Take $f = c\,\chi_{[0,1)}$ with $c = 2\lambda - \varepsilon$ slightly below $2\lambda$, and $\lambda$ chosen so $\fint_{[0,1)}f = c > \lambda$ while $\fint_{[0,2)}f = c/2 < \lambda$, i.e. $\lambda < c < 2\lambda$. Then $[0,1)$ is the maximal selected interval, $g = c$ on it, and $\|g\|_\infty = c = 2\lambda - \varepsilon$, which approaches $2\lambda = 2^n\lambda$ as $\varepsilon \to 0$. The bound cannot be improved below the parent-volume ratio $2^n$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (good and bad parts are genuinely in their stated spaces).** $g \in L^1 \cap L^\infty \subseteq L^2$ and each $b_j \in L^1$ with $\int b_j = 0$; moreover $b \in L^1$ with $\|b\|_1 \le 2\|f\|_1$.

*Proof.* The bounds $\|g\|_\infty \le 2^n\lambda$ and $\|g\|_1 \le \|f\|_1$ are Steps 4 of the Key Theorem; their conjunction gives $g \in L^2$ via $\|g\|_2^2 \le \|g\|_\infty\|g\|_1$. Each $b_j = (f - \fint_{Q_j}f)\chi_{Q_j}$ is a difference of $L^1$ functions supported in $Q_j$, hence $L^1$, with $\int b_j = 0$ by Step 5. Disjoint supports give $\|b\|_1 = \sum_j\|b_j\|_1 \le 2\sum_j\int_{Q_j}|f| = 2\int_\Omega|f| \le 2\|f\|_1$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (the decomposition reproduces the weak-$(1,1)$ measure bound).** $|\{M_{\mathcal{D}}f > \lambda\}| \le \lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1$.

*Proof.* By Proposition 1 the level set is $\bigcup_j Q_j$ with the $Q_j$ disjoint, and $|Q_j| < \lambda^{-1}\int_{Q_j}|f|$. Summing, $|\{M_{\mathcal{D}}f > \lambda\}| = \sum_j|Q_j| < \lambda^{-1}\int_\Omega|f| \le \lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (good-$\lambda$/bad-part reduction is exhaustive).** For any sublinear $T$ and any $\lambda > 0$, $\{|Tf| > \lambda\} \subseteq \{|Tg| > \lambda/2\}\cup\{|Tb| > \lambda/2\}$, and the second set splits further as $(\bigcup_j 2Q_j) \cup \{x \notin \bigcup_j 2Q_j : |Tb(x)| > \lambda/2\}$.

*Proof.* If $|Tf(x)| > \lambda$ and $|Tg(x)| \le \lambda/2$ then sublinearity $|Tf| \le |Tg| + |Tb|$ forces $|Tb(x)| > \lambda/2$, giving the first inclusion. The second is a plain set decomposition by intersecting with $\bigcup_j 2Q_j$ and its complement; the union of doubled cubes has measure $\le 2^n\sum_j|Q_j| \le 2^n\lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1$ by Proposition 4, so it is already of the required size and only the off-doubled-cube part needs the kernel estimate. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (atoms).** With $a_j = b_j/(2^{n+1}\lambda|Q_j|)$, each $a_j$ is supported in $Q_j$, has $\int a_j = 0$, and $\|a_j\|_{L^\infty} \le 2^{-1}\cdot 2^{-n}|Q_j|^{-1}\cdot 2\cdot 2^n\lambda \cdot (2^{n+1}\lambda)^{-1}$ — more usefully $\|a_j\|_{L^2} \le |Q_j|^{-1/2}$, the normalization of an $H^1$ $(1,2)$-atom.

*Proof.* Support and mean zero are inherited from $b_j$. For the $L^2$ size, $\|b_j\|_2^2 = \int_{Q_j}|f - \fint_{Q_j}f|^2 \le \int_{Q_j}|f|^2$; using only the crude $|b_j| \le |f| + \fint_{Q_j}|f| \le |f| + 2^n\lambda$ and $\int_{Q_j}|b_j| \le 2^{n+1}\lambda|Q_j|$, one gets $\|b_j\|_2^2 \le \|b_j\|_\infty\|b_j\|_1 \le (2\cdot 2^n\lambda)(2^{n+1}\lambda|Q_j|) = 2^{2n+2}\lambda^2|Q_j|$. Hence $\|a_j\|_2 = \|b_j\|_2/(2^{n+1}\lambda|Q_j|) \le (2^{n+1}\lambda|Q_j|^{1/2})/(2^{n+1}\lambda|Q_j|) = |Q_j|^{-1/2}$, the atomic normalization. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Hardy-Littlewood maximal function and the Vitali covering lemma [02.19.01].** The direct prerequisite and dual sibling. The dyadic stopping-time selection used here to build the cubes $\{Q_j\}$ is the same selection that produced the weak-$(1,1)$ bound for the dyadic maximal operator there; the level set $\{M_{\mathcal{D}}f > \lambda\}$ is literally the bad set $\Omega = \bigcup_j Q_j$, and the measure bound $\sum_j|Q_j| \le \lambda^{-1}\|f\|_1$ is the maximal inequality re-read as a statement about the support of the bad part.

- **$L^p$ spaces, Hölder, Minkowski, Riesz-Fischer completeness [02.07.06].** The direct prerequisite supplying the function-space scaffolding: the $L^2$ estimate on the good part, the $L^1$ control on the bad part, the Tonelli interchange in the bad-part kernel estimate, and the Marcinkiewicz interpolation that converts the weak-$(1,1)$ plus strong-$(2,2)$ endpoints into the full $L^p$ range $1 < p < \infty$.

- **Singular integral operators and the Hilbert transform [forward: 02.19.03].** The principal successor. Every Calderón-Zygmund operator — the Hilbert transform, the Riesz transforms, convolution with a Calderón-Zygmund kernel — is shown bounded on $L^p$ and of weak type $(1,1)$ by feeding its $L^2$-boundedness and kernel regularity into the good-$\lambda$/bad-part argument proved in Exercises 7 and 8.

- **Littlewood-Paley theory and Fourier multipliers [forward: 02.20.01].** The vector-valued decomposition (Theorem 4) with $H = \ell^2$ is the device that promotes the scalar singular-integral bounds to the square-function and Mikhlin-Hörmander multiplier theorems, where each frequency-localized piece is tracked simultaneously.

- **BMO and the John-Nirenberg inequality [forward: 02.20.04].** The iterated stopping-time selection — re-decomposing each bad cube at a higher height — is the mechanism behind the John-Nirenberg exponential integrability of BMO functions and the Fefferman-Stein sharp-maximal characterization (Theorem 3) that identifies $\mathrm{BMO}$ as the dual of the Hardy space $H^1$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The decomposition was introduced by Alberto Calderón and Antoni Zygmund in their 1952 *Acta Mathematica* paper *On the existence of certain singular integrals* [ref: TODO_REF Calderón-Zygmund 1952], the founding document of the real-variable theory of singular integrals. Their problem was to extend the $L^p$-boundedness of the Hilbert transform — known on the line since Marcel Riesz's 1927 work via complex-analytic methods — to higher-dimensional convolution operators with Calderón-Zygmund kernels, where complex methods are unavailable. The decomposition supplied the missing real-variable tool: it let them prove the weak-type $(1,1)$ endpoint directly, then interpolate. Their original formulation selected cubes by a covering argument on the level set of the function itself; the now-standard dyadic stopping-time form, which makes the parent-cube estimate transparent, was systematized in Elias Stein's 1970 monograph *Singular Integrals and Differentiability Properties of Functions* [ref: TODO_REF Stein 1970].

Lars Hörmander's 1960 *Acta Mathematica* paper *Estimates for translation invariant operators in $L^p$ spaces* [ref: TODO_REF Hörmander 1960] isolated the integral smoothness condition on the kernel that is exactly what the bad-part argument consumes, replacing the pointwise gradient bound of Calderón-Zygmund and thereby covering kernels of merely Dini regularity. The decomposition's reach into the theory of Hardy spaces was made explicit by Charles Fefferman and Elias Stein in their 1972 *Acta Mathematica* paper *$H^p$ spaces of several variables* [ref: TODO_REF Fefferman-Stein 1972], where the bad bumps were recognized as atoms and the duality $H^1$-$\mathrm{BMO}$ was established, recasting the weak-$(1,1)$ bound as the $L^1$ trace of a strong estimate on $H^1$.
