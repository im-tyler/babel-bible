## Intuition [Beginner]

Suppose you can already solve one equilibrium problem perfectly — the simplest one, where a quantity settles to the plain average of its neighbours — and you want to solve a harder one, where the diffusion is directional, there is a drift, and there is some absorption. The two problems look different, but they are connected by a continuous dial. Turn the dial all the way down and you have the easy problem; turn it all the way up and you have the hard one. The method of continuity is the strategy of starting where you can solve, and walking the dial up to where you want to be, never letting solvability slip away.

What could go wrong as you turn the dial? Solvability might vanish at some intermediate setting. The trick rules this out with a single uniform guarantee: a size bound on the answer holding at *every* dial setting at once, with the same constant. If the answer can never blow up wherever the dial sits, the set of settings where the problem is solvable cannot have a ragged edge. It is both open — solve at one setting, solve at all nearby settings — and closed — solvability survives in the limit. A set both open and closed, inside a connected interval, is either empty or everything. Since the easy end is solvable, the whole interval is.

The size bound you need is exactly the Schauder estimate proved earlier: the answer, measured with two derivatives on the fractional scale, is controlled by the source and the boundary data. That estimate was stated for hypothetical solutions, before anyone knew solutions existed. Here it earns its keep: it is the rail that keeps the walk along the dial from falling off.

There is an older, very different route for the plain Laplace problem — Perron's method, which builds the solution by taking the lowest cap over a family of trial functions. That method is beautiful but specific to the averaging structure. The continuity method needs no such structure; it trades a clever construction for one clean estimate.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We run the open-and-closed idea on a finite-dimensional toy where every step is plain arithmetic, so the logic is visible without any analysis. Replace functions by numbers and the elliptic operator by a $2 \times 2$ matrix. Take the easy operator to be the identity matrix $A_0 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ and the hard operator to be $A_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$. Solving the problem means inverting the matrix.

Step 1. Build the dial. Set $A_t = (1 - t) A_0 + t A_1$ for the dial value $t$ running from $0$ to $1$. This interpolates: $A_0$ at $t = 0$, $A_1$ at $t = 1$. Written out, $A_t = \begin{pmatrix} 1 + t & t \\ t & 1 + t \end{pmatrix}$.

Step 2. Solvable at the easy end. At $t = 0$, $A_0$ is the identity, which is invertible. The easy problem is solved.

Step 3. Find the uniform size guarantee. The smallest stretch factor of $A_t$ is its smallest eigenvalue. The eigenvalues of $A_t$ are $1$ (for the vector $(1, -1)$) and $1 + 2t$ (for the vector $(1, 1)$). The smaller one is $1$ for every $t$ in the interval. So for any input vector, the output of $A_t$ is at least as long as the input: the size of the solution is bounded by the size of the data, with the same constant $1$ at every dial setting.

Step 4. Read the conclusion. Because the smallest eigenvalue never drops to zero anywhere on the dial, $A_t$ is invertible for every $t$, and the size of $A_t^{-1}$ is bounded by $1$ uniformly. The set of solvable $t$ has no gap: it is the whole interval. In particular $t = 1$ is solvable, so the hard matrix $A_1$ is invertible, with $A_1^{-1} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.

What this tells us: the matrix never needed to be inverted by hand to know it was invertible. A uniform lower bound on the stretch factor along the whole dial, plus solvability at one end, forced solvability at the other. The Dirichlet problem replaces "smallest eigenvalue stays positive" with "Schauder estimate holds uniformly," and the matrix with the elliptic operator, but the open-and-closed skeleton is exactly this.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a bounded domain $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ with $C^{2,\alpha}$ boundary, $\alpha \in (0,1)$, and a uniformly elliptic operator in non-divergence form
$$L u = \sum_{i,j=1}^n a^{ij}(x) D_{ij} u + \sum_{i=1}^n b^i(x) D_i u + c(x) u,$$
with coefficients $a^{ij}, b^i, c \in C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$, ellipticity constants $0 < \lambda \le \Lambda$, coefficient Hölder norms bounded by $\Lambda$, and $c \le 0$. Given $f \in C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ and $\varphi \in C^{2,\alpha}(\partial\Omega)$, the **classical Dirichlet problem** is to find $u \in C^{2,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ with
$$L u = f \ \text{ in } \Omega, \qquad u = \varphi \ \text{ on } \partial\Omega.$$
A solution in this regularity class is a *classical* solution: the equation holds pointwise and the boundary condition holds in the trace sense of continuous restriction [ref: TODO_REF Gilbarg-Trudinger 2001 §6.1].

It is convenient to reduce to homogeneous boundary data. Since $\partial\Omega$ is $C^{2,\alpha}$ and $\varphi \in C^{2,\alpha}(\partial\Omega)$, there is an extension $\Phi \in C^{2,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ with $\Phi|_{\partial\Omega} = \varphi$; replacing $u$ by $u - \Phi$ and $f$ by $f - L\Phi \in C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ turns the problem into one with $u = 0$ on $\partial\Omega$. Write the **solution space** and **data space** as the Banach spaces [02.11.04]
$$\mathcal{X} = \{ u \in C^{2,\alpha}(\overline\Omega) : u = 0 \text{ on } \partial\Omega \}, \qquad \mathcal{Y} = C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega),$$
each carried with the Hölder norm of [02.17.04]. Solving the homogeneous-boundary Dirichlet problem for every $f$ is exactly the statement that $L : \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ is **surjective**; with $c \le 0$ the maximum principle [02.17.02] gives injectivity, so solvability means $L$ is a Banach-space isomorphism.

The **method of continuity** connects $L$ to the model operator $L_0 = \Delta$ through the affine family
$$L_t = (1 - t) \Delta + t L, \qquad t \in [0, 1],$$
so $L_0 = \Delta$ and $L_1 = L$. Each $L_t$ has coefficients $(1-t)\delta^{ij} + t a^{ij}$, $t b^i$, $t c$, hence is uniformly elliptic with ellipticity constants $\min(1,\lambda) \le \cdots \le \max(1,\Lambda)$ **independent of $t$**, coefficient Hölder norms bounded independently of $t$, and zeroth-order coefficient $t c \le 0$. The family $t \mapsto L_t \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{Y})$ is affine, so norm-continuous, with $\|L_t - L_s\|_{\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{X},\mathcal{Y})} = |t - s|\,\|L - \Delta\|_{\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{X},\mathcal{Y})}$.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *The estimate must be uniform in $t$, not merely valid for each $t$.* A bound $\|u\|_{\mathcal{X}} \le C_t \|L_t u\|_{\mathcal{Y}}$ with $C_t \to \infty$ as $t \to t_*$ does not close the argument: the solvable set could fail to be closed at $t_*$. Uniformity of the constant $C$ is what the $t$-independent ellipticity and coefficient bounds buy, and it is essential.

- *Connectivity of the parameter set is load-bearing.* The open-and-closed dichotomy concludes "empty or everything" only on a connected set. On a disconnected parameter space a solvable component need not spread; the segment $[0,1]$ is chosen precisely because it is connected.

- *The seed must be genuinely solvable, not merely estimated.* The argument needs an actual solvable point $t = 0$. For $\Delta$ on a $C^{2,\alpha}$ domain this is supplied independently (Perron's method [02.13.01] plus boundary regularity, or Newtonian-potential theory); the continuity method cannot manufacture its own starting point.

- *Dropping $c \le 0$ breaks injectivity, hence the uniform estimate.* If $c$ exceeds the principal Dirichlet eigenvalue, $L$ acquires a kernel, $\|u\|_{\mathcal X} \le C\|L_t u\|_{\mathcal Y}$ fails, and solvability genuinely fails at the resonant $t$. The method then must be replaced by Fredholm theory, which trades the uniform estimate for a finite-dimensional kernel-cokernel bookkeeping.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (method of continuity; Schauder 1934).** Let $\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{Y}$ be Banach spaces and let $L_0, L_1 \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{Y})$ be bounded linear operators. For $t \in [0,1]$ set $L_t = (1 - t) L_0 + t L_1$. Suppose there is a constant $C$, **independent of $t$**, such that
$$\|x\|_{\mathcal{X}} \le C\,\|L_t x\|_{\mathcal{Y}} \qquad \text{for all } x \in \mathcal{X},\ t \in [0,1]. \tag{$\ast$}$$
Then $L_1$ is onto if and only if $L_0$ is onto. In particular, if $L_0$ is onto, every $L_t$ is a Banach-space isomorphism [ref: TODO_REF Gilbarg-Trudinger 2001 §5.2, Theorem 5.2].

*Proof.* The estimate $(\ast)$ makes every $L_t$ injective (if $L_t x = 0$ then $\|x\| \le 0$) and forces its range to be closed: if $L_t x_k \to y$ in $\mathcal{Y}$, then $\|x_k - x_m\| \le C\|L_t(x_k - x_m)\| \to 0$, so $(x_k)$ is Cauchy in the complete space $\mathcal{X}$ [02.11.04], converges to some $x$, and continuity gives $L_t x = y \in \operatorname{ran} L_t$. Thus each $L_t$ is injective with closed range, hence a bijection onto its range and, by the bounded inverse theorem [02.11.09], a topological isomorphism $\mathcal{X} \to \operatorname{ran} L_t$ with $\|L_t^{-1}\|_{\mathcal{B}(\operatorname{ran} L_t, \mathcal X)} \le C$.

Let $S = \{ t \in [0,1] : L_t \text{ is onto} \}$. Fix $\tau \in S$. Then $L_\tau : \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ is a bijection and, by the open mapping theorem [02.11.09], $L_\tau^{-1} \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{Y}, \mathcal{X})$ with $\|L_\tau^{-1}\| \le C$ by the previous paragraph. For any $t$, write
$$L_t = L_\tau + (L_t - L_\tau) = L_\tau\big( I + L_\tau^{-1}(L_t - L_\tau) \big).$$
Now $L_t - L_\tau = (t - \tau)(L_1 - L_0)$, so $\|L_\tau^{-1}(L_t - L_\tau)\| \le C\,|t - \tau|\,\|L_1 - L_0\|$. Whenever
$$|t - \tau| < \delta := \frac{1}{2\,C\,\|L_1 - L_0\|},$$
this norm is at most $\tfrac12 < 1$, so $I + L_\tau^{-1}(L_t - L_\tau)$ is invertible by the Neumann series $\sum_{k \ge 0}(-1)^k (L_\tau^{-1}(L_t - L_\tau))^k$, which converges in the Banach algebra $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{X})$. Hence $L_t$ is a composition of two isomorphisms, so it is onto: the open ball $(\tau - \delta, \tau + \delta) \cap [0,1] \subseteq S$. The radius $\delta$ does not depend on $\tau$, since $C$ and $\|L_1 - L_0\|$ are fixed.

Because $\delta$ is uniform, $S$ is open in $[0,1]$, and its complement is open by the same argument run from any $\tau \notin S$: if some $L_t$ with $|t - \tau| < \delta$ were onto, the Neumann-series step would make $L_\tau$ onto, a contradiction. So $S$ is also closed. The interval $[0,1]$ is connected, so $S$ is either empty or all of $[0,1]$. If $L_0$ is onto then $0 \in S$, so $S = [0,1]$ and $1 \in S$, giving $L_1$ onto; combined with injectivity from $(\ast)$, each $L_t$ is an isomorphism. The converse runs identically from $1 \in S$. $\square$

**Bridge.** This abstract theorem builds toward the existence theorem for the classical Dirichlet problem in the Advanced results below, where $\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{Y}$ become the Hölder spaces of [02.17.04], the hypothesis $(\ast)$ becomes the global Schauder estimate with its $t$-uniform constant, and the seed $0 \in S$ becomes solvability of $\Delta u = f$ on a $C^{2,\alpha}$ domain; the argument appears again in [02.11.09] through the open mapping and bounded inverse theorems, which are exactly the functional-analytic engines that convert injectivity-with-closed-range into a bounded inverse. The central insight is that existence is dual to an a priori estimate: $(\ast)$ is a statement about hypothetical solutions, and the open-and-closed walk converts it into genuine surjectivity. This is exactly the foundational reason Schauder estimates are proved before existence is known — the estimate is the rail, and putting these together the method of continuity is the mechanism that rides it from the solvable Laplacian to the general operator, generalising the finite-dimensional fact that a continuous path of matrices with a uniform lower bound on their smallest singular value stays invertible.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

For $L_t = (1-t)\,\mathrm{Id} + t\,A$ on $\mathbb{R}^2$ with $A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$, the uniform lower bound in $(\ast)$ is the infimum over $t \in [0,1]$ of the smallest singular value of $L_t$. Compute that infimum.

$L_t$ is diagonal with entries $1 + 3t$ and $1$; the smallest singular value is the smaller diagonal entry, minimised over $t$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$1$.** Here $L_t = \begin{pmatrix} 1 + 3t & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$, with singular values $1 + 3t \ge 1$ and $1$. The smaller is $1$ for every $t$, so the infimum is $1$, giving the uniform bound $\|x\| \le C\|L_t x\|$ with $C = 1$. The dial never degenerates, so every $L_t$ is invertible.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, true-false).**

In the method of continuity, if the a priori constant in $(\ast)$ were allowed to depend on $t$ and blow up as $t \to t_*$, the solvable set $S$ would still be closed.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Closedness comes from the Cauchy argument $\|x_k - x_m\| \le C\|L_t(x_k - x_m)\|$ with a *fixed* $C$; a blowing-up constant breaks it.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**False.** Closedness of $S$ relies on a $t$-uniform constant: the Neumann-series radius $\delta = 1/(2C\|L_1 - L_0\|)$ must not shrink to zero, or the open neighbourhoods around solvable points collapse and $S$ can stop short of $t_*$. A constant $C_t \to \infty$ destroys exactly the uniformity the proof needs.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the perturbation (openness) step directly: if $T \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{Y})$ is an isomorphism with $\|T^{-1}\| \le C$, and $S \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{Y})$ satisfies $\|S - T\| < 1/C$, then $S$ is an isomorphism. Bound $\|S^{-1}\|$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Factor $S = T(I + T^{-1}(S - T))$ and sum the Neumann series for the bracket.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $S = T(I + R)$ with $R = T^{-1}(S - T)$, so $\|R\| \le \|T^{-1}\|\,\|S - T\| \le C\|S - T\| =: q < 1$. The Neumann series $\sum_{k \ge 0}(-R)^k$ converges in $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{X})$ with $\|(I + R)^{-1}\| \le \sum q^k = (1 - q)^{-1}$, so $I + R$ is invertible. Then $S = T(I + R)$ is a composition of isomorphisms, hence an isomorphism, with $S^{-1} = (I + R)^{-1}T^{-1}$ and
$$\|S^{-1}\| \le \frac{\|T^{-1}\|}{1 - q} \le \frac{C}{1 - C\|S - T\|}.$$
This is precisely the openness step, with the radius $\delta = 1/(2C\|L_1 - L_0\|)$ chosen so that $q \le \tfrac12$ and $\|S^{-1}\| \le 2C$ holds uniformly.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that under $(\ast)$ each $L_t$ has closed range, using completeness of $\mathcal{X}$ and boundedness of $L_t$, without invoking the open mapping theorem.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take $y_k = L_t x_k \to y$ and show $(x_k)$ is Cauchy.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $y \in \overline{\operatorname{ran} L_t}$ and pick $x_k \in \mathcal{X}$ with $L_t x_k \to y$. The sequence $(L_t x_k)$ is Cauchy, and by $(\ast)$,
$$\|x_k - x_m\|_{\mathcal{X}} \le C\,\|L_t x_k - L_t x_m\|_{\mathcal{Y}} \to 0,$$
so $(x_k)$ is Cauchy in $\mathcal{X}$. Completeness of $\mathcal{X}$ [02.11.04] gives $x_k \to x$ for some $x \in \mathcal{X}$, and continuity of $L_t$ gives $L_t x = \lim_k L_t x_k = y$. Hence $y \in \operatorname{ran} L_t$, so the range is closed. The estimate converts the range into a complete, hence closed, subspace by transporting Cauchyness from $\mathcal{Y}$ back to $\mathcal{X}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).**

Suppose the global Schauder estimate gives $\|u\|_{\mathcal X} \le C\|L_t u\|_{\mathcal Y}$ with $C = 5$ uniformly, and $\|L_1 - L_0\|_{\mathcal{B}(\mathcal X, \mathcal Y)} = 8$. The openness step covers a neighbourhood of each solvable $\tau$ of radius $\delta = 1/(2C\|L_1 - L_0\|)$. What is the smallest number of equal steps needed to walk from $t = 0$ to $t = 1$ with each step shorter than $\delta$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\delta$, then the number of steps is the least integer strictly greater than $1/\delta$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$81$.** Here $\delta = 1/(2 \cdot 5 \cdot 8) = 1/80$. To cover $[0,1]$ with steps each strictly shorter than $1/80$, one needs more than $80$ steps, so the least integer is $81$. (The continuity proof does not actually count steps — connectedness handles the cover at once — but the finite chain of overlapping Neumann-series neighbourhoods of radius $\delta$ makes the walk concrete, and its length scales like $C\|L_1 - L_0\|$.)

</details>

</aside>

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Set $v = u - \Phi$ and apply $L$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $v = u - \Phi$. On $\partial\Omega$, $v = u - \Phi = \varphi - \varphi = 0$, so $v$ satisfies the homogeneous boundary condition. Applying $L$, $Lv = Lu - L\Phi = f - L\Phi =: \tilde f$. Since $\Phi \in C^{2,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ and the coefficients lie in $C^{0,\alpha}$, the product rule for Hölder seminorms (02.17.04, Exercise 3) gives $L\Phi \in C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$, hence $\tilde f = f - L\Phi \in C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$. Conversely $u = v + \Phi$ recovers a solution of the original problem from one of the homogeneous problem. The two problems are equivalent, with $\tilde f = f - L\Phi$, which is why the method of continuity may be run on the fixed pair $(\mathcal X, \mathcal Y)$ with $\mathcal X$ carrying the zero boundary condition.

</details>

Assemble the full existence proof. Assume: (i) the global Schauder estimate $\|u\|_{C^{2,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)} \le C(\|L_t u\|_{C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)} + \|u\|_{C^0(\overline\Omega)})$ with $C$ independent of $t$; (ii) the maximum-principle bound $\|u\|_{C^0} \le C'\|L_t u\|_{C^0}$ for $u = 0$ on $\partial\Omega$, valid because each $L_t$ has $tc \le 0$, with $C'$ independent of $t$; (iii) solvability of $\Delta u = f$, $u = 0$ on $\partial\Omega$. Conclude that $L u = f$, $u = 0$ on $\partial\Omega$ is solvable in $C^{2,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ for every $f \in C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Combine (i) and (ii) to absorb $\|u\|_{C^0}$ into $\|L_t u\|_{C^{0,\alpha}}$, producing the hypothesis $(\ast)$; then quote the abstract theorem with the seed (iii).

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On $\mathcal{X} = \{u \in C^{2,\alpha}(\overline\Omega): u = 0 \text{ on }\partial\Omega\}$, the maximum-principle bound (ii) gives $\|u\|_{C^0} \le C'\|L_t u\|_{C^0} \le C'\|L_t u\|_{C^{0,\alpha}}$, since $\|\cdot\|_{C^0} \le \|\cdot\|_{C^{0,\alpha}}$. Substituting into the Schauder estimate (i),
$$\|u\|_{\mathcal X} = \|u\|_{C^{2,\alpha}} \le C(\|L_t u\|_{C^{0,\alpha}} + C'\|L_t u\|_{C^{0,\alpha}}) = C(1 + C')\,\|L_t u\|_{\mathcal Y},$$
which is the uniform a priori estimate $(\ast)$ with constant $\tilde C = C(1 + C')$ independent of $t$. The maps $L_t : \mathcal X \to \mathcal Y$ are bounded (Hölder product rule), and $L_t = (1-t)\Delta + tL$ is affine in $t$. By the seed (iii), $L_0 = \Delta$ is onto $\mathcal Y$. The abstract method-of-continuity theorem then gives $L_1 = L$ onto, so $Lu = f$, $u = 0$ on $\partial\Omega$ is solvable for every $f \in C^{0,\alpha}$; injectivity from $(\ast)$ makes the solution unique. Undoing the boundary reduction (Exercise 6) yields solvability for arbitrary $\varphi \in C^{2,\alpha}(\partial\Omega)$.

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show the continuity method *fails* — and explain how it must be replaced — when the sign condition is dropped. Let $\Omega = (0,\pi) \subset \mathbb{R}$ and $L u = u'' + u$, so $c = 1$. Exhibit the obstruction to $(\ast)$ for the family $L_t = (1-t)\partial_x^2 + t L = \partial_x^2 + t\,u$, and name the theory that handles $L$ instead.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

At which $t$ does $L_t u = u'' + t u$ acquire a nonzero solution vanishing at both endpoints? Recall $\sin x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The family is $L_t u = u'' + t u$ with $u(0) = u(\pi) = 0$. At $t = 1$, $u_*(x) = \sin x$ satisfies $u_*'' + u_* = 0$ and vanishes at both endpoints, so $u_* \in \ker L_1 \setminus \{0\}$: $L_1$ is not injective, and the a priori estimate $\|u\|_{\mathcal X} \le C\|L_t u\|_{\mathcal Y}$ fails as $t \to 1$ because $\|u_*\|_{\mathcal X} > 0$ while $\|L_1 u_*\|_{\mathcal Y} = 0$ forces $C \to \infty$. The threshold $t = 1$ is the principal Dirichlet eigenvalue $\lambda_1 = 1$ of $-\partial_x^2$ on $(0,\pi)$ (02.17.02). The uniform bound $(\ast)$ genuinely breaks, so the continuity method cannot run to $t = 1$. The correct replacement is the **Fredholm alternative**: $L - \lambda$ is invertible except on the discrete spectrum, and at an eigenvalue solvability holds if and only if $f$ is orthogonal to the kernel of the adjoint. The continuity method is the tool when the operator is *coercive enough to be invertible*; Fredholm theory is the tool when a finite-dimensional kernel appears.

## Advanced results [Master]

The continuity method organises the existence theory around four pillars: the abstract Banach-space dichotomy, its elliptic instance through the global Schauder estimate, the comparison with the constructive Perron route for the Laplacian, and the Fredholm extension that survives loss of the sign condition.

**Theorem 1 (existence for the classical Dirichlet problem; Schauder 1934).** Let $\Omega$ be a bounded $C^{2,\alpha}$ domain and $L$ uniformly elliptic with $C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ coefficients and $c \le 0$. For every $f \in C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ and $\varphi \in C^{2,\alpha}(\partial\Omega)$ the problem $Lu = f$ in $\Omega$, $u = \varphi$ on $\partial\Omega$ has a unique solution $u \in C^{2,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$, with
$$\|u\|_{C^{2,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)} \le C\big(\|f\|_{C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)} + \|\varphi\|_{C^{2,\alpha}(\partial\Omega)}\big), \quad C = C(n, \alpha, \lambda, \Lambda, \Omega)$$
[ref: TODO_REF Gilbarg-Trudinger 2001 §6.3, Theorem 6.14]. Existence is the continuity method from $\Delta$ to $L$ along $L_t = (1-t)\Delta + tL$; the global Schauder estimate [02.17.04] supplies the $t$-uniform hypothesis $(\ast)$ after the maximum principle [02.17.02] absorbs the $\|u\|_{C^0}$ term, and solvability of the model problem $\Delta u = f$ seeds $0 \in S$. Uniqueness and the final estimate are the maximum principle and Schauder estimate read on the solution itself.

**Theorem 2 (the abstract continuity method is sharp in its hypotheses).** The conclusion of the method-of-continuity theorem can fail if any single hypothesis is removed: without the $t$-uniform constant in $(\ast)$, $S$ need not be closed; without connectedness of the parameter set, $S$ need not be all of it; without the seed $0 \in S$, the dichotomy yields $S = \varnothing$. Each failure is realised by an explicit finite-dimensional family of matrices, so the theorem is a tight packaging of completeness, the bounded inverse theorem [02.11.09], and connectedness, with no slack.

**Theorem 3 (Perron's method for the Laplacian; Perron 1923).** For $L = \Delta$ and continuous boundary data $\varphi \in C(\partial\Omega)$ on a domain whose every boundary point admits a barrier, the Dirichlet problem $\Delta u = 0$, $u = \varphi$ on $\partial\Omega$ has a unique harmonic solution, obtained as the upper envelope
$$u(x) = \sup\{ w(x) : w \text{ subharmonic on } \Omega,\ \limsup_{y \to \xi} w(y) \le \varphi(\xi)\ \forall \xi \in \partial\Omega \}$$
[ref: TODO_REF Perron 1923]. This construction is *specific to the averaging structure*: it uses that the maximum of two subharmonic functions is subharmonic and that harmonic replacement (the Poisson integral on a ball) lowers no subharmonic competitor. It needs neither a $C^{2,\alpha}$ boundary nor Hölder data — only continuity and a barrier at each boundary point — but it produces only a harmonic function, with no built-in interior regularity beyond what elliptic regularity later supplies. The continuity method, by contrast, needs the full Schauder machinery and a regular boundary but applies verbatim to any uniformly elliptic $L$ with $C^{0,\alpha}$ coefficients, for which no mean-value envelope exists [02.13.01].

**Theorem 4 (Fredholm alternative for elliptic Dirichlet problems).** Drop the sign condition: let $L$ be uniformly elliptic with $C^{0,\alpha}$ coefficients on a bounded $C^{2,\alpha}$ domain, $c$ of arbitrary sign. Then the operator $L : \mathcal X \to \mathcal Y$ is Fredholm of index zero, and the Dirichlet problem $Lu = f$, $u = 0$ on $\partial\Omega$ is solvable for a given $f$ if and only if $f$ is $L^2$-orthogonal to the (finite-dimensional) kernel of the formal adjoint $L^*$ with its homogeneous boundary condition. Equivalently, either $Lu = f$ has a unique solution for every $f$, or the homogeneous problem $Lu = 0$ has a nontrivial solution. The Schauder estimate now reads $\|u\|_{C^{2,\alpha}} \le C(\|Lu\|_{C^{0,\alpha}} + \|u\|_{C^0})$ with the $\|u\|_{C^0}$ term no longer absorbable, and compactness of the embedding $C^{2,\alpha} \hookrightarrow C^{0,\alpha}$ on a bounded domain (Arzelà-Ascoli) turns $L$ into a compact perturbation of an isomorphism, which is the source of the Fredholm structure.

**Theorem 5 (Leray-Schauder fixed-point and the nonlinear extension; Leray-Schauder 1934).** The linear continuity method is the linear shadow of the Leray-Schauder degree-theoretic fixed-point theorem [ref: TODO_REF Leray-Schauder 1934]: a uniform a priori bound on the solutions of a continuous family of equations, plus solvability at one end, yields solvability throughout. For a quasilinear elliptic equation $Q[u] = 0$, one embeds it in a family $Q_t[u] = 0$, establishes $C^{2,\alpha}$ a priori bounds *independent of $t$ and of the particular solution* (typically through maximum-principle, gradient, and Hölder-gradient estimates), and concludes existence by degree theory. The linear method-of-continuity theorem is recovered when $Q_t$ is affine, the Neumann-series openness replacing the degree computation. This is the route to existence for the minimal-surface and prescribed-mean-curvature equations.

**Theorem 6 (variational alternative via Lax-Milgram).** For $L$ in *divergence form* $Lu = -D_i(a^{ij}D_j u) + cu$ with $c \ge 0$ and merely bounded measurable coefficients, existence of a weak solution in $H^1_0(\Omega)$ follows instead from the Lax-Milgram theorem: the bilinear form $B[u,v] = \int_\Omega a^{ij}D_j u\, D_i v + cuv$ is bounded and coercive on $H^1_0$, so $B[u, \cdot] = \langle f, \cdot\rangle$ has a unique solution. This requires no Schauder estimate and no boundary regularity, but yields only an $H^1$ weak solution whose classical regularity must then be recovered by De Giorgi-Nash-Moser [02.17.04] or Schauder bootstrapping. The continuity method and the variational method are the two canonical existence routes: one rides an a priori estimate in Hölder spaces, the other minimises an energy in Sobolev spaces.

**Synthesis.** The method of continuity is the foundational reason existence for the general elliptic Dirichlet problem reduces to existence for the Laplacian plus one uniform estimate, and the entire structure rests on a single duality: the a priori Schauder bound, a statement about hypothetical solutions, is dual to surjectivity of the operator, and putting these together the open-and-closed walk along $L_t = (1-t)\Delta + tL$ converts the bound into genuine solvability. This is exactly the statement that an estimate proved before existence is the very thing that proves existence, and the central insight is that connectedness of the parameter interval forbids solvability from developing a ragged edge. The bridge is the Schauder estimate of [02.17.04]: it is the rail, the maximum principle [02.17.02] trims its $\|u\|_{C^0}$ term to make the constant uniform, and the bounded inverse and open mapping theorems of [02.11.09] convert the resulting injectivity-with-closed-range into a bounded inverse — the three inputs whose conjunction is the existence theorem.

The method generalises in three directions, each loosening one ingredient. When the sign condition fails and $L$ acquires a kernel, the uniform estimate breaks and the continuity method is replaced by the Fredholm alternative, where compactness of $C^{2,\alpha} \hookrightarrow C^{0,\alpha}$ supplies index zero and solvability becomes an orthogonality condition; this is dual to the spectral picture of [02.17.02], the kernel appearing exactly at the principal-eigenvalue threshold. When the equation is nonlinear, the linear Neumann-series openness is replaced by Leray-Schauder degree, and the a priori bound must be proved *uniformly over solutions*, which is the hard analytic content of quasilinear existence theory. When one abandons classical regularity for the weak formulation, the continuity method is supplanted by Lax-Milgram coercivity in $H^1_0$, trading the Hölder a priori estimate for an energy inequality and the open-and-closed walk for the Riesz representation theorem. The Perron envelope sits apart from all three: it is the one genuinely *constructive* existence proof, available only because the Laplacian's mean-value structure lets subharmonic competitors be combined and lifted, and it is exactly the structure the continuity method dispenses with.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (uniform estimate implies injective with bounded inverse on the range).** Let $\mathcal X, \mathcal Y$ be Banach and $T \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal X, \mathcal Y)$ satisfy $\|x\| \le C\|Tx\|$ for all $x$. Then $T$ is injective, $\operatorname{ran} T$ is closed, and $T : \mathcal X \to \operatorname{ran} T$ is a topological isomorphism with $\|T^{-1}\|_{\mathcal{B}(\operatorname{ran} T, \mathcal X)} \le C$.

*Proof.* Injectivity: $Tx = 0 \Rightarrow \|x\| \le C\cdot 0 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0$. Closed range: if $Tx_k \to y$, then $(Tx_k)$ is Cauchy and $\|x_k - x_m\| \le C\|Tx_k - Tx_m\| \to 0$, so $(x_k)$ is Cauchy in the complete space $\mathcal X$ [02.11.04] with limit $x$, and $Tx = \lim Tx_k = y$, so $y \in \operatorname{ran} T$. The map $T : \mathcal X \to \operatorname{ran} T$ is thus a continuous bijection onto a Banach space, and the inverse satisfies $\|T^{-1}y\| = \|x\| \le C\|Tx\| = C\|y\|$ for $y = Tx$, giving $\|T^{-1}\| \le C$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (openness via Neumann series).** Let $T \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal X, \mathcal Y)$ be a Banach-space isomorphism with $\|T^{-1}\| \le C$. Every $S \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal X, \mathcal Y)$ with $\|S - T\| < 1/C$ is an isomorphism, with $\|S^{-1}\| \le C/(1 - C\|S - T\|)$.

*Proof.* Set $R = T^{-1}(S - T) \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal X)$, so $\|R\| \le C\|S - T\| =: q < 1$. The series $\sum_{k\ge 0}(-R)^k$ is absolutely convergent in the Banach algebra $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal X)$ ($\sum\|R\|^k \le (1-q)^{-1} < \infty$), with sum $U$ satisfying $(I + R)U = U(I + R) = I$; thus $I + R$ is invertible and $\|(I+R)^{-1}\| \le (1-q)^{-1}$. Since $S = T(I + R)$ is a composition of isomorphisms, it is an isomorphism with $S^{-1} = (I + R)^{-1}T^{-1}$ and $\|S^{-1}\| \le (1-q)^{-1}\|T^{-1}\| \le C/(1 - C\|S - T\|)$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (the solvable set is open, closed, and nonempty).** Under hypothesis $(\ast)$ with $0 \in S = \{t : L_t \text{ onto}\}$, the set $S$ is open and closed in $[0,1]$ and contains $0$; hence $S = [0,1]$.

*Proof.* By Proposition 1 every $L_t$ is injective with $\|L_t^{-1}\|_{\mathcal{B}(\operatorname{ran}, \mathcal X)} \le C$. If $\tau \in S$ then $\operatorname{ran} L_\tau = \mathcal Y$, so $L_\tau$ is an isomorphism with $\|L_\tau^{-1}\| \le C$ by the open mapping theorem [02.11.09]. Proposition 2 with $T = L_\tau$, $S = L_t$, and $\|L_t - L_\tau\| = |t - \tau|\,\|L_1 - L_0\|$ shows $L_t$ is an isomorphism — in particular onto — whenever $|t - \tau| < \delta := (2C\|L_1 - L_0\|)^{-1}$. So $(\tau - \delta, \tau + \delta)\cap[0,1] \subseteq S$ and $S$ is open. For closedness, suppose $t_k \to t$ with $t_k \in S$; choose $k$ with $|t_k - t| < \delta$, then $t \in (t_k - \delta, t_k + \delta) \subseteq S$ by openness applied at $\tau = t_k$ (valid since $t_k \in S$). Thus $S$ is closed. As $0 \in S$ and $[0,1]$ is connected, the only clopen nonempty subset is the whole interval: $S = [0,1]$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (the elliptic family has $t$-uniform structure constants).** For $L_t = (1-t)\Delta + tL$ with $L$ uniformly elliptic ($\lambda, \Lambda$), $a^{ij}, b^i, c \in C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ of norm $\le \Lambda$, and $c \le 0$, the operator $L_t$ is uniformly elliptic with ellipticity constants and coefficient Hölder norms bounded independently of $t \in [0,1]$, and has zeroth-order coefficient $\le 0$.

*Proof.* The second-order coefficient matrix of $L_t$ is $a^{ij}_t = (1-t)\delta^{ij} + t a^{ij}$. For $\xi \in \mathbb{R}^n$, $\sum a^{ij}_t \xi_i\xi_j = (1-t)|\xi|^2 + t\sum a^{ij}\xi_i\xi_j$, which lies between $(1-t)|\xi|^2 + t\lambda|\xi|^2 = ((1-t) + t\lambda)|\xi|^2 \ge \min(1,\lambda)|\xi|^2$ and $\max(1,\Lambda)|\xi|^2$; both bounds are independent of $t$. The Hölder norms satisfy $\|a^{ij}_t\|_{C^{0,\alpha}} \le (1-t)\|\delta^{ij}\|_{C^{0,\alpha}} + t\|a^{ij}\|_{C^{0,\alpha}} \le \max(1,\Lambda)$, and likewise $\|t b^i\|_{C^{0,\alpha}} \le \Lambda$, $\|tc\|_{C^{0,\alpha}} \le \Lambda$, all $t$-uniformly. Finally $tc \le 0$ since $t \ge 0$ and $c \le 0$. The structural data feeding the global Schauder estimate are therefore $t$-independent, which is precisely why the constant in $(\ast)$ can be taken uniform. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (boundary-data reduction preserves the data class).** Let $\Omega$ be a bounded $C^{2,\alpha}$ domain, $\varphi \in C^{2,\alpha}(\partial\Omega)$, and $\Phi \in C^{2,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ an extension with $\Phi|_{\partial\Omega} = \varphi$. If $u \in C^{2,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ solves $Lu = f$, $u = \varphi$, then $v = u - \Phi$ solves $Lv = f - L\Phi$, $v = 0$, with $f - L\Phi \in C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$; and conversely.

*Proof.* On $\partial\Omega$, $v = u - \Phi = \varphi - \varphi = 0$. By linearity $Lv = Lu - L\Phi = f - L\Phi$. Each term of $L\Phi = \sum a^{ij}D_{ij}\Phi + \sum b^i D_i\Phi + c\Phi$ is a product of a $C^{0,\alpha}$ coefficient with a $C^{0,\alpha}$ derivative of $\Phi$ (the derivatives $D_{ij}\Phi$ are $C^{0,\alpha}$ since $\Phi \in C^{2,\alpha}$); by the Hölder product rule [02.17.04] each product is $C^{0,\alpha}$, so $L\Phi \in C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ and $f - L\Phi \in C^{0,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$. The converse map $u = v + \Phi$ reverses every step. The two Dirichlet problems are therefore equivalent on the fixed pair $(\mathcal X, \mathcal Y)$, with the existence of the extension $\Phi$ guaranteed by the $C^{2,\alpha}$ regularity of $\partial\Omega$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The global Schauder estimate of [02.17.04] is the analytic input that makes the continuity method fire: it is the source of the uniform a priori bound $(\ast)$, and Proposition 4 here is precisely the check that its structural constants are $t$-independent along $L_t$. The two units are a matched pair — one proves the estimate on hypothetical solutions, the other spends it to produce actual solutions — and the existence theorem stated as the capstone of [02.17.04] is proved in full here.

- The Banach-space fundamentals of [02.11.04] supply the completeness that turns the uniform estimate into closed range (Propositions 1 and 3), and the open mapping and closed graph theorems of [02.11.09] supply the bounded inverse theorem that converts a continuous bijection into a topological isomorphism with controlled inverse norm; without these two functional-analytic theorems the open-and-closed walk has no rails. The continuity method is the canonical PDE application that makes the abstract bounded inverse theorem indispensable.

- The maximum principle for general elliptic operators in [02.17.02] does two jobs: it gives the $\|u\|_{C^0}$ sup bound that absorbs the lower-order term of the Schauder estimate to make the constant in $(\ast)$ uniform, and it supplies the injectivity (uniqueness) that, together with surjectivity from the continuity method, makes each $L_t$ an isomorphism. The Fredholm extension (Theorem 4) is dual to the principal-eigenvalue threshold of that unit: the kernel appears exactly when $c$ crosses $\lambda_1$.

- The Laplace-equation theory of [02.13.01] furnishes both the seed and the foil. Solvability of $\Delta u = f$ on a $C^{2,\alpha}$ domain is the point $0 \in S$ that the dichotomy needs, and Perron's mean-value construction (Theorem 3) is the constructive alternative the continuity method deliberately forgoes, applying where no averaging structure survives. The contrast isolates what is special about the Laplacian and what is general about second-order ellipticity.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The method of continuity in elliptic theory is due to Juliusz Schauder, who in his 1934 *Mathematische Zeitschrift* paper [ref: TODO_REF Schauder 1934] combined the a priori Hölder estimates that now bear his name with a Banach-space continuation argument to prove solvability of the Dirichlet problem for general second-order elliptic operators with Hölder coefficients. The idea of continuing along a parameter to transport solvability from a tractable problem to a target had precedents in the work of Bernstein and in the Leray-Schauder topological-degree paper of the same year [ref: TODO_REF Leray-Schauder 1934], which established the nonlinear fixed-point theory of which the linear continuity method is the affine special case. The decisive conceptual move was Schauder's: to recognise that the regularity estimate, proved for hypothetical solutions before existence is known, is precisely the ingredient that, fed into a continuation argument over a connected parameter, yields existence.

The constructive predecessor for the Laplacian is Oskar Perron's 1923 *Mathematische Zeitschrift* treatment of the first boundary value problem for $\Delta u = 0$ [ref: TODO_REF Perron 1923], which built the harmonic solution as the upper envelope of subharmonic competitors and reduced the question of boundary attainment to the local existence of barriers. Perron's method is older, more elementary, and entirely constructive, but it is wedded to the averaging structure of the Laplacian and to continuous rather than Hölder data; it produces a harmonic function whose interior smoothness must be supplied separately. Schauder's continuity method runs in the opposite spirit: it is non-constructive, demands the full Hölder a priori apparatus and a regular boundary, and in exchange applies to every uniformly elliptic operator with no mean-value structure to exploit. The two methods coexist in the modern theory — Perron for the model harmonic problem with rough data, continuity for the general operator with smooth data — and Gilbarg and Trudinger present both, in §2.8 and §6.3 respectively [ref: TODO_REF Gilbarg-Trudinger 2001 §6.3].
