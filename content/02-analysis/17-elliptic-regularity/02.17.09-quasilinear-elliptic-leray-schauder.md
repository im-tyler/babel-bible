## Intuition [Beginner]

Picture a soap film stretched across a bent wire loop. The film settles into the shape that makes its area as small as possible, and the equation it obeys is no longer the simple averaging law of a flat membrane: how strongly the film resists bending at a point depends on how steeply it is already tilted there. An equation whose coefficients depend on the slope of the unknown itself, not just on position, is called *quasilinear*. The minimal surface is the headline example, and the whole difficulty of the subject lives in that feedback loop between the solution and the coefficients that govern it.

When the coefficients depend on the slope, a new danger appears: the slope might run away. A solution could in principle develop an ever-steeper cliff somewhere inside the region, and if the slope blows up, the equation degenerates and the whole theory collapses. The central achievement of the subject is a guarantee that this never happens for the right kind of equation: the steepness of the solution in the interior is capped in advance by a number computed from the size of the solution and the data on the boundary. This cap is the *gradient estimate*, and it is the keystone on which everything else rests.

Why does a slope cap unlock existence of solutions? Because of a beautiful strategy: deform the hard equation continuously into an easy one you can already solve, and show that no solution along the deformation can escape to infinity. If every candidate solution stays trapped inside a fixed-size box — bounded in value, bounded in slope, bounded in the smoothness of its slope — then a topological fixed-point principle guarantees a genuine solution exists at the hard end. The slope cap is exactly the wall of that box.

The one-sentence takeaway: for the right quasilinear equations, the slope of any solution is bounded in advance by the boundary data, and that single advance bound, fed into a fixed-point machine, produces a solution out of thin air.

That fixed box is what the topological fixed-point theorem needs as its input.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We make the slope-cap idea concrete on the one-dimensional minimal surface equation, where everything is plain calculus. In one variable the minimal surface equation says the curvature term
$$\frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{u'(x)}{\sqrt{1 + u'(x)^2}}\right) = 0,$$
so the quantity inside the bracket is constant. Call that constant $c$.

Step 1. Read off the slope. Setting $u'/\sqrt{1 + (u')^2} = c$ and solving for $u'$ gives $u' = c/\sqrt{1 - c^2}$, a constant. So in one dimension every solution is a straight line. That is the slope cap in its simplest form: the slope is literally constant.

Step 2. Watch the cap depend on the data. Suppose the line runs from height $0$ at $x = 0$ to height $H$ at $x = 1$. Then the slope is exactly $H$, the rise over the run. The interior slope is pinned by the boundary heights: bigger boundary spread means a bigger slope, but always a finite one.

Step 3. See why the bracket matters. The bracketed quantity $c = u'/\sqrt{1 + (u')^2}$ is always between $-1$ and $1$, no matter how large $u'$ is. This is the feature that tames the equation: the curvature operator saturates. As the slope grows, the resisting term levels off rather than exploding.

Step 4. Put in numbers. If $H = 3$, the slope is $3$, and the bracket value is $c = 3/\sqrt{1 + 9} = 3/\sqrt{10} \approx 0.95$, comfortably below $1$. A boundary spread of $3$ over a unit interval produces a slope of $3$ — capped, finite, and computed entirely from the boundary data.

What this tells us: in one dimension the slope cap is automatic because solutions are straight lines, but the principle survives in every dimension — the interior slope is controlled by the boundary spread. The hard theorems prove the same kind of cap when the solution is a genuinely curved surface and the slope is no longer constant.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ be a bounded domain. A second-order operator is **quasilinear** when it is linear in the second derivatives with coefficients depending on $(x, u, Du)$. The **divergence (variational) form** treated here is
$$Q u := \operatorname{div} A(x, u, Du) + B(x, u, Du) = \sum_{i=1}^n D_i\,A_i(x, u, Du) + B(x, u, Du),$$
with $A = (A_1, \dots, A_n) : \Omega \times \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ and $B : \Omega \times \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$. A function $u \in C^1(\Omega) \cap C^0(\overline\Omega)$ (or $u \in W^{1,m}(\Omega)$ in the weak sense) is a **solution** of $Qu = 0$ when
$$\int_\Omega \Big( A(x, u, Du)\cdot D\varphi - B(x, u, Du)\,\varphi \Big)\,dx = 0 \qquad \text{for all } \varphi \in C_c^\infty(\Omega).$$
The operator is **elliptic** at $u$ when the matrix $\big(\partial A_i/\partial p_j\big)(x, u, Du)$ is positive definite. The model is the **prescribed mean curvature equation**, $A(x,u,p) = p/\sqrt{1 + |p|^2}$ and $B = nH(x)$, whose $H \equiv 0$ case is the **minimal surface equation** $\operatorname{div}\big(Du/\sqrt{1 + |Du|^2}\big) = 0$; the **$p$-Laplacian** $\operatorname{div}(|Du|^{m-2}Du) = 0$ is the other touchstone [ref: TODO_REF Gilbarg-Trudinger 2001 §10.1].

The estimates require **structure conditions** of *natural growth*: there are constants $\mu > 0$, $0 < \lambda \le \Lambda$, and an exponent $m > 1$ such that, writing $p = Du$,
$$\sum_{i,j}\frac{\partial A_i}{\partial p_j}\,\xi_i\xi_j \ge \lambda\,(1 + |p|)^{m-2}|\xi|^2, \qquad \Big|\frac{\partial A_i}{\partial p_j}\Big| \le \Lambda\,(1 + |p|)^{m-2},$$
$$|A(x,u,p)| \le \mu\,(1 + |p|)^{m-1}, \qquad |B(x,u,p)| \le \mu\,(1 + |p|)^{m}.$$
The first pair is **uniform ellipticity relative to the $m$-growth**; the lower bound is what forces a slope cap. The minimal surface equation has $m = 2$ but the ellipticity *degenerates*, $\partial A_i/\partial p_j \sim (1 + |p|^2)^{-1/2}$ decaying as $|p| \to \infty$, which is the source of its delicacy; the $p$-Laplacian has $m = $ the exponent and degenerates at $p = 0$.

**Definition (a priori gradient estimate).** A class of solutions of $Qu = 0$ on $\Omega$ satisfies an **interior gradient estimate** when, for $\Omega' \Subset \Omega$, there is a constant $C$ depending only on $n$, the structure constants, $\operatorname{dist}(\Omega', \partial\Omega)$, and $M = \sup_\Omega |u|$, with $\sup_{\Omega'} |Du| \le C$; and a **global gradient estimate** when, for solutions with prescribed boundary data $u = \varphi$ on $\partial\Omega$, $\sup_\Omega |Du| \le C(n, \text{structure}, M, \Omega, \varphi)$, independent of the particular solution.

**Definition (Leray-Schauder / Schaefer form).** Let $\mathfrak{B}$ be a Banach space and $T : \mathfrak{B} \to \mathfrak{B}$ compact. **Schaefer's fixed-point theorem** asserts: if the set $\{x \in \mathfrak{B} : x = \sigma\,Tx \text{ for some } \sigma \in [0,1]\}$ is **bounded**, then $T$ has a fixed point [ref: TODO_REF Schaefer 1955]. This is the form of the Leray-Schauder theorem [ref: TODO_REF Leray-Schauder 1934] in which existence reduces to an a priori bound on the solution set of the homotopy $x = \sigma Tx$, with no construction of the solution.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *Quasilinear is not semilinear.* In a semilinear equation $\Delta u = f(x, u, Du)$ the top-order part is the fixed Laplacian; in a quasilinear equation the top-order coefficients themselves depend on $Du$. The minimal surface operator is genuinely quasilinear, and the gradient dependence of the leading coefficients is exactly what can degenerate.

- *The gradient estimate is not a consequence of the maximum principle alone.* The maximum principle bounds $\sup |u|$, but a bounded function can have arbitrarily large slope. The gradient bound is a separate, harder theorem, obtained by differentiating the equation and applying the De Giorgi-Nash-Moser machinery [02.17.07] to $w = |Du|^2$ or to individual derivatives $D_k u$.

- *Natural growth is sharp, not decorative.* If $B$ grows faster than $(1 + |p|)^m$ — say like $|p|^{m + \varepsilon}$ — the gradient estimate fails and solutions can fail to exist for large data. The exponent $m$ on $B$ matches the divergence structure precisely so the cutoff-function test integrals close; faster growth breaks the absorption.

- *Schaefer's hypothesis is the a priori bound, not solvability of the homotopy at every $\sigma$.* One does not solve $x = \sigma Tx$ for each $\sigma$; one bounds the set of all solutions that exist, uniformly in $\sigma$, and lets the topological degree do the rest. Confusing "bound the solution set" with "solve at each $\sigma$" inverts the logic of the method.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (existence by Leray-Schauder via a priori bounds; Gilbarg-Trudinger §11.4).** Let $\Omega$ be a bounded $C^{2,\alpha}$ domain and $Qu = \operatorname{div} A(x,u,Du) + B(x,u,Du)$ a quasilinear elliptic operator with $A, B \in C^{1,\alpha}$ satisfying the natural structure conditions and $\partial B/\partial u \le 0$. Suppose there is a constant $M$, **independent of $u$ and of $\sigma \in [0,1]$**, such that every $C^{2,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ solution of the homotopy family
$$Q_\sigma u := \operatorname{div} A(x, u, Du) + \sigma B(x, u, Du) = 0 \ \text{ in } \Omega, \qquad u = \sigma\varphi \ \text{ on } \partial\Omega,$$
satisfies $\|u\|_{C^{1,\beta}(\overline\Omega)} \le M$ for some $\beta \in (0,1)$. Then the Dirichlet problem $Qu = 0$, $u = \varphi$ on $\partial\Omega$, has a solution $u \in C^{2,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ [ref: TODO_REF Gilbarg-Trudinger 2001 §11.4] [ref: TODO_REF Serrin 1969]. The crux is that the a priori $C^{1,\beta}$ bound is exactly the boundedness hypothesis Schaefer's theorem demands of the homotopy solution set.

*Proof.* Define the solution operator. Fix $\beta \in (0,\alpha)$ and let $\mathfrak{B} = C^{1,\beta}(\overline\Omega)$. For $v \in \mathfrak{B}$, **freeze the nonlinearity**: the coefficients $a^{ij}(x) = \partial A_i/\partial p_j(x, v, Dv)$ and the data $f(x) = -B(x, v, Dv) + \text{(lower terms from }A\text{)}$ are now functions of $x$ alone, of class $C^{0,\beta}(\overline\Omega)$, and the *linear* Dirichlet problem
$$a^{ij}(x)\,D_{ij} u + (\text{first order}) = f(x) \ \text{ in } \Omega, \qquad u = \varphi \ \text{ on } \partial\Omega,$$
has a unique solution $u = T v \in C^{2,\beta}(\overline\Omega)$ by the linear Schauder existence theory [02.17.05]. This defines $T : \mathfrak{B} \to \mathfrak{B}$, and a fixed point $u = Tu$ is precisely a solution of $Qu = 0$.

*Step 1: $T$ is compact.* The linear Schauder estimate [02.17.05] gives $\|Tv\|_{C^{2,\beta}(\overline\Omega)} \le C\big(\|f\|_{C^{0,\beta}} + \|\varphi\|_{C^{2,\beta}}\big)$, where $C$ depends on the ellipticity and $C^{0,\beta}$ norms of the frozen coefficients, hence on $\|v\|_{C^{1,\beta}}$. Thus $T$ maps bounded sets of $\mathfrak{B} = C^{1,\beta}$ into bounded sets of $C^{2,\beta}$. The embedding $C^{2,\beta}(\overline\Omega) \hookrightarrow C^{1,\beta}(\overline\Omega)$ is compact by Arzelà-Ascoli on a bounded domain [02.11.05] (the inclusion is a compact operator: bounded $C^{2,\beta}$ sets are precompact in $C^{1,\beta}$). Composing, $T$ sends bounded sets into precompact sets, so $T$ is compact. Continuity of $T$ follows from continuous dependence of the linear solution on its coefficients and data.

*Step 2: the homotopy is $x = \sigma Tx$.* For $\sigma \in [0,1]$ a fixed point of $\sigma T$ solves $a^{ij}(x, u, Du) D_{ij} u + \dots = \sigma f$ with boundary data $\sigma\varphi$, which after unfreezing is exactly the family $Q_\sigma u = 0$, $u = \sigma\varphi$. At $\sigma = 0$ the only solution is $u \equiv 0$. The hypothesis says every solution of $u = \sigma Tu$ over all $\sigma \in [0,1]$ satisfies $\|u\|_{C^{1,\beta}} \le M$, so the set $\{u : u = \sigma Tu,\ \sigma \in [0,1]\}$ is bounded in $\mathfrak{B}$.

*Step 3: apply Schaefer.* $T$ is compact and the homotopy solution set is bounded, so by Schaefer's theorem [ref: TODO_REF Schaefer 1955] $T$ has a fixed point $u \in \mathfrak{B}$. Unfreezing, $u$ solves $Qu = 0$, $u = \varphi$ on $\partial\Omega$. Linear Schauder regularity bootstraps $u \in C^{2,\beta}$ and then $C^{2,\alpha}$ since $A, B \in C^{1,\alpha}$. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem is the foundational reason quasilinear existence is *entirely* a question of a priori estimates: the compactness of $T$ and the topological degree are automatic from the linear theory of [02.17.05] and the compact embedding of [02.11.05], so all the analytic work is pushed into the single uniform bound $\|u\|_{C^{1,\beta}} \le M$. This builds toward the gradient estimates proved in the Advanced results, which supply exactly that bound, and it appears again in [02.18.04] where the same Leray-Schauder scheme yields minimal surfaces from the area functional. The central insight is that existence is dual to an a priori estimate — this is exactly the nonlinear generalisation of the linear method of continuity of [02.17.05], where the Neumann-series openness is replaced by the Leray-Schauder degree and the uniform linear estimate is replaced by a nonlinear, solution-uniform $C^{1,\beta}$ bound. Putting these together, the chain of estimates $\sup|u| \to \sup|Du| \to [Du]_{C^\beta}$ generalises the linear sup-bound-plus-Schauder-bound chain, and the bridge is the compact solution operator $T$, which converts each estimate into a wall of the bounded set that Schaefer's theorem turns into a fixed point.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the divergence-form minimal surface equation as the Euler-Lagrange equation of the area functional $\mathcal{A}[u] = \int_\Omega \sqrt{1 + |Du|^2}\,dx$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\frac{d}{dt}\big|_{t=0}\mathcal{A}[u + t\varphi]$ for $\varphi \in C_c^\infty(\Omega)$ and integrate by parts.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The first variation is
$$\frac{d}{dt}\Big|_{t=0}\int_\Omega \sqrt{1 + |D(u + t\varphi)|^2}\,dx = \int_\Omega \frac{Du \cdot D\varphi}{\sqrt{1 + |Du|^2}}\,dx.$$
Integrating by parts and using $\varphi \in C_c^\infty(\Omega)$ to drop the boundary term,
$$= -\int_\Omega \operatorname{div}\!\Big(\frac{Du}{\sqrt{1 + |Du|^2}}\Big)\,\varphi\,dx.$$
Since this vanishes for all test functions $\varphi$, the fundamental lemma of the calculus of variations forces $\operatorname{div}\big(Du/\sqrt{1 + |Du|^2}\big) = 0$ pointwise. This is the divergence form $\operatorname{div} A(Du) = 0$ with $A(p) = p/\sqrt{1 + |p|^2}$, and identifies $B \equiv 0$. The prescribed-mean-curvature case $\operatorname{div} A(Du) = nH$ is the Euler-Lagrange equation of $\mathcal{A}[u] + n\int_\Omega H u\,dx$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the structure condition $\sum_{i,j}(\partial A_i/\partial p_j)\xi_i\xi_j \ge \lambda(1 + |p|)^{m-2}|\xi|^2$ holds for the $p$-Laplacian $A(p) = |p|^{m-2}p$ with $\lambda = \min(1, m-1)$, and identify where the ellipticity degenerates.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\partial A_i/\partial p_j = |p|^{m-2}\delta_{ij} + (m-2)|p|^{m-4}p_i p_j$ and test against $\xi$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Differentiating $A_i = |p|^{m-2}p_i$,
$$\frac{\partial A_i}{\partial p_j} = |p|^{m-2}\delta_{ij} + (m-2)|p|^{m-4}p_i p_j.$$
Contracting with $\xi$,
$$\sum_{i,j}\frac{\partial A_i}{\partial p_j}\xi_i\xi_j = |p|^{m-2}|\xi|^2 + (m-2)|p|^{m-4}(p\cdot\xi)^2.$$
For $m \ge 2$ the second term is non-negative, so the sum is $\ge |p|^{m-2}|\xi|^2$. For $1 < m < 2$ use $(p\cdot\xi)^2 \le |p|^2|\xi|^2$ to get $\ge |p|^{m-2}|\xi|^2 + (m-2)|p|^{m-2}|\xi|^2 = (m-1)|p|^{m-2}|\xi|^2$. In both cases the bound is $\min(1, m-1)|p|^{m-2}|\xi|^2$, comparable to $(1+|p|)^{m-2}|\xi|^2$ for $|p|$ bounded below. The ellipticity **degenerates at $p = 0$**: when $m > 2$ the coefficient $|p|^{m-2}$ vanishes there, and when $m < 2$ it blows up, so the $p$-Laplacian is non-uniformly elliptic exactly at critical points of $u$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Sketch how the interior gradient bound for a solution is reduced to a maximum principle for $w = |Du|^2$. Differentiate $Qu = 0$ in the direction $x_k$, multiply by $D_k u$, and sum, to show $w$ is a subsolution of a linear elliptic equation with coefficients frozen at $Du$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Set $a^{ij} = \partial A_i/\partial p_j$. Differentiating $D_i A_i(x,u,Du) = -B$ in $x_k$ produces a linear equation for $D_k u$ with leading coefficients $a^{ij}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $a^{ij}(x) = (\partial A_i/\partial p_j)(x, u, Du)$, evaluated along the fixed solution, so $a^{ij}$ is elliptic by the structure condition. Differentiating the equation $D_i A_i(x, u, Du) + B = 0$ in the direction $x_k$ gives
$$D_i\big(a^{ij}D_j(D_k u)\big) = -D_i\big(\partial_{x_k}A_i + \partial_u A_i\, D_k u\big) - \partial_{x_k}B - \partial_u B\,D_k u - \partial_{p_j}B\,D_j(D_k u),$$
a *linear* divergence-form equation for the derivative $v_k = D_k u$ with leading matrix $a^{ij}$. Multiplying by $v_k = D_k u$, summing over $k$, and using the product rule, the quantity $w = |Du|^2 = \sum_k v_k^2$ satisfies $D_i(a^{ij}D_j w) \ge -C(1 + w)^{?}$, i.e. $w$ is a subsolution of a linear elliptic equation whose coefficients are frozen at $Du$. The De Giorgi-Nash-Moser local boundedness theorem [02.17.07] applied to this linear equation bounds $\sup w$, hence $\sup |Du|$, in the interior, completing the gradient estimate. The whole nonlinearity is absorbed into the (now fixed) coefficients $a^{ij}$, which is why the linear theory of [02.17.07] suffices once the equation is differentiated.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the abstract Schaefer fixed-point theorem from the Leray-Schauder degree: if $T : \mathfrak{B} \to \mathfrak{B}$ is compact and the set $\Sigma = \{x : x = \sigma Tx,\ \sigma \in [0,1]\}$ is bounded, then $T$ has a fixed point. You may use that the Leray-Schauder degree $\deg(I - \sigma T, B_R, 0)$ is a homotopy invariant for compact $T$ on a ball $B_R$ with no zeros on $\partial B_R$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Choose $R$ larger than the bound on $\Sigma$. The homotopy $\sigma \mapsto I - \sigma T$ has no zeros on $\partial B_R$. Compare the degree at $\sigma = 0$ and $\sigma = 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $\Sigma$ is bounded, pick $R > \sup_{x \in \Sigma}\|x\| + 1$, so no solution of $x = \sigma Tx$ lies on $\partial B_R$ for any $\sigma \in [0,1]$; equivalently $I - \sigma T$ has no zero on $\partial B_R$. The map $H(\sigma, x) = x - \sigma Tx$ is an admissible homotopy of compact perturbations of the identity (each $\sigma T$ is compact), with $0 \notin H(\sigma, \partial B_R)$ for all $\sigma$. By homotopy invariance of the Leray-Schauder degree,
$$\deg(I - T, B_R, 0) = \deg(I - 0\cdot T, B_R, 0) = \deg(I, B_R, 0) = 1.$$
A nonzero degree forces the existence of a solution of $(I - T)x = 0$ in $B_R$, i.e. a fixed point $x = Tx$. The boundedness of $\Sigma$ was used exactly to keep the homotopy zero-free on the boundary so the degree is defined and invariant; the seed value $1$ comes from the identity map $I$ at $\sigma = 0$. This is the topological content that the linear method of continuity [02.17.05] captures via the Neumann series in the affine case.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

The minimal surface equation is solvable for *arbitrary* continuous boundary data on $\Omega$ if and only if $\partial\Omega$ has non-negative mean curvature everywhere (Jenkins-Serrin). Explain, via the global gradient estimate, why a boundary-curvature condition enters, and what fails on a non-convex-enough domain.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The global gradient bound needs control of $|Du|$ on $\partial\Omega$; this boundary gradient bound is built from a barrier whose existence requires the boundary to curve the right way relative to the operator.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The interior gradient estimate is unconditional, but the *global* estimate requires bounding $\sup_{\partial\Omega}|Du|$, since the maximum of $|Du|$ for a minimal surface is attained on the boundary (the quantity $|Du|^2$ satisfies a maximum principle from Exercise 5). The boundary gradient bound is obtained by constructing **barriers**: upper and lower comparison surfaces that pin the normal derivative of $u$ at each boundary point. For the minimal surface operator such a barrier exists at a point $\xi \in \partial\Omega$ precisely when the mean curvature of $\partial\Omega$ at $\xi$ (with respect to the inner normal) is non-negative — the boundary must not curve away from the domain faster than the operator can follow. When $\partial\Omega$ has a point of negative mean curvature, no barrier exists for large boundary data: one can prescribe $\varphi$ so steep that any minimal surface would need an infinite boundary slope, and the global gradient estimate fails, so by Schaefer's scheme existence is not guaranteed — and indeed solutions genuinely fail to exist (Bernstein, Finn). The mean-curvature condition is thus the exact compatibility between the boundary geometry and the degenerate ellipticity of the minimal surface operator, and it is sharp: non-negativity of $\partial\Omega$'s mean curvature is both necessary and sufficient for solvability with arbitrary continuous data [ref: TODO_REF Serrin 1969].

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The quasilinear Dirichlet problem is solved by a single architecture: a chain of a priori estimates — sup bound, gradient bound, Hölder-gradient bound — feeding the Leray-Schauder fixed-point theorem. The estimates are where the analysis lives; the topology is a black box once the estimates close.

**Theorem 1 (existence for the quasilinear Dirichlet problem; Gilbarg-Trudinger §11.4, Ladyzhenskaya-Uraltseva).** Let $\Omega$ be a bounded $C^{2,\alpha}$ domain and $Qu = \operatorname{div} A(x,u,Du) + B(x,u,Du)$ quasilinear elliptic, $A, B \in C^{1,\alpha}$, with the natural structure conditions and $\partial B/\partial u \le 0$. If every solution of the homotopy family $Q_\sigma u = 0$, $u = \sigma\varphi$, admits a uniform a priori bound $\|u\|_{C^{1,\beta}(\overline\Omega)} \le M$ independent of $\sigma$, then $Qu = 0$, $u = \varphi$ on $\partial\Omega$, has a solution $u \in C^{2,\alpha}(\overline\Omega)$ [ref: TODO_REF Gilbarg-Trudinger 2001 §11.4] [ref: TODO_REF Ladyzhenskaya-Uraltseva 1968]. The proof is the freeze-and-fix solution operator of the Key theorem; the linear engine is the Schauder solvability of [02.17.05] and the compactness is Arzelà-Ascoli of [02.11.05]. The entire problem is thereby reduced to the four estimates below.

**Theorem 2 (the a priori chain: sup, gradient, Hölder-gradient).** Under the structure conditions, every solution of $Qu = 0$, $u = \varphi$ on $\partial\Omega$, satisfies in order: (i) the **maximum-principle sup bound** $\sup_\Omega |u| \le \sup_{\partial\Omega}|\varphi| + C\,\operatorname{diam}(\Omega)^2$, from the divergence structure and $\partial B/\partial u \le 0$ via the weak maximum principle [02.17.02]; (ii) the **boundary gradient bound** $\sup_{\partial\Omega}|Du| \le C(M, \partial\Omega, \varphi)$, from barriers adapted to the boundary curvature; (iii) the **interior gradient bound**, reducing $\sup_{\Omega'}|Du|$ to $\sup w$ for $w = |Du|^2$, which satisfies a linear elliptic differential inequality whose local boundedness is the De Giorgi-Nash-Moser theorem [02.17.07]; combining (ii) and (iii) gives the **global gradient bound** $\sup_\Omega |Du| \le M_1$; (iv) the **Hölder-gradient bound** $[Du]_{C^\beta(\overline\Omega)} \le M_2$, applying the De Giorgi-Nash Hölder estimate [02.17.07] to the now uniformly-elliptic-with-bounded-coefficients differentiated equation [ref: TODO_REF Gilbarg-Trudinger 2001 §13.2] [ref: TODO_REF Ladyzhenskaya-Uraltseva 1968]. The chain is strictly ordered: each estimate consumes the previous one as a hypothesis.

**Theorem 3 (interior gradient estimate for the minimal surface equation; Bombieri-De Giorgi-Miranda).** For a solution $u$ of the minimal surface equation $\operatorname{div}\big(Du/\sqrt{1+|Du|^2}\big) = 0$ on $B_R(x_0)$,
$$|Du(x_0)| \le C_1 \exp\!\Big(C_2\,\frac{\sup_{B_R(x_0)} u - u(x_0)}{R}\Big),$$
with $C_1, C_2$ depending only on $n$ [ref: TODO_REF Gilbarg-Trudinger 2001 §16.1]. Strikingly, the interior slope is controlled by the *oscillation of $u$ alone*, with **no boundary-gradient input**: the minimal surface operator self-improves, a consequence of the equation governing the volume element of the graph and the resulting Sobolev inequality on the minimal hypersurface. This is the feature that distinguishes the model case from general quasilinear operators, for which the interior bound genuinely needs the global structure.

**Theorem 4 (the Bernstein theorem and the role of dimension).** An entire solution of the minimal surface equation on all of $\mathbb{R}^n$ — a complete minimal graph — is an affine function (a hyperplane) for $n \le 7$, and this fails for $n \ge 8$ (Bombieri-De Giorgi-Giusti) [ref: TODO_REF Bernstein 1910] [ref: TODO_REF Gilbarg-Trudinger 2001 §17]. The proof for low dimensions runs the interior gradient estimate of Theorem 3 at every scale $R \to \infty$: an entire solution with controlled oscillation has vanishing gradient oscillation, forcing affinity, exactly as the Liouville theorem follows from the Harnack inequality of [02.17.08]. The dimensional ceiling $n = 7$ is the same threshold at which the Simons cone becomes a stable singular minimal hypersurface, tying scalar PDE rigidity to the geometry of minimal cones.

**Theorem 5 (degenerate and singular cases: the $p$-Laplacian; DiBenedetto, Uhlenbeck, Lewis).** For the $p$-Laplacian $\operatorname{div}(|Du|^{p-2}Du) = 0$, $1 < p < \infty$, solutions are of class $C^{1,\alpha}_{\mathrm{loc}}$ but generally **no better** than $C^{1,\alpha}$: second derivatives need not exist where $Du = 0$, because ellipticity degenerates ($p > 2$) or blows up ($p < 2$) at critical points [ref: TODO_REF Gilbarg-Trudinger 2001 §13]. The gradient estimate and the $C^{1,\alpha}$ regularity survive the degeneration via a Caccioppoli estimate for $|Du|$ combined with a De Giorgi iteration on the level sets of $|Du|$; the optimal Hölder exponent $\alpha = \alpha(n,p)$ is not explicit. This is the canonical example where the natural-growth structure conditions with $m = p \ne 2$ are not a technical convenience but the genuine generality the theory was built to handle.

**Theorem 6 (uniqueness and comparison).** If $A$ is monotone in $p$ ($\big(A(x,u,p) - A(x,u,q)\big)\cdot(p - q) > 0$ for $p \ne q$) and $B$ is non-increasing in $u$, then solutions of the Dirichlet problem with the same data are unique, and the comparison principle holds: $u \le v$ on $\partial\Omega$ and $Qu \ge Qv$ in $\Omega$ force $u \le v$ in $\Omega$ [ref: TODO_REF Gilbarg-Trudinger 2001 §10.1] [ref: TODO_REF Serrin 1969]. The proof tests the weak formulation of $Q u - Qv$ against $(u - v)^+$ and uses monotonicity to kill the leading term, the nonlinear analogue of the linear maximum principle of [02.17.02]. Monotonicity of $A$ is the divergence-form expression of ellipticity integrated along the gradient, and it is what converts the local ellipticity into a global comparison.

**Synthesis.** The quasilinear theory is the foundational reason elliptic PDE has a unified existence architecture: every second-order elliptic Dirichlet problem, linear or nonlinear, is solved by pairing solvability of a model problem with an a priori estimate, and putting these together the Leray-Schauder scheme is exactly the nonlinear lift of the linear method of continuity of [02.17.05] — the Neumann-series openness becomes the topological degree, and the $t$-uniform linear estimate becomes the solution-uniform $C^{1,\beta}$ chain. This is exactly the duality between estimates and existence read one level up, and the central insight is that the entire nonlinear difficulty is compressed into the single passage $\sup|u| \to \sup|Du|$, the gradient estimate, obtained by differentiating the equation and feeding the result to the De Giorgi-Nash-Moser machine of [02.17.07] and the Harnack-oscillation mechanism of [02.17.08]. The bridge is the compact solution operator $T$, whose derivative-gaining linear inverse and Arzelà-Ascoli compactness [02.11.05] turn each a priori bound into a wall of a bounded set, so the topology is inert once the estimates close and the subject is the art of the a priori bound. The minimal surface equation is the keystone, generalising the linear gradient theory because its interior estimate needs no boundary data — and it is dual to the geometry of minimal cones, where the dimension-$7$ Bernstein threshold of Theorem 4 is the same number governing the stability of the Simons cone, where scalar PDE rigidity and the singular structure of area-minimising hypersurfaces become one phenomenon.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Schaefer's fixed-point theorem).** Let $\mathfrak{B}$ be a Banach space, $T : \mathfrak{B} \to \mathfrak{B}$ compact, and suppose $\Sigma = \{x \in \mathfrak{B} : x = \sigma Tx \text{ for some } \sigma \in [0,1]\}$ is bounded. Then $T$ has a fixed point.

*Proof.* Choose $R > \sup_{x \in \Sigma}\|x\|$, so that $x \ne \sigma Tx$ for all $\|x\| = R$ and all $\sigma \in [0,1]$. The Leray-Schauder degree $\deg(I - \sigma T, B_R, 0)$ is defined for each $\sigma$ since $I - \sigma T$ is a compact perturbation of the identity with no zero on $\partial B_R$. The family $\sigma \mapsto I - \sigma T$ is a continuous homotopy of such maps (continuity in the operator norm, compactness of each $\sigma T$), zero-free on $\partial B_R$ throughout, so by homotopy invariance $\deg(I - T, B_R, 0) = \deg(I, B_R, 0) = 1 \ne 0$. The solution property of a nonzero degree yields $x \in B_R$ with $(I - T)x = 0$, i.e. $x = Tx$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (the freeze-and-fix operator is compact).** Let $\Omega$ be a bounded $C^{2,\alpha}$ domain, $\beta \in (0,\alpha)$, and for $v \in C^{1,\beta}(\overline\Omega)$ let $Tv \in C^{2,\beta}(\overline\Omega)$ be the unique solution of the linear Dirichlet problem with coefficients frozen at $(x, v, Dv)$ and data $\varphi$. Then $T : C^{1,\beta}(\overline\Omega) \to C^{1,\beta}(\overline\Omega)$ is compact and continuous.

*Proof.* The frozen coefficients $a^{ij}(x) = (\partial A_i/\partial p_j)(x, v, Dv)$ lie in $C^{0,\beta}(\overline\Omega)$ with norm controlled by $\|v\|_{C^{1,\beta}}$ (composition of the $C^{1,\alpha}$ map $\partial A/\partial p$ with the $C^{0,\beta}$ argument $(x, v, Dv)$, using the Hölder chain rule), and they are uniformly elliptic by the structure conditions. The global linear Schauder estimate [02.17.05] then gives $\|Tv\|_{C^{2,\beta}(\overline\Omega)} \le C(\|v\|_{C^{1,\beta}})\big(\|f\|_{C^{0,\beta}} + \|\varphi\|_{C^{2,\beta}}\big)$. Hence $T$ maps each bounded set $\{\|v\|_{C^{1,\beta}} \le K\}$ into a bounded subset of $C^{2,\beta}(\overline\Omega)$. By Arzelà-Ascoli, bounded subsets of $C^{2,\beta}(\overline\Omega)$ are precompact in $C^{1,\beta}(\overline\Omega)$ on the bounded domain $\Omega$ — the inclusion $C^{2,\beta} \hookrightarrow C^{1,\beta}$ is a compact operator [02.11.05]. Thus $T$ sends bounded sets to precompact sets: $T$ is compact. For continuity, if $v_k \to v$ in $C^{1,\beta}$ the frozen coefficients converge in $C^{0,\beta'}$ for $\beta' < \beta$, and continuous dependence of the linear Schauder solution on its coefficients gives $Tv_k \to Tv$ in $C^{1,\beta}$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (interior gradient estimate via the differentiated equation).** Let $u \in C^3(\Omega)$ solve $Qu = \operatorname{div} A(x,u,Du) + B = 0$ with structure constants $\lambda, \Lambda, \mu$ and $m = 2$. Then for $\Omega' \Subset \Omega$, $\sup_{\Omega'}|Du| \le C(n, \lambda, \Lambda, \mu, \operatorname{dist}(\Omega', \partial\Omega), \sup_\Omega |u|)$.

*Proof.* Write $a^{ij} = (\partial A_i/\partial p_j)(x, u, Du)$, uniformly elliptic with constants $\lambda, \Lambda$ by the structure condition. Differentiating the equation in $x_k$, the derivative $v_k = D_k u$ satisfies the linear divergence-form equation
$$D_i(a^{ij}D_j v_k) = -D_i g_{ik}, \qquad g_{ik} = \partial_{x_k}A_i + (\partial_u A_i)v_k - (\text{terms from }B),$$
with $|g_{ik}| \le C(1 + |Du|)$ by the growth bounds. Consider $w = |Du|^2 = \sum_k v_k^2$. Multiplying the equation for $v_k$ by $v_k$ and summing,
$$D_i(a^{ij}D_j w) = 2\sum_k a^{ij}D_j v_k\,D_i v_k - 2\sum_k D_i g_{ik}\,v_k \ge 2\lambda|D^2u|^2 - C(1 + |Du|)|D^2u| - C|Du|^2.$$
By Young's inequality the cross term is absorbed: $D_i(a^{ij}D_j w) \ge \lambda|D^2u|^2 - C(1 + w)$, so $w$ is a weak subsolution of a linear uniformly elliptic equation with right-hand side controlled by $1 + w$. The local boundedness half of the De Giorgi-Nash-Moser theorem [02.17.07], applied on a ball $B_{2r}(x_0) \subseteq \Omega$ with $2r = \operatorname{dist}(\Omega', \partial\Omega)$, gives
$$\sup_{B_r}w \le C\Big(\fint_{B_{2r}}w\,dx + 1\Big),$$
and the energy (Caccioppoli) estimate for the equation $Qu = 0$ tested against $\eta^2 u$ bounds $\int_{B_{2r}}|Du|^2 \le C(\sup_\Omega|u|^2 + 1)r^{n-2}$, controlling the average of $w$ by $\sup_\Omega|u|$ and the geometry. Combining, $\sup_{\Omega'}w \le C(n,\lambda,\Lambda,\mu,r,\sup_\Omega|u|)$, whence $\sup_{\Omega'}|Du| = (\sup_{\Omega'}w)^{1/2}$ is bounded as claimed. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (uniqueness by monotone testing).** Let $A(x,u,p)$ be monotone in $p$ and $B(x,u,p)$ non-increasing in $u$. If $u, v \in C^1(\overline\Omega)$ solve $Qu = Qv = 0$ in $\Omega$ with $u = v$ on $\partial\Omega$, then $u \equiv v$.

*Proof.* Subtract the weak formulations and test against $\varphi = (u - v)^+ \in W^{1,2}_0(\Omega)$ (it vanishes on $\partial\Omega$ since $u = v$ there):
$$\int_\Omega \big(A(x,u,Du) - A(x,v,Dv)\big)\cdot D(u-v)^+\,dx = \int_\Omega \big(B(x,u,Du) - B(x,v,Dv)\big)(u-v)^+\,dx.$$
On the set $\{u > v\}$ where $(u-v)^+ > 0$, split the left integrand: $\big(A(x,u,Du) - A(x,u,Dv)\big)\cdot D(u - v) \ge 0$ by monotonicity in $p$ (strictly positive where $Du \ne Dv$), while the remaining $u$-dependence of $A$ and the $u$-dependence of $B$ are controlled by the non-increase of $B$ and a Lipschitz bound, which after a standard absorption forces the left side $\ge 0$ and the right side $\le 0$. Hence both vanish, so $D(u - v) = 0$ on $\{u > v\}$; with $(u-v)^+ = 0$ on $\partial\Omega$ this gives $(u-v)^+ \equiv 0$, i.e. $u \le v$. By symmetry $v \le u$, so $u \equiv v$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (the homotopy is seeded at $\sigma = 0$).** In the family $Q_\sigma u = \operatorname{div} A(x,u,Du) + \sigma B = 0$, $u = \sigma\varphi$, the value $\sigma = 0$ has the unique solution $u \equiv 0$, and the maximum-principle sup bound is uniform in $\sigma$.

*Proof.* At $\sigma = 0$ the problem is $\operatorname{div} A(x, u, Du) = 0$, $u = 0$ on $\partial\Omega$. Testing against $u$ and using the lower structure bound $A(x,u,p)\cdot p \ge \lambda(1+|p|)^{m-2}|p|^2 - C \ge \tfrac{\lambda}{2}|p|^2 - C$ gives $\int_\Omega |Du|^2 \le C\int_\Omega 1$ on $\{|Du|$ large$\}$, and with zero boundary data the Poincaré inequality forces $\int|Du|^2 = 0$, so $u \equiv 0$. For general $\sigma$, the weak maximum principle [02.17.02] applied to the divergence structure with $\partial(\sigma B)/\partial u = \sigma\,\partial B/\partial u \le 0$ yields $\sup_\Omega|u| \le \sigma\sup_{\partial\Omega}|\varphi| + C\operatorname{diam}(\Omega)^2 \le \sup_{\partial\Omega}|\varphi| + C\operatorname{diam}(\Omega)^2$, a bound independent of $\sigma \in [0,1]$. This $\sigma$-uniform sup bound is the first link of the a priori chain and the seed of the Leray-Schauder homotopy. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The De Giorgi-Nash-Moser theory of [02.17.07] is the analytic engine of the gradient estimate: differentiating the quasilinear equation produces a linear divergence-form equation for $w = |Du|^2$ with bounded measurable coefficients, and the local boundedness theorem of that unit caps $\sup|Du|$ in the interior, while its Hölder estimate produces the $[Du]_{C^\beta}$ bound that closes the a priori chain — the entire nonlinearity is absorbed into frozen coefficients to which the linear measurable-coefficient theory applies verbatim.

- The Harnack inequality and oscillation-decay mechanism of [02.17.08] are what convert the gradient bound into the Bernstein rigidity of Theorem 4: running the interior gradient estimate at every scale $R \to \infty$ is the exact analogue of reading the Harnack inequality at infinity to obtain the Liouville theorem, so the affineness of low-dimensional entire minimal graphs is the quasilinear shadow of the Liouville theorem proved there.

- The linear method of continuity of [02.17.05] is the affine special case of the Leray-Schauder scheme used here: when the equation is linear the homotopy $L_t = (1-t)\Delta + tL$ replaces the nonlinear homotopy $Q_\sigma$, the Neumann-series openness replaces the topological degree, and the $t$-uniform Schauder estimate replaces the solution-uniform $C^{1,\beta}$ chain; the two units are the linear and nonlinear faces of the single principle that existence is dual to an a priori estimate, and the linear Schauder solvability proved there is the very engine that defines the compact operator $T$ here.

- The compact-operator theory of [02.11.05] supplies the Arzelà-Ascoli compactness of the embedding $C^{2,\beta} \hookrightarrow C^{1,\beta}$ that makes the solution operator $T$ compact, without which the Leray-Schauder degree is undefined; the quasilinear existence theorem is the canonical PDE application that makes the abstract compactness of [02.11.05] indispensable.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The quasilinear theory grew from the calculus of variations: the minimal surface equation is the Euler-Lagrange equation of the area functional, and Sergei Bernstein's 1910 study of surfaces defined by their mean curvature [ref: TODO_REF Bernstein 1910] introduced both the a priori estimate method and the rigidity theorem now bearing his name, proving that entire minimal graphs over $\mathbb{R}^2$ are planes. Bernstein's insight — that bounds on a hypothetical solution, established before any solution is known to exist, are the route to existence — became the organising principle of the whole subject, and his method of differentiating the equation to estimate the gradient is the seed of every gradient estimate proved here.

The abstract existence machinery is due to Jean Leray and Juliusz Schauder, whose 1934 *Annales de l'ENS* paper [ref: TODO_REF Leray-Schauder 1934] built the topological degree for compact perturbations of the identity in Banach spaces and reduced existence to an a priori bound; Helmut Schaefer's 1955 note [ref: TODO_REF Schaefer 1955] isolated the clean fixed-point form used today. The analytic estimates that feed the scheme came in the 1950s and 1960s: Ennio De Giorgi's 1957 resolution of Hilbert's nineteenth problem [ref: TODO_REF De Giorgi 1957] supplied the Hölder continuity of solutions of divergence-form equations with measurable coefficients, the precise tool needed for the differentiated quasilinear equation, and the comprehensive theory was assembled by Olga Ladyzhenskaya and Nina Uraltseva [ref: TODO_REF Ladyzhenskaya-Uraltseva 1968] and, with sharp structure conditions and the definitive Dirichlet existence theorem, by James Serrin in his 1969 *Philosophical Transactions* memoir [ref: TODO_REF Serrin 1969]. The Bernstein theorem's dimensional ceiling was settled by Bombieri, De Giorgi, and Giusti in 1969, who exhibited a non-affine entire minimal graph in dimension eight, linking the scalar PDE to the singular geometry of the Simons cone; Gilbarg and Trudinger's chapters 10-11, 13, and 15-17 [ref: TODO_REF Gilbarg-Trudinger 2001] give the modern synthesis proved above.
