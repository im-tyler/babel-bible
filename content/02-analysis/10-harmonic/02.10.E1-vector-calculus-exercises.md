## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Apostol Vol. 2 Chapters 10-12 develop the integral calculus of vector fields in $\mathbb{R}^2$ and $\mathbb{R}^3$. Chapter 10 covers the line integral $\int_\gamma P\,dx + Q\,dy + R\,dz$, the work integral $\int_\gamma \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$, and the equivalence of path independence, the conservative condition $\mathbf{F} = \nabla\phi$, and vanishing of every closed-loop integral. Chapter 11 builds the multiple integral, Fubini's theorem, and the change-of-variables formula with the Jacobian factor, including polar, cylindrical, and spherical coordinates. Chapter 12 treats parametric surfaces, surface area, flux integrals, and the three integral theorems: Green's in the plane, Stokes's on a surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$, and the divergence theorem.

This pack collects ten problems from those chapters — three easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a complete worked solution. It tests operational competence: evaluating a line integral, recognising and integrating a conservative field, converting a multiple integral to polar or spherical coordinates, computing a flux, and applying Green's, Stokes's, and the divergence theorems.

Conventions follow the prerequisite units and Apostol: $\mathbf{F}$ a vector field; $\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ the work form; $\nabla\phi$ a gradient field; $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$ the curl, $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$ the divergence; $d\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{n}\,dS$ the oriented area element on a surface. A field is conservative on a simply connected region iff $\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = 0$ there.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one problem in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining nine follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead problem.** *Divergence theorem on a sphere.* Compute the outward flux of $\mathbf{F}(x, y, z) = (x, y, z)$ through the sphere $S$ of radius $a$ centred at the origin, both directly and via the divergence theorem.

**Solution.** *Direct.* On the sphere of radius $a$, the outward unit normal is $\mathbf{n} = \frac{1}{a}(x, y, z)$, so $\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n} = \frac{1}{a}(x^2 + y^2 + z^2) = \frac{a^2}{a} = a$ (constant on $S$). The flux is
$$
\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_S a\, dS = a\cdot\operatorname{area}(S) = a\cdot 4\pi a^2 = 4\pi a^3.
$$

*Via the divergence theorem.* The divergence is $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = \partial_x x + \partial_y y + \partial_z z = 3$. The divergence theorem converts the flux to a volume integral over the enclosed ball $B$:
$$
\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_B \nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}\,dV = \iiint_B 3\,dV = 3\cdot\operatorname{vol}(B) = 3\cdot\tfrac{4}{3}\pi a^3 = 4\pi a^3.
$$

Both routes give $4\pi a^3$. $\square$

The field $\mathbf{F} = (x,y,z)$ is the position field, with constant divergence $3$; its flux through any closed surface equals $3$ times the enclosed volume. The divergence theorem turns a two-dimensional flux computation into a one-line volume calculation — its operational payoff (Apostol Ch. 12).

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).** *Line integral along a parametrised curve.*

Evaluate $\int_\gamma (x\,dx + y\,dy)$ where $\gamma$ is the straight segment from $(0, 0)$ to $(1, 2)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Parametrise $\gamma(t) = (t, 2t)$, $t \in [0, 1]$, substitute $dx = dt$, $dy = 2\,dt$, and integrate. Or notice the integrand is exact.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Parametrise $\gamma(t) = (t, 2t)$, $t\in[0,1]$, so $dx = dt$, $dy = 2\,dt$. Then
$$
\int_\gamma x\,dx + y\,dy = \int_0^1 \big(t\cdot 1 + 2t\cdot 2\big)\,dt = \int_0^1 5t\,dt = \tfrac{5}{2}.
$$
Alternatively, $x\,dx + y\,dy = d\big(\tfrac12(x^2 + y^2)\big)$ is exact, so the integral is $\tfrac12(x^2+y^2)$ evaluated endpoint-to-endpoint: $\tfrac12(1 + 4) - 0 = \tfrac{5}{2}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).** *Recognise a conservative field and find its potential.*

Show $\mathbf{F} = (2xy, x^2 + 2y)$ is conservative on $\mathbb{R}^2$ and find a scalar potential $\phi$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Check $\partial_y(2xy) = \partial_x(x^2 + 2y)$ (the planar curl-free condition). Then integrate $\phi_x = 2xy$ in $x$ and fix the integration "constant" using $\phi_y$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Curl-free check: $\partial_y(2xy) = 2x$ and $\partial_x(x^2 + 2y) = 2x$. They agree, and $\mathbb{R}^2$ is simply connected, so $\mathbf{F}$ is conservative.

Integrate $\phi_x = 2xy$ in $x$: $\phi = x^2 y + g(y)$. Then $\phi_y = x^2 + g'(y)$ must equal $x^2 + 2y$, so $g'(y) = 2y$, $g(y) = y^2 + C$. Thus
$$
\phi(x, y) = x^2 y + y^2 + C.
$$
Check: $\nabla\phi = (2xy,\; x^2 + 2y) = \mathbf{F}$. The work integral between any two points is $\phi(\text{end}) - \phi(\text{start})$, independent of path.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, numeric).** *Double integral in polar coordinates.*

Evaluate $\iint_D (x^2 + y^2)\,dA$ where $D$ is the disk $x^2 + y^2 \leq 4$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Switch to polar: $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$ and $dA = r\,dr\,d\theta$, with $r \in [0, 2]$, $\theta \in [0, 2\pi]$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In polar coordinates $dA = r\,dr\,d\theta$ and $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$:
$$
\iint_D (x^2 + y^2)\,dA = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\int_0^2 r^2\cdot r\,dr\,d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi}d\theta\int_0^2 r^3\,dr = 2\pi\cdot\frac{r^4}{4}\Big|_0^2 = 2\pi\cdot 4 = 8\pi.
$$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).** *Green's theorem to compute an area.*

Use Green's theorem to compute the area enclosed by the ellipse $\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Area $= \frac12\oint_{\partial D}(x\,dy - y\,dx)$ by Green's theorem. Parametrise the ellipse as $(a\cos t, b\sin t)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Green's theorem with $P = -\tfrac{y}{2}$, $Q = \tfrac{x}{2}$ gives $Q_x - P_y = \tfrac12 + \tfrac12 = 1$, so $\operatorname{area}(D) = \iint_D 1\,dA = \tfrac12\oint_{\partial D}(x\,dy - y\,dx)$.

Parametrise $\partial D$: $x = a\cos t$, $y = b\sin t$, $t\in[0, 2\pi]$. Then $dx = -a\sin t\,dt$, $dy = b\cos t\,dt$, and
$$
x\,dy - y\,dx = a\cos t\cdot b\cos t\,dt - b\sin t\cdot(-a\sin t)\,dt = ab(\cos^2 t + \sin^2 t)\,dt = ab\,dt.
$$
So $\operatorname{area} = \tfrac12\int_0^{2\pi} ab\,dt = \tfrac12\cdot ab\cdot 2\pi = \pi ab$, the standard ellipse area.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).** *Triple integral in spherical coordinates.*

Compute $\iiint_B \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}}\,dV$ where $B$ is the ball of radius $1$ centred at the origin.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use spherical coordinates: the integrand is $1/\rho$ and $dV = \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta$. The angular integrals factor out.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In spherical coordinates $\rho^2 = x^2 + y^2 + z^2$ and $dV = \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta$, with $\rho\in[0,1]$, $\phi\in[0,\pi]$, $\theta\in[0,2\pi]$:
$$
\iiint_B \frac{1}{\rho}\,dV = \int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\int_0^\pi\!\!\int_0^1 \frac{1}{\rho}\cdot\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta = \Big(\int_0^{2\pi}d\theta\Big)\Big(\int_0^\pi\sin\phi\,d\phi\Big)\Big(\int_0^1\rho\,d\rho\Big).
$$
These evaluate to $2\pi$, $2$, and $\tfrac12$ respectively, so the integral is $2\pi\cdot 2\cdot\tfrac12 = 2\pi$. The $1/\rho$ singularity at the origin is integrable because the Jacobian $\rho^2$ tames it.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, numeric).** *Flux through a parametric surface.*

Compute the upward flux of $\mathbf{F} = (0, 0, z)$ through the part of the paraboloid $z = 1 - x^2 - y^2$ above the plane $z = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Parametrise by $(x, y)$ over the unit disk; on a graph $z = g(x,y)$, the upward flux is $\iint_D \mathbf{F}\cdot(-g_x, -g_y, 1)\,dx\,dy$. Then switch to polar.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For the graph $z = g(x, y) = 1 - x^2 - y^2$ over the unit disk $D$, the upward normal element is $(-g_x, -g_y, 1)\,dx\,dy = (2x, 2y, 1)\,dx\,dy$. With $\mathbf{F} = (0, 0, z) = (0, 0, 1 - x^2 - y^2)$ on the surface,
$$
\mathbf{F}\cdot(2x, 2y, 1) = 1 - x^2 - y^2.
$$
So the flux is $\iint_D (1 - x^2 - y^2)\,dA$. In polar coordinates over $r\in[0,1]$:
$$
\int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\int_0^1 (1 - r^2)\,r\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\int_0^1 (r - r^3)\,dr = 2\pi\Big(\tfrac12 - \tfrac14\Big) = 2\pi\cdot\tfrac14 = \tfrac{\pi}{2}.
$$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, proof).** *Curl-free is not conservative on a punctured plane.*

Let $\mathbf{F} = \left(\frac{-y}{x^2 + y^2}, \frac{x}{x^2 + y^2}\right)$ on $\mathbb{R}^2\setminus\{0\}$. Show $\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = 0$ everywhere it is defined, yet $\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 2\pi \neq 0$ around the unit circle.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the scalar curl $Q_x - P_y$. For the loop integral, parametrise the unit circle and recognise $\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = d\theta$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $P = \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}$, $Q = \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}$. Compute (away from the origin)
$$
Q_x = \frac{(x^2+y^2) - x\cdot 2x}{(x^2+y^2)^2} = \frac{y^2 - x^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2}, \qquad P_y = \frac{-(x^2+y^2) + y\cdot 2y}{(x^2+y^2)^2} = \frac{y^2 - x^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2}.
$$
So $Q_x - P_y = 0$: the field is curl-free on $\mathbb{R}^2\setminus\{0\}$.

But around the unit circle $C$, parametrised $x = \cos t$, $y = \sin t$, one finds $\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = d\theta$ (this is the angular form $d\theta$), so
$$
\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^{2\pi} dt = 2\pi \neq 0.
$$
A nonzero loop integral means $\mathbf{F}$ is not conservative. There is no contradiction with Green's theorem: the domain $\mathbb{R}^2\setminus\{0\}$ is not simply connected, and the origin is excluded, so the closed form $\mathbf{F}$ need not be exact. This is the generator of $H^1_{\mathrm{dR}}$ of the punctured plane.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, numeric).** *Stokes's theorem.*

Verify Stokes's theorem for $\mathbf{F} = (y, z, x)$ over the surface $S$ that is the part of the plane $x + y + z = 1$ in the first octant, with boundary triangle $C$, oriented so the normal has positive $z$-component.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$ and integrate its flux through $S$ (use the unit normal $\frac{1}{\sqrt3}(1,1,1)$ and the triangle's area). Then independently compute $\oint_C\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ around the three edges.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Curl side.* $\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \big(\partial_y x - \partial_z z,\; \partial_z y - \partial_x x,\; \partial_x z - \partial_y y\big) = (0 - 1,\; 0 - 1,\; 0 - 1) = (-1, -1, -1)$. The plane $x + y + z = 1$ has unit normal $\mathbf{n} = \frac{1}{\sqrt3}(1,1,1)$, so $(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n} = \frac{-3}{\sqrt3} = -\sqrt3$. The triangle has vertices $(1,0,0), (0,1,0), (0,0,1)$; its area is $\frac{\sqrt3}{2}$. Flux of curl: $-\sqrt3\cdot\frac{\sqrt3}{2} = -\frac{3}{2}$.

*Boundary side.* Traverse $C$ as $(1,0,0)\to(0,1,0)\to(0,0,1)\to(1,0,0)$ (this matches the upward normal by the right-hand rule). On edge $1$, $\mathbf{r}(t) = (1-t, t, 0)$, $d\mathbf{r} = (-1,1,0)dt$, $\mathbf{F} = (y,z,x) = (t, 0, 1-t)$, so $\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = -t\,dt$, integrating to $-\tfrac12$. By the cyclic symmetry of $\mathbf{F} = (y,z,x)$ under $(x,y,z)\to(y,z,x)$, the other two edges each also give $-\tfrac12$. Total: $-\tfrac32$.

Both sides equal $-\frac32$, verifying Stokes's theorem.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, numeric).** *Change of variables with a nontrivial Jacobian.*

Evaluate $\iint_D (x + y)\,dA$ where $D$ is the parallelogram with vertices $(0,0), (2,1), (3,3), (1,2)$, using the substitution $x = 2u + v$, $y = u + 2v$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Check that $(u, v)\in[0,1]^2$ maps onto $D$. Compute the Jacobian determinant, transform the integrand, and integrate over the unit square.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The map $(u,v)\mapsto(2u+v, u+2v)$ sends $(0,0)\mapsto(0,0)$, $(1,0)\mapsto(2,1)$, $(1,1)\mapsto(3,3)$, $(0,1)\mapsto(1,2)$ — exactly the four vertices of $D$. So the unit square $[0,1]^2$ maps onto $D$.

Jacobian: $\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \det\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2\end{pmatrix} = 3$, so $dA = 3\,du\,dv$. The integrand $x + y = (2u+v) + (u+2v) = 3u + 3v = 3(u+v)$. Therefore
$$
\iint_D (x+y)\,dA = \int_0^1\!\!\int_0^1 3(u+v)\cdot 3\,du\,dv = 9\int_0^1\!\!\int_0^1 (u+v)\,du\,dv = 9\cdot 1 = 9,
$$
using $\int_0^1\!\int_0^1(u+v)\,du\,dv = \tfrac12 + \tfrac12 = 1$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, proof).** *Divergence of a curl is zero, and the consequence for flux.*

Prove that $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}) = 0$ for any $C^2$ field $\mathbf{F}$, and deduce that the flux of a curl through any closed surface vanishes.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write out $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$ in components and take the divergence; mixed partials cancel by equality of second partials. For the flux, apply the divergence theorem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\mathbf{F} = (F_1, F_2, F_3)$. The curl is
$$
\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = (\partial_y F_3 - \partial_z F_2,\; \partial_z F_1 - \partial_x F_3,\; \partial_x F_2 - \partial_y F_1).
$$
Take the divergence:
$$
\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}) = \partial_x(\partial_y F_3 - \partial_z F_2) + \partial_y(\partial_z F_1 - \partial_x F_3) + \partial_z(\partial_x F_2 - \partial_y F_1).
$$
Group the six terms into pairs: $\partial_x\partial_y F_3 - \partial_y\partial_x F_3$, $-\partial_x\partial_z F_2 + \partial_z\partial_x F_2$, $\partial_y\partial_z F_1 - \partial_z\partial_y F_1$. Each pair vanishes by equality of mixed second partials (Clairaut/Schwarz, valid since $\mathbf{F}$ is $C^2$). So $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}) = 0$.

Consequence: for any closed surface $S$ bounding a region $V$, the divergence theorem gives
$$
\iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V \nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\,dV = \iiint_V 0\,dV = 0.
$$
In differential-forms language this is $d^2 = 0$: the curl corresponds to $d$ on $1$-forms, the divergence to $d$ on $2$-forms, and their composite is zero.

</details>

</aside>
