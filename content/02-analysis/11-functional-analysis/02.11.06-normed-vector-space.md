## Intuition [Beginner]

A vector space lets you add vectors and stretch them. A norm adds a way to measure the size of each vector.

In the plane, the usual norm is arrow length. For a function, a norm might measure the largest height of the graph. For a list of numbers, a norm might measure the root of the sum of squared entries.

Once vectors have sizes, distance comes for free: the distance from $v$ to $w$ is the size of $v-w$.

## Visual [Beginner]

A norm turns vectors into lengths, and lengths turn vector differences into distances.

![Vectors with lengths and a distance measured by the norm of their difference.](/img/normed-vector-space-placeholder.svg)

The same vector space can sometimes carry different useful norms.

## Worked example [Beginner]

In the plane, the vector $(3,4)$ has usual length $5$. That is the familiar right-triangle measurement.

The distance between $(3,4)$ and $(1,1)$ is the length of their difference:

$$
(3,4)-(1,1)=(2,3).
$$

So the distance is the length of $(2,3)$. The norm measures vectors; the induced metric measures separation between points.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $V$ be a vector space over $\mathbb R$ or $\mathbb C$. A **norm** on $V$ is a function $\|\cdot\|:V\to[0,\infty)$ such that:

1. $\|v\|=0$ if and only if $v=0$.
2. $\|\lambda v\|=|\lambda|\|v\|$ for every scalar $\lambda$.
3. $\|v+w\|\leq\|v\|+\|w\|$.

A **normed vector space** is a vector space equipped with a norm [ref: TODO_REF Conway §III]. The norm induces a metric

$$
d(v,w)=\|v-w\|.
$$

The topology from this metric is the **norm topology**.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (A norm induces a metric).** If $(V,\|\cdot\|)$ is a normed vector space, then $d(v,w)=\|v-w\|$ is a metric on $V$.

**Proof.** Non-negativity is part of the norm definition. Also $d(v,w)=0$ if and only if $\|v-w\|=0$, which holds if and only if $v-w=0$, equivalently $v=w$.

Symmetry follows from homogeneity:

$$
d(v,w)=\|v-w\|=\|-(w-v)\|=\|w-v\|=d(w,v).
$$

For the triangle inequality,

$$
d(v,u)=\|v-u\|=\|(v-w)+(w-u)\|\leq\|v-w\|+\|w-u\|=d(v,w)+d(w,u).
$$

Thus $d$ is a metric. $\square$

## Advanced results [Master]

Bounded linear maps between normed spaces are precisely continuous linear maps. The operator norm

$$
\|T\|=\sup_{\|v\|\leq1}\|Tv\|
$$

measures the best Lipschitz constant of a linear map when it is finite [ref: TODO_REF Reed-Simon §III].

On a finite-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb R$ or $\mathbb C$, all norms define the same topology. In infinite dimensions, different norms can define genuinely different topologies and completions.

**Synthesis.** Normed vector spaces extend the notion of length from Euclidean space to infinite-dimensional settings, providing the analytic framework for differential equations (function spaces with sup-norm or Sobolev norms), quantum mechanics (the state space is a normed space of wavefunctions), and numerical analysis (convergence of iterative methods is measured in operator norms). The Hahn-Banach theorem (extension of linear functionals), the open mapping theorem (surjective bounded operators are open), and the uniform boundedness principle (pointwise bounded families are uniformly bounded) are the three pillars of the theory, collectively called the Baire category theorem corollaries because they rely on the completeness of Banach spaces. The completion of a normed space to a Banach space mirrors the Dedekind completion of the rationals and is the prerequisite for applying the contraction mapping principle and the spectral theory of bounded operators.

## Full proof set [Master]

For a linear map $T:V\to W$, boundedness means $\|Tv\|\leq C\|v\|$ for some $C$. This implies continuity at every point because $\|T(v)-T(w)\|=\|T(v-w)\|\leq C\|v-w\|$.

Conversely, if $T$ is continuous at $0$, choose a radius $\delta>0$ such that $\|v\|<\delta$ implies $\|Tv\|<1$. For nonzero $v$, apply this to $(\delta/2)v/\|v\|$ to obtain $\|Tv\|\leq(2/\delta)\|v\|$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Normed spaces entered functional analysis as spaces of functions and sequences required a common language for convergence and bounded linear operations. Reed and Simon use normed and Banach spaces as the analytic setting for operators in mathematical physics [ref: TODO_REF Reed-Simon §III].

Conway develops normed spaces as the gateway from linear algebra to Banach-space theory and operator theory [ref: TODO_REF Conway §III].
