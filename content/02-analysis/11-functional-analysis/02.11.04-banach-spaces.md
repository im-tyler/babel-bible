## Intuition [Beginner]

A vector space lets you add vectors and stretch them. A norm adds a way to measure size. A Banach space adds one more promise: if a list of vectors settles down so tightly that its later terms become indistinguishable, then it actually settles on a vector inside the space.

That last promise is called completeness. It rules out missing limit points.

The rational numbers are not complete as a line: decimal approximations to $\sqrt2$ get closer and closer to each other, but their target is not rational. The real numbers repair that hole. Banach spaces are the same repair for spaces of functions, sequences, and solutions to equations.

## Visual [Beginner]

The left space contains the endpoint of every Cauchy path. The right space has a hole, so a path can settle toward a point that is not present.

![Cauchy sequence landing inside a complete space and approaching a missing point in an incomplete space.](/img/banach-spaces-placeholder.svg)

Completeness is not about drawing every possible curve. It is about whether the space contains the limits that its own distance measurement forces.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the space of continuous functions on the interval from $0$ to $1$. Measure a function by its largest absolute height:

$$
\|f\|=\text{largest value of }|f(x)|.
$$

If a list of continuous functions gets uniformly closer and closer, then it has a uniform limit. That limit is still continuous. So this function space is Banach.

Now take only polynomial functions with the same measurement. A list of polynomials can settle toward $|x|$ on the interval from $-1$ to $1$. The target is continuous but not a polynomial. The polynomial space has a missing limit, so it is not complete.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be a vector space over $\mathbb R$ or $\mathbb C$ [01.01.03]. A **norm** on $X$ is a function $\|\cdot\|:X\to[0,\infty)$ such that:

1. $\|x\|=0$ iff $x=0$;
2. $\|\lambda x\|=|\lambda|\|x\|$ for every scalar $\lambda$;
3. $\|x+y\|\leq\|x\|+\|y\|$.

The norm defines a metric $d(x,y)=\|x-y\|$. A sequence $(x_n)$ is **Cauchy** if, for every $\varepsilon>0$, there is $N$ such that $m,n\geq N$ implies $\|x_m-x_n\|<\varepsilon$.

A **Banach space** is a normed vector space in which every Cauchy sequence converges to an element of the same space [ref: TODO_REF Conway §III].

Standard examples include $\mathbb R^n$, $\mathbb C^n$, the sequence spaces $\ell^p$ for $1\leq p\leq\infty$, and $C(K)$ with the supremum norm when $K$ is compact. The space of polynomials with the supremum norm on a compact interval is not Banach: its completion is the closure of the polynomials inside $C(K)$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (closed subspaces of Banach spaces are Banach).** Let $X$ be a Banach space and let $Y\subset X$ be a linear subspace with the restricted norm. Then $Y$ is Banach if and only if $Y$ is closed in $X$.

**Proof.** First assume $Y$ is closed. Let $(y_n)$ be a Cauchy sequence in $Y$. It is also Cauchy in $X$, so completeness of $X$ gives a limit $x\in X$ with $y_n\to x$. Since $Y$ is closed and every $y_n$ lies in $Y$, the limit $x$ lies in $Y$. Thus every Cauchy sequence in $Y$ converges in $Y$.

Conversely, assume $Y$ is Banach. Let $(y_n)$ be a sequence in $Y$ converging in $X$ to some $x\in X$. Every convergent sequence is Cauchy, so $(y_n)$ is Cauchy in $Y$. Completeness of $Y$ gives $y\in Y$ such that $y_n\to y$ in the restricted norm. Limits in a metric space are unique, so $x=y$. Hence $x\in Y$, and $Y$ is closed. $\square$

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [02.11.01] (bounded linear operators), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [02.11.05] (compact operators). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. ## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, conceptual).**

Why is every finite-dimensional normed vector space Banach over $\mathbb R$ or $\mathbb C$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use coordinates and the completeness of the scalar field.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Choose a basis and write a Cauchy sequence in coordinates. Each coordinate sequence is Cauchy in $\mathbb R$ or $\mathbb C$, hence converges. The coordinatewise limit defines a vector in the finite-dimensional space, and the original sequence converges to it.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the zero subspace of a Banach space is Banach.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the closed-subspace theorem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The zero subspace $\{0\}$ is closed in any normed space. By the closed-subspace theorem, it is Banach with the restricted norm.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Let $X$ be Banach and let $Y\subset X$ be a closed linear subspace. Prove that a Cauchy sequence in $Y$ has the same limit whether it is viewed in $Y$ or in $X$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The norm on $Y$ is the restriction of the norm on $X$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The distances used in $Y$ and $X$ are identical for points of $Y$. If $y_n\to y$ in $Y$, then $\|y_n-y\|_X=\|y_n-y\|_Y\to0$, so the same convergence holds in $X$. Uniqueness of limits in $X$ gives the same limit.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, conceptual).**

Why does incompleteness matter for solving equations by approximation?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Approximation schemes often construct Cauchy sequences.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

An approximation scheme may produce a Cauchy sequence of candidate solutions. In a Banach space, completeness guarantees a limit inside the same space. Without completeness, the limiting object can fall outside the space, so the approximation process need not produce a solution in the chosen setting.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, proof).**

Let $X$ and $Z$ be Banach spaces. With the norm $\|(x,z)\|=\|x\|+\|z\|$, prove that $X\times Z$ is Banach.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Project a Cauchy sequence in the product to each factor.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $(x_n,z_n)$ be Cauchy in $X\times Z$. Then $\|x_n-x_m\|\leq\|(x_n,z_n)-(x_m,z_m)\|$ and similarly for $z_n-z_m$, so $(x_n)$ and $(z_n)$ are Cauchy. Since $X$ and $Z$ are Banach, $x_n\to x$ and $z_n\to z$. Then

$$
\|(x_n,z_n)-(x,z)\|=\|x_n-x\|+\|z_n-z\|\to0.
$$

Thus the product is Banach.

</details>

</aside>

## Fundamental theorems [Master]

Three theorems make Banach spaces the natural domain for functional analysis. The **Baire category theorem** implies that a complete metric space cannot be written as a countable union of nowhere dense closed sets. From it follow the uniform boundedness principle, the open mapping theorem, and the closed graph theorem [ref: TODO_REF Reed-Simon Vol. I §III].

The open mapping theorem says that a surjective bounded linear map between Banach spaces sends open sets to open sets. Equivalently, a bijective bounded linear map between Banach spaces has a bounded inverse. The closed graph theorem says that a linear map between Banach spaces is bounded exactly when its graph is closed in the product Banach space.

These results fail in useful forms without completeness. Completeness is not a decorative hypothesis; it is the engine that turns pointwise or algebraic control into norm control.

## Completions and examples [Master]

Every normed vector space $X$ has a completion $\widehat X$, together with an isometric linear map $X\to\widehat X$ whose image is dense. The completion is unique up to unique isometric isomorphism. This universal property lets one build Banach spaces from dense algebraic subspaces without changing the intended limiting theory.

Important completions include $L^p$ spaces from simple functions, $\ell^p$ spaces from finite-support sequences, and Sobolev spaces from smooth functions under weak-derivative norms. Differential equations and elliptic operators use these completions because estimates naturally control norms rather than pointwise formulas [ref: quantum-well Analysis (index).md].

The Fredholm unit [03.09.06] assumes Banach spaces because kernels, ranges, cokernels, and inverse-modulo-compact arguments require the closed-subspace and bounded-inverse machinery.

## Connections [Master]

- **Vector space [01.01.03]** — Banach spaces add norms and completeness to vector spaces.

- **Hilbert space [02.11.08]** — Hilbert spaces are Banach spaces whose norm comes from an inner product.

- **Bounded linear operators [02.11.01]** — operator norms and continuity are formulated between Banach spaces.

- **Compact operators [02.11.05]** — compactness in infinite-dimensional Banach spaces controls Fredholm perturbations.

- **Fredholm operators [03.09.06]** — Fredholm theory is a Banach-space theory before it becomes an elliptic-operator theory.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Banach's 1932 monograph placed complete normed linear spaces at the center of analysis, unifying sequence spaces, function spaces, and linear operators. The name "Banach space" reflects that synthesis rather than a single example.

The early twentieth-century shift from formulas to spaces changed the status of existence theorems. Completeness allowed analysts to construct solutions as limits of approximations, while bounded linear maps became the stable morphisms of the category [ref: TODO_REF Conway §III].
