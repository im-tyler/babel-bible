## Intuition [Beginner]

A real symmetric matrix can always be diagonalised: rotate the axes to line up with a set of perpendicular "preferred directions," and the matrix becomes a diagonal list of real numbers called eigenvalues. That fact is the finite-dimensional spectral theorem, one of the most useful tools in linear algebra.

The spectral theorem of this unit extends that diagonalisation to spaces of functions and sequences, where the dimension is infinite. The role of "symmetric matrix" is played by a self-adjoint operator, and the finite list of eigenvalues may become a continuous spread of values across the real line.

The payoff is called functional calculus. Once the operator is resolved into its spectral pieces, you can apply any function to it — square roots, exponentials, heat kernels. That machinery is the engine behind quantum mechanics, vibration analysis, and partial differential equations.

## Visual [Beginner]

A self-adjoint operator stretches space along mutually perpendicular axes. In finite dimensions the picture is an ellipsoid aligned with those axes. In infinite dimensions the axes form an orthonormal basis, or — when the spectrum spreads continuously — a continuous family of perpendicular directions indexed by the real line.

![An ellipsoid with its principal axes aligned to perpendicular eigendirections, generalising to an infinite orthonormal eigenbasis or a continuous spectral family.](/img/spectral-theorem-placeholder.svg)

The operator's spectrum — the set of allowed eigenvalues together with any continuous spread of values — replaces the finite list of diagonal entries found in the matrix case.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the symmetric matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 2 \\ 2 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$ acting on the plane. Its eigenvalues are the values of $\lambda$ that make $A - \lambda I$ non-invertible. Solving $(5 - \lambda)^2 - 4 = 0$ gives $\lambda = 3$ and $\lambda = 7$.

The value $3$ pairs with the direction $(1, -1)$, and the value $7$ pairs with $(1, 1)$. Those two directions are perpendicular. After rotating the coordinate axes to align with them, the operator just stretches by $3$ along one axis and by $7$ along the other, with no mixing between them.

That diagonalisation, written $A = Q D Q^{\ast}$ with $D$ the diagonal matrix of eigenvalues and $Q$ the rotation matrix whose columns are the perpendicular eigendirections, is the finite-dimensional spectral theorem in action.

An infinite-dimensional self-adjoint operator behaves the same way in spirit: perpendicular directions decompose it into stretching factors. When the operator is compact, the stretching factors form a sequence of eigenvalues tending to zero, and the diagonalisation generalises almost verbatim.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\mathcal{H}$ be a complex Hilbert space [02.11.08]. A bounded operator $A \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ is **self-adjoint** when $A = A^\ast$, i.e. $\langle Ax, y \rangle = \langle x, Ay \rangle$ for all $x, y \in \mathcal{H}$ [02.11.01]. The **spectrum** of $A$ is the compact set
$$
\sigma(A) = \{\lambda \in \mathbb{C} : A - \lambda I \text{ is not invertible in } \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})\}.
$$
For self-adjoint $A$, the spectrum is a non-empty compact subset of the real line.

A **projection-valued measure** (also called a **resolution of the identity**) on $\mathbb{R}$ is a map $E : \mathcal{B}_{\mathbb{R}} \to \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ from the Borel $\sigma$-algebra of $\mathbb{R}$ to the orthogonal projections on $\mathcal{H}$, satisfying:

1. $E(\emptyset) = 0$, $E(\mathbb{R}) = I$.
2. $E(\Delta_1 \cap \Delta_2) = E(\Delta_1) E(\Delta_2)$ for all Borel $\Delta_1, \Delta_2$.
3. $E\left(\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty \Delta_n\right) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty E(\Delta_n)$ in the strong operator topology, for pairwise disjoint Borel $\Delta_n$.

For each $x, y \in \mathcal{H}$, the map $\Delta \mapsto \langle E(\Delta) x, y \rangle$ is a bounded complex Borel measure of total variation at most $\|x\|\|y\|$, denoted $d\langle E(\lambda) x, y \rangle$. Integration against a bounded Borel function $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{C}$ is defined by
$$
\int f(\lambda)\, d\langle E(\lambda) x, y \rangle \;=\; \langle f(A) x, y \rangle,
$$
where $f(A) := \int f \, dE$ is the operator defined by the Riesz lemma (the sesquilinear form on the right is bounded). This is the **Borel functional calculus**.

The three settings of the spectral theorem are:

- **(Compact case)** $A \in \mathcal{K}(\mathcal{H})$ self-adjoint [02.11.05]: orthonormal eigenbasis on $(\ker A)^\perp$, eigenvalues real, tending to $0$.
- **(Bounded case)** $A \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ self-adjoint: a projection-valued measure $E_A$ supported on $\sigma(A) \subset \mathbb{R}$ with $A = \int_{\sigma(A)} \lambda\, dE_A(\lambda)$.
- **(Unbounded case)** $A : D(A) \subset \mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{H}$ self-adjoint [02.11.03]: a projection-valued measure $E_A$ on $\mathbb{R}$ with $A = \int_\mathbb{R} \lambda\, dE_A(\lambda)$ on the natural domain $D(A) = \{x : \int \lambda^2\, d\langle E(\lambda) x, x \rangle < \infty\}$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem 1 (compact self-adjoint spectral theorem).** *Let $T \in \mathcal{K}(\mathcal{H})$ be a compact self-adjoint operator on a Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$. Then there exists a finite or countably infinite orthonormal set $\{e_n\}$ of eigenvectors of $T$, with corresponding real eigenvalues $\{\lambda_n\}$ satisfying $\lambda_n \to 0$ whenever the list is infinite, such that*
$$
T x \;=\; \sum_n \lambda_n \langle e_n, x \rangle\, e_n \qquad (x \in \mathcal{H}),
$$
*with convergence in operator norm. The orthogonal complement of $\overline{\operatorname{span}}\{e_n\}$ is $\ker T$. Equivalently, $\mathcal{H} = \overline{\operatorname{span}}\{e_n\} \oplus \ker T$, and $T$ acts as zero on $\ker T$ and as multiplication by $\lambda_n$ on each coordinate $\langle e_n, x \rangle$.*

**Proof.** If $T = 0$ the claim is vacuous (take the empty list). Assume $T \neq 0$. We use the eigenvalue-existence lemma (proved in full in the Full proof set below): a non-zero compact self-adjoint operator has a non-zero real eigenvalue $\lambda_1$ with $|\lambda_1| = \|T\|$, witnessed by a unit eigenvector $e_1$.

Decompose $\mathcal{H} = \mathbb{C} e_1 \oplus e_1^\perp$. The subspace $e_1^\perp$ is $T$-invariant: if $y \perp e_1$ then $\langle Ty, e_1 \rangle = \langle y, Te_1 \rangle = \lambda_1 \langle y, e_1 \rangle = 0$, using self-adjointness. The restriction $T_1 := T|_{e_1^\perp}$ is again compact and self-adjoint, with $\|T_1\| \leq \|T\|$.

Iterate: if $T_1 \neq 0$, apply the lemma to obtain a unit eigenvector $e_2 \perp e_1$ with eigenvalue $\lambda_2$ satisfying $|\lambda_2| = \|T_1\| \leq |\lambda_1|$. Continue. Either the process terminates at step $N$ (in which case $T_N = 0$, so $T$ has finite rank and the list $\{e_1, \ldots, e_N\}$ captures all of $T$ on $(\ker T)^\perp$), or it produces an infinite orthonormal sequence $\{e_n\}$ with $|\lambda_n|$ monotone non-increasing.

In the infinite case, $|\lambda_n| \to 0$: compactness of $T$ implies $\{Te_n\} = \{\lambda_n e_n\}$ has a convergent subsequence, but
$$
\|\lambda_n e_n - \lambda_m e_m\|^2 = |\lambda_n|^2 + |\lambda_m|^2
$$
for $n \neq m$ (orthonormality of $\{e_n\}$), so convergence of a subsequence forces $|\lambda_n| \to 0$.

Define $T' x := \sum_n \lambda_n \langle e_n, x \rangle e_n$. The partial sums of $T'$ converge in operator norm (since $|\lambda_n| \to 0$ and the tail is bounded by $\sup_{n > N} |\lambda_n|$), so $T'$ is compact self-adjoint with the same eigenvectors and eigenvalues as $T$ on $\overline{\operatorname{span}}\{e_n\}$. The difference $S := T - T'$ is compact self-adjoint, vanishes on each $e_n$, and hence descends to $(\overline{\operatorname{span}}\{e_n\})^\perp$.

If $S \neq 0$, the eigenvalue-existence lemma supplies a unit eigenvector $e_\ast \perp \{e_n\}$ with $Se_\ast = \mu e_\ast$ and $|\mu| = \|S\| > 0$. But then $Te_\ast = T'e_\ast + Se_\ast = \mu e_\ast$ (since $e_\ast \perp \{e_n\}$ means $T' e_\ast = 0$), contradicting the maximality of the construction. So $S = 0$ and $T = T'$. Finally, on $(\overline{\operatorname{span}}\{e_n\})^\perp$ the operator $T$ vanishes, so that complement is $\ker T$. $\square$

**Theorem 2 (bounded self-adjoint spectral theorem; statement, sketched).** *Let $A \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ be self-adjoint. There exists a unique projection-valued measure $E_A$ on the Borel $\sigma$-algebra of $\mathbb{R}$, supported on $\sigma(A)$, such that*
$$
A \;=\; \int_{\sigma(A)} \lambda\, dE_A(\lambda).
$$
*The map $f \mapsto f(A) = \int f \, dE_A$ is an isometric $\ast$-homomorphism from the bounded Borel functions on $\sigma(A)$ into $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$, extending the continuous functional calculus $C(\sigma(A)) \to C^\ast(A) \subset \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ [ref: TODO_REF Reed-Simon §VII].*

The construction proceeds in three stages. First, the continuous functional calculus: the commutative unital C\*-algebra $C^\ast(A, I)$ generated by $A$ is isometrically $\ast$-isomorphic to $C(\sigma(A))$ via the Gelfand transform, with $A$ corresponding to the identity function $\lambda \mapsto \lambda$. Second, for each pair $x, y \in \mathcal{H}$, the functional $f \mapsto \langle f(A) x, y \rangle$ is a bounded linear functional on $C(\sigma(A))$ of norm at most $\|x\|\|y\|$; by the Riesz-Markov representation theorem, it is represented by a unique complex Borel measure $\mu_{x,y}$ of total variation at most $\|x\|\|y\|$. Third, the measures $\{\mu_{x,y}\}$ are assembled into the projection-valued measure $E_A$ via $\langle E_A(\Delta) x, y \rangle = \mu_{x,y}(\Delta)$, and the calculus extends from continuous to bounded Borel functions. Uniqueness of $E_A$ follows from the uniqueness of each $\mu_{x,y}$ on continuous test functions, plus a monotone-class argument that promotes equality on $C(\sigma(A))$ to equality on all Borel sets.

**Theorem 3 (unbounded self-adjoint spectral theorem; statement).** *Let $A : D(A) \subset \mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{H}$ be a self-adjoint operator in the sense of [02.11.03] (densely defined, closed, $A = A^\ast$ as operators including domains). There exists a unique projection-valued measure $E_A$ on $\mathbb{R}$ such that*
$$
A x \;=\; \int_{\mathbb{R}} \lambda\, dE_A(\lambda)\, x, \qquad D(A) = \left\{x \in \mathcal{H} : \int_{\mathbb{R}} \lambda^2\, d\langle E_A(\lambda) x, x \rangle < \infty \right\}.
$$
*The map $f \mapsto \int f \, dE_A$ extends to unbounded Borel functions, defining operators on the maximal domains where the relevant quadratic integral is finite [ref: TODO_REF Reed-Simon §VIII]. The proof and the role of the resolution of the identity $E_A$ are developed in [02.11.03]; this unit states the result and connects it to the bounded setting via the Cayley transform $U = (A - i)(A + i)^{-1}$, which is unitary and reduces the unbounded theorem to the bounded spectral theorem for unitary operators.*

**Bridge.** The spectral theorem builds toward the rigorous mathematical framework of quantum mechanics [12.02.02], where every observable is a self-adjoint operator resolved into its projection-valued measure, and appears again in the Dirac operator [03.09.08] and elliptic operators [03.09.09], whose spectra encode geometric and topological data of the underlying manifold. This is exactly the mechanism by which the Laplacian's eigenvalues reveal the shape of a drum or a Riemannian manifold, and the central insight linking functional analysis to both physics and geometry: unbounded self-adjoint operators [02.11.03] become tractable only through their spectral resolutions. Putting these together, the spectral theorem is the diagonalisation engine by which infinite-dimensional operator theory inherits the clarity of finite-dimensional linear algebra [01.01.13], and the bridge is that every self-adjoint operator is, up to a unitary change of variables, a multiplication operator.

Write the action of a compact self-adjoint operator $T$ with orthonormal eigenvectors $\{e_n\}$ and eigenvalues $\{\lambda_n\}$ on a vector $x \in \mathcal{H}$.

Decompose $x$ in the eigenbasis and use linearity.

Tx \;=\; \sum_n \lambda_n \langle e_n, x \rangle\, e_n,

with convergence in the norm of $\mathcal{H}$ for every $x \in \mathcal{H}$ (and in operator norm as a sum of rank-one operators).

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the spectrum of a bounded self-adjoint operator $A \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ is contained in the interval $[m, M]$, where $m = \inf_{\|x\| = 1} \langle Ax, x \rangle$ and $M = \sup_{\|x\| = 1} \langle Ax, x \rangle$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $\lambda = a + ib$ with $b \neq 0$, estimate $\|(A - \lambda I)x\|^2$ from below using the imaginary part.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For any $x$ with $\|x\| = 1$,
$$
\|(A - \lambda I)x\|^2 = \|Ax - ax\|^2 + b^2 \|x\|^2 \geq b^2,
$$
since $\langle Ax - ax, x \rangle = \langle Ax, x \rangle - a$ is real, making $Ax - ax$ orthogonal to the imaginary-direction contribution. Hence $\|(A - \lambda I)^{-1}\| \leq 1/|b|$, so $\lambda$ lies in the resolvent set. Therefore $\sigma(A) \subset \mathbb{R}$. To localise within $[m, M]$: if $\lambda > M$, then $A - \lambda I \leq M - \lambda < 0$, so for $x \neq 0$, $-\langle (A - \lambda I)x, x \rangle \geq (\lambda - M)\|x\|^2 > 0$, giving invertibility by the Lax-Milgram / bounded-inverse argument; similarly for $\lambda < m$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $M$ be the multiplication operator $(Mf)(x) = x f(x)$ on $L^2([0,1])$. Identify the spectrum $\sigma(M)$ and the projection-valued measure $E_M(\Delta)$ for a Borel set $\Delta \subset [0,1]$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Think of $M$ as the canonical model of the bounded spectral theorem; the measure $E_M$ should "cut out" the part of $[0,1]$ where the function is supported.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\sigma(M) = [0,1]$. The projection-valued measure is multiplication by the indicator function: $(E_M(\Delta) f)(x) = \mathbf{1}_\Delta(x) \cdot f(x)$. This is the canonical "multiplication-operator form" of the spectral theorem: every bounded self-adjoint operator is unitarily equivalent to such a multiplication operator on some $L^2$ space.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why does the functional calculus $f \mapsto f(A)$ preserve multiplication, i.e. why is $(fg)(A) = f(A)\, g(A)$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Approximate $f$ and $g$ by simple functions and use the multiplicativity of the projections $E_A(\Delta)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For indicator functions $f = \mathbf{1}_{\Delta_1}$ and $g = \mathbf{1}_{\Delta_2}$, the identity $(fg)(A) = E_A(\Delta_1 \cap \Delta_2) = E_A(\Delta_1) E_A(\Delta_2) = f(A) g(A)$ follows from the multiplicative axiom of projection-valued measures. By linearity this extends to simple functions, and by norm-limits (using that the integral is an isometric $\ast$-homomorphism on bounded functions) to all bounded Borel functions. This multiplicativity is what makes the calculus powerful: applying a composition $g \circ f$ to $A$ yields $g(f(A))$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Let $T$ be compact self-adjoint with eigenvalues $\{\lambda_n\}$ (listed with multiplicity, $|\lambda_1| \geq |\lambda_2| \geq \cdots$). Prove the variational characterisation (Courant-Fischer):
$$
\lambda_n \;=\; \max_{\substack{V \subset \mathcal{H} \\ \dim V = n}} \; \min_{0 \neq x \in V} \frac{\langle Tx, x \rangle}{\|x\|^2}
$$
when the right-hand maximum is taken over $n$-dimensional subspaces $V$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take $V_n = \operatorname{span}\{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$ for the lower bound and project any candidate $V$ onto $V_n^\perp$ for the upper bound.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\{e_k\}$ be the orthonormal eigenvectors with $Te_k = \lambda_k e_k$, $|\lambda_1| \geq |\lambda_2| \geq \cdots$. On $V_n := \operatorname{span}\{e_1, \ldots, e_n\}$, the Rayleigh quotient $\langle Tx, x \rangle / \|x\|^2$ takes values in the convex hull of $\{\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n\}$, so its minimum over $V_n \setminus \{0\}$ is $\min_{k \leq n} \lambda_k \geq \lambda_n$ (using the signed ordering $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots$ from the self-adjoint variational form). This exhibits $V_n$ achieving value $\lambda_n$, giving the lower bound.

For the upper bound, let $V$ be any $n$-dimensional subspace. Then $V \cap \operatorname{span}\{e_1, \ldots, e_{n-1}\}^\perp$ is at least one-dimensional (dimension counting: $\dim V + \dim(\text{that span})^\perp \geq n + (something)$, or by the pigeonhole / dimension formula $\dim(V \cap W^\perp) \geq \dim V - \dim W \geq n - (n-1) = 1$). Pick a unit vector $x \in V \cap \operatorname{span}\{e_1, \ldots, e_{n-1}\}^\perp$. Then $x = \sum_{k \geq n} c_k e_k$ and $\langle Tx, x \rangle = \sum_{k \geq n} \lambda_k |c_k|^2 \leq \lambda_n \sum |c_k|^2 = \lambda_n$. So $\min_{0 \neq x \in V} \langle Tx, x \rangle / \|x\|^2 \leq \lambda_n$, hence the maximum over $V$ is at most $\lambda_n$. Combining both bounds, the identity holds.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Explain why the spectral theorem for an unbounded self-adjoint operator $A$ can be reduced to the bounded case via the Cayley transform $U = (A - iI)(A + iI)^{-1}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show $U$ is unitary, that $1 \notin \sigma(U)$, and that $A$ can be recovered from $U$ by $A = i(I + U)(I - U)^{-1}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Because $A$ is self-adjoint, the operators $A \pm iI$ map $D(A)$ bijectively onto $\mathcal{H}$ (the range condition is one of the equivalent characterisations of self-adjointness). Hence $U = (A - iI)(A + iI)^{-1}$ is a well-defined bounded operator on all of $\mathcal{H}$. A direct computation shows $\|Ux\| = \|x\|$ and $U$ is surjective, so $U$ is unitary. Moreover $1 \notin \sigma(U)$ (otherwise $(A + iI)^{-1}$ would fail to exist). The bounded spectral theorem applied to $U$ yields a projection-valued measure on the unit circle minus the point $1$; transporting this measure to the real line via the inverse Cayley map $z \mapsto i(1 + z)/(1 - z)$ produces the resolution of the identity $E_A$ for $A$. This is the standard reduction used in [02.11.03] and in von Neumann's original treatment.

</details>

</aside>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

`lean_status: none` — Mathlib provides the continuous functional calculus for bounded self-adjoint and normal operators (via `ContinuousMap` and the Gelfand transform on the commutative C\*-subalgebra `CStarAlgebra.adjoin`), and the spectral theorem for finite-dimensional Hermitian endomorphisms via eigenvector decomposition. What is absent in packaged curriculum form is the infinite-dimensional content of this unit:

- Projection-valued measures as countably-additive Boolean-algebra homomorphisms from the Borel $\sigma$-algebra of $\sigma(A)$ to orthogonal projections, with their integration theory.
- The equivalence between the projection-valued measure form and the multiplication-operator form (unitary equivalence to multiplication by $\lambda$ on some $L^2(\sigma(A), \mu)$).
- The Borel functional calculus (extending the continuous calculus to bounded Borel functions, not just continuous ones).
- The unbounded self-adjoint spectral resolution, including the domain characterisation via square-integrability against the resolution of the identity.

Each of these requires new Mathlib infrastructure: a type of projection-valued measures, a Bochner-style integration theory for them, and the Riesz-Markov representation argument that lifts the continuous calculus on $C(\sigma(A))$ to a Borel calculus. ## Advanced results [Master]

**Multiplication-operator form.** The spectral theorem admits an equivalent and often more concrete statement. A bounded self-adjoint operator $A \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ is unitarily equivalent to a multiplication operator: there exists a measure space $(X, \mu)$, a unitary $U : \mathcal{H} \to L^2(X, \mu)$, and a real-valued essentially bounded function $\varphi \in L^\infty(X, \mu; \mathbb{R})$ such that
$$
UAU^{-1} f \;=\; \varphi \cdot f \qquad (f \in L^2(X, \mu)).
$$
The function $\varphi$ plays the role of the diagonal of the matrix: the spectrum $\sigma(A)$ is the essential range of $\varphi$. For a cyclic vector $x_0 \in \mathcal{H}$ (one for which $\{E_A(\Delta) x_0\}$ spans a dense subspace), the measure space can be taken as $\sigma(A)$ with measure $\mu_{x_0, x_0}$, and $\varphi(\lambda) = \lambda$. The general case decomposes $\mathcal{H}$ into at most countably many cyclic subspaces and takes the disjoint union [ref: TODO_REF Reed-Simon §VII].

**Continuous functional calculus via Gelfand duality.** For self-adjoint $A$, the closed unital subalgebra $C^\ast(A, I) \subset \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ generated by $A$ is a commutative unital C\*-algebra. Gelfand duality [39.01.01] identifies commutative C\*-algebras with spaces of continuous functions on compact Hausdorff spaces: $C^\ast(A, I) \cong C(\sigma(A))$, with $A$ mapping to the identity function $\mathrm{id}_\lambda$. This is the continuous functional calculus: for $f \in C(\sigma(A))$, the operator $f(A)$ is the inverse image of $f$ under this isomorphism. The map $f \mapsto f(A)$ is an isometric $\ast$-isomorphism preserving composition: $(g \circ f)(A) = g(f(A))$ whenever $f$ maps $\sigma(A)$ into the domain of $g$ [39.01.02].

**Spectral measures and the Riesz-Markov lift.** The continuous calculus extends to bounded Borel functions by a measure-theoretic lifting. For each pair $x, y \in \mathcal{H}$, the functional $f \mapsto \langle f(A) x, y \rangle$ on $C(\sigma(A))$ is bounded of norm at most $\|x\|\|y\|$; the Riesz-Markov representation theorem [02.11.02] supplies a unique regular complex Borel measure $\mu_{x,y}$ on $\sigma(A)$ realising it. The family $\{\mu_{x,y}\}$ is sesquilinear in $(x, y)$ and satisfies the positivity condition $\mu_{x,x}(\sigma(A)) = \|x\|^2$, $\mu_{x,x}(\Delta) \geq 0$ for Borel $\Delta$. This positivity is what allows the measures to be assembled into a single projection-valued measure $E_A$ via $\langle E_A(\Delta) x, y \rangle = \mu_{x,y}(\Delta)$ [ref: TODO_REF Reed-Simon §VII].

**Functional calculus as the payoff.** The Borel functional calculus turns the self-adjoint operator $A$ into a variable that can be substituted into any Borel function. Three flagship consequences:

- **Square roots and absolute values.** $|A| = (A^2)^{1/2}$ and the polar decomposition $A = U|A|$ are defined via the calculus; this is the input to the Schatten ideal theory and the singular-value decomposition for compact operators [02.11.05].
- **Exponentials and groups.** For self-adjoint $A$, the operator $e^{itA}$ (defined by the calculus applied to $\lambda \mapsto e^{it\lambda}$) is unitary, and $t \mapsto e^{itA}$ is a strongly continuous one-parameter unitary group. Stone's theorem [ref: TODO_REF Stone 1932] identifies strongly continuous one-parameter unitary groups with self-adjoint generators: $U(t) = e^{itA}$ for a unique self-adjoint $A$. This is the mathematical statement that self-adjoint operators are the infinitesimal generators of quantum-mechanical time evolution.
- **Resolvents and the Laplace transform.** The resolvent $(A - z)^{-1}$ for $\operatorname{Im} z \neq 0$ is recovered from the spectral measure by $(A - z)^{-1} = \int (\lambda - z)^{-1} dE_A(\lambda)$. This identity underlies scattering theory, the spectral shift function, and the Birman-Krein formula.

**Unbounded theorem and quantum mechanics.** The unbounded spectral theorem is the rigorous form of the Born rule and the spectral measurement postulate: the possible outcomes of measuring the observable $A$ on a state $\psi$ are the points of $\sigma(A)$, and the probability of finding the outcome in a Borel set $\Delta$ is $\langle E_A(\Delta) \psi, \psi \rangle$. The expectation value is $\langle A \psi, \psi \rangle = \int \lambda\, d\langle E_A(\lambda) \psi, \psi \rangle$. This is why observables must be self-adjoint: only self-adjoint operators admit projection-valued measures, hence well-defined measurement statistics [ref: TODO_REF von Neumann 1932].

**Sturm-Liouville and elliptic PDE.** A regular Sturm-Liouville operator on $[a, b]$ (such as $-d^2/dx^2 + V(x)$ with suitable boundary conditions) is self-adjoint on $L^2([a,b])$ and has compact resolvent; hence its spectrum is a discrete list of real eigenvalues tending to $+\infty$, with an orthonormal eigenbasis. This is the content of Sturm-Liouville theory, and it generalises to elliptic self-adjoint operators on compact manifolds [03.09.09]: the Laplacian on a compact Riemannian manifold has discrete spectrum $0 = \lambda_0 < \lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2 \leq \cdots \to \infty$, and Weyl's law gives the asymptotic counting function $N(\Lambda) \sim \Lambda^{n/2}/(2\pi)^n \operatorname{Vol}(M)$ [ref: TODO_REF Reed-Simon §XIII].

**Synthesis.** The spectral theorem is the foundational reason that self-adjoint operators — bounded or unbounded — admit a complete structural description as multiplication operators, or equivalently as operator-valued integrals against projection-valued measures. The compact case generalises finite-dimensional diagonalisation [01.01.13] to operators whose eigenvalues accumulate only at zero; the bounded case resolves every self-adjoint operator into a direct integral over its spectrum; and the unbounded case supplies the functional calculus that quantum mechanics [12.02.02] and the theory of elliptic partial differential equations [02.18.03] absolutely require. This is exactly the bridge between algebra and measure: the C\*-algebra generated by a self-adjoint operator is isomorphic to continuous functions on its spectrum, the central insight of Gelfand duality [39.01.01]. Putting these together, the spectral theorem unifies matrix theory, integral equations, quantum observables, vibration analysis, and elliptic operator theory [03.09.09] into a single diagonalisation framework, and the bridge is that every self-adjoint operator, no matter how abstractly presented, is ultimately a multiplication operator in disguise.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (eigenvalue existence for compact self-adjoint operators).** *Let $T \in \mathcal{K}(\mathcal{H})$ be self-adjoint with $T \neq 0$. Then either $\|T\|$ or $-\|T\|$ is a non-zero eigenvalue of $T$.*

**Proof.** For self-adjoint $T$, the operator norm is computed by the quadratic form:
$$
\|T\| \;=\; \sup_{\|x\| = 1} |\langle Tx, x \rangle|.
$$
To see this, set $M := \sup_{\|x\|=1} \langle Tx, x \rangle$ and $m := \inf_{\|x\|=1} \langle Tx, x \rangle$. The bound $\|T\| \geq \max(|M|, |m|)$ is immediate. For the reverse, use the polarisation-like identity (valid for self-adjoint $T$):
$$
4 \operatorname{Re} \langle Tx, y \rangle \;=\; \langle T(x + y), x + y \rangle - \langle T(x - y), x - y \rangle,
$$
which yields, after Cauchy-Schwarz applied to each quadratic form on the right, $|\operatorname{Re}\langle Tx, y\rangle| \leq \max(|M|, |m|) \cdot \|x\| \|y\|$. Replacing $x$ by a phase-rotated $e^{i\theta} x$ controls $|\langle Tx, y \rangle|$, and taking $\sup$ over unit $x, y$ gives $\|T\| \leq \max(|M|, |m|)$. Hence $\|T\| = \max(|M|, |m|)$.

Assume $\|T\| = M$ (the case $\|T\| = -m$ is analogous, replacing $T$ by $-T$). Take a sequence $\{x_n\}$ of unit vectors with $\langle Tx_n, x_n \rangle \to M$. Expand:
$$
\|Tx_n - M x_n\|^2 \;=\; \|Tx_n\|^2 - 2M \langle Tx_n, x_n \rangle + M^2 \|x_n\|^2.
$$
Using $\|Tx_n\| \leq \|T\| = M$ and $\|x_n\| = 1$, the right side is bounded above by $M^2 - 2M \langle Tx_n, x_n \rangle + M^2 \to 2M^2 - 2M^2 = 0$. So $\|Tx_n - M x_n\| \to 0$.

By compactness of $T$, the sequence $\{Tx_n\}$ has a convergent subsequence $Tx_{n_k} \to y \in \mathcal{H}$. Then $M x_{n_k} \to y$ as well (since $\|Tx_n - M x_n\| \to 0$), so $x_{n_k} \to y / M$; call this limit $e$. By continuity of $T$ and the convergence, $Te = \lim T x_{n_k} = y = M e$. Finally, $\|e\| = \lim \|x_{n_k}\| = 1$, and $\langle Te, e \rangle = M = \|T\| \neq 0$, so $e \neq 0$. Hence $M$ is a non-zero eigenvalue with unit eigenvector $e$. $\square$

**Proposition (uniqueness of the projection-valued measure).** *Let $A \in \mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ be self-adjoint. If $E$ and $E'$ are two projection-valued measures on $\sigma(A)$ both satisfying $A = \int \lambda \, dE(\lambda) = \int \lambda \, dE'(\lambda)$, then $E = E'$.*

**Proof.** By hypothesis, the two measures give the same operator when integrated against the identity function $\lambda \mapsto \lambda$. The polynomial approximation argument (Weierstrass) shows they also agree on every polynomial in $\lambda$, and by norm-limits, on every continuous function $f \in C(\sigma(A))$. Hence for every $x, y \in \mathcal{H}$ and every $f \in C(\sigma(A))$,
$$
\int f \, d\langle E(\lambda) x, y \rangle \;=\; \int f \, d\langle E'(\lambda) x, y \rangle.
$$
Two finite regular Borel measures that integrate every continuous function identically are equal (Riesz-Markov uniqueness). So $\langle E(\Delta) x, y \rangle = \langle E'(\Delta) x, y \rangle$ for every Borel $\Delta$ and every $x, y$, giving $E(\Delta) = E'(\Delta)$ as operators. $\square$

**Proposition (spectral mapping for the functional calculus).** *Let $A$ be self-adjoint and $f : \sigma(A) \to \mathbb{C}$ be a bounded Borel function. Then $\sigma(f(A)) \subset \overline{f(\sigma(A))}$, with equality when $f$ is continuous.*

**Proof.** For continuous $f$: if $\zeta \notin f(\sigma(A))$, then $g(\lambda) = (\zeta - f(\lambda))^{-1}$ is a well-defined continuous function on $\sigma(A)$, and $g(A)$ is a two-sided inverse of $\zeta I - f(A)$ by the multiplicativity of the continuous functional calculus. So $\zeta \notin \sigma(f(A))$, giving $\sigma(f(A)) \subset f(\sigma(A))$. Conversely, if $\zeta = f(\lambda_0)$ for some $\lambda_0 \in \sigma(A)$, take a sequence of unit vectors $x_n$ "concentrated near $\lambda_0$" via the spectral measure: $x_n := E_A((\lambda_0 - 1/n, \lambda_0 + 1/n)) y / \|E_A(\cdots) y\|$ for suitable $y$ with $E_A(\{\lambda_0\}) = 0$ (or an eigenvector when $E_A(\{\lambda_0\}) \neq 0$). Then $\|(\zeta I - f(A)) x_n\| \to 0$ by continuity of $f$ at $\lambda_0$, so $\zeta I - f(A)$ is not bounded below, hence not invertible: $\zeta \in \sigma(f(A))$. For bounded Borel $f$, only the inclusion survives: $\sigma(f(A)) \subset \overline{f(\sigma(A))}$, since the essential range of $f$ replaces the pointwise image. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Bounded linear operators [02.11.01]** — the spectral theorem is a statement about elements of $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ (in the bounded case); the operator norm, spectrum, and adjoint operation are the inputs that the theorem reorganises into a diagonal form.

- **Unbounded self-adjoint operators [02.11.03]** — the unbounded case of the spectral theorem lives here; the resolution of the identity $E_A$ and the natural domain $D(A)$ defined by square-integrability are the load-bearing output for quantum mechanics and differential operators.

- **Hahn-Banach theorem [02.11.02]** — the Riesz-Markov representation theorem, used to construct the spectral measure from the continuous functional calculus, is a direct descendant of the Hahn-Banach extension machinery in its measure-theoretic form.

- **Compact operators [02.11.05]** — the compact self-adjoint case is the cleanest entry point to the spectral theorem, producing an honest eigenvector basis; the Riesz-Schauder theory of compact operator spectra is the finite-rank-limit backbone of the general theorem.

- **Hilbert space [02.11.08]** — the ambient structure; the inner product, the adjoint, and the notion of orthonormal basis all live here, and without the Hilbert structure (in mere Banach spaces) the spectral theorem fails.

- **Spectral theorem for normal operators on a finite-dimensional inner product space [01.01.13]** — the finite-dimensional prototype; this unit is the infinite-dimensional generalisation that handles continuous spectrum and unbounded operators.

- **Dirac operator [03.09.08]** and **elliptic operators [03.09.09]** — geometric operators whose spectra (eigenvalues of the Laplacian, Dirac spectrum) encode topological and geometric data of the underlying manifold; the spectral theorem is what makes "eigenvalue counting" rigorous.

- **C\*-algebras [39.01.01]** and **Gelfand duality [39.01.02]** — the continuous functional calculus is a direct application of Gelfand duality for commutative C\*-algebras; the spectral theorem is the Hilbert-space-concrete realisation of the abstract duality.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The spectral theorem has its origin in David Hilbert's work on integral equations, communicated in five notes between 1904 and 1906 and collected in his 1912 book *Grundzuge einer allgemeinen Theorie der linearen Integralgleichungen*. Hilbert studied what are now called Hilbert-Schmidt integral operators with symmetric kernels and discovered that they admit a complete orthonormal eigenbasis with real eigenvalues — the first form of the spectral theorem, in the compact self-adjoint case. His "spectral decomposition" of bounded quadratic forms in infinitely many variables was the seed from which the entire theory of operator algebras grew [ref: TODO_REF Hilbert 1912].

Hellinger and Toeplitz, in their monumental 1927 *Enzyklopadie* article *Integralgleichungen und Gleichungen mit unendlichvielen Unbekannten*, systematised and extended Hilbert's theory to handle operators with continuous spectrum. They introduced the spectral measure viewpoint, in which the operator is resolved into a continuous family of perpendicular projections indexed by the real line, rather than a discrete list of eigenvectors. This was the conceptual leap that made the bounded spectral theorem possible as a statement about general self-adjoint operators, not just compact ones [ref: TODO_REF Hellinger-Toeplitz 1927].

The definitive treatment of the unbounded case is due to John von Neumann and Marshall Stone, working independently in the early 1930s. Von Neumann's 1932 *Mathematische Grundlagen der Quantenmechanik* gave the spectral theorem for unbounded self-adjoint operators its modern form, with the resolution of the identity as the central object, and used it to put the newly-discovered matrix mechanics of Heisenberg and the wave mechanics of Schrodinger on a common rigorous footing. Stone's 1932 *Linear Transformations in Hilbert Space* developed the same theory from the angle of one-parameter unitary groups (Stone's theorem, identifying strongly continuous unitary groups with self-adjoint generators) [ref: TODO_REF Stone 1932]. The equivalence of the von Neumann and Stone approaches — via the Cayley transform relating self-adjoint operators to unitary operators — is now the standard pedagogical route.

Philosophically, the spectral theorem marks the moment when infinite-dimensional analysis ceased to be a collection of clever tricks for specific integral and differential equations and became a unified structural theory. The theorem says that self-adjointness is the exact condition under which an operator admits a "diagonal form," and that this diagonal form — whether a discrete eigenbasis or a continuous spectral measure — is unique. It is the conceptual hinge on which quantum mechanics, the theory of elliptic PDE, and the modern theory of operator algebras all turn.
