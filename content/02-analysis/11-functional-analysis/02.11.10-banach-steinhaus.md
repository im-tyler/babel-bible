## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine a large collection of stretching machines, each labelled by an index. Feed the same input vector into every machine. For each fixed input, the outputs are all bounded by some number that depends on the input. The uniform boundedness principle says that, in a complete space, the machines themselves share a single global speed limit: there is one constant that bounds every machine's worst-case stretch.

The surprise is the jump from "bounded input by input" to "bounded all at once." Each individual input gives its own bound, and there is no obvious way to merge infinitely many such bounds into one. The theorem says the merge always happens — but only in a complete space.

This is the third pillar of Banach-space theory. Hahn-Banach builds enough linear measurements. The open mapping theorem turns surjections into open maps. Banach-Steinhaus turns pointwise bounds into a uniform bound on a whole family of operators. Together they are the soft-analysis toolkit that makes infinite-dimensional geometry tractable.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a stack of dials, one per operator, each reporting the worst-case stretch of its machine. The theorem promises that, when every input produces bounded readings across the stack, every dial sits below one shared red line.

![A family of operator-norm dials all sitting below a single shared uniform bound line.](/img/banach-steinhaus-placeholder.svg)

The red line is the uniform bound. The theorem is non-constructive: it guarantees the line exists without telling you its numerical value. You learn that a finite shared bound exists, then you go compute it by hand for the specific family.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Work over the space of real sequences that end in zeros: vectors like $(3, -1, 2, 0, 0, \ldots)$ where from some index onward every entry is $0$. Use the natural size given by the largest absolute entry. This space is incomplete — it has "missing limit points."

For each positive whole number $n$, define a machine $T_n$ that multiplies the $n$-th entry by $n$.

Take the concrete vector $x = (5, 2, -7, 0, 0, \ldots)$. Then $T_1(x) = 5$, $T_2(x) = 4$, $T_3(x) = -21$, and $T_n(x) = 0$ for every $n \geq 4$. The largest reading across the whole stack is $21$. The same holds for any fixed vector, because any fixed vector has only finitely many non-zero entries.

So the family is pointwise bounded: each input has its own finite bound. But the worst-case stretch of $T_n$ is exactly $n$. The unit vector $e_n$ with a single $1$ in slot $n$ has size $1$, and $T_n(e_n) = n$. So the machine sizes are $1, 2, 3, 4, \ldots$, which grow without bound.

The principle fails here because the space is incomplete. Completeness is the hidden ingredient that forces the pointwise bounds to merge into one uniform bound.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $X$ and $Y$ denote Banach spaces [02.11.04] over the scalar field $\mathbb K \in \{\mathbb R, \mathbb C\}$, and $\mathcal B(X, Y)$ denotes the space of bounded linear operators $X \to Y$ [02.11.01].

**Definition (pointwise boundedness).** A family $\{T_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A} \subset \mathcal B(X, Y)$ is **pointwise bounded** if for every $x \in X$,

$$
\sup_{\alpha \in A} \|T_\alpha x\|_Y < \infty.
$$

**Definition (uniform boundedness).** The family is **uniformly bounded** if

$$
\sup_{\alpha \in A} \|T_\alpha\|_{\mathcal B(X,Y)} < \infty.
$$

Uniform boundedness implies pointwise boundedness by the operator-norm inequality $\|T_\alpha x\| \leq \|T_\alpha\| \cdot \|x\|$. The reverse implication is the content of the theorem below, and it is the direction that demands completeness.

**Theorem (Baire category, complete metric form).** Let $(M, d)$ be a complete metric space. Then $M$ is not a countable union of closed sets with empty interior. Equivalently, a countable intersection of dense open subsets of $M$ is dense. A complete metric space is called a **Baire space** [ref: TODO_REF Baire 1899].

**Theorem (uniform boundedness principle).** Let $X$ be a Banach space, $Y$ a normed space, and $\{T_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A} \subset \mathcal B(X, Y)$ a pointwise-bounded family. Then the family is uniformly bounded:

$$
\sup_{\alpha \in A} \|T_\alpha\| < \infty.
$$

The conclusion is often abbreviated as "pointwise bounded implies uniformly bounded." The index set $A$ may be uncountable; the Baire argument uses only the closed sets cut out by each operator, not a countability hypothesis on $A$.

**Theorem (Banach-Steinhaus, dual formulation).** Let $X$ be a Banach space. A subset $E \subset X$ is norm-bounded if and only if it is weakly bounded, that is, if for every $f \in X^*$,

$$
\sup_{x \in E} |f(x)| < \infty.
$$

The forward direction is immediate from $|f(x)| \leq \|f\| \cdot \|x\|$. The reverse direction applies the uniform boundedness principle to the family of evaluation functionals $\{\operatorname{ev}_x\}_{x \in E} \subset \mathcal B(X^*, \mathbb K)$, where $\operatorname{ev}_x(f) = f(x)$, and then invokes Hahn-Banach [02.11.02] to identify $\|\operatorname{ev}_x\| = \|x\|$ via the canonical isometric embedding $J : X \hookrightarrow X^{**}$.

The two formulations are equivalent in content. The first, in terms of operator families, is the form most often applied in convergence theory. The second, in terms of weak versus norm boundedness, is the form most often applied in weak-topology arguments and underpins the spectral theory developed in [02.11.11].

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

We prove the uniform boundedness principle directly from Baire, then derive the dual formulation as a corollary that uses Hahn-Banach.

**Proof of the uniform boundedness principle.** For each $n \geq 1$, define the level set

$$
E_n = \{x \in X : \sup_{\alpha \in A} \|T_\alpha x\| \leq n\}.
$$

Each $E_n$ is closed, because it is the intersection $\bigcap_{\alpha \in A} \{x \in X : \|T_\alpha x\| \leq n\}$ and each set in the intersection is the preimage of the closed interval $[0, n]$ under the continuous map $x \mapsto \|T_\alpha x\|$. The hypothesis of pointwise boundedness gives

$$
X = \bigcup_{n \geq 1} E_n.
$$

Since $X$ is a Banach space, Baire's theorem applies: at least one $E_N$ has non-empty interior. So there exist $x_0 \in X$ and $r >  0$ with the open ball $B_X(x_0, r)$ contained in $E_N$.

For any $z \in B_X(0, r)$ and any $\alpha$, both $x_0 + z$ and $x_0$ lie in $E_N$, so by linearity of $T_\alpha$,

$$
\|T_\alpha z\| = \|T_\alpha(x_0 + z) - T_\alpha x_0\| \leq \|T_\alpha(x_0 + z)\| + \|T_\alpha x_0\| \leq N + N = 2N.
$$

Now scale. For any non-zero $x \in X$, the rescaled vector $r x / (2\|x\|)$ lies in $B_X(0, r)$, so

$$
\|T_\alpha x\| = \frac{2\|x\|}{r} \cdot \left\|T_\alpha\!\left(\frac{r x}{2\|x\|}\right)\right\| \leq \frac{2\|x\|}{r} \cdot 2N = \frac{4N}{r} \cdot \|x\|.
$$

Taking the supremum over $\|x\| \leq 1$ gives $\|T_\alpha\| \leq 4N/r$ for every $\alpha \in A$, which is the desired uniform bound. $\square$

The numerical constant $4N/r$ is an artefact of the proof; the theorem only asserts that some finite uniform bound exists. The bound is non-constructive in the sense that Baire's theorem supplies $N$ and $r$ qualitatively, without an algorithm to compute them from the family.

**Corollary (dual formulation).** Let $E \subset X$ be weakly bounded. Apply the uniform boundedness principle to the family $\{\operatorname{ev}_x\}_{x \in E} \subset \mathcal B(X^*, \mathbb K)$, where $X^*$ is always a Banach space. Weak boundedness of $E$ is exactly pointwise boundedness of this family:

$$
\sup_{x \in E} |\operatorname{ev}_x(f)| = \sup_{x \in E} |f(x)| < \infty \quad \text{for every } f \in X^*.
$$

The principle gives $M := \sup_{x \in E} \|\operatorname{ev}_x\| < \infty$. By the Hahn-Banach theorem [02.11.02], the canonical embedding $J : X \to X^{**}$ is an isometry, so $\|\operatorname{ev}_x\| = \|J(x)\| = \|x\|$. Therefore $\sup_{x \in E} \|x\| = M < \infty$, and $E$ is norm-bounded. $\square$

**Remark (necessity of completeness).** The proof uses completeness of $X$ in exactly one place: the application of Baire's theorem to the decomposition $X = \bigcup_n E_n$. On an incomplete normed space the conclusion fails; the worked example in the Beginner tier exhibits a pointwise-bounded family on the space of finitely supported sequences whose operator norms are $1, 2, 3, \ldots$ and hence unbounded.

**Bridge.** The uniform boundedness principle builds toward the resonance theorem and the Fourier-divergence application below, and it appears again in the weak-compactness and weak-convergence arguments of the spectral theory in [02.11.11]. The foundational reason the principle works is that Baire category forces one of the level sets $E_n$ to be fat somewhere; this is exactly the same completeness mechanism that drove the open mapping theorem [02.11.09]. Putting these together with the extension machinery of Hahn-Banach [02.11.02], the three pillars exhaust the soft-analysis toolkit of Banach-space theory: one pillar builds duals, one builds inverses, and one builds uniform bounds from pointwise data.

**Exercise 1 (easy, conceptual).**

Where in the proof of the uniform boundedness principle is the completeness of $X$ used, and why does the proof break down for an incomplete normed space?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Track the single appeal to a category theorem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Completeness is used exactly once, to apply the Baire category theorem to the closed covering $X = \bigcup_n E_n$. Baire guarantees that some $E_N$ has non-empty interior, which produces the ball $B_X(x_0, r) \subset E_N$ from which the uniform bound $4N/r$ is extracted. On an incomplete normed space, Baire's theorem may fail: a complete-looking space can be written as a countable union of closed sets with empty interior (the rationals are the canonical example). Without a fat level set, the geometric estimate never starts, and indeed the finitely-supported-sequence family of the Beginner worked example realizes the failure.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

On the space $c_{00}$ of finitely supported real sequences with the supremum norm, define $T_n(x) = n \cdot x_n$ for $n \geq 1$. Compute the operator norm $\|T_n\|$ for $n = 1, 2, 3$, and give the value of $\sup_{n \geq 1} \|T_n\|$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Test each $T_n$ on the standard basis vector $e_n$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For each $n$, the basis vector $e_n$ (with $1$ in slot $n$ and $0$ elsewhere) has $\|e_n\|_\infty = 1$ and $T_n(e_n) = n$, so $\|T_n\| \geq n$. Conversely $|T_n(x)| = |n x_n| \leq n \|x\|_\infty$, so $\|T_n\| \leq n$. Hence $\|T_n\| = n$ for each $n$, giving values $1, 2, 3$ for $n = 1, 2, 3$, and $\sup_{n \geq 1} \|T_n\| = +\infty$. The family is pointwise bounded (each $x \in c_{00}$ has only finitely many non-zero entries) yet not uniformly bounded, confirming that the conclusion of Banach-Steinhaus fails on this incomplete space.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Let $X, Y$ be Banach spaces and let $(T_n)$ be a sequence in $\mathcal B(X, Y)$ such that $T_n x \to T x$ for every $x \in X$. Show that $T \in \mathcal B(X, Y)$ and $\|T\| \leq \liminf_{n \to \infty} \|T_n\|$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Pointwise convergence makes the family pointwise bounded; apply Banach-Steinhaus, then pass the norm inequality through the limit.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Linearity of $T$ follows from linearity of each $T_n$ and linearity of limits. For boundedness: convergent sequences in $Y$ are bounded, so for each $x \in X$ the sequence $(\|T_n x\|)$ is bounded, which is to say $\sup_n \|T_n x\| < \infty$. The family $\{T_n\}$ is pointwise bounded, and Banach-Steinhaus gives $M := \sup_n \|T_n\| < \infty$. Then for every $x$,

$$
\|T x\| = \lim_{n \to \infty} \|T_n x\| \leq \liminf_{n \to \infty} \|T_n\| \cdot \|x\| \leq M \|x\|,
$$

so $T$ is bounded with $\|T\| \leq \liminf_n \|T_n\|$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Let $X$ be a Banach space and let $E \subset X$. Show that $E$ is norm-bounded if and only if $\sup_{x \in E} |f(x)| < \infty$ for every $f \in X^*$.

For the reverse direction, view $E$ as a family of evaluation functionals on $X^*$ and apply the uniform boundedness principle.

The hypothesis says that for every $f \in X^*$, $\sup_{x \in E} |\operatorname{ev}_x(f)| < \infty$, so the family $\{\operatorname{ev}_x\}_{x \in E} \subset \mathcal B(X^*, \mathbb K)$ is pointwise bounded. Since $X^*$ is always a Banach space, Banach-Steinhaus yields $\sup_{x \in E} \|\operatorname{ev}_x\| < \infty$. On the Banach space $c_0$ of real sequences converging to zero (with the supremum norm), define the linear functionals $f_n(x) = n \cdot x_n$. Express $\|f_n\|$ as a function of $n$, and determine whether the family $\{f_n\}$ is pointwise bounded on $c_0$.

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

Let $X, Y$ be Banach spaces and let $T \in \mathcal B(X, Y)$. Suppose $(S_n)$ is a sequence in $\mathcal B(Y, X)$ with $\|S_n T x - x\| \to 0$ for every $x \in X$ (so $S_n$ is an approximate left-inverse of $T$ on the range of $T$). Show that if $T$ is not bounded below — meaning there is no $c > 0$ with $\|T x\| \geq c \|x\|$ for all $x$ — then $\sup_n \|S_n\| = \infty$. Conclude, using the contrapositive of Banach-Steinhaus, that if $\sup_n \|S_n\| < \infty$ then $T$ must be bounded below.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If $\|S_n\| \leq M$ for all $n$ and $\|T x\| \geq c \|x\|$, combine the two inequalities; for the converse, suppose $\|S_n\|$ is uniformly bounded and extract a lower bound on $\|T x\|$ from the approximate-inverse identity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $\sup_n \|S_n\| = M < \infty$. For any $x \in X$,

$$
\|x\| = \lim_n \|S_n T x\| \leq \limsup_n \|S_n\| \cdot \|T x\| \leq M \|T x\|,
$$

so $\|T x\| \geq \|x\| / M$ for every $x$, which is the bounded-below condition with $c = 1/M > 0$. Taking the contrapositive: if $T$ is not bounded below, then no such uniform $M$ can exist, so $\sup_n \|S_n\| = \infty$. By the contrapositive of Banach-Steinhaus (if the operator norms are unbounded then some point must blow up), some $x_0 \in X$ then has $\sup_n \|S_n T x_0\| = \infty$, even though $S_n T x \to x$ for those $x$ where the convergence holds — a manifestation of condensation of singularities.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**The resonance theorem.** The contrapositive of the uniform boundedness principle is a statement about where unboundedness must concentrate. If $\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha\| = \infty$, the principle forces the existence of a point $x \in X$ with $\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha x\| = \infty$. The resonance theorem strengthens existence to genericity.

**Theorem (condensation of singularities; Banach-Steinhaus 1927).** Let $X$ be a Banach space, $Y$ a normed space, and $\{T_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A} \subset \mathcal B(X, Y)$ with $\sup_{\alpha \in A} \|T_\alpha\| = \infty$. Then the set

$$
S = \{x \in X : \sup_{\alpha \in A} \|T_\alpha x\| = \infty\}
$$

is a dense $G_\delta$ subset of $X$. In particular, $S$ is residual: its complement is of the first category.

The proof is a Baire argument applied to the complements of the level sets $E_n$ used in the direct proof. Each $X \setminus E_n$ is open and dense (density uses $\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha\| = \infty$), so their countable intersection $S = \bigcap_n (X \setminus E_n)$ is a dense $G_\delta$ by Baire. The name "condensation of singularities" records that the bad points — where the family blows up — are not isolated pathologies but a topologically generic phenomenon.

**The resonance theorem (countable iteration).** Let $(T_{m,n})_{m,n \geq 1}$ be a double sequence in $\mathcal B(X, Y)$ with $\sup_n \|T_{m,n}\| = \infty$ for every $m$. Then there exists $x \in X$ with $\sup_n \|T_{m,n} x\| = \infty$ for every $m$ simultaneously.

The proof iterates the condensation theorem over $m$: each set $S_m = \{x : \sup_n \|T_{m,n} x\| = \infty\}$ is a dense $G_\delta$, and the countable intersection $\bigcap_m S_m$ is again a dense $G_\delta$ by Baire. This is the form Banach and Steinhaus used to produce functions with prescribed divergence behaviour across a countable family of points.

**Application: divergence of Fourier series.** The historically decisive application, due in essence to Du Bois-Reymond (1873) and re-derived abstractly by Banach and Steinhaus, shows that continuous periodic functions whose Fourier series diverge at a specified point are not anomalies but a generic phenomenon.

Let $C(\mathbb T)$ denote the Banach space of continuous $2\pi$-periodic functions with the supremum norm. For $f \in C(\mathbb T)$, the $n$-th symmetric partial sum of the Fourier series evaluated at $0$ is

$$
(S_n f)(0) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(t) D_n(t) \, dt,
$$

where $D_n(t) = \sin((n + \tfrac12) t) / \sin(t/2)$ is the Dirichlet kernel. The map $f \mapsto (S_n f)(0)$ is a bounded linear functional $\Lambda_n$ on $C(\mathbb T)$ with operator norm equal to the $L^1$-norm of $D_n / (2\pi)$, the $n$-th **Lebesgue constant** $L_n$.

The Lebesgue constants grow logarithmically: $L_n \sim (4/\pi^2) \log n$ as $n \to \infty$ [ref: TODO_REF Dunford-Schwartz §II.11]. In particular $\sup_n L_n = \infty$, so $\sup_n \|\Lambda_n\| = \infty$. By condensation of singularities, the set

$$
\{f \in C(\mathbb T) : \sup_n |(S_n f)(0)| = \infty\}
$$

is a dense $G_\delta$ in $C(\mathbb T)$. For a generic continuous function, the Fourier series at the origin is unbounded and in particular fails to converge.

**Remark on Carleson's theorem.** Carleson (1966) proved that the Fourier series of every $L^2$ function on $\mathbb T$ converges almost everywhere. This does not contradict the Banach-Steinhaus conclusion, because "everywhere convergence at a single fixed point for every continuous function" and "almost-everywhere convergence for every $L^2$ function" have different quantifier structures. The set of continuous functions whose series diverges at the origin is generic in the Baire sense; the set of points at which any given $L^2$ function's series diverges has measure zero. Both statements hold simultaneously.

**Continuity of pointwise limits.** A direct corollary converts the principle into a tool for building bounded operators by approximation. If $(T_n) \subset \mathcal B(X, Y)$ converges pointwise to a map $T : X \to Y$, then $T$ is automatically bounded and $\|T\| \leq \liminf_n \|T_n\|$. Pointwise convergence makes the family pointwise bounded; Banach-Steinhaus supplies the uniform bound $M = \sup_n \|T_n\|$; the limit inherits $\|T x\| \leq M \|x\|$. This is the standard route for constructing operators as pointwise limits of finite-rank or otherwise explicit approximants.

**Banach-Steinhaus for Frechet spaces.** On a Frechet space $X$ (a complete metrisable locally convex space) the principle takes its most general form: a pointwise-bounded family of continuous linear maps from $X$ to any topological vector space is **equicontinuous**. The same Baire iteration supplies the proof, with equicontinuity replacing the uniform norm bound because no single norm is available. This form underlies the theory of distributional convergence: pointwise limits of distributions remain distributions because the test-function space is Frechet (or LF) and Banach-Steinhaus grants the requisite equicontinuity [ref: TODO_REF Rudin §2].

**The Banach-Steinhaus theorem as a closedness criterion.** The principle combines with the closed graph theorem [02.11.09] to give a useful criterion: a linear map $T : X \to Y$ between Banach spaces is bounded if and only if for every sequence $(x_n)$ with $x_n \to 0$ and $T x_n \to y$, one has $y = 0$ (closability) together with a graph closure argument. The two pillars thus cooperate: closed graph supplies automatic continuity for everywhere-defined maps, and Banach-Steinhaus controls families that arise as approximants to such maps.

**Synthesis.** Banach-Steinhaus generalises pointwise information into uniform control, and the central insight is that completeness converts a countable intersection of dense open sets into a dense residual set. This is exactly the structural mechanism shared with the open mapping theorem [02.11.09]: both results build toward the spectral and weak-compactness theory of [02.11.11] by extracting quantitative bounds from purely qualitative hypotheses. The principle is dual to the extension machinery of Hahn-Banach [02.11.02] — one pillar builds functionals, the other bounds families of them — and putting these together yields the resonance theorem and the condensation of singularities that make pointwise convergence of operators automatically bounded and automatically continuous. The bridge is that the three pillars together convert the soft hypotheses of completeness and linearity into the hard estimates on which spectral theory, harmonic analysis, and PDE depend.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (resonance theorem).** *Let $X$ be a Banach space, $Y$ a normed space, and $\{T_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A} \subset \mathcal B(X, Y)$ with $\sup_{\alpha \in A} \|T_\alpha\| = \infty$. Then $S = \{x \in X : \sup_{\alpha \in A} \|T_\alpha x\| = \infty\}$ is a dense $G_\delta$ subset of $X$.*

**Proof.** For each $n \geq 1$ let $E_n = \{x \in X : \sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha x\| \leq n\}$ as in the direct proof; each $E_n$ is closed. We claim each $E_n$ has empty interior. Suppose for contradiction that some $E_N$ contains a ball $B_X(x_0, r)$. Repeating the estimate from the direct proof verbatim yields $\|T_\alpha\| \leq 4N/r$ for every $\alpha$, contradicting $\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha\| = \infty$. So each $E_n$ is closed with empty interior.

Therefore each $U_n = X \setminus E_n$ is open and dense. By the Baire category theorem applied to the complete metric space $X$,

$$
S = \{x : \sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha x\| = \infty\} = \bigcap_{n \geq 1} U_n
$$

is a dense $G_\delta$. Membership $x \in \bigcap_n U_n$ means $x \notin E_n$ for every $n$, that is, $\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha x\| > n$ for every $n$, which is exactly $\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha x\| = \infty$. $\square$

**Proposition (continuity of pointwise limits).** *Let $X, Y$ be Banach spaces and $(T_n) \subset \mathcal B(X, Y)$ with $T_n x \to T x$ for every $x \in X$. Then $T \in \mathcal B(X, Y)$ and $\|T\| \leq \liminf_{n \to \infty} \|T_n\|$.*

**Proof.** Linearity of $T$ follows from linearity of each $T_n$ and of the limit. For boundedness, observe that for each fixed $x \in X$ the convergent sequence $(T_n x)$ is bounded in $Y$, so $\sup_n \|T_n x\| < \infty$; the family $\{T_n\}$ is pointwise bounded. Banach-Steinhaus gives $M := \sup_n \|T_n\| < \infty$. For every $x \in X$ and every $n$,

$$
\|T_n x\| \leq \|T_n\| \cdot \|x\| \leq M \|x\|.
$$

Passing to the limit and using continuity of the norm,

$$
\|T x\| = \lim_{n \to \infty} \|T_n x\| \leq \liminf_{n \to \infty} \|T_n\| \cdot \|x\| \leq M \|x\|.
$$

So $T$ is bounded with $\|T\| \leq \liminf_n \|T_n\|$. $\square$

**Proposition (gliding-hump consequence).** *Let $X$ be a Banach space and $(f_n) \subset X^*$ a sequence of functionals with $\limsup_{n \to \infty} \|f_n\| = \infty$. Then there exists $x \in X$ with $\limsup_{n \to \infty} |f_n(x)| = \infty$, and the set of such $x$ is a dense $G_\delta$.*

**Proof.** Apply the resonance theorem with $Y = \mathbb K$ and $T_\alpha$ running over the sequence $(f_n)$. The hypothesis $\limsup_n \|f_n\| = \infty$ is stronger than $\sup_n \|f_n\| = \infty$, so the theorem applies and $S = \{x : \sup_n |f_n(x)| = \infty\}$ is a dense $G_\delta$. Any $x \in S$ satisfies $\limsup_n |f_n(x)| = \infty$. $\square$

This consequence is the abstract engine behind classical "gliding hump" constructions, in which a sequence of functionals growing in norm is used to manufacture a single vector at which the values escape to infinity. The Banach-Steinhaus theorem replaces the explicit recursive construction of Banach's school with a single category argument.

## Connections [Master]

- **Hahn-Banach theorem [02.11.02]** — the dual formulation of Banach-Steinhaus (weakly bounded sets are norm-bounded) depends on Hahn-Banach through the isometric embedding $J : X \hookrightarrow X^{**}$, which identifies $\|\operatorname{ev}_x\|$ with $\|x\|$. The two theorems are complementary: Hahn-Banach builds the dual space that Banach-Steinhaus then uses to convert pointwise into uniform data.

- **Open mapping and closed graph theorems [02.11.09]** — open mapping, closed graph, and Banach-Steinhaus share the same Baire-category engine and together form two of the three pillars of Banach-space theory. Where open mapping turns a single surjection into an open map, Banach-Steinhaus turns a pointwise-bounded family into a uniformly bounded one; the proofs are dual uses of the same completeness mechanism.

- **Bounded linear operators [02.11.01]** — the principle is a statement about families in $\mathcal B(X, Y)$, and the operator-norm bound it supplies is the central quantitative output. The continuity-of-pointwise-limits corollary is the standard tool for constructing new bounded operators as limits of explicit approximants.

- **Banach spaces [02.11.04]** — completeness of the domain is the essential hypothesis; the worked example on finitely supported sequences shows the theorem fails without it. The Baire category theorem, the topological input, is precisely the statement that completeness forbids a countable union of closed nowhere-dense sets from covering the space.

- **The spectral theorem [02.11.11]** — weak convergence of operators and weak compactness of bounded sequences in the dual are controlled by Banach-Steinhaus. The principle underwrites the resolvent estimates and the convergence of spectral approximations that the spectral theorem organizes, and it is the bridge from pointwise convergence of approximants to convergence in the operator norm.

- **Harmonic analysis and Fourier series** — the divergence of Fourier series for generic continuous functions (Du Bois-Reymond, re-derived by Banach-Steinhaus) is the canonical application. The Lebesgue constants $L_n \sim (4/\pi^2) \log n$ witness $\sup_n \|\Lambda_n\| = \infty$, and condensation of singularities produces the generic divergent function.

- **Weak topologies and reflexivity** — the equivalence of weak boundedness and norm boundedness is the seed from which weak-* compactness (Banach-Alaoglu) and weak compactness characterisations grow. Every weak-convergence argument in modern analysis invokes Banach-Steinhaus at the step where pointwise bounds must become uniform.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

The uniform boundedness principle was announced by Stefan Banach and Hugo Steinhaus in the 1927 paper "Sur le principe de la condensation de singularités" in *Fundamenta Mathematicae*, with a precursor already present in Steinhaus's 1919 work on additive functional operations [ref: TODO_REF Banach-Steinhaus 1927]. The paper framed the result as a method for producing functions with prescribed singular behaviour — the "condensation of singularities" of the title — and applied it to exhibit continuous functions whose Fourier series diverge at prescribed points, recovering abstractly what Du Bois-Reymond had constructed by hand in 1873.

The unifying engine behind the principle, the Baire category theorem, had been proved by René Baire in his 1899 thesis on real-valued functions [ref: TODO_REF Baire 1899]. Baire's original motivation was the classification of pointwise limits of continuous functions into the transfinite hierarchy now bearing his name. The recognition that the category theorem also drives quantitative functional-analytic conclusions came two decades later, when the Lwów school around Banach recognised that completeness plus a countable closed covering forces one piece to be topologically fat. The structural insight — that a qualitative topological hypothesis yields quantitative norm bounds — was the conceptual breakthrough that made soft analysis possible.

The abstract framework that made the principle natural was set out in Banach's 1922 paper "Sur les opérations dans les ensembles abstraits et leur application aux équations intégrales," which introduced the axiomatics of normed complete vector spaces and fixed-point and duality arguments in the abstract setting [ref: TODO_REF Banach 1922]. The 1922 framework, the 1927 uniform boundedness principle, and the 1929–1932 open mapping and closed graph theorems together crystallised what is now called the three pillars of Banach-space theory, consolidated in Banach's 1932 monograph *Théorie des opérations linéaires*.

Hahn's 1922 paper and Banach's 1929 extension of functionals supplied the first pillar; the open mapping theorem of Banach and Schauder supplied the second; and Banach-Steinhaus supplied the third. The philosophical lesson the three pillars teach is that completeness is a surprisingly strong hypothesis: combined with linearity, it converts qualitative statements (existence of extensions, openness of surjections, pointwise boundedness) into quantitative ones (norm-preserving extensions, bounded inverses, uniform operator-norm bounds) without a single explicit estimate. This soft-analysis style — no constants written down, yet quantitative information extracted — became the signature of twentieth-century functional analysis and influenced the development of category theory, operator algebras, and the abstract approach to partial differential equations.
