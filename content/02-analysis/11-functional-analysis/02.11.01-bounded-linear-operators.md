## Intuition [Beginner]

A *linear operator* between two normed vector spaces is a rule that takes a vector and returns another vector, in a way that respects addition and scalar multiplication. Think of it as a linear transformation, but on possibly infinite-dimensional spaces.

A linear operator is **bounded** if it doesn't blow up: there's a constant $C$ such that $\|Tx\| \leq C \|x\|$ for every input $x$. Geometrically, $T$ stretches every vector by at most a factor of $C$. The smallest such $C$ is called the **operator norm** of $T$.

Why does boundedness matter? On finite-dimensional spaces, every linear operator is automatically bounded. On infinite-dimensional spaces (function spaces, sequence spaces), this fails: differentiation is unbounded on $L^2(\mathbb{R})$, for example. The bounded operators form the well-behaved class — they are continuous, they form a Banach space themselves, and they are the natural setting for spectral theory and Fredholm theory.

## Visual [Beginner]

A unit ball in the source space mapped by $T$ to an ellipsoid (or its infinite-dimensional analogue) in the target space. Boundedness means the image is contained in a finite-radius ball; the operator norm is the radius of the smallest such enclosing ball.

![Unit ball stretched and rotated by T into an ellipsoid; the operator norm is the largest semi-axis.](/img/bounded-linear-operators-placeholder.svg)

The operator norm captures the worst-case stretch, taken over all unit-norm inputs.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Let $\ell^2$ be the space of square-summable sequences $x = (x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots)$, with the norm equal to the square root of $|x_1|^2 + |x_2|^2 + |x_3|^2 + \cdots$.

Define the *right shift operator* $S : \ell^2 \to \ell^2$ by $S(x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots) = (0, x_1, x_2, \ldots)$. This is a linear operator: shifting commutes with addition and scalar multiplication.

Is $S$ bounded? Compute:

$$
\|S x\|^2 = 0 + |x_1|^2 + |x_2|^2 + \cdots = \|x\|^2,
$$

so $\|S x\| = \|x\|$ for every $x$. The operator norm is $\|S\| = 1$, and $S$ is *isometric*. The right shift is a bounded operator with norm exactly $1$.

Compare with the *differentiation operator* on smooth functions in $L^2([0, 1])$: differentiation sends $\sin(n \pi x)$ to $n \pi \cos(n \pi x)$, which has the same $L^2$ norm but the prefactor $n$ grows without bound. So differentiation is *unbounded* on $L^2$, even though it is linear. This is the fundamental phenomenon that distinguishes infinite-dimensional from finite-dimensional functional analysis.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ and $Y$ be Banach spaces over $K = \mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$ [02.11.04]. A linear map $T : X \to Y$ is **bounded** if there exists a constant $C \geq 0$ such that

$$
\|Tx\|_Y \leq C \|x\|_X \quad \text{for all } x \in X.
$$

The **operator norm** of $T$ is

$$
\|T\| := \sup_{\|x\|_X \leq 1} \|Tx\|_Y = \sup_{x \neq 0} \frac{\|Tx\|_Y}{\|x\|_X}.
$$

Write $\mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ for the set of bounded linear operators $X \to Y$, and $\mathcal{B}(X) := \mathcal{B}(X, X)$.

**Theorem (basic properties).** With pointwise addition and scalar multiplication,

$$
(T_1 + T_2)(x) := T_1 x + T_2 x, \qquad (\lambda T)(x) := \lambda \cdot T x,
$$

$\mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ is a vector space, and the operator norm makes it a normed vector space. **$\mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ is complete (a Banach space) whenever $Y$ is.** (Proved below.)

When $X = Y$, composition makes $\mathcal{B}(X)$ into an associative algebra. The operator norm is *submultiplicative*: $\|ST\| \leq \|S\| \|T\|$. So $\mathcal{B}(X)$ is a **unital Banach algebra**.

**Equivalence of boundedness and continuity.** For a linear map $T : X \to Y$ between normed spaces, the following are equivalent:

1. $T$ is bounded.
2. $T$ is continuous on $X$.
3. $T$ is continuous at $0$.
4. $T$ is continuous at some single point.

(Proved below.) This equivalence is the fundamental reason "bounded" and "continuous" are interchangeable in functional analysis.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Boundedness is continuity).** *Let $T : X \to Y$ be a linear map between normed vector spaces. The following are equivalent:*

*(i) $T$ is bounded: there exists $C \geq 0$ with $\|Tx\| \leq C \|x\|$ for all $x \in X$.*

*(ii) $T$ is continuous everywhere.*

*(iii) $T$ is continuous at $0$.*

**Proof.** *(i) $\Rightarrow$ (ii)*: Suppose $T$ is bounded with constant $C$. For any $x_0 \in X$ and $\varepsilon > 0$, take $\delta = \varepsilon / C$ (or any positive value if $C = 0$). For $\|x - x_0\| < \delta$,

$$
\|Tx - Tx_0\| = \|T(x - x_0)\| \leq C \|x - x_0\| < C \delta = \varepsilon.
$$

So $T$ is continuous at $x_0$, hence everywhere.

*(ii) $\Rightarrow$ (iii)*: immediate, since continuity everywhere includes continuity at $0$.

*(iii) $\Rightarrow$ (i)*: Suppose $T$ is continuous at $0$. By the definition of continuity, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $\|x\| < \delta$ implies $\|Tx\| < 1$. For any nonzero $x \in X$, set $x' = (\delta/2) \cdot x / \|x\|$. Then $\|x'\| = \delta/2 < \delta$, so $\|Tx'\| < 1$. By linearity,

$$
\|Tx\| = \frac{2 \|x\|}{\delta} \cdot \|Tx'\| < \frac{2 \|x\|}{\delta}.
$$

This shows $T$ is bounded with $C \leq 2/\delta$. $\square$

**Theorem ($\mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ is complete when $Y$ is).** *Let $X, Y$ be normed vector spaces with $Y$ complete (a Banach space). Then $\mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ is complete with the operator norm.*

**Proof.** Let $\{T_n\}$ be a Cauchy sequence in $\mathcal{B}(X, Y)$. For each fixed $x \in X$,

$$
\|T_n x - T_m x\| \leq \|T_n - T_m\| \cdot \|x\|,
$$

so $\{T_n x\}$ is Cauchy in $Y$. By completeness of $Y$, define $T x := \lim_n T_n x$. The map $T : X \to Y$ is linear (limits of linear maps are linear).

To show $T$ is bounded: $\{T_n\}$ Cauchy implies $\{\|T_n\|\}$ bounded by some $M$. Then $\|T_n x\| \leq M \|x\|$ for every $n$, so $\|T x\| = \lim_n \|T_n x\| \leq M \|x\|$. Hence $T$ is bounded with $\|T\| \leq M$.

To show $T_n \to T$ in operator norm: given $\varepsilon > 0$, pick $N$ so that $\|T_n - T_m\| < \varepsilon$ for $n, m \geq N$. For each $x$ with $\|x\| \leq 1$ and $n \geq N$,

$$
\|T_n x - T x\| = \lim_m \|T_n x - T_m x\| \leq \sup_{m \geq N} \|T_n - T_m\| \cdot \|x\| \leq \varepsilon.
$$

Taking sup over $\|x\| \leq 1$, $\|T_n - T\| \leq \varepsilon$ for $n \geq N$. So $T_n \to T$ in operator norm. $\square$

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [02.11.05] (compact operators), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [02.11.03] (unbounded self-adjoint operators). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

Compute the operator norm of $T : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$, $T(x, y) = (3x, 4y)$, with the Euclidean norm.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The operator norm of a diagonal matrix is its largest singular value.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For diagonal $T$ with entries $3, 4$, the maximum stretch is $4$ (achieved on the $y$-axis). So $\|T\| = 4$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, proof).**

Show that $\|S T\| \leq \|S\| \|T\|$ for $S, T \in \mathcal{B}(X)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the operator norm to $\|S T x\|$ for $\|x\| \leq 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For any $x$ with $\|x\| \leq 1$, $\|S T x\| \leq \|S\| \|Tx\| \leq \|S\| \|T\| \|x\| \leq \|S\| \|T\|$. Taking sup over $\|x\| \leq 1$, $\|S T\| \leq \|S\| \|T\|$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the right shift $S : \ell^2 \to \ell^2$, $S(x_1, x_2, \ldots) = (0, x_1, x_2, \ldots)$, has $\|S\| = 1$ and is *not* surjective.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\|Sx\|$ in terms of $\|x\|$, and check whether $(1, 0, 0, \ldots)$ is in the range.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\|Sx\|^2 = \sum_{n \geq 1} |x_n|^2 = \|x\|^2$, so $\|Sx\| = \|x\|$ and $\|S\| = 1$. For surjectivity: every element of $\mathrm{ran}(S)$ has first coordinate zero, so $(1, 0, 0, \ldots) \notin \mathrm{ran}(S)$. The right shift is isometric but not invertible — a phenomenon impossible in finite dimension.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Define $T : C([0, 1]) \to C([0, 1])$ by $(Tf)(x) = \int_0^x f(t) \, dt$. Show $T$ is bounded with $\|T\| \leq 1$ in the sup norm.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Bound the integral by the length times the supremum of $|f|$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $f \in C([0, 1])$ and $x \in [0, 1]$, $|(Tf)(x)| = \big|\int_0^x f(t) \, dt\big| \leq x \cdot \|f\|_\infty \leq \|f\|_\infty$. So $\|Tf\|_\infty \leq \|f\|_\infty$, giving $\|T\| \leq 1$. (One can verify $\|T\| = 1$ by taking $f \equiv 1$, which gives $Tf(x) = x$ with $\|Tf\|_\infty = 1$.)

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why is differentiation $\frac{d}{dx} : C^1([0, 1]) \to C([0, 1])$ a bounded operator if we use the $C^1$ norm on the source, but unbounded in the sup norm?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Look up the $C^1$ norm and recall the worst-case behaviour of derivatives in the sup norm.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The $C^1$ norm is $\|f\|_{C^1} := \|f\|_\infty + \|f'\|_\infty$, which already controls the derivative. So $\|f'\|_\infty \leq \|f\|_{C^1}$, giving boundedness with constant $1$. In the sup norm alone, the family $f_n(x) = \sin(n\pi x)/n$ has $\|f_n\|_\infty \to 0$ but $\|f_n'\|_\infty = \pi$ for all $n$, so differentiation is unbounded.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Let $T \in \mathcal{B}(X)$ with $\|T\| < 1$ where $X$ is a Banach space. Show that $I - T$ is invertible with $(I - T)^{-1} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty T^n$ (Neumann series).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show the partial sums form a Cauchy sequence in $\mathcal{B}(X)$ and verify the limit is the inverse.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The partial sums $S_N = \sum_{n=0}^N T^n$ satisfy $\|S_M - S_N\| = \|\sum_{n=N+1}^M T^n\| \leq \sum_{n=N+1}^M \|T\|^n$, which is bounded by $\|T\|^{N+1}/(1 - \|T\|) \to 0$. So $\{S_N\}$ is Cauchy, hence convergent in $\mathcal{B}(X)$ (which is Banach since $X$ is). The limit $S = \sum_n T^n$ satisfies $S(I - T) = (I - T) S = I$ by direct expansion (telescoping cancellation), so $S = (I - T)^{-1}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Why is the bounded-operator algebra $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ on a separable infinite-dimensional Hilbert space *not* commutative, while $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}^n)$ has a commutative subalgebra of diagonal matrices?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Even on $\mathbb{R}^n$, the full algebra is non-commutative.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathcal{B}(X)$ is non-commutative whenever $\dim X \geq 2$, because matrix multiplication is non-commutative in dimension $\geq 2$. On $\mathbb{R}^n$ (or $\mathbb{C}^n$) the diagonal matrices commute among themselves, but not with general matrices. On infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces, the same is true: the algebra of multiplication operators on a fixed orthonormal basis is commutative (it is a commutative subalgebra of $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$), but the full $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ is non-commutative because it includes ladder operators, shifts, and other non-diagonal pieces. The non-commutativity is essential for spectral theory and quantum mechanics.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**The three pillars of bounded-operator theory** are the *uniform boundedness principle* (Banach-Steinhaus), the *open mapping theorem*, and the *closed graph theorem*. All three rely on the Baire category theorem and Banach-space completeness.

**Banach-Steinhaus.** A pointwise-bounded family of bounded operators between Banach spaces is uniformly bounded. *If $\{T_\alpha\} \subseteq \mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ satisfies $\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha x\| < \infty$ for each $x$, then $\sup_\alpha \|T_\alpha\| < \infty$.* This converts pointwise control into operator-norm control.

**Open mapping theorem.** A surjective bounded linear operator between Banach spaces is an *open map* (sends open sets to open sets). Equivalently, the inverse of a bijective bounded linear operator is automatically bounded.

**Closed graph theorem.** A linear operator $T : X \to Y$ between Banach spaces is bounded iff its graph $\{(x, Tx) : x \in X\} \subseteq X \times Y$ is closed.

These three theorems are the foundation of the modern theory: they make it possible to *prove* boundedness from less obvious data.

**Adjoint operators.** For $T \in \mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ with $X, Y$ Hilbert spaces, the adjoint $T^\ast \in \mathcal{B}(Y, X)$ is defined by $\langle Tx, y\rangle_Y = \langle x, T^\ast y\rangle_X$. The map $T \mapsto T^\ast$ is *anti-linear* (over $\mathbb{C}$), satisfies $T^{\ast\ast} = T$, $\|T^\ast\| = \|T\|$, $\|T^\ast T\| = \|T\|^2$ (the C*-identity), and $(ST)^\ast = T^\ast S^\ast$. The C*-identity makes $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ a *C*-algebra*, the prototype for non-commutative algebra.

**Spectrum.** For $T \in \mathcal{B}(X)$, the spectrum $\sigma(T) := \{\lambda \in \mathbb{C} : T - \lambda I \text{ is not invertible}\}$ is a non-empty compact subset of $\mathbb{C}$ contained in the closed disc of radius $\|T\|$. The spectral radius is $r(T) := \sup_{\lambda \in \sigma(T)} |\lambda| = \lim_{n \to \infty} \|T^n\|^{1/n}$ (Gelfand). For self-adjoint $T$ on a Hilbert space, $\sigma(T) \subseteq \mathbb{R}$.

**Compact operators.** A *bounded* operator $T \in \mathcal{B}(X)$ is **compact** if it sends bounded sets to relatively compact sets. Compact operators form a *closed two-sided ideal* $\mathcal{K}(X) \subseteq \mathcal{B}(X)$; the quotient $\mathcal{B}(X) / \mathcal{K}(X)$ is the **Calkin algebra**, the home of Fredholm theory [02.11.05] [03.09.06].

**Synthesis.** This construction generalises the pattern fixed in [02.11.04] (banach space fundamentals), with the symmetric data replaced by its skew or twisted analogue. Read in the opposite direction, the construction is dual to the metric story: complements and orthogonality are taken with respect to the bilinear datum of this unit, not a metric, and the resulting category of subobjects is the one the rest of the strand classifies. The central insight is that this datum identifies algebra with geometry: functions become vector fields, subspaces become quotients, and invariants become cohomology classes — and that identification is the engine driving every theorem downstream.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Operator norm is a norm.** Non-negativity is clear from the supremum. Definiteness: if $\|T\| = 0$, then $\|Tx\| = 0$ for all unit $x$, hence by linearity for all $x$, so $T = 0$. Triangle inequality: for unit $x$, $\|(T_1 + T_2)x\| \leq \|T_1 x\| + \|T_2 x\| \leq \|T_1\| + \|T_2\|$, so $\|T_1 + T_2\| \leq \|T_1\| + \|T_2\|$. Homogeneity: $\|\lambda T\| = |\lambda| \cdot \|T\|$ by direct computation.

**Submultiplicativity.** Proved as Exercise 2.

**Equivalence of boundedness and continuity at any point.** Proved in §"Key theorem". The argument uses linearity to translate boundedness at the origin to a global bound.

**Banach property of $\mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ when $Y$ is Banach.** Proved in §"Key theorem". The proof is the prototype Cauchy-completion argument that recurs throughout functional analysis: pointwise Cauchy gives a pointwise limit, the limit inherits linearity and boundedness, and uniform-on-the-unit-ball bounds give convergence in operator norm.

**Spectrum is non-empty.** Suppose for contradiction $\sigma(T)$ is empty. Then the resolvent $\lambda \mapsto (T - \lambda I)^{-1}$ is an entire $\mathcal{B}(X)$-valued function. By the Liouville-type theorem in operator algebras, a bounded entire $\mathcal{B}(X)$-valued function is constant. The resolvent goes to zero at infinity (in operator norm, by Neumann series), so it must be identically zero, contradicting that resolvent values are *invertible*. So $\sigma(T) \neq \emptyset$.

**Spectral radius formula.** $r(T) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \|T^n\|^{1/n}$. The Neumann series for $(T - \lambda I)^{-1}$ converges for $|\lambda| > \|T^n\|^{1/n}$ for some $n$, giving $r(T) \leq \limsup \|T^n\|^{1/n}$. The reverse inequality follows from holomorphy of the resolvent.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The systematic study of bounded operators on infinite-dimensional spaces emerged from Banach's 1932 *Théorie des opérations linéaires*, which abstracted from Hilbert's earlier work on integral equations and from Riesz's spectral theory of compact operators. Banach-Steinhaus (1927), the open mapping theorem (Banach 1929, Schauder 1930), and the closed graph theorem are products of this period.

The C*-algebra perspective on $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H})$ — with adjoints, the C*-identity, and the spectrum — was developed by Gelfand, Naimark, and Murray-von Neumann in the 1930s and 1940s, providing the algebraic foundation for noncommutative geometry, K-theory, and (via the GNS construction) the rigorous mathematical formulation of quantum mechanics. Atkinson's 1951 paper [ref: TODO_REF Reed-Simon §III] characterised Fredholm operators as the invertible elements modulo the compact ideal, opening the bridge from operator theory to topology.
