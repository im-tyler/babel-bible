## Intuition [Beginner]

A *compact* linear operator is one that is "almost finite-dimensional" in a specific topological sense: it sends bounded sequences to sequences with convergent subsequences. On finite-dimensional spaces, every bounded operator is compact (Bolzano-Weierstrass). On infinite-dimensional spaces, the identity operator is *never* compact, and neither is most of the algebra.

Compact operators are the well-behaved corner of operator theory: they have point spectra, they admit eigenvector expansions, and (after Atkinson) they form the ideal modulo which Fredholm operators live.

The simplest examples: integral operators with continuous kernels, finite-rank operators (which form a dense subset of the compacts in any Hilbert space), and operators that are uniform limits of finite-rank operators.

## Visual [Beginner]

The unit ball in an infinite-dimensional space is *not* compact — sequences in it can fail to have convergent subsequences. A compact operator squeezes the unit ball down to something whose closure *is* compact: a relatively compact image.

![Unit ball mapped by a compact operator into a relatively compact set — a set with the property that every sequence has a convergent subsequence.](/img/compact-operators-placeholder.svg)

So while bounded operators preserve "bounded," compact operators upgrade to "preserves bounded into relatively compact." The upgrade is what makes them "almost finite-dimensional."

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the diagonal operator $T : \ell^2 \to \ell^2$ defined by $T(x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots) = (x_1, x_2/2, x_3/3, \ldots)$ — multiply the $n$-th coordinate by $1/n$.

This $T$ is bounded (operator norm $1$, achieved on the first coordinate). Is it compact?

Compute: a bounded sequence $\{y^{(k)}\}$ in $\ell^2$ has $\|y^{(k)}\| \leq M$ for some $M$. Apply $T$: each component of $T y^{(k)}$ is multiplied by $1/n$, so the tail of $T y^{(k)}$ is small uniformly in $k$. Concretely, for any $\varepsilon > 0$, pick $N$ with $1/N < \varepsilon/M$; then the tail beyond index $N$ contributes at most $\varepsilon$. The first $N$ coordinates are bounded in $\mathbb{R}^N$ (a finite-dimensional space), so by Bolzano-Weierstrass they have a convergent subsequence. Combine: $\{T y^{(k)}\}$ has a Cauchy subsequence, hence convergent (since $\ell^2$ is complete).

So $T$ is compact. The compactness comes from the diagonal entries decaying to zero. The non-compact diagonal operator $S(x_n) = (x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots)$ (the identity) has constant diagonal $1$ and fails Bolzano-Weierstrass on the standard basis sequence.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X, Y$ be Banach spaces. A bounded linear operator $T \in \mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ [02.11.01] is **compact** if it sends every bounded set to a *relatively compact* set — a set whose closure is compact.

Equivalently:

- $T(B_X)$ has compact closure in $Y$, where $B_X$ is the unit ball of $X$.
- For every bounded sequence $\{x_n\} \subset X$, the sequence $\{T x_n\}$ has a Cauchy (hence convergent) subsequence in $Y$.

Write $\mathcal{K}(X, Y)$ for the set of compact operators $X \to Y$, and $\mathcal{K}(X) := \mathcal{K}(X, X)$.

**Theorem (basic structure).**

1. $\mathcal{K}(X, Y)$ is a closed vector subspace of $\mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ in the operator norm.
2. The composition of a compact operator with any bounded operator (on either side) is compact: $\mathcal{K}(X) \subset \mathcal{B}(X)$ is a *two-sided ideal*.
3. **Schauder's theorem**: $T \in \mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ is compact iff its adjoint $T^\ast \in \mathcal{B}(Y^\ast, X^\ast)$ is compact.
4. Every finite-rank operator is compact, and on a Hilbert space the finite-rank operators are *dense* in $\mathcal{K}(X)$.

(Proofs in §"Full proof set".)

The Calkin algebra $\mathcal{B}(X) / \mathcal{K}(X)$ is the quotient of the bounded operators by the compact ideal — the natural setting for Fredholm theory [03.09.06]: an operator is Fredholm iff its image in the Calkin algebra is invertible (Atkinson).

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Compact operators form a closed two-sided ideal in $\mathcal{B}(X)$).** *Let $X$ be a Banach space. The set $\mathcal{K}(X) \subseteq \mathcal{B}(X)$ of compact operators is:*

*(i) a vector subspace, closed under sums and scalar multiples;*

*(ii) closed in operator norm;*

*(iii) closed under left and right composition by bounded operators: $A \in \mathcal{B}(X), K \in \mathcal{K}(X) \Rightarrow AK, KA \in \mathcal{K}(X)$.*

**Proof.**

*(i) Subspace*: Let $K_1, K_2 \in \mathcal{K}(X)$ and $\lambda \in K$. For a bounded sequence $\{x_n\}$, extract subsequences: from $\{K_1 x_n\}$ a convergent subsequence, then from the corresponding $\{K_2 x_n\}$ on that subsequence another convergent subsequence. The combined subsequence makes both $K_1 x_n$ and $K_2 x_n$ converge, hence $(K_1 + \lambda K_2) x_n$ converges. So $K_1 + \lambda K_2$ is compact.

*(ii) Closed*: Let $K_n \to K$ in operator norm with $K_n \in \mathcal{K}(X)$. Show $K$ is compact. Take a bounded sequence $\{x_m\}$, $\|x_m\| \leq M$. By a diagonal argument, extract a subsequence $\{x_{m_k}\}$ on which every $K_n x_{m_k}$ converges (extract along $K_1$, then a sub-subsequence on which $K_2$ converges, etc.; the diagonal subsequence works for every $K_n$). For this subsequence, estimate

$$
\|K x_{m_k} - K x_{m_l}\| \leq \|K - K_n\| \cdot \|x_{m_k} - x_{m_l}\| + \|K_n x_{m_k} - K_n x_{m_l}\|.
$$

Given $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $n$ with $\|K - K_n\| < \varepsilon/(4M)$, then choose $k_0$ with $\|K_n x_{m_k} - K_n x_{m_l}\| < \varepsilon/2$ for $k, l \geq k_0$. Then $\|K x_{m_k} - K x_{m_l}\| < 2M \cdot \varepsilon/(4M) + \varepsilon/2 = \varepsilon$. So $\{K x_{m_k}\}$ is Cauchy. By completeness it converges, and $K$ is compact.

*(iii) Two-sided ideal*: Let $A \in \mathcal{B}(X)$, $K \in \mathcal{K}(X)$. For a bounded sequence $\{x_n\}$, $K x_n$ has a convergent subsequence $K x_{n_k} \to y$. Then $A K x_{n_k} \to A y$ by continuity of $A$. So $AK$ is compact. Similarly, $K A x_n$: $\{A x_n\}$ is bounded (since $A$ is bounded), and applying $K$ to it has a convergent subsequence, so $KA$ is compact. $\square$

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [03.09.06] (fredholm operators), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [03.09.10] (atiyah-singer index theorem). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).**

Show that every finite-rank operator $T \in \mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ is compact.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Bounded sets in finite-dimensional spaces are relatively compact.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $T$ has finite rank, $T(B_X) \subseteq \mathrm{ran}(T)$, a finite-dimensional subspace of $Y$. The image $T(B_X)$ is bounded (since $T$ is bounded), and bounded sets in finite-dimensional normed spaces are relatively compact (Heine-Borel for finite-dimensional spaces). So $T$ is compact.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, multiple choice).**

If $T \in \mathcal{B}(X)$ is compact and $X$ is infinite-dimensional, can $T$ have a bounded inverse?

A. Yes
B. No
C. Only if $T$ is the identity
D. Only on Hilbert spaces

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If $T$ has a bounded inverse, then $T \cdot T^{-1} = I$ would be compact.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. No.**

Feedback-correct: correct; if $T^{-1}$ existed in $\mathcal{B}(X)$, then $I = T T^{-1}$ would be compact (compact $\circ$ bounded $=$ compact). But the identity on an infinite-dimensional space is not compact. Contradiction. Feedback-wrong: this is a basic structural fact independent of the choice of $T$ or the metric structure of $X$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the diagonal operator $T : \ell^2 \to \ell^2$, $T(x_n) = (\lambda_n x_n)$, is compact iff $\lambda_n \to 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Approximate by truncating to finite-rank operators.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $\lambda_n \to 0$: define $T_N(x_n) = (\lambda_1 x_1, \ldots, \lambda_N x_N, 0, 0, \ldots)$. Each $T_N$ has finite rank, hence compact. $\|T - T_N\| = \sup_{n > N} |\lambda_n| \to 0$ as $N \to \infty$. By closedness of compact operators in operator norm, $T = \lim T_N$ is compact.

If $\lambda_n \not\to 0$: there is $\varepsilon > 0$ and a subsequence with $|\lambda_{n_k}| \geq \varepsilon$. Apply $T$ to the standard basis vectors $e_{n_k}$: $T e_{n_k} = \lambda_{n_k} e_{n_k}$ has norm $\geq \varepsilon$, and any two such are orthogonal, so $\|T e_{n_k} - T e_{n_l}\| \geq \varepsilon \sqrt{2}$ for $k \neq l$. No convergent subsequence; $T$ is not compact.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Show that an integral operator $(T f)(x) = \int_0^1 K(x, y) f(y) \, dy$ on $L^2([0, 1])$ with continuous kernel $K$ is compact.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Approximate $K$ uniformly by polynomials in $x$ and $y$ (Stone-Weierstrass) — this gives finite-rank approximations to $T$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Stone-Weierstrass, polynomials $p_n(x, y)$ converge uniformly to $K$ on $[0, 1]^2$. Each polynomial kernel is a finite linear combination of products $f(x) g(y)$, and an integral operator with such a kernel is finite-rank: the image of $f \mapsto \int g(y) f(y) \, dy \cdot p(x)$ is one-dimensional. The corresponding operators $T_n$ converge to $T$ in operator norm because $\|T - T_n\| \leq \|K - p_n\|_\infty$. Compact operators are closed, so $T$ is compact.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why does the spectrum of a compact operator on an infinite-dimensional space consist of $0$ together with at most countably many isolated eigenvalues that can only accumulate at $0$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The non-zero spectrum is purely point spectrum; eigenvalues bounded away from zero are finite in number.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a compact $T$, the resolvent equation $(T - \lambda I) x = y$ for $\lambda \neq 0$ becomes Fredholm: $T - \lambda I = -\lambda(I - T/\lambda)$, and $T/\lambda$ is compact. Riesz's spectral theorem says any non-zero spectral value of $T$ is an isolated eigenvalue with finite-dimensional generalised eigenspace, and the non-zero eigenvalues form an at-most-countable set whose only possible accumulation point is $0$. The point $0$ itself is in the spectrum (since $T$ is non-invertible on infinite-dimensional spaces, see Exercise 2), but may or may not be an eigenvalue.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Show that on a Hilbert space, the finite-rank operators are dense in $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{H})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use orthonormal bases and approximate by truncating.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $K \in \mathcal{K}(\mathcal{H})$. Choose an orthonormal basis $\{e_n\}$ for $\mathcal{H}$. Let $P_N$ be the orthogonal projection onto $\mathrm{span}(e_1, \ldots, e_N)$ — a finite-rank operator with $\|P_N\| = 1$. Define $K_N := P_N K$, a finite-rank operator (rank $\leq N$). Show $K_N \to K$ in operator norm.

Compute $\|K - K_N\| = \|(I - P_N) K\|$. This is the norm of $K$ projected onto the orthogonal complement of $\mathrm{span}(e_1, \ldots, e_N)$. As $N \to \infty$, this complement shrinks. By compactness of $K$, $K(B_\mathcal{H})$ is relatively compact, hence totally bounded; for any $\varepsilon > 0$, finitely many $\varepsilon$-balls cover $K(B_\mathcal{H})$. Their centres span a finite-dimensional subspace, captured by $P_N$ for $N$ large enough. Concretely: for sufficiently large $N$, $\|(I - P_N) K x\| < \varepsilon$ uniformly for $\|x\| \leq 1$. So $\|K - K_N\| \to 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

State and verify Schauder's theorem: $T \in \mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ is compact iff its adjoint $T^\ast : Y^\ast \to X^\ast$ is compact.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compactness of $T$ corresponds to relative compactness of $T(B_X)$; via Banach-Alaoglu and equicontinuity arguments, this transfers to $T^\ast(B_{Y^\ast})$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Sketch: given $\{\varphi_n\} \subset Y^\ast$ bounded, restrict each $\varphi_n$ to the relatively compact set $T(B_X)$. This restricted family is bounded and equicontinuous (as $T(B_X)$ is totally bounded), so by Arzelà-Ascoli it has a uniformly convergent subsequence $\varphi_{n_k}$ on $T(B_X)$. Then $T^\ast \varphi_{n_k}$ converges in operator norm in $X^\ast$ (the supremum over $B_X$ is the same as the supremum of $|\varphi_{n_k}(Tx)|$ over $\|x\| \leq 1$, which is precisely the uniform-on-$T(B_X)$ convergence). Hence $T^\ast$ is compact. The converse follows by the same argument applied to $T^{\ast\ast}$ in place of $T$, using that $T \to T^{\ast\ast}$ embeds $T$ into the bidual.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Spectral theory for compact operators.** The Riesz-Schauder theorem: for compact $T \in \mathcal{B}(X)$ on an infinite-dimensional Banach space $X$,

- $\sigma(T) = \{0\} \cup \{\lambda_n\}$ where $\{\lambda_n\}$ is at most a countable set of non-zero eigenvalues with no accumulation points other than possibly $0$;
- each non-zero $\lambda_n$ is an eigenvalue with finite-dimensional generalised eigenspace;
- $\lambda = 0$ is in the spectrum (since $T$ is not invertible on infinite-dimensional space) and may or may not be an eigenvalue.

This generalises the finite-dimensional spectral theorem: compact operators are the class of bounded operators with a discrete-eigenvalue structure modelled on the matrix case.

**Compact self-adjoint operators on Hilbert spaces.** The spectral theorem in its sharpest form: if $T \in \mathcal{K}(\mathcal{H})$ is self-adjoint, then $\mathcal{H}$ has an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors of $T$, and

$$
T = \sum_n \lambda_n \langle e_n, \cdot\rangle e_n,
$$

where the $\lambda_n$ are real and tend to $0$. Diagonal-matrix-like behaviour, but on infinite-dimensional spaces.

**Hilbert-Schmidt and trace-class operators.** Inside $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{H})$ are two further ideals. **Hilbert-Schmidt**: operators with $\sum \|T e_n\|^2 < \infty$ for some (equivalently, every) orthonormal basis $\{e_n\}$. **Trace-class**: operators with $\sum \|T e_n\|_{\mathcal{H}} < \infty$ for an orthonormal basis (equivalently, $T$ is a finite product of Hilbert-Schmidt operators). For trace-class $T$, $\mathrm{tr}(T) := \sum_n \langle e_n, T e_n\rangle$ is well-defined and basis-independent. These are *Schatten ideals* $S^p$ for $p = 2$ and $p = 1$ respectively.

**Atkinson's theorem.** $T \in \mathcal{B}(X)$ is Fredholm iff there exists $S \in \mathcal{B}(X)$ with $ST - I, TS - I \in \mathcal{K}(X)$ — i.e., iff the image of $T$ in the Calkin algebra $\mathcal{B}(X) / \mathcal{K}(X)$ is invertible. This is the operator-algebraic content of Fredholm theory [03.09.06] and the input to the Atiyah-Singer index theorem [03.09.10].

**Approximation property.** A Banach space $X$ has the approximation property (AP) if the identity is a limit of finite-rank operators in the *strong* operator topology. On Hilbert spaces and $L^p$-spaces (for $1 \leq p < \infty$), AP holds; on general Banach spaces it can fail (Enflo, 1973). When AP holds, finite-rank operators are dense in compact operators in operator norm.

**Synthesis.** Compact operators are the infinite-dimensional analogue of finite-rank operators: they map bounded sets to relatively compact sets, making their spectral theory closely parallel the finite-dimensional eigenvalue theory. The spectral theorem for compact operators — eigenvalues accumulate only at zero, each nonzero eigenvalue has finite multiplicity — is the foundation for solving integral equations (Hilbert-Schmidt theory), boundary value problems (Sturm-Liouville theory), and the Atiyah-Singer index theorem (where the analytic index is the trace of a compact operator). Compact operators also connect to the approximation property of Banach spaces (whether finite-rank operators approximate compact operators in norm), to the theory of Fredholm operators (whose invertibility modulo compacts gives rise to K-theory), and to the Schatten classes that quantify compactness through singular-value decay.

## Full proof set [Master]

**$\mathcal{K}(X)$ is a closed two-sided ideal.** Proved in §"Key theorem".

**Schauder's theorem.** Sketched in Exercise 7; full proof uses Banach-Alaoglu + Arzelà-Ascoli on the relatively compact image $T(B_X)$.

**Riesz-Schauder spectral theorem.** Let $\lambda \neq 0$ be in $\sigma(T)$ for $T$ compact. Then $T - \lambda I$ is not invertible. Write $T - \lambda I = -\lambda(I - \lambda^{-1} T)$. Riesz's lemma: if $\|S\|$ is sufficiently controlled (which $\lambda^{-1} T$ is, modulo finite-dimensional subspaces), then $I + S$ is "Fredholm of index 0" — surjectivity is equivalent to injectivity. So $\lambda I - T$ has a nonzero kernel iff it has a nonzero cokernel, both finite-dimensional. The eigenvalue $\lambda$ thus has a finite-dimensional generalised eigenspace. Discreteness of the non-zero spectrum follows from the fact that compact operators have "isolated points away from $0$" — concretely, if $\lambda_n \to \lambda \neq 0$ in $\sigma(T)$, the corresponding eigenvectors $x_n$ form an orthonormal-like sequence, but compactness forces them to converge to zero, contradiction.

**Density of finite-rank operators in $\mathcal{K}(\mathcal{H})$.** Proved in Exercise 6. The argument uses that $\mathcal{H}$ has a countable orthonormal basis (Hilbert-space separability) and that compact images are totally bounded.

**Atkinson's theorem.** Proved in unit [03.09.06] §"Key theorem" (Fredholm operators). The Calkin algebra is the natural quotient encoding compact-modulo-bounded structure.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Hilbert's 1906 work on integral equations introduced what we now call compact operators (in the special case of integral operators with continuous kernels). Riesz's 1918 spectral theory abstracted Hilbert's framework to operators on arbitrary Banach spaces, identifying the discrete-spectrum / finite-eigenspace structure that characterises compact operators. Schauder's 1930 theorem cemented compactness as a self-adjoint property under operator-theoretic duality.

The realisation that compact operators form a *two-sided ideal* — and that the Calkin algebra $\mathcal{B}/\mathcal{K}$ is the natural setting for index theory — was Atkinson's 1951 contribution [ref: TODO_REF Reed-Simon §VI]. Schatten and von Neumann developed the trace-class / Hilbert-Schmidt refinements in the 1940s, opening the door to the modern theory of operator algebras and noncommutative geometry.

In quantum mechanics, compact operators are the *bounded observables with discrete spectra*: the Hamiltonian of a confined system, density matrices (which are positive trace-class operators of trace one), and projection onto finite-energy subspaces. The infinite-dimensional generalisations of matrix-mechanics — eigenvalue expansions, perturbation theory — all live in the compact-operator universe.
