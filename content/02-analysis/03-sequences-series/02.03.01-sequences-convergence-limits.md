## Intuition [Beginner]

A sequence is an infinite list of real numbers, one entry for each counting number: $a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots$. The list never ends. The question that drives this whole chapter is whether the numbers in the list eventually settle down toward a single target value, or whether they keep wandering forever.

Take the list $1, 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/5, \dots$ where the $n$-th entry is $1/n$. The entries shrink as you read down the list. Pick any tiny positive target distance, say $0.001$. Once you read past entry $1000$, every remaining entry is smaller than $0.001$. Pick a tinier distance, say $0.000001$. Once you read past entry one million, every remaining entry is smaller still. The list settles toward $0$, and we say the sequence converges to $0$.

Compare the list $1, -1, 1, -1, 1, -1, \dots$ where the entries flip sign forever. No matter how far you read, the entries never settle. This sequence does not converge.

The precise rule for "settles toward a target" is the heart of this unit. The rule turns the vague idea of approaching into a checkable test: name any positive tolerance, however small, and there is a point in the list past which every entry sits within that tolerance of the target. That single rule, written down carefully, is what makes the rest of calculus possible.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We check that the sequence $a_n = 1/n$ converges to $0$ using the tolerance rule with a specific tolerance $\varepsilon = 0.001$.

The rule asks for a cutoff index $N$ such that every entry $a_n$ with index $n$ at least $N$ lies within distance $0.001$ of the target $0$. That requirement reads $1/n < 0.001$.

The inequality $1/n < 0.001$ rearranges to $n > 1000$. So $N = 1000$ works: for every index $n$ at least $1000$, the value $1/n$ is at most $1/1000 = 0.001$. The entry $a_{1000} = 0.001$ and every later entry is smaller, so all sit within the tolerance band around $0$.

The same calculation produces a cutoff for any positive tolerance. For tolerance $0.0001$, choose $N = 10000$. For tolerance $0.0000007$, choose $N = 2000000$ (rounded up). Each tolerance yields its own cutoff, and a cutoff exists for every positive tolerance. That existence is exactly what the statement "the sequence converges to $0$" means.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix the real line $\mathbb{R}$ with its absolute value and the usual order from [02.02.01]. A *sequence* of real numbers is a function $a : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{R}$, written $(a_n)_{n \geq 1}$ or simply $(a_n)$, where $a_n = a(n)$ is the $n$-th term.

**Definition (convergence).** The sequence $(a_n)$ *converges* to $L \in \mathbb{R}$ iff for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that for every $n \geq N$, $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$. Write $a_n \to L$ or $\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L$.

**Definition (divergence).** A sequence that does not converge is *divergent*. Divergence covers sequences that escape to infinity ($a_n = n$), sequences that oscillate without settling ($a_n = (-1)^n$), and sequences with several accumulating values.

**Definition (bounded sequence).** A sequence $(a_n)$ is *bounded* iff some $M > 0$ satisfies $|a_n| \leq M$ for every $n \in \mathbb{N}$.

**Definition (monotone sequence).** $(a_n)$ is *monotone increasing* iff $a_n \leq a_{n+1}$ for every $n$; *monotone decreasing* iff $a_n \geq a_{n+1}$ for every $n$; *monotone* if either holds.

**Definition (subsequence).** A *subsequence* of $(a_n)$ is a sequence $(a_{n_k})_{k \geq 1}$ where $n_1 < n_2 < n_3 < \cdots$ is a strictly increasing sequence of indices in $\mathbb{N}$.

**Definition (Cauchy sequence).** A sequence $(a_n)$ is *Cauchy* iff for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $N \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $m, n \geq N$ implies $|a_m - a_n| < \varepsilon$.

The Cauchy definition does not name a limit. Its equivalence with convergence over $\mathbb{R}$ is a deep theorem that depends on the completeness axiom of [02.02.01]; that equivalence, together with the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem, is proved in full in [02.03.02]. This unit builds the rest of the convergence toolkit — the limit laws, the squeeze theorem, the monotone convergence theorem — directly on the ε–$N$ definition, and states the Cauchy and Bolzano-Weierstrass results as forward references.

The presentation follows Rudin [ref: TODO_REF Rudin Ch. 3] and Abbott [ref: TODO_REF Abbott Ch. 2–3]; both organise the chapter so that the ε–$N$ definition and the algebraic limit theorem precede subsequences and the Cauchy criterion.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Consecutive entries getting closer to $L$ does not imply convergence to $L$.* The sequence $a_n = 1 + 1/n$ has entries whose distance to $0$ decreases at every step, yet $a_n \to 1$. Convergence requires the distance to drop below every positive tolerance, not merely to decrease.
- *A bounded sequence need not converge.* The sequence $a_n = (-1)^n$ is bounded by $1$ but oscillates between two values. Boundedness plus monotonicity is what forces convergence (the monotone convergence theorem below); boundedness alone gives only a convergent *subsequence* (Bolzano-Weierstrass, [02.03.02]).
- *A sequence can have several subsequential limits without converging.* The sequence $a_n = (-1)^n$ has a subsequence converging to $1$ and another converging to $-1$. The full sequence converges iff every subsequence converges to the same limit.
- *Strict inequalities are not preserved by limits.* If $a_n < b_n$ for every $n$, the limits may agree: take $a_n = 0$ and $b_n = 1/n$. The limit preserves only non-strict inequality ($a_n \leq b_n \Rightarrow \lim a_n \leq \lim b_n$), proved below.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Proposition (uniqueness of limits).** *If $a_n \to L$ and $a_n \to L'$ in $\mathbb{R}$, then $L = L'$.*

*Proof.* Suppose $L \neq L'$. Set $\varepsilon = |L - L'|/2 > 0$. Choose $N$ so that $n \geq N$ gives both $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$ and $|a_n - L'| < \varepsilon$. The triangle inequality of [00.01.02] gives $|L - L'| \leq |L - a_N| + |a_N - L'| < \varepsilon + \varepsilon = |L - L'|$, a contradiction. So $L = L'$. $\square$

**Theorem (algebraic limit theorem).** *If $a_n \to a$ and $b_n \to b$, then: (i) $a_n + b_n \to a + b$; (ii) $c \cdot a_n \to c \cdot a$ for any $c \in \mathbb{R}$; (iii) $a_n b_n \to ab$; (iv) if $b \neq 0$ and $b_n \neq 0$ for all $n$, then $a_n / b_n \to a/b$.*

*Proof of (i).* Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. Choose $N_1$ so $n \geq N_1$ gives $|a_n - a| < \varepsilon/2$. Choose $N_2$ so $n \geq N_2$ gives $|b_n - b| < \varepsilon/2$. Set $N = \max(N_1, N_2)$. For $n \geq N$, $|(a_n + b_n) - (a + b)| \leq |a_n - a| + |b_n - b| < \varepsilon/2 + \varepsilon/2 = \varepsilon$.

*Proof of (ii).* If $c = 0$ the claim reads $0 \to 0$. If $c \neq 0$, fix $\varepsilon > 0$ and choose $N$ so $n \geq N$ gives $|a_n - a| < \varepsilon/|c|$. Then $|c a_n - c a| = |c| \cdot |a_n - a| < \varepsilon$.

*Proof of (iii).* Decompose $a_n b_n - ab = (a_n - a)b + a_n(b_n - b)$. Because $a_n \to a$, the sequence $(a_n)$ is bounded: some $M > 0$ has $|a_n| \leq M$ for every $n$ (a convergent sequence is bounded, proved in the Full proof set). Set $B = \max(|b|, M, 1)$. Fix $\varepsilon > 0$ and choose $N$ so $n \geq N$ gives both $|a_n - a| < \varepsilon/(2B)$ and $|b_n - b| < \varepsilon/(2B)$. Then $|a_n b_n - ab| \leq |b| \cdot |a_n - a| + |a_n| \cdot |b_n - b| \leq B \cdot \varepsilon/(2B) + B \cdot \varepsilon/(2B) = \varepsilon$.

*Proof of (iv).* It is enough to show $1/b_n \to 1/b$; the quotient rule then follows from (iii). Since $b_n \to b \neq 0$, eventually $|b_n - b| < |b|/2$, so for large $n$, $|b_n| > |b|/2$. Fix $\varepsilon > 0$ and choose $N$ so $n \geq N$ gives $|b_n - b| < \varepsilon b^2/2$ and $|b_n| > |b|/2$. Then $|1/b_n - 1/b| = |b - b_n|/(|b_n| \cdot |b|) < (\varepsilon b^2/2)/(b^2/2) = \varepsilon$. $\square$

**Theorem (squeeze theorem).** *If $a_n \leq c_n \leq b_n$ for every $n$ and $a_n \to L$, $b_n \to L$, then $c_n \to L$.*

*Proof.* Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. Choose $N$ so $n \geq N$ gives both $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$ and $|b_n - L| < \varepsilon$, i.e., $L - \varepsilon < a_n$ and $b_n < L + \varepsilon$. Sandwiching, $L - \varepsilon < a_n \leq c_n \leq b_n < L + \varepsilon$, so $|c_n - L| < \varepsilon$. $\square$

**Theorem (monotone convergence theorem).** *Every bounded monotone sequence of real numbers converges. A bounded-above monotone-increasing sequence converges to $\sup \{a_n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$; a bounded-below monotone-decreasing sequence converges to $\inf \{a_n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$.*

*Proof.* Let $(a_n)$ be monotone increasing and bounded above. The set $S = \{a_n : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ is non-empty and bounded above, so by the LUB axiom of [02.02.01] the supremum $L = \sup S$ exists in $\mathbb{R}$.

Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. Because $L$ is the *least* upper bound, $L - \varepsilon$ is not an upper bound of $S$: some $a_N \in S$ has $a_N > L - \varepsilon$. By monotonicity, for every $n \geq N$, $a_n \geq a_N > L - \varepsilon$. Because $L$ is an upper bound, $a_n \leq L < L + \varepsilon$. Combining, $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$ for every $n \geq N$. So $a_n \to L$.

The monotone-decreasing case applies the same argument to $(-a_n)$, or directly to the greatest lower bound. $\square$

**Theorem (subsequence characterization).** *A sequence $(a_n)$ converges to $L$ iff every subsequence of $(a_n)$ also converges to $L$.*

*Proof.* ($\Rightarrow$) If $a_n \to L$, fix $\varepsilon > 0$ and choose $N$ so $n \geq N$ gives $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$. For any subsequence $(a_{n_k})$, the index map $k \mapsto n_k$ is strictly increasing, so $n_k \geq k$; thus for $k \geq N$, $n_k \geq N$ and $|a_{n_k} - L| < \varepsilon$. ($\Leftarrow$) The full sequence is a subsequence of itself. $\square$

**Corollary.** *If $(a_n)$ has two subsequences converging to different limits, then $(a_n)$ diverges.*

**Theorem (Bolzano-Weierstrass, statement).** *Every bounded sequence in $\mathbb{R}$ has a convergent subsequence.* The full proof — via interval bisection producing a nested sequence of closed intervals whose lengths shrink to zero — appears in [02.03.02], where the theorem is paired with the Cauchy criterion and shown to be one of the equivalent reformulations of the completeness axiom of [02.02.01].

**Bridge.** The ε–$N$ definition of convergence builds toward [02.03.02] (where the Cauchy criterion and Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem are proved, and convergence is characterised without naming the limit) and appears again in [02.03.03] (where an infinite series is declared convergent exactly when its partial-sum sequence satisfies this definition). The foundational reason the definition is set up this way — naming the limit $L$ first, then producing an index $N$ for each tolerance $\varepsilon$ — is that it makes the limit laws and the squeeze theorem direct consequences of the triangle inequality of [00.01.02]. This is exactly the structural fact that lets later analysis inherit the same toolkit: a function-limit statement reduces to a sequence-limit statement by testing $f$ along sequences $x_n \to x$, and continuity in [02.04.02] becomes "preserve convergent sequences". The central insight is that convergence is preserved under the field operations (the algebraic limit theorem) and under order-sandwiching (the squeeze theorem), and these two preservation principles cover almost every concrete limit computation. Putting these together, the limit laws package the field structure of $\mathbb{R}$ as a calculus of limits, and the monotone convergence theorem packages the order structure of $\mathbb{R}$ (via the LUB axiom) as a limit-existence theorem; the two together form the working foundation on which [02.03.02] and [02.03.03] build.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Using the ε–$N$ definition, find the smallest cutoff $N$ such that $n \geq N$ implies $|2/(n+1) - 0| < 0.01$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Solve $2/(n+1) < 0.01$ for $n$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$N = 200$.** We need $2/(n+1) < 0.01$, i.e., $n + 1 > 200$, i.e., $n \geq 200$. For $n \geq 200$, $2/(n+1) \leq 2/201 < 2/200 = 0.01$. Note $n = 199$ gives $2/200 = 0.01$, which is not strictly less than the tolerance, so $N = 199$ fails.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Using the algebraic limit theorem, compute $\lim_{n \to \infty} (2n+1)/(3n-7)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Divide numerator and denominator by $n$, then apply the limit laws.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$2/3$.** Divide numerator and denominator by $n$:

$$(2n+1)/(3n-7) = (2 + 1/n)/(3 - 7/n).$$

Since $1/n \to 0$ and $7/n \to 0$, the algebraic limit theorem gives the numerator tending to $2$ and the denominator tending to $3$. The quotient rule (part iv, denominator limit $3 \neq 0$) gives the limit $2/3$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show directly from the ε–$N$ definition that $a_n = (3n+1)/(n+2) \to 3$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $|a_n - 3|$ explicitly and bound it in terms of $1/n$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Compute $|a_n - 3| = |(3n+1 - 3(n+2))/(n+2)| = |(3n+1-3n-6)/(n+2)| = 5/(n+2)$. Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. We need $5/(n+2) < \varepsilon$, i.e., $n > 5/\varepsilon - 2$. By the Archimedean property of [02.02.01], choose $N \in \mathbb{N}$ with $N > 5/\varepsilon$. For $n \geq N$, $5/(n+2) \leq 5/n \leq 5/N < \varepsilon$. So $a_n \to 3$. Rubric: full credit for the explicit difference $5/(n+2)$ and the Archimedean choice of $N$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Using the squeeze theorem, show that $a_n = (-1)^n / n \to 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Bound $(-1)^n / n$ between two sequences that both converge to $0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For every $n \geq 1$, $-1 \leq (-1)^n \leq 1$, so dividing by $n$ gives $-1/n \leq (-1)^n / n \leq 1/n$. Both bounding sequences converge to $0$ ($1/n \to 0$ and $-1/n \to 0$, the latter by the algebraic limit theorem with $c = -1$). By the squeeze theorem, $(-1)^n / n \to 0$. Rubric: full credit for the two-sided bound and the invocation of both limits.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Let $a_n = n/(n+1)$. Show that $(a_n)$ is monotone increasing and bounded above, then use the monotone convergence theorem to conclude it converges and identify the limit.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $a_{n+1} - a_n$ and look for an obvious upper bound. The limit can be read off by rewriting $n/(n+1)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Monotonicity:* $a_{n+1} - a_n = (n+1)/(n+2) - n/(n+1) = ((n+1)^2 - n(n+2))/((n+2)(n+1)) = 1/((n+2)(n+1)) > 0$. So $a_{n+1} > a_n$.

*Bounded above:* $n/(n+1) < 1$ for every $n$, so $1$ is an upper bound.

By the monotone convergence theorem, $(a_n)$ converges to $L = \sup\{n/(n+1)\}$. Reading the limit directly: $n/(n+1) = 1 - 1/(n+1) \to 1 - 0 = 1$ by the algebraic limit theorem. Rubric: full credit for the monotonicity computation, the bound, and the limit identification.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that if $a_n \to L$ and $a_n \geq c$ for every $n$, then $L \geq c$. Use this to exhibit a sequence that is strictly positive for every $n$ yet has a limit that is not strictly positive.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Argue by contradiction: assume $L < c$ and pick $\varepsilon = (c - L)/2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $L < c$. Set $\varepsilon = (c - L)/2 > 0$. Choose $N$ so $n \geq N$ gives $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$, i.e., $a_n < L + \varepsilon = (L + c)/2 < c$, contradicting $a_n \geq c$. So $L \geq c$.

For the example: $a_n = 1/n > 0$ for every $n$, yet $\lim a_n = 0$, which is not strictly positive. So strict inequalities are not preserved by limits — only non-strict ones ($a_n > 0 \Rightarrow \lim a_n \geq 0$). Rubric: full credit for the contradiction argument and the $1/n$ counterexample.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove Cesàro's theorem: if $a_n \to a$, then the sequence of arithmetic means $b_n = (a_1 + a_2 + \cdots + a_n)/n$ also converges to $a$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Split the sum at an index $N_0$ past which $|a_k - a|$ is small. The finite head is bounded by a constant divided by $n$; the tail is small on average.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Given $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $N_0$ so $k \geq N_0$ implies $|a_k - a| < \varepsilon/2$. Set $C = \sum_{k=1}^{N_0} |a_k - a|$, a finite constant independent of $n$. Write

$$b_n - a = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^{N_0}(a_k - a) + \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=N_0+1}^{n}(a_k - a).$$

The first term satisfies $|(1/n)\sum_{k=1}^{N_0}(a_k - a)| \leq C/n$, which is below $\varepsilon/2$ for $n > 2C/\varepsilon$. The second term satisfies $|(1/n)\sum_{k=N_0+1}^{n}(a_k - a)| \leq (n - N_0)/n \cdot \varepsilon/2 < \varepsilon/2$. Choose $N = \max(N_0, \lceil 2C/\varepsilon \rceil)$. For $n \geq N$, $|b_n - a| < \varepsilon/2 + \varepsilon/2 = \varepsilon$. So $b_n \to a$.

The converse fails: $a_n = (-1)^n$ does not converge, but its Cesàro means $b_n \to 0$. Rubric: full credit for the head/tail split and the explicit choice of $N$.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The ε–$N$ definition supports two refinements that turn "does the sequence converge?" into a quantitative theory: the order-theoretic refinement (limsup and liminf) and the subsequence refinement (the set of subsequential limits). Both are grounded in the monotone convergence theorem and lead into the Cauchy and Bolzano-Weierstrass theory of [02.03.02].

**Definition (limit superior and limit inferior).** For a bounded sequence $(a_n)$, set $u_n = \sup_{k \geq n} a_k$ and $\ell_n = \inf_{k \geq n} a_k$. The sequence $(u_n)$ is monotone decreasing and bounded below; $(\ell_n)$ is monotone increasing and bounded above. Both converge by the monotone convergence theorem. Define $\limsup_{n \to \infty} a_n = \lim_{n \to \infty} u_n = \inf_{n} \sup_{k \geq n} a_k$ and $\liminf_{n \to \infty} a_n = \lim_{n \to \infty} \ell_n = \sup_{n} \inf_{k \geq n} a_k$. Both exist as real numbers by the completeness axiom of [02.02.01].

**Theorem (limsup/liminf characterization).** *Let $(a_n)$ be a bounded sequence. Then (i) $\liminf a_n \leq \limsup a_n$; (ii) $(a_n)$ converges iff $\liminf a_n = \limsup a_n$, in which case $\lim a_n$ equals their common value; (iii) $\limsup a_n$ is the largest subsequential limit of $(a_n)$ and $\liminf a_n$ is the smallest.*

*Proof sketch.* (i) For every $n$, $\ell_n \leq u_n$; taking limits preserves the non-strict inequality by Exercise 6. (ii) If $a_n \to L$, then $\ell_n \leq a_n \leq u_n$ for every $n$ and both $\ell_n, u_n$ are squeezed to $L$, so $\liminf = \limsup = L$. Conversely, if $\liminf = \limsup = L$, then $\ell_n \leq a_n \leq u_n$ with both bounds converging to $L$, and the squeeze theorem gives $a_n \to L$. (iii) is a refinement of Bolzano-Weierstrass: extract a subsequence converging to $\limsup a_n$ by choosing indices $n_k$ with $a_{n_k}$ close to $u_{n_k}$; the full construction appears in [02.03.02]. [ref: TODO_REF Royden Ch. 2]

**Theorem (subsequential limits form a closed set).** *The set $E$ of subsequential limits of a bounded sequence $(a_n)$ in $\mathbb{R}$ is a non-empty closed subset of $\mathbb{R}$. Its maximum is $\limsup a_n$ and its minimum is $\liminf a_n$.*

Bolzano-Weierstrass ([02.03.02]) gives $E \neq \emptyset$. The order-completeness of $\mathbb{R}$ gives $\sup E \in E$ and $\inf E \in E$ by extracting subsequences whose terms approach the supremum and infimum. Closure under limits of sequences in $E$ is a diagonal argument: a sequence of subsequential limits, each realised by a subsequence of $(a_n)$, has a limit realised by a diagonal subsequence. The details belong with the compactness theory of [02.03.02].

**Theorem (completeness equivalences, summary).** *Over the Archimedean ordered-field axioms of [02.02.01], the following are equivalent: (a) the LUB axiom; (b) the monotone convergence theorem; (c) the nested-interval property; (d) the Cauchy criterion (every Cauchy sequence converges); (e) the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem.*

The monotone convergence theorem implies the LUB axiom by approximating $\sup S$ with a monotone-increasing sequence of elements of $S$; the LUB axiom implies monotone convergence by the proof above; the nested-interval property is an equivalent geometric form; the Cauchy criterion's equivalence with convergence is proved in [02.03.02]; and the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem is shown there to be derivable from any of the other four. The five reformulations are the structural content of completeness on the real line. [ref: TODO_REF Rudin Ch. 3] [ref: TODO_REF Abbott Ch. 2–3]

**Theorem (convergence in general metric spaces).** *The ε–$N$ definition specialises $\mathbb{R}$ to the case where the distance is $d(x, y) = |x - y|$. In a general metric space $(X, d)$ from [02.01.05], a sequence $(x_n)$ converges to $L \in X$ iff for every $\varepsilon > 0$ some $N$ has $d(x_n, L) < \varepsilon$ for $n \geq N$.*

The algebraic limit theorem does *not* generalise to metric spaces (it relies on the field structure of $\mathbb{R}$), but the squeeze theorem generalises in the form: if $d(x_n, L) \leq d(y_n, L)$ and $y_n \to L$, then $x_n \to L$. The monotone convergence theorem relies essentially on the order structure of $\mathbb{R}$ and has no metric-space analogue. The Cauchy criterion, by contrast, generalises verbatim and becomes the working definition of completeness for metric spaces, leading to the Banach-space theory of [02.11.04]. [ref: TODO_REF Dieudonné Ch. 3]

**Synthesis.** The convergence theory of real sequences identifies three faces of limit existence on $\mathbb{R}$: the algebraic face (the limit laws), the order-theoretic face (the monotone convergence theorem together with limsup and liminf), and the metric face (the ε–$N$ definition itself, and the Cauchy criterion of [02.03.02]). The foundational reason the three faces cohere is the completeness axiom of [02.02.01]: each face reformulates completeness, and each reformulation generalises to a different broader setting — the algebraic face to topological groups and vector spaces, the order-theoretic face to complete lattices and ordered sets, the metric face to complete metric spaces and uniform spaces. This is exactly the structural decomposition that explains why calculus works on $\mathbb{R}$ but fails on $\mathbb{Q}$: the field operations are identical on both, but only $\mathbb{R}$ carries the completeness that turns bounded monotone lists into convergent lists. Putting these together identifies the algebraic limit theorem as the bridge from the field axioms to computable limits, the squeeze theorem as the bridge from the order axioms to inherited convergence, and the monotone convergence theorem as the bridge from the LUB axiom to limit existence. The bridge is the equivalence of the ε–$N$ definition, the Cauchy criterion, the monotone convergence theorem, and the LUB axiom — four formulations of one structural fact about $\mathbb{R}$, each natural in a different downstream setting.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (uniqueness of limits).** *Proved in §"Key theorem with proof" above.*

**Proposition (algebraic limit theorem, product rule, full estimate).** *If $a_n \to a$ and $b_n \to b$, then $a_n b_n \to ab$.*

*Proof.* Decompose $a_n b_n - ab = (a_n - a)b + a_n(b_n - b)$. Because $a_n \to a$, the sequence $(a_n)$ is bounded: choose $N_0$ so $n \geq N_0$ gives $|a_n - a| < 1$, and set $M_a = \max(|a_1|, \dots, |a_{N_0}|, |a| + 1)$, so $|a_n| \leq M_a$ for every $n$. Set $B = \max(M_a, |b|, 1)$. Fix $\varepsilon > 0$ and choose $N \geq N_0$ so $n \geq N$ gives both $|a_n - a| < \varepsilon/(2B)$ and $|b_n - b| < \varepsilon/(2B)$. Then

$$|a_n b_n - ab| \leq |b| \cdot |a_n - a| + |a_n| \cdot |b_n - b| \leq B \cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{2B} + B \cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{2B} = \varepsilon. \quad \square$$

**Proposition (convergent sequences are bounded).** *If $a_n \to a$, then $(a_n)$ is bounded.*

*Proof.* Take $\varepsilon = 1$ in the convergence definition: some $N$ has $|a_n - a| < 1$ for $n \geq N$, so $|a_n| \leq |a| + 1$ for $n \geq N$. Set $M = \max(|a_1|, \dots, |a_{N-1}|, |a| + 1)$; then $|a_n| \leq M$ for every $n$. $\square$

**Proposition (squeeze theorem).** *Proved in §"Key theorem with proof" above.*

**Proposition (monotone convergence theorem).** *Proved in §"Key theorem with proof" above.*

**Proposition (preservation of non-strict inequalities).** *If $a_n \to a$, $b_n \to b$, and $a_n \leq b_n$ for every $n$, then $a \leq b$.*

*Proof.* Suppose $a > b$. Set $\varepsilon = (a - b)/2 > 0$. Choose $N$ so $n \geq N$ gives $|a_n - a| < \varepsilon$ and $|b_n - b| < \varepsilon$. Then $a_n > a - \varepsilon = (a+b)/2$ and $b_n < b + \varepsilon = (a+b)/2$, so $a_n > b_n$, contradicting the hypothesis. So $a \leq b$. $\square$

**Proposition (Cesàro mean theorem).** *If $a_n \to a$, then $b_n = (a_1 + \cdots + a_n)/n \to a$.*

*Proof.* Given $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $N_0$ so $n \geq N_0$ implies $|a_n - a| < \varepsilon/2$. Set $C = \sum_{k=1}^{N_0} |a_k - a|$, a finite constant. Then

$$|b_n - a| = \left|\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n}(a_k - a)\right| \leq \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^{N_0}|a_k - a| + \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=N_0+1}^{n}|a_k - a| \leq \frac{C}{n} + \frac{n - N_0}{n}\cdot\frac{\varepsilon}{2}.$$

Choose $N \geq \max(N_0, \lceil 2C/\varepsilon \rceil)$. For $n \geq N$, $C/n \leq \varepsilon/2$ and $(n - N_0)/n < 1$, so $|b_n - a| < \varepsilon/2 + \varepsilon/2 = \varepsilon$. $\square$

The converse fails: $a_n = (-1)^n$ diverges but its Cesàro means converge to $0$. Cesàro summability is therefore strictly weaker than ordinary convergence, a fact used in the summability theory of Fourier series.

**Proposition (subsequence characterization).** *$a_n \to L$ iff every subsequence of $(a_n)$ converges to $L$.*

*Proof.* ($\Rightarrow$) If $a_n \to L$, fix $\varepsilon > 0$ and choose $N$ so $n \geq N$ gives $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$. For any subsequence $(a_{n_k})$, strict increase of $k \mapsto n_k$ gives $n_k \geq k$, so $k \geq N$ implies $n_k \geq N$ and $|a_{n_k} - L| < \varepsilon$. ($\Leftarrow$) The full sequence is a subsequence of itself. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Real-number axioms [02.02.01].** The ε–$N$ definition of sequence convergence is the sequential translation of the LUB completeness axiom: every bounded monotone sequence converges because the supremum of its range exists as a real number. The monotone convergence theorem proved in this unit is one of the equivalent reformulations of completeness identified in [02.02.01] and shown equivalent to the Cauchy criterion in [02.03.02]. The Archimedean property of [02.02.01] is the explicit engine behind every concrete ε–$N$ estimate, including the $1/n \to 0$ computation in the worked example and the $(3n+1)/(n+2) \to 3$ argument of Exercise 3.

- **Cauchy sequences and Bolzano-Weierstrass [02.03.02].** The Cauchy criterion — convergence characterised without naming the limit — is the metric reformulation of the ε–$N$ definition developed here. The Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem — every bounded sequence has a convergent subsequence — is the compactness reformulation. Both are proved in [02.03.02], and their equivalence with the monotone convergence theorem of this unit is the structural content of the completeness axiom. The set of subsequential limits studied in the Advanced results above is closed and non-empty by Bolzano-Weierstrass, with $\limsup$ and $\liminf$ as its extreme points.

- **Infinite series [02.03.03].** An infinite series $\sum a_n$ converges iff its sequence of partial sums $s_N = a_1 + \cdots + a_N$ converges in the sense of this unit. Every convergence test in [02.03.03] — comparison, ratio, alternating — ultimately reduces to checking that the partial-sum sequence satisfies the ε–$N$ definition, usually via the Cauchy criterion of [02.03.02] or the monotone convergence theorem of this unit. The limit laws proved here are what license termwise algebraic manipulation of convergent series.

- **Continuous functions [02.04.02].** A function $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous at $x$ iff for every sequence $x_n \to x$, the image sequence $f(x_n) \to f(x)$. This sequential characterization reduces function limits to sequence limits, and every theorem about continuous functions (the intermediate value theorem, the extreme value theorem, uniform continuity) is proved by composing sequences with $f$ and applying the limit laws and the squeeze theorem of this unit.

- **Metric spaces [02.01.05].** The ε–$N$ definition generalises from $\mathbb{R}$ with absolute value to any metric space by replacing $|x - y|$ with $d(x, y)$. The algebraic limit theorem does not generalise (it needs field structure), but the squeeze theorem and the Cauchy criterion do. Metric-space convergence is the natural language for the Banach-space and Hilbert-space theory of [02.11.04], where the Cauchy criterion becomes the defining property of completeness.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Augustin-Louis Cauchy's *Cours d'analyse* of 1821 [ref: TODO_REF Cauchy 1821] gave the first systematic treatment of sequence convergence. Cauchy wrote that a sequence $(a_n)$ converges to $L$ when the differences $|a_n - L|$ become "as small as one wishes" for sufficiently large $n$ — the informal statement of the ε–$N$ definition. Cauchy used this definition to prove the algebraic limit theorem, the squeeze principle, and the convergence of geometric series, establishing the working toolkit that this unit formalises. Cauchy did not separate the tolerance from the cutoff symbolically; that notational step came later.

Bernard Bolzano's 1817 *Rein analytischer Beweis* [ref: TODO_REF Bolzano 1817], written four years before Cauchy's *Cours*, contained the bounded-monotone convergence argument as a lemma embedded in the first analytic proof of the intermediate value theorem. Bolzano recognised that a bounded set of real numbers has a least upper bound (his formulation of completeness) and used it to extract convergent subsequences; the monotone convergence theorem of this unit is a direct descendant of Bolzano's lemma. The paper was largely unread outside Prague for half a century.

Karl Weierstrass's Berlin lectures of the 1860s [ref: TODO_REF Weierstrass 1894] standardised the ε–$N$ (and ε–$\delta$) notation that made the limit definition fully rigorous. The notation separates the two quantifiers — "for every $\varepsilon$" and "there exists an $N$" — that Cauchy had run together, and is the form in which the definition is taught today. Weierstrass's rigorisation is what allowed the late-nineteenth-century unification of sequence convergence, continuous functions, and Riemann integration under a single ε-based framework.

The modern textbook presentation in Rudin's *Principles of Mathematical Analysis* Ch. 3 [ref: TODO_REF Rudin Ch. 3] and Abbott's *Understanding Analysis* Ch. 2–3 [ref: TODO_REF Abbott Ch. 2–3] arranges the results in the order: ε–$N$ definition, algebraic limit theorem, squeeze theorem, monotone convergence theorem, subsequences, then Bolzano-Weierstrass and the Cauchy criterion (here deferred to [02.03.02]). This unit follows that arrangement, with Dieudonné's filter-based treatment [ref: TODO_REF Dieudonné Ch. 3] and Royden's limsup/liminf presentation [ref: TODO_REF Royden Ch. 2] supplying the Master-tier generalisations.
