## Intuition [Beginner]

Hyperbolic dynamics in its cleanest form asks that every nearby orbit either runs away from yours at a fixed rate or rushes toward it at a fixed rate, the same rate everywhere on the set. That uniform demand is too strong for most systems that actually arise — a typical chaotic map stretches hard in some regions and barely at all in others. Pesin theory is the repair: it asks only that the stretching and contracting rates be nonzero *on average* along a typical orbit, and shows that almost everything you wanted from uniform hyperbolicity survives in a weakened, measurable form.

The first payoff is geometric. Even though the contraction rate may sag to nearly zero at some moments, at almost every point there is still a curved surface of points whose future orbits converge to yours — a stable manifold — and a matching surface of points whose past orbits converge — an unstable manifold. The catch is that these surfaces can be tiny at some points and large at others, and their size changes only slowly as you move along an orbit. They exist almost everywhere; they are just not uniform.

The second payoff is a number. The amount of unpredictability a system generates — its entropy, the rate at which watching it reveals new information — is controlled by how fast it stretches. Stretching faster means more ways for orbits to spread apart, so more disorder to track. The sum of the positive stretching rates is an upper bound on the entropy; and for the physically natural measures, the kind a computer simulation actually samples, the bound is exact. Entropy equals total stretching.

The takeaway: drop the demand that hyperbolicity hold everywhere uniformly and keep only that the average rates are nonzero, and you still get stable and unstable manifolds at almost every point and an exact accounting of the system's disorder as the sum of its positive growth rates.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture an orbit as a horizontal line of dots marching to the right, one dot per time step. At each dot, attach a small cross: one arm points along the contracting direction, one along the expanding direction. The length of each arm is how big the local stable or unstable surface is at that moment.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We compute the total stretching rate and the entropy for the simplest genuinely hyperbolic map: the cat map on the square, which acts on displacements by the matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$.

Step 1. The growth factors. The amount this matrix stretches in its two special directions are the absolute values of its eigenvalues. The eigenvalues solve $\mu^2 - 3\mu + 1 = 0$ (the trace is $3$, the determinant is $1$), giving $\mu = \frac{3 + \sqrt 5}{2} \approx 2.618$ and $\frac{3 - \sqrt 5}{2} \approx 0.382$. One direction stretches by about $2.618$ per step; the other shrinks by about $0.382$ per step.

Step 2. The stretching rates. A growth rate is the log of the growth factor. The expanding rate is $\log(2.618) \approx 0.962$. The contracting rate is $\log(0.382) \approx -0.962$, the same size with the opposite sign, because the determinant is $1$ so area is preserved and the two rates cancel.

Step 3. The sum of positive rates. There is exactly one positive rate here, about $0.962$. So the sum of the positive stretching rates is $0.962$.

Step 4. The entropy. The natural measure for the cat map is ordinary area, which is exactly the physically natural kind of measure where the bound is tight. So the entropy equals the sum of positive rates: $h \approx 0.962$.

What this tells us: for this map the disorder you can measure, $0.962$ units per step, is identical to the rate at which it stretches space apart. You did not have to track orbits or count anything by hand — the single positive growth rate handed you the entropy directly. That exact equality, for natural measures, is Pesin's formula.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $f : M \to M$ is a $C^{1+\alpha}$ diffeomorphism ($\alpha > 0$) of a compact Riemannian manifold preserving a Borel probability measure $\mu$, and $A(x) = D_x f$ is its derivative cocycle [38.07.01]. By the Oseledets theorem [38.07.01], for $\mu$-a.e. $x$ the tangent space splits as $T_x M = \bigoplus_i E_i(x)$ with exponents $\lambda_1(x) > \cdots > \lambda_{r(x)}(x)$, and we write $E^s(x) = \bigoplus_{\lambda_i < 0} E_i(x)$ and $E^u(x) = \bigoplus_{\lambda_i > 0} E_i(x)$ for the contracting and expanding subbundles. We use the sign convention that $E^s$ is contracted in *forward* time.

**Definition (nonuniform hyperbolicity).** The measure $\mu$ is *nonuniformly hyperbolic* for $f$ if all Lyapunov exponents are nonzero $\mu$-a.e., i.e. $\lambda_i(x) \neq 0$ for every $i$ at $\mu$-a.e. $x$; equivalently $T_x M = E^s(x) \oplus E^u(x)$ with no central direction. The set $\{x : \text{all } \lambda_i(x) \neq 0\}$ is the *Pesin region*.

**Definition (tempering function).** A measurable function $C : \Lambda \to [1, \infty)$ is *$\varepsilon$-tempered* along orbits if $e^{-\varepsilon} \leq C(fx)/C(x) \leq e^{\varepsilon}$ for $\mu$-a.e. $x$, equivalently $\lim_{n \to \pm\infty} \frac1n \log C(f^n x) = 0$. The *tempering lemma* asserts that any positive measurable function $D(x)$ with $\frac1n \log D(f^n x) \to 0$ is dominated by an $\varepsilon$-tempered $C \geq D$; this converts the merely-subexponential angle and norm bounds from Oseledets into bounds with a slowly varying constant.

**Definition (Pesin sets / Lyapunov-regular blocks).** Fix $\varepsilon > 0$ small relative to the spectral gaps. For $\ell \geq 1$ the *Pesin set* $\Lambda_\ell = \Lambda_\ell(\varepsilon)$ is the set of Lyapunov-regular points $x$ at which the hyperbolicity estimates hold with constant $\ell$:
$$\|D_x f^n|_{E^s(x)}\| \leq \ell\, e^{(\lambda^s + \varepsilon)n}, \quad \|D_x f^{-n}|_{E^u(x)}\| \leq \ell\, e^{-(\lambda^u - \varepsilon)n} \quad (n \geq 0),$$
and the angle $\angle(E^s(x), E^u(x)) \geq 1/\ell$, where $\lambda^s = \max_{\lambda_i < 0}\lambda_i < 0$ and $\lambda^u = \min_{\lambda_i > 0}\lambda_i > 0$. The $\Lambda_\ell$ are *compact* (after restricting to a set of full measure and taking closures of the level sets), increasing in $\ell$, with $\mu(\bigcup_\ell \Lambda_\ell) = 1$; the splitting $E^s \oplus E^u$ is *continuous* (indeed uniformly hyperbolic with constant $\ell$) on each $\Lambda_\ell$, though the $\Lambda_\ell$ themselves are *not* invariant — $f(\Lambda_\ell) \subseteq \Lambda_{\ell'}$ with $\ell'$ at most $e^{\varepsilon}\ell$, the tempering.

**Definition (local stable / unstable manifolds).** For $x$ in the Pesin region the *local stable manifold* is
$$W^s_{\mathrm{loc}}(x) = \{ y : d(f^n x, f^n y) \leq r(x)\, e^{(\lambda^s + \varepsilon)n} \text{ for all } n \geq 0 \},$$
a $C^{1+\alpha}$ embedded disk tangent to $E^s(x)$ at $x$, whose radius $r(x) > 0$ is tempered; $W^u_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)$ is defined symmetrically using $f^{-1}$.

**Definition (SRB / smooth measure; absolute continuity).** An $f$-invariant $\mu$ with positive exponents a.e. is an *SRB (Sinai-Ruelle-Bowen) measure* if its conditional measures on local unstable manifolds $W^u_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)$ are absolutely continuous with respect to the induced Riemannian (Lebesgue) volume on those manifolds. A measure absolutely continuous with respect to the Riemannian volume of $M$ is automatically SRB. The *unstable foliation is absolutely continuous* if its holonomy maps between transversals carry Lebesgue-class measures to Lebesgue-class measures.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *Nonuniform hyperbolicity does not give uniform constants.* The constant $\ell$ in $\Lambda_\ell$ is unavoidable: there is in general no single $C$ and $\lambda < 1$ working at every point, which is exactly the difference from a hyperbolic set [38.03.01]. Treating a Pesin set as if it were an Anosov set — expecting a global adapted metric — is the standard error.

- *Pesin sets are not invariant.* $f(\Lambda_\ell) \not\subseteq \Lambda_\ell$ in general; only $f(\Lambda_\ell) \subseteq \Lambda_{e^\varepsilon \ell}$. The estimates leak by a factor $e^\varepsilon$ each step. One must work with the whole exhaustion $\{\Lambda_\ell\}$ and the tempering, not a single block.

- *Stable manifolds can be arbitrarily small.* The radius $r(x)$ is only tempered, so $\inf_x r(x)$ can be $0$; there is no uniform lower bound on manifold size. Choosing a single $\delta$-neighborhood for all $x$ fails.

- *The Margulis-Ruelle inequality is not an equality in general.* For a generic invariant measure (e.g. a measure supported on a hyperbolic periodic orbit of an Axiom-A diffeomorphism) the entropy is strictly less than the average sum of positive exponents. Equality is special to SRB / absolutely continuous measures; asserting Pesin's formula for an arbitrary $\mu$ is wrong.

- *Positive exponents alone do not give an SRB measure.* The defining condition is absolute continuity of the conditional measures on unstable manifolds, not merely $\lambda^u > 0$. A measure can have positive exponents yet be singular along $W^u$ (its unstable conditionals supported on a Cantor set), in which case $h_\mu(f) < \int \sum \lambda_i^+\,d\mu$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Margulis-Ruelle inequality).** Let $f$ be a $C^1$ diffeomorphism of a compact Riemannian manifold preserving a Borel probability measure $\mu$. Then
$$h_\mu(f) \leq \int_M \sum_i \lambda_i^+(x)\, d\mu(x), \qquad \lambda_i^+ = \max(\lambda_i, 0),$$
where the sum is over the Lyapunov exponents counted with multiplicity and $h_\mu(f)$ is the Kolmogorov-Sinai entropy [38.06.02].

*Proof.* Write $\chi^+(x) = \sum_i \lambda_i^+(x) = \lim_n \frac1n \log\|\wedge^{\bullet} D_x f^n\|_{\max}$, where $\|\wedge^\bullet A\|_{\max} = \max_k \|\wedge^k A\|$ is the maximal volume-growth factor; by Oseledets [38.07.01], $\chi^+(x) = \lim_n \frac1n \log^+ |\det(D_x f^n|_{E^u})|$, the exponential rate of expansion of unstable volume. Fix a small $\rho > 0$ and a finite partition $\mathcal{P}$ of $M$ into sets of diameter $< \rho$ with piecewise-smooth boundaries; refine to $\mathcal{P}_0^{n-1} = \bigvee_{k=0}^{n-1} f^{-k}\mathcal{P}$. Entropy is computed from how finely $f^n$ subdivides: $h_\mu(f, \mathcal P) = \lim_n \frac1n H_\mu(\mathcal{P}_0^{n-1})$.

The mechanism is that $H_\mu(\mathcal{P}_0^{n-1}) = H_\mu(\mathcal P) + \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} H_\mu(f^{-k}\mathcal P \mid \mathcal P_0^{k-1}) \leq H_\mu(\mathcal P) + \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} H_\mu(f^{-k}\mathcal P \mid f^{-(k-1)}\mathcal P)$ by monotonicity of conditional entropy [38.06.02]. By $f$-invariance each term equals $H_\mu(f^{-1}\mathcal P \mid \mathcal P)$, so $h_\mu(f, \mathcal P) \leq H_\mu(f^{-1}\mathcal P \mid \mathcal P)$. Now estimate $H_\mu(f^{-1}\mathcal P \mid \mathcal P)$: conditioned on an atom $P$, the number of atoms of $f^{-1}\mathcal P$ that meet $P$ — equivalently atoms of $\mathcal P$ meeting $f(P)$ — is at most the number of $\rho$-balls needed to cover $f(P)$. Since $f$ expands volume on a set of diameter $\rho$ by at most $\sup_{y \in P}\|\wedge^\bullet D_y f\|_{\max}$ up to a dimensional constant, the covering number is $\lesssim \prod_i \max(1, e^{\lambda_i})$-many to leading exponential order, and a counting bound $H \leq \log(\#\text{atoms})$ gives, after passing to $f^n$ in place of $f$ and dividing by $n$,
$$h_\mu(f, \mathcal P) \leq \int_M \log^+|\det(D_x f|_{E^u(x)})|\,d\mu + O(\rho).$$
The boundary terms and the gap between $\log^+|\det D f|_{E^u}|$ and $\sum_i\lambda_i^+$ for a single step are absorbed by replacing $f$ with $f^n$, dividing by $n$, and using $\frac1n\log^+|\det(D_x f^n|_{E^u})| \to \chi^+(x)$ from Oseledets together with the dominated convergence theorem. Letting $\rho \to 0$ and taking the supremum over partitions gives $h_\mu(f) \leq \int \chi^+\,d\mu = \int \sum_i\lambda_i^+\,d\mu$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The Margulis-Ruelle inequality builds toward Pesin's entropy formula and appears again in the Ledrappier-Young theory, where the deficit $\int\sum\lambda_i^+ - h_\mu$ is shown to measure the failure of $\mu$ to be Lebesgue along unstable manifolds. The foundational reason the bound takes this form is that entropy counts how many distinguishable orbit-segments fit in the dynamics, and $f^n$ can separate at most as many as its unstable volume expansion $e^{n\chi^+}$ allows — this is exactly the statement that entropy is bounded by unstable volume growth. The construction generalises the Anosov computation $h = \log\lambda$ on a hyperbolic set [38.03.01], where the partition is a Markov partition and the inequality is an equality because the SRB measure is the natural one. Putting these together, the inequality is one half of a dichotomy whose other half — equality — is Pesin's theorem, and the central insight is that the gap is governed by the transverse dimension of $\mu$ along $E^u$: this is dual to the dimension theory that the Ledrappier-Young formula makes precise.

A $C^{1+\alpha}$ surface diffeomorphism preserves a smooth measure and has Lyapunov exponents $\lambda_1 = \log 4$ and $\lambda_2 = -\log 4$ a.e. Use Pesin's formula to compute $h_\mu(f)$. Give $\log 4$ to three places.

For a $C^1$ diffeomorphism, every invariant measure with all exponents zero a.e. has zero entropy.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that for an area-preserving $C^{1+\alpha}$ surface diffeomorphism with a nonuniformly hyperbolic smooth measure, $h_\mu(f) = \int_M \lambda_1^+\,d\mu = \int_M |\lambda_1|\,d\mu$ where $\lambda_1 > 0 > \lambda_2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Area preservation forces $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = \int\log|\det Df|\,d\mu = 0$ pointwise in the ergodic case; smoothness gives Pesin equality.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In dimension two with one positive and one negative exponent, $\sum_i\lambda_i^+ = \lambda_1$. Area preservation means $|\det D_x f| \equiv 1$, so by the trace identity [38.07.01] $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = \int\log|\det Df|\,d\mu = 0$, giving $\lambda_2 = -\lambda_1$ and hence $\lambda_1 = |\lambda_2| = |\lambda_1|$ as the single positive exponent. The smooth measure is SRB, so Pesin's formula yields $h_\mu(f) = \int\lambda_1^+\,d\mu = \int|\lambda_1|\,d\mu$. The entropy is exactly the integrated expansion rate.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the tempering lemma in the scalar form: if $D : \Lambda \to (0,\infty)$ is measurable with $\frac1n\log D(f^n x) \to 0$ as $n \to \pm\infty$ for a.e. $x$, then for each $\varepsilon > 0$ there is a measurable $C \geq D$ with $e^{-\varepsilon} \leq C(fx)/C(x) \leq e^{\varepsilon}$ a.e.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Define $C(x) = \sup_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} D(f^n x)\, e^{-\varepsilon|n|}$ and check the supremum is finite and slowly varying.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Set $C(x) = \sup_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} D(f^n x)\,e^{-\varepsilon|n|}$. The supremum is finite a.e.: since $\frac1n\log D(f^n x) \to 0$, for large $|n|$ one has $D(f^n x) \leq e^{(\varepsilon/2)|n|}$, so $D(f^n x)e^{-\varepsilon|n|} \leq e^{-(\varepsilon/2)|n|} \to 0$, and only finitely many terms exceed any threshold. Taking $n = 0$ gives $C(x) \geq D(x)$. For the slow-variation bound, $C(fx) = \sup_n D(f^{n+1}x)e^{-\varepsilon|n|} = \sup_m D(f^m x)e^{-\varepsilon|m-1|}$, and $|m-1| \in [|m|-1, |m|+1]$ gives $e^{-\varepsilon}C(x) \leq C(fx) \leq e^{\varepsilon}C(x)$. Thus $C$ is $\varepsilon$-tempered and dominates $D$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the definition: on $\Lambda_\ell$ the norms and the angle $\angle(E^s,E^u) \geq 1/\ell$ are uniformly controlled. A bounded angle plus uniform rates is exactly a cone field.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On $\Lambda_\ell$ the contraction $\|D f^n|_{E^s}\| \leq \ell e^{(\lambda^s+\varepsilon)n}$ and expansion $\|Df^{-n}|_{E^u}\| \leq \ell e^{-(\lambda^u-\varepsilon)n}$ hold with the *same* $\ell$ for all $x \in \Lambda_\ell$, and $\angle(E^s(x),E^u(x)) \geq 1/\ell$. Define stable and unstable cones $\mathcal{C}^u(x) = \{v = v^s + v^u : \|v^s\| \leq \|v^u\|\}$ in the $E^s\oplus E^u$ decomposition; the uniform angle bound makes these cones non-degenerate uniformly in $x \in \Lambda_\ell$, and the uniform rates give $Df(\mathcal C^u(x)) \subseteq \mathcal C^u(fx)$ with uniform expansion *whenever $fx \in \Lambda_\ell$ as well*. Thus the estimates defining uniform hyperbolicity hold with constant depending only on $\ell$; the failure of invariance ($fx$ may lie in $\Lambda_{\ell'}$, $\ell' \leq e^\varepsilon\ell$) is the only difference from a genuine hyperbolic set.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Sketch why absolute continuity of the unstable foliation is the mechanism turning the Margulis-Ruelle inequality into Pesin's equality for an SRB measure. Identify the precise place the inequality in the proof of Margulis-Ruelle becomes an equality.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In Margulis-Ruelle the loss is in the covering bound $H_\mu(f^{-1}\mathcal P \mid \mathcal P) \leq \log(\#\text{atoms})$. Equality in $H \leq \log(\#)$ needs the conditional measure on atoms to be *uniform* — i.e. Lebesgue — along the expanding direction.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Margulis-Ruelle bound uses two inequalities: the entropy-counting bound $H_\mu(\cdot\mid\cdot) \leq \log(\#\text{atoms})$, and the geometric covering bound (number of atoms $\lesssim$ unstable volume growth). The counting bound is an equality precisely when the conditional measures of $\mu$ on the refining atoms are equidistributed — uniform — across the atoms generated by the unstable expansion. For an SRB measure the conditional measures on local unstable manifolds are absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue, and absolute continuity of the unstable holonomy propagates this uniformity through the partition refinement: the conditional entropy then captures the full unstable volume growth $\int\log|\det Df|_{E^u}|\,d\mu = \int\sum_i\lambda_i^+\,d\mu$ rather than a strict undercount. Without absolute continuity the unstable conditionals are singular (e.g. supported on a Cantor set transverse to $E^u$), the counting bound is strict, and $h_\mu < \int\sum\lambda_i^+$. This is the qualitative content that Ledrappier-Young make quantitative: the deficit is $\sum_i\lambda_i^+(1 - \gamma_i)$ for partial dimensions $\gamma_i \in [0,1]$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $f$ be Anosov on a compact manifold with a hyperbolic invariant set equal to all of $M$ [38.03.01]. Show that the Riemannian volume class, when $f$ preserves a smooth measure $\mu$, makes $\mu$ an SRB measure and that Pesin's formula reduces to the Anosov entropy computation $h_\mu(f) = \int\log|\det Df|_{E^u}|\,d\mu$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For Anosov maps the unstable foliation is a genuine (Hölder) foliation with absolutely continuous holonomy; a smooth $\mu$ has Lebesgue conditionals on its leaves.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For an Anosov diffeomorphism the unstable distribution $E^u$ integrates to a continuous foliation $W^u$ with $C^{1+\alpha}$ leaves, and (Anosov's theorem) the holonomy along $W^u$ is absolutely continuous. If $f$ preserves a measure $\mu$ absolutely continuous with respect to Riemannian volume, then the conditional measures of $\mu$ on unstable leaves are absolutely continuous w.r.t. the induced leaf-volume — so $\mu$ is SRB by definition. Pesin's formula then applies: $h_\mu(f) = \int\sum_i\lambda_i^+\,d\mu = \int\log|\det(D_x f|_{E^u(x)})|\,d\mu$, since $\sum_i\lambda_i^+ = \lim_n\frac1n\log|\det(D_xf^n|_{E^u})| = \int\log|\det Df|_{E^u}|\,d\mu$ by Oseledets [38.07.01] and Birkhoff. This recovers the uniform-hyperbolic entropy formula as the special case of Pesin's where the hyperbolicity is uniform and the foliations are genuine.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (Pesin stable-manifold theorem; nonuniform Hadamard-Perron).** Let $f$ be a $C^{1+\alpha}$ diffeomorphism preserving $\mu$, nonuniformly hyperbolic. For $\mu$-a.e. $x$ there is a local stable manifold $W^s_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)$, a $C^{1+\alpha}$ embedded disk through $x$ tangent to $E^s(x)$, characterised by exponential forward convergence of orbits, with the following structure: the radius $r(x)$ and the $C^1$-size of the chart are tempered ($\frac1n\log r(f^n x) \to 0$); within each Pesin block $\Lambda_\ell$ the manifolds $W^s_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)$ depend continuously (indeed Hölder-continuously) on $x \in \Lambda_\ell$ in the $C^1$ topology; and they are $f$-equivariant, $f(W^s_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)) \subseteq W^s_{\mathrm{loc}}(fx)$. Symmetrically for $W^u_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)$ tangent to $E^u(x)$ using $f^{-1}$ [ref: TODO_REF Pesin 1977].

**Theorem 2 (absolute continuity of the Pesin lamination).** On each Pesin block $\Lambda_\ell$ the family of local unstable manifolds forms a measurable lamination whose holonomy maps — the maps between two transversals obtained by sliding along unstable leaves — are absolutely continuous: they carry the Lebesgue measure class on one transversal to the Lebesgue measure class on the other, with Radon-Nikodym derivative (Jacobian) bounded above and below on $\Lambda_\ell$ and given by the infinite product $\prod_{n\geq 0} \frac{|\det Df|_{E^u}(f^{-n}y)|}{|\det Df|_{E^u}(f^{-n}y')|}$ along paired orbits. The same holds for the stable lamination [ref: TODO_REF Pesin 1977].

**Theorem 3 (Pesin's entropy formula).** Let $f$ be a $C^{1+\alpha}$ diffeomorphism preserving a measure $\mu$ that is absolutely continuous with respect to the Riemannian volume on $M$. Then the Margulis-Ruelle inequality is an equality:
$$h_\mu(f) = \int_M \sum_i \lambda_i^+(x)\,d\mu(x).$$
The hypothesis can be weakened: equality holds if and only if $\mu$ is an SRB measure, i.e. its conditional measures on unstable manifolds are absolutely continuous with respect to leaf-volume. The proof produces, from absolute continuity of the unstable lamination, a measurable partition $\xi$ subordinate to the unstable foliation whose conditional entropy realises the full unstable volume growth, closing the gap left open in Margulis-Ruelle [ref: TODO_REF Pesin 1977].

**Theorem 4 (SRB measures; existence and basin property).** For a $C^2$ diffeomorphism with a hyperbolic attractor (Axiom A [38.03.01], or more generally a partially hyperbolic attractor with nonzero unstable exponents), there exists a unique SRB measure $\mu$ supported on the attractor, and Lebesgue-almost every point in the basin of attraction is generic for $\mu$:
$$\frac1n\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\varphi(f^k x) \to \int\varphi\,d\mu \quad \text{for Lebesgue-a.e. } x \text{ in the basin}, \ \varphi \in C(M).$$
This is the property that makes SRB measures the *physical* measures: they describe the statistics of orbits seen by a volume-typical initial condition, not merely a measure-typical one. Constructed by Sinai for Anosov systems, Ruelle and Bowen for Axiom A [ref: TODO_REF Sinai 1972].

**Theorem 5 (Ledrappier-Young formula).** For a $C^2$ diffeomorphism preserving an ergodic measure $\mu$ with exponents $\lambda_1 > \cdots > \lambda_u > 0 \geq \cdots$, there exist *partial dimensions* $\gamma_i \in [0, d_i]$ of $\mu$ along the Oseledets subspaces $E_i$ such that
$$h_\mu(f) = \sum_{i : \lambda_i > 0} \gamma_i\, \lambda_i.$$
The number $\gamma_i$ is the dimension of the conditional measure of $\mu$ in the direction $E_i$. The measure is SRB exactly when $\gamma_i = d_i$ (full dimension) for every $i$ with $\lambda_i > 0$, recovering Pesin's formula $h_\mu = \sum_i d_i\lambda_i^+$; for a general $\mu$ the entropy is the *dimension-weighted* sum of positive exponents, exhibiting entropy as a product of expansion rate and transverse dimension layer by layer [ref: TODO_REF Ledrappier-Young 1985].

**Synthesis.** Pesin theory is the systematic transfer of uniform-hyperbolic geometry to the nonuniform, measurable setting, and the foundational reason every piece survives is the tempering lemma: the Oseledets estimates [38.07.01], which hold only with subexponentially-varying constants, are upgraded to slowly-varying ones, and on the resulting Pesin blocks the dynamics is uniformly hyperbolic [38.03.01] with a constant that drifts by at most $e^\varepsilon$ per step. The stable-manifold theorem is exactly the Hadamard-Perron graph transform run on these blocks; the entropy formula is exactly the Margulis-Ruelle counting bound made sharp by absolute continuity of the unstable lamination, and this is the central insight that entropy is unstable volume growth weighted by how Lebesgue the measure is transverse to $E^u$. Putting these together, the Ledrappier-Young formula $h_\mu = \sum_i\gamma_i\lambda_i$ generalises and completes the picture: Pesin's equality is the full-dimension ($\gamma_i = d_i$) case, the Margulis-Ruelle inequality is the bound $\gamma_i \leq d_i$, and SRB measures are dual to volume in the sense that they are precisely the invariant measures whose conditionals along $E^u$ are Lebesgue. The bridge from Oseledets to physics is that this single absolute-continuity condition is what makes a measure the one a computer simulation samples, so the entropy formula is exactly the identity relating the disorder you observe to the stretching you can compute.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (the Pesin sets exhaust full measure and carry a continuous splitting).** With $\varepsilon$ fixed, $\mu(\bigcup_{\ell\geq 1}\Lambda_\ell) = 1$, each $\Lambda_\ell$ is precompact, and $x \mapsto (E^s(x), E^u(x))$ is uniformly continuous on $\Lambda_\ell$.

*Proof.* By Oseledets [38.07.01], for $\mu$-a.e. $x$ the regularity estimates $\|Df^n|_{E^s}\| \leq C(x)e^{(\lambda^s+\varepsilon)n}$, $\|Df^{-n}|_{E^u}\| \leq C(x)e^{-(\lambda^u-\varepsilon)n}$ and $\angle(E^s(x),E^u(x)) \geq c(x) > 0$ hold with finite measurable $C(x)$ and positive $c(x)$. Set $\Lambda_\ell = \{x : C(x) \leq \ell,\ c(x) \geq 1/\ell\}$ intersected with the regular set. Since $C, c$ are finite/positive a.e., the $\Lambda_\ell$ increase to a full-measure set: $\mu(\bigcup_\ell\Lambda_\ell) = 1$. On $\Lambda_\ell$ the bounds are uniform, so the maps $x\mapsto E^{s/u}(x)$ are uniformly continuous — a sequence $x_k \to x$ in $\Lambda_\ell$ has $E^{s/u}(x_k)$ converging, because the uniform estimates force the spaces $\{v : \|Df^n v\|$ controlled$\}$ to vary continuously; passing to the closure keeps the bounds (non-strict inequalities) and yields compactness. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (tempering of the manifold radius).** The radius $r(x)$ of $W^s_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)$ may be chosen $\varepsilon$-tempered: $e^{-\varepsilon} \leq r(fx)/r(x) \leq e^{\varepsilon}$.

*Proof.* The graph-transform construction produces a radius $r_0(x)$ comparable to a negative power of the local hyperbolicity constant $C(x)$ from Proposition 1, so $r_0(x) \geq \kappa\, C(x)^{-p}$ for constants $\kappa, p > 0$. Since $\frac1n\log C(f^n x) \to 0$ (regularity), $\frac1n\log r_0(f^n x) \to 0$ as well. Apply the tempering lemma (Exercise 4) to $D(x) = 1/r_0(x)$: there is an $\varepsilon$-tempered $\widetilde C \geq 1/r_0$, and $r(x) := 1/\widetilde C(x) \leq r_0(x)$ is a valid (smaller) radius that is $\varepsilon$-tempered. The local stable manifold of radius $r(x)$ still satisfies the defining exponential-convergence estimate because shrinking the radius only strengthens it. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Margulis-Ruelle as a volume-growth bound, sharp form).** For $\mu$-a.e. $x$, $\sum_i\lambda_i^+(x) = \lim_n\frac1n\log\big|\det\big(D_x f^n|_{E^u(x)}\big)\big|$, and consequently $\int\sum_i\lambda_i^+\,d\mu = \int\log|\det Df|_{E^u}|\,d\mu$.

*Proof.* On $E^u(x) = \bigoplus_{\lambda_i>0}E_i(x)$ the restricted cocycle $D f|_{E^u}$ has exactly the positive exponents $\lambda_i$ with multiplicities $d_i$. By the trace identity for the Oseledets splitting [38.07.01] applied to the sub-cocycle on $E^u$ (which is invariant, $Df(E^u(x)) = E^u(fx)$), $\lim_n\frac1n\log|\det(D_xf^n|_{E^u})| = \sum_{\lambda_i>0}d_i\lambda_i = \sum_i\lambda_i^+$ pointwise a.e. The function $g(x) = \log|\det(D_xf|_{E^u(x)})|$ is integrable (bounded by $d\log^+\|Df\|$) and $\log|\det(D_xf^n|_{E^u})| = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1}g(f^kx)$ telescopes by the cocycle property of the determinant, so Birkhoff's theorem [38.06.02] gives $\frac1n\log|\det(D_xf^n|_{E^u})| \to \int g\,d\mu$ a.e.; equating the two limits and integrating yields $\int\sum_i\lambda_i^+\,d\mu = \int g\,d\mu = \int\log|\det Df|_{E^u}|\,d\mu$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (rigidity: equality forces SRB).** If $f$ is $C^2$ and $h_\mu(f) = \int\sum_i\lambda_i^+\,d\mu$, then $\mu$ is an SRB measure.

*Proof (structure).* Build the increasing partition $\xi$ subordinate to the unstable foliation: each atom $\xi(x)$ is an open neighborhood of $x$ inside $W^u_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)$, with $f^{-1}\xi \succeq \xi$ (refinement under the inverse). The conditional entropy $H_\mu(f^{-1}\xi \mid \xi) = h_\mu(f)$ for such a partition (a theorem of Rokhlin-Ledrappier identifying entropy with the unstable conditional entropy). On the other hand, the transverse Jacobian of $f$ along $\xi$ is $|\det Df|_{E^u}|$, so the conditional entropy is bounded by $\int\log|\det Df|_{E^u}|\,d\mu$ with equality iff the conditional measures $\mu^\xi_x$ along the unstable atoms are equivalent to leaf-Lebesgue. Given the hypothesis $h_\mu = \int\sum\lambda_i^+ = \int\log|\det Df|_{E^u}|$ (Proposition 3), equality is forced, so $\mu^\xi_x \ll \mathrm{Leb}_{W^u}$: the conditionals on unstable manifolds are absolutely continuous, which is the definition of an SRB measure. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The Oseledets multiplicative ergodic theorem [38.07.01] is the linear engine on which all of Pesin theory is mounted: the splitting $T_xM = E^s\oplus E^u$, the exponents, the tempered constants, and the volume-growth identity $\sum_i\lambda_i^+ = \lim\frac1n\log|\det Df^n|_{E^u}|$ are all Oseledets output, and the tempering lemma is what converts the subexponential angle decay of the Oseledets splitting into the slowly-varying Pesin constants. Pesin theory is the nonlinear, geometric integral of the linear Oseledets datum.

- Kolmogorov-Sinai entropy and the generator theorem [38.06.02] supply the left-hand side of both the Margulis-Ruelle inequality and Pesin's formula: $h_\mu(f)$ is the KS entropy, the partition-refinement machinery and conditional-entropy chain rule used in the Margulis-Ruelle proof are exactly the tools built there, and the Rokhlin-Ledrappier identification of entropy with unstable conditional entropy in the rigidity argument is the entropy theory specialised to the unstable foliation.

- The uniform theory of hyperbolic sets and Anosov systems [38.03.01] is the special case Pesin theory generalises: on a Pesin block the splitting is uniformly hyperbolic with constant $\ell$, the stable-manifold theorem is the Hadamard-Perron graph transform from the Anosov setting run with tempered constants, and for an Anosov diffeomorphism preserving volume the SRB measure is volume itself, so Pesin's formula reduces to the Anosov entropy computation $h = \int\log|\det Df|_{E^u}|$.

- The dimension theory and thermodynamic formalism of dynamical systems [38.06.01] consume Pesin's output through the Ledrappier-Young formula $h_\mu = \sum_i\gamma_i\lambda_i$, which expresses entropy as a dimension-weighted sum of exponents and links the entropy formula to the Hausdorff dimension of invariant measures and to the variational principle for equilibrium states.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Yakov Pesin announced the theory in a 1976 *Doklady* note [ref: TODO_REF Pesin 1976] and developed it fully in his 1977 *Russian Mathematical Surveys* memoir [ref: TODO_REF Pesin 1977], which constructed stable and unstable manifolds for smooth measure-preserving systems wherever the Lyapunov exponents are nonzero and proved that for measures absolutely continuous with respect to volume the metric entropy equals the integrated sum of positive exponents. The upper bound — entropy at most the sum of positive exponents — was isolated by David Ruelle in 1978 [ref: TODO_REF Ruelle 1978], who attributed the geodesic-flow case to an unpublished argument of Grigory Margulis [ref: TODO_REF Margulis (cf. Ruelle 1978)], giving the inequality the name Margulis-Ruelle.

The equality case was clarified through the 1980s. Sinai's 1972 construction of Gibbs measures for Anosov systems [ref: TODO_REF Sinai 1972], extended to Axiom A by Ruelle and Bowen, identified the special invariant measures — later called SRB measures — that govern the statistics of volume-typical orbits. François Ledrappier and Lai-Sang Young proved in their two-part 1985 *Annals* paper [ref: TODO_REF Ledrappier-Young 1985] that entropy is the dimension-weighted sum $\sum_i\gamma_i\lambda_i$ of positive exponents, with Pesin's equality recovered exactly when the transverse dimensions $\gamma_i$ are full, so that the entropy formula and the absolute continuity of the unstable foliation are two faces of one condition. The numerical and physical reach of the theory followed from the basin property: SRB measures are the ones a generic initial condition samples, so the entropy formula connects an abstract ergodic invariant to the observable statistics of a chaotic attractor.
