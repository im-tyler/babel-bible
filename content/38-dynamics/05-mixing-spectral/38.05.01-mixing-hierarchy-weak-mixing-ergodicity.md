## Intuition [Beginner]

Ergodicity, from the previous chapter, says a single orbit eventually visits every region in correct proportion — but it says nothing about *how* the visits are spread out in time. A rule can be ergodic and still march in lockstep: rotate a circle by a fixed irrational step and the orbit fills the circle evenly, yet two nearby starting points stay exactly the same distance apart forever. The rule rearranges the circle but never scrambles it. *Mixing* is the stronger demand that the rule genuinely scrambles — that after enough steps the system forgets where it started.

Here is the everyday picture. Pour a drop of cream into coffee and stir. At first the cream is a tight blob; after a few stirs it streaks; after many stirs it is spread so uniformly that every spoonful of coffee holds the same tiny fraction of cream, no matter which spoonful you take. *Mixing* is exactly this: take any region $A$ and any region $B$; follow $A$ forward many steps; the fraction of the moved-$A$ that now sits inside $B$ approaches the size of $A$ times the size of $B$ — as if $A$ had been smeared evenly across the whole space and simply meets $B$ in proportion to $B$'s size.

Between "fills evenly" (ergodic) and "scrambles completely" (mixing) sits a middle condition called *weak mixing*: the system scrambles eventually, but is allowed rare bad moments when it briefly lines up again — as long as those bad moments are vanishingly sparse in the long run. So three conditions stack up: mixing is the strongest, weak mixing is in the middle, and ergodicity is the weakest. Each is strictly stronger than the next: there are systems that are ergodic but not weakly mixing, and systems that are weakly mixing but not mixing.

The takeaway: ergodicity guarantees even visiting; mixing guarantees the system *forgets its starting point*, so that distant-in-time observations become statistically independent; weak mixing is the in-between condition that forgets the past except for a negligibly thin set of exceptional times. This three-step ladder — mixing, then weak mixing, then ergodic — is the *mixing hierarchy*, and it is the backbone of how we measure how thoroughly a dynamical rule disorders the space.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We compare a rotation (ergodic, not mixing) with the doubling-style scrambling on a small finite model, watching whether overlaps settle.

Step 1. A non-mixing rule. Take the circle as the numbers from $0$ up to $1$, wrapping. The rule adds $0.5$ and wraps. Starting blob $A$ is the arc from $0$ to $0.5$ (half the circle). Apply the rule: $A$ moves to the arc from $0.5$ to $1$. Apply again: it moves back to $0$ to $0.5$. So the moved blob alternates between the two halves forever — it never spreads out.

Step 2. Test the overlap. Let $B$ be the arc from $0$ to $0.5$. The size of $A$ is $0.5$ and the size of $B$ is $0.5$, so mixing would require the overlap fraction to settle near $0.5 \times 0.5 = 0.25$. But the actual overlap of moved-$A$ with $B$ is the full $0.5$ at even steps and $0$ at odd steps. It bounces between $0.5$ and $0$ and never approaches $0.25$. This rule is not mixing.

Step 3. A scrambling rule. Now use the doubling rule: replace $x$ by $2x$ and wrap. Take $A$ to be the small arc from $0$ to $0.25$ (size $0.25$). One step doubles it to the arc $0$ to $0.5$; the next step doubles that to the whole circle, but because of the wrap it arrives as two evenly spaced copies; after a few more steps the image is many thin evenly spaced strips covering the circle uniformly.

Step 4. Test the overlap again. Let $B$ be any arc of size $0.25$. Once the image of $A$ has become many thin evenly spaced strips, the fraction of those strips landing in $B$ is just the size of $B$, namely $0.25$, times the total size of $A$, namely $0.25$, giving overlap $0.25 \times 0.25 = 0.0625$ — and it stays there. The overlap settles to size-of-$A$ times size-of-$B$.

What this tells us: the rotation merely relocates a blob, so overlaps oscillate and never settle — ergodic but not mixing. The doubling rule shreds a blob into evenly spread strips, so overlaps settle to the product of the two sizes — that settling *is* mixing. The difference between relocating and shredding is the whole content of the mixing hierarchy.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $(X, \mathcal{B}, \mu, T)$ is a measure-preserving system on a probability space in the sense of [38.04.01], with Koopman operator $U_T f = f \circ T$ acting as an isometry of $L^2(\mu)$ [02.11.08]. Write $L^2_0(\mu) = \{f \in L^2(\mu) : \int f\,d\mu = 0\}$ for the orthocomplement of the constants. The *correlation* of $f, g \in L^2(\mu)$ at lag $n$ is $\langle U_T^n f, g\rangle = \int (f\circ T^n)\,\bar g\,d\mu$.

**Definition (strong mixing).** The system is *(strongly) mixing* if for all $A, B \in \mathcal{B}$,
$$\mu(T^{-n}A \cap B) \;\longrightarrow\; \mu(A)\,\mu(B) \qquad (n \to \infty).$$
Equivalently (by an $L^2$ approximation argument), $\langle U_T^n f, g\rangle \to \langle f, 1\rangle\langle 1, g\rangle = \big(\int f\,d\mu\big)\big(\overline{\int g\,d\mu}\big)$ for all $f, g \in L^2(\mu)$; on $L^2_0(\mu)$ this reads $\langle U_T^n f, g\rangle \to 0$ — the correlations decay to zero.

**Definition (weak mixing).** The system is *weakly mixing* if for all $A, B \in \mathcal{B}$,
$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\big|\mu(T^{-k}A \cap B) - \mu(A)\,\mu(B)\big| \;\longrightarrow\; 0 \qquad (n \to \infty).$$
The absolute value inside the Cesàro average distinguishes this from ergodicity, where (by [38.04.02], characterisation (4)) only the signed average $\frac1n\sum_k \mu(T^{-k}A\cap B) \to \mu(A)\mu(B)$ is required.

**Definition (eigenfunction; continuous spectrum).** A function $f \in L^2(\mu)$, $f \neq 0$, is an *eigenfunction* of $U_T$ with eigenvalue $\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$ if $U_T f = \lambda f$, i.e. $f \circ T = \lambda f$ a.e.; necessarily $|\lambda| = 1$ since $U_T$ is an isometry. The constants are always eigenfunctions with $\lambda = 1$. The system has *continuous spectrum on $L^2_0(\mu)$* if $U_T$ has no eigenfunction in $L^2_0(\mu)$ — the only eigenfunctions are the constants.

**The hierarchy.** The three conditions are nested:
$$\text{(strong) mixing} \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{weak mixing} \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{ergodic}.$$
The first implication is immediate: termwise convergence $a_k \to 0$ forces $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}|a_k| \to 0$ (Cesàro means of a null sequence vanish), applied to $a_k = \mu(T^{-k}A\cap B) - \mu(A)\mu(B)$. The second follows because the signed Cesàro average is dominated by the absolute one, recovering ergodicity characterisation (4). Both implications are strict (canonical examples below).

**Canonical examples.** (i) *Irrational rotation* $Tx = x+\alpha \bmod 1$: ergodic but **not** weakly mixing — $f(x) = e^{2\pi i x}$ is an eigenfunction, $U_T f = e^{2\pi i\alpha} f$, a nonconstant $L^2_0$ eigenfunction. (ii) *Doubling map* $Tx = 2x\bmod 1$ and every *Bernoulli shift*: mixing for the natural measure. (iii) *Hyperbolic toral automorphism* $T = A\bmod 1$, $A \in \mathrm{SL}_d(\mathbb{Z})$ with no eigenvalue on the unit circle (the *cat map* $\begin{psmallmatrix}2&1\\1&1\end{psmallmatrix}$ for $d=2$): mixing. (iv) *Chacón's map* and rank-one rigid constructions: weakly mixing but **not** mixing.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *Ergodic is not weakly mixing.* The signed Cesàro average vanishing (ergodicity) is genuinely weaker than the *absolute* Cesàro average vanishing (weak mixing). The irrational rotation has ergodic equidistribution but its correlations $\mu(T^{-n}A\cap B) - \mu(A)\mu(B)$ oscillate without their absolute averages decaying, because $e^{2\pi i x}$ is a surviving eigenfunction.

- *Weak mixing is not mixing.* Weak mixing only kills correlations along a *density-one* set of times; a thin set of exceptional times where correlations stay large is permitted. Rank-one constructions (Chacón) exploit rigidity — there is a sequence $n_j \to \infty$ with $U_T^{n_j} \to \mathrm{Id}$ strongly — so $\mu(T^{-n_j}A\cap A) \to \mu(A)$, not $\mu(A)^2$; mixing fails while weak mixing holds.

- *Mixing of all orders is a separate, stronger ladder.* The condition defined here is *mixing of order two*. Whether two-fold mixing implies higher-order mixing (Rokhlin's problem) is open in general; do not assume the term "mixing" includes all orders unless stated.

- *Eigenvalues lie on the unit circle, never inside.* Since $U_T$ is an isometry, $|\lambda| = 1$ for every eigenvalue; an apparent eigenvalue with $|\lambda| \neq 1$ signals a computational error. Weak mixing is the statement that $1$ is the *only* eigenvalue and it is simple.

- *Weak mixing is a property of $T$, not of $T \times T$ alone.* The clean characterisation is that $T$ is weakly mixing iff $T\times T$ is ergodic; but $T\times T$ ergodic is strictly stronger than $T$ ergodic, and it is exactly this gap that weak mixing fills. Checking ergodicity of $T$ does not detect weak mixing.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Koopman-von Neumann; the equivalence of weak-mixing characterisations).** For a measure-preserving system $(X,\mathcal{B},\mu,T)$ the following are equivalent:

1. $T$ is weakly mixing: $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}|\mu(T^{-k}A\cap B)-\mu(A)\mu(B)| \to 0$ for all $A,B$.
2. For all $f,g \in L^2(\mu)$, $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}|\langle U_T^k f, g\rangle - \langle f,1\rangle\langle 1,g\rangle| \to 0$.
3. $U_T$ has no eigenfunction in $L^2_0(\mu)$: the only eigenfunctions of $U_T$ are the constants (continuous spectrum on $L^2_0$).
4. $T\times T$ is ergodic on $(X\times X,\,\mu\times\mu)$.

The proof rests on the **Koopman-von Neumann lemma**: a bounded sequence $(a_k)_{k\geq0}$ of nonnegative reals satisfies $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k \to 0$ if and only if there is a set $J\subseteq\mathbb{N}$ of *density one* (meaning $\frac1n|J\cap[0,n)|\to1$) with $a_k \to 0$ as $k\to\infty$ along $k\in J$; and this holds iff $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k^2\to 0$ [ref: TODO_REF Koopman-von Neumann 1932].

*Proof.* We prove the lemma, then close the loop $(3)\Rightarrow(2)\Rightarrow(1)\Rightarrow(3)$ and $(3)\Leftrightarrow(4)$.

*The lemma.* Suppose $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k\to0$. For each integer $m\geq1$ the set $E_m=\{k:a_k>1/m\}$ has upper density zero, since $\frac1n|E_m\cap[0,n)|\leq m\cdot\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k\to0$ by Markov's inequality. Choose an increasing sequence $N_1<N_2<\cdots$ with $\frac1n|E_m\cap[0,n)|<1/m$ for all $n\geq N_m$, and set $J=\mathbb{N}\setminus\bigcup_m\big(E_m\cap[N_m,N_{m+1})\big)$. Then $J$ has density one, and for $k\in J$ with $k\geq N_m$ one has $a_k\leq1/m$, so $a_k\to0$ along $J$. Conversely, if $a_k\to0$ along a density-one set $J$ and $|a_k|\leq C$, then $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k\leq\frac{C}{n}|J^c\cap[0,n)|+\sup_{k\in J,k\geq M}a_k$ for any $M$, and both terms vanish in the limit. The equivalence with $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k^2\to0$ follows by applying the density-one criterion to both $a_k$ and $a_k^2$ (the same $J$ works since $a_k\to0$ along $J$ iff $a_k^2\to0$ along $J$, using boundedness).

$(3)\Rightarrow(2)$. Assume $U_T$ has no nonconstant eigenfunction. It suffices to treat $f,g\in L^2_0(\mu)$ (subtract the means; the constant parts contribute exactly $\langle f,1\rangle\langle1,g\rangle$). On $L^2_0$ we must show $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}|\langle U_T^k f,g\rangle|\to0$. The correlation sequence $c_k=\langle U_T^k f,f\rangle$ is positive-definite, so by the Herglotz-Bochner theorem there is a finite positive measure $\sigma_f$ (the *spectral measure* of $f$) on the circle $\mathbb{T}$ with $c_k=\int_{\mathbb{T}}z^k\,d\sigma_f(z)$. By the lemma it is enough to show $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}|c_k|^2\to0$. Compute
$$\frac1n\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}|c_k|^2=\frac1n\sum_{k<n}\int\!\!\int z^k\bar w^k\,d\sigma_f(z)\,d\sigma_f(w)=\int\!\!\int\Big(\frac1n\sum_{k<n}(z\bar w)^k\Big)\,d\sigma_f(z)\,d\sigma_f(w).$$
The inner average tends to $\mathbf{1}_{\{z=w\}}$ pointwise and is bounded by $1$, so by dominated convergence the limit is $\sigma_f\times\sigma_f(\{z=w\})=\sum_{z}\sigma_f(\{z\})^2$, the sum of squares of the atoms of $\sigma_f$. An atom of $\sigma_f$ at $z=\lambda$ corresponds to an eigenfunction of $U_T$ with eigenvalue $\lambda$ (the projection onto the $\lambda$-eigenspace is nonzero); since $f\in L^2_0$ and there are no nonconstant eigenfunctions, $\sigma_f$ is non-atomic and the limit is $0$. Polarisation extends $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}|c_k|^2\to0$ from $\langle U_T^kf,f\rangle$ to $\langle U_T^kf,g\rangle$, giving (2).

$(2)\Rightarrow(1)$ is the specialisation $f=\mathbf{1}_A$, $g=\mathbf{1}_B$, since $\langle U_T^k\mathbf{1}_A,\mathbf{1}_B\rangle=\mu(T^{-k}A\cap B)$ and $\langle\mathbf{1}_A,1\rangle\langle1,\mathbf{1}_B\rangle=\mu(A)\mu(B)$.

$(1)\Rightarrow(3)$. Suppose $U_Tf=\lambda f$ with $f\in L^2_0$, $f\neq0$; we derive a contradiction. The function $|f|^2$ then satisfies $|f|^2\circ T=|\lambda|^2|f|^2=|f|^2$, so $|f|^2$ is $T$-invariant; weak mixing implies ergodicity, so $|f|$ is a.e. a nonzero constant $c$. Normalise $c=1$. For $A=\{x:\arg f(x)\in I\}$ over an arc $I$, the correlation $\mu(T^{-k}A\cap A)$ is governed by $\langle U_T^k f,f\rangle=\lambda^k\|f\|^2=\lambda^k$, whose modulus is $1$ for every $k$; then $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}|\langle U_T^kf,f\rangle-0|=1\not\to0$, contradicting (2)$\Leftarrow$(1) applied to $f=g$. Hence no nonconstant eigenfunction exists.

$(3)\Leftrightarrow(4)$. The Koopman operator of $T\times T$ on $L^2(\mu\times\mu)$ is $U_T\otimes U_T$, with eigenvalues the products $\lambda\bar\mu$ of eigenvalues of $U_T$. $T\times T$ is ergodic iff $1$ is a simple eigenvalue of $U_T\otimes U_T$ (by [38.04.02], characterisation (3)). If $U_T$ has a nonconstant eigenfunction $f$ with eigenvalue $\lambda$, then $f(x)\overline{f(y)}$ is a nonconstant eigenfunction of $U_T\otimes U_T$ with eigenvalue $\lambda\bar\lambda=1$, so $T\times T$ is non-ergodic. Conversely, if $U_T$ has only constant eigenfunctions, the spectral measures on $L^2_0$ are non-atomic, the same atom-counting computation shows $1$ is simple for $U_T\otimes U_T$, and $T\times T$ is ergodic. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem builds toward the spectral viewpoint of [38.05.02], where the absence of eigenfunctions becomes the statement that the maximal spectral type of $U_T$ on $L^2_0$ is continuous, and it appears again in the Halmos-von Neumann classification, which is exactly the opposite extreme — *pure point* spectrum identifies the system with a group rotation. The foundational reason the equivalences hold is that a single object, the spectral measure $\sigma_f$ furnished by Herglotz-Bochner, encodes the whole correlation sequence, and weak mixing is precisely the vanishing of its atoms; this is exactly the Koopman-von Neumann dichotomy that a Cesàro-null nonnegative sequence is null along density one. The central insight is that weak mixing is the dynamical face of continuous spectrum, the way ergodicity is the dynamical face of a simple eigenvalue $1$ — so weak mixing generalises ergodicity from "the eigenvalue $1$ is simple" to "$1$ is the *only* eigenvalue." Putting these together, the $T\times T$ criterion is dual to the eigenfunction criterion, because an eigenfunction of $U_T$ and its conjugate manufacture an invariant function on the product, and the bridge is that self-joinings detect exactly the rigidity that a thin set of correlation-resonant times can hide.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the irrational rotation $Tx=x+\alpha\bmod1$ is ergodic but not weakly mixing by exhibiting a nonconstant $L^2_0$ eigenfunction of $U_T$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Try the character $f(x)=e^{2\pi i x}$ and compute $U_Tf$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Ergodicity for irrational $\alpha$ was proved in [38.04.02] via Fourier coefficients. For weak mixing, take $f(x)=e^{2\pi i x}\in L^2_0$ (it has integral $0$ and is nonconstant). Then $(U_Tf)(x)=f(x+\alpha)=e^{2\pi i(x+\alpha)}=e^{2\pi i\alpha}\,e^{2\pi i x}=e^{2\pi i\alpha}f(x)$, so $f$ is an eigenfunction with eigenvalue $\lambda=e^{2\pi i\alpha}$, of modulus $1$ and not equal to $1$ since $\alpha\notin\mathbb{Z}$. By characterisation (3) of the Koopman-von Neumann theorem, the existence of a nonconstant eigenfunction shows $T$ is not weakly mixing. Concretely $\langle U_T^kf,f\rangle=e^{2\pi ik\alpha}$ has modulus $1$ for all $k$, so $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}|\langle U_T^kf,f\rangle|=1\not\to0$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the Koopman-von Neumann lemma direction: if a bounded sequence $a_k\geq0$ satisfies $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k^2\to0$, then $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k\to0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Cauchy-Schwarz relates the average of $a_k$ to the average of $a_k^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality applied to the sequences $(a_k)$ and $(1)$ over $0\leq k<n$,
$$\frac1n\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}a_k\leq\Big(\frac1n\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}a_k^2\Big)^{1/2}\Big(\frac1n\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}1\Big)^{1/2}=\Big(\frac1n\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}a_k^2\Big)^{1/2}.$$
The right-hand side tends to $0$ by hypothesis, so $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k\to0$. (The reverse implication uses boundedness: $a_k\leq C$ gives $a_k^2\leq Ca_k$, hence $\frac1n\sum a_k^2\leq C\cdot\frac1n\sum a_k\to0$.) The two Cesàro conditions are therefore equivalent for bounded nonnegative sequences.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the doubling map $Tx=2x\bmod1$ is mixing for Lebesgue measure, by checking the correlation criterion on characters $f=e_m$, $g=e_\ell$ where $e_k(x)=e^{2\pi ikx}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$U_T e_m = e_{2m}$, so $U_T^n e_m = e_{2^n m}$. Use orthonormality $\langle e_j,e_\ell\rangle=\delta_{j\ell}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The characters $\{e_k\}_{k\in\mathbb{Z}}$ form an orthonormal basis of $L^2[0,1)$. Since $e_m\circ T=e_m(2x)=e^{2\pi i(2m)x}=e_{2m}$, we have $U_T^n e_m=e_{2^n m}$. For fixed nonzero $m$ and any $\ell$, $\langle U_T^n e_m,e_\ell\rangle=\langle e_{2^n m},e_\ell\rangle=\delta_{2^n m,\,\ell}$, which equals $1$ for at most one $n$ (as $2^n m$ is strictly increasing in $n$ for $m\neq0$) and is $0$ for all large $n$. Hence $\langle U_T^n e_m,e_\ell\rangle\to0=\big(\int e_m\big)\big(\overline{\int e_\ell}\big)$ when $m\neq0$ (the integral of $e_m$ vanishes), and for $m=0$, $e_0=1$ gives $\langle1,e_\ell\rangle=\delta_{0\ell}$ constant in $n$, matching $\int e_\ell$. By bilinearity and density of trigonometric polynomials, $\langle U_T^nf,g\rangle\to(\int f)(\overline{\int g})$ for all $f,g\in L^2$, so $T$ is mixing.

</details>

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that weak mixing implies ergodicity directly from the correlation definitions.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Bound the signed Cesàro average by the absolute Cesàro average, then invoke [38.04.02] characterisation (4).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $a_k=\mu(T^{-k}A\cap B)-\mu(A)\mu(B)$. Weak mixing gives $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}|a_k|\to0$. Since $\big|\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k\big|\leq\frac1n\sum_{k<n}|a_k|$, the signed average also tends to $0$, i.e. $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}\mu(T^{-k}A\cap B)\to\mu(A)\mu(B)$ for all $A,B$. This is exactly characterisation (4) of ergodicity from [38.04.02], so $T$ is ergodic. The implication is strict because the signed average can vanish (rotation) while the absolute average does not.

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that $T$ is weakly mixing iff $T\times T$ is ergodic, assuming the eigenfunction characterisation (3) of weak mixing.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Koopman operator of $T\times T$ is $U_T\otimes U_T$; relate its eigenfunctions to those of $U_T$, and use that $T\times T$ ergodic iff $1$ is a simple eigenvalue of $U_T\otimes U_T$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $T$ is weakly mixing, so $U_T$ has only constant eigenfunctions and every spectral measure $\sigma_f$ on $L^2_0(\mu)$ is non-atomic. Let $F\in L^2(\mu\times\mu)$ be $T\times T$-invariant: $(U_T\otimes U_T)F=F$. Decompose $F$ into the orthogonal pieces $1\otimes1$, $L^2_0\otimes1$, $1\otimes L^2_0$, $L^2_0\otimes L^2_0$. The mixed-constant pieces are invariant functions of one variable, hence constant by ergodicity of $T$ (so they live in $1\otimes1$). On $L^2_0\otimes L^2_0$, the invariance forces a vector fixed by $U_T\otimes U_T$; but for non-atomic $\sigma_f$ the operator $U_T\otimes U_T$ has no nonzero invariant vector there (the joint spectral measure $\sigma_f\times\sigma_g$ gives no mass to the diagonal antipode $\{z\bar w=1\}$). Hence $F$ is constant and $T\times T$ is ergodic. ($\Leftarrow$) If $T$ is not weakly mixing, take a nonconstant eigenfunction $U_Tf=\lambda f$. Then $F(x,y)=f(x)\overline{f(y)}$ satisfies $(U_T\otimes U_T)F=\lambda\bar\lambda F=F$ and is nonconstant, so $T\times T$ is not ergodic. Contrapositively, $T\times T$ ergodic implies $T$ weakly mixing.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (the strict hierarchy).** For measure-preserving systems, (strong) mixing $\Rightarrow$ weak mixing $\Rightarrow$ ergodic, and both implications are strict. Strictness of the first is witnessed by rank-one *rigid* systems (Chacón's transformation): there is $n_j\to\infty$ with $U_T^{n_j}\to\mathrm{Id}$ strongly, so $\mu(T^{-n_j}A\cap A)\to\mu(A)\not=\mu(A)^2$, defeating mixing, while the system is weakly mixing. Strictness of the second is witnessed by the irrational rotation, ergodic with the nonconstant eigenfunction $e^{2\pi ix}$ obstructing weak mixing [ref: TODO_REF Halmos 1956].

**Theorem 2 (spectral characterisation of the hierarchy).** Let $\sigma$ denote the maximal spectral type of $U_T$ on $L^2_0(\mu)$. Then: $T$ is ergodic iff $U_T$ restricted to $L^2_0$ has no eigenvalue $1$ (equivalently $1$ is a simple eigenvalue of $U_T$ on $L^2$); $T$ is weakly mixing iff $\sigma$ is *continuous* (no atoms) on $L^2_0$, i.e. $U_T$ has no eigenfunction off the constants; $T$ is mixing iff $\sigma$ is a *Rajchman* measure, $\hat\sigma(n)=\int_{\mathbb{T}}z^n\,d\sigma(z)\to0$ as $|n|\to\infty$. Since every Rajchman measure is continuous but not conversely (there exist continuous measures, e.g. certain Riesz products, with non-vanishing Fourier coefficients along a subsequence), the spectral picture reproduces the strict hierarchy at the level of the spectral measure [ref: TODO_REF Cornfeld-Fomin-Sinai 1982].

**Theorem 3 (mixing of algebraic and Bernoulli systems).** Bernoulli shifts $(p^{\otimes\mathbb{Z}},\text{shift})$ are mixing of all orders and are the strongest models in the hierarchy (Kolmogorov / K-systems sit just below Bernoulli). A toral automorphism $A\in\mathrm{SL}_d(\mathbb{Z})$ is mixing for Haar measure iff it is ergodic iff no eigenvalue of $A$ is a root of unity; the *hyperbolic* ones (no eigenvalue on the unit circle, the cat map being the $2\times2$ prototype) are Bernoulli by the Adler-Weiss and Sinai theory of Markov partitions, hence mixing of all orders. The character computation $U_T\chi_m=\chi_{A^\top m}$ exhibits the mixing as the escape of every nonzero frequency to infinity under $A^\top$ [ref: TODO_REF Walters 1982].

**Theorem 4 (genericity: weak mixing is generic, mixing is meagre; Rokhlin, Halmos).** In the group of invertible measure-preserving transformations of a Lebesgue probability space, equipped with the weak topology, the weakly mixing transformations form a dense $G_\delta$ (residual) set, while the mixing transformations form a meagre (first-category) set and the ergodic transformations a dense $G_\delta$. Thus the "typical" measure-preserving transformation is weakly mixing but not mixing — the middle rung of the hierarchy is the generic one. The proof uses the Kakutani-Rokhlin tower approximation of [38.04.01] to perturb any transformation into one with prescribed weak-mixing behaviour while showing the mixing condition is a countable intersection of nowhere-dense conditions [ref: TODO_REF Rokhlin 1948].

**Theorem 5 (weak mixing as disjointness from rotations; the Koopman-von Neumann dichotomy).** A system is weakly mixing iff it is *disjoint* (in Furstenberg's sense) from every ergodic rotation, equivalently iff it has no nontrivial factor with discrete spectrum. The Koopman-von Neumann decomposition splits $L^2_0(\mu)=H_{\mathrm{pp}}\oplus H_{\mathrm{c}}$ into the closed span of eigenfunctions (the *Kronecker factor*, a group rotation by Halmos-von Neumann) and its complement, on which the action is weakly mixing relative to the factor; weak mixing of the whole system is precisely the vanishing of the Kronecker factor. This relative decomposition is the first step of the Furstenberg structure theorem underlying the ergodic-theoretic proof of Szemerédi's theorem [ref: TODO_REF Koopman-von Neumann 1932].

**Synthesis.** The five results are one statement read at successive depths, and the foundational reason they cohere is that the spectral measure of the Koopman operator on $L^2_0$ is a single invariant whose increasing regularity — having the eigenvalue $1$ absent, then being continuous, then being Rajchman — is exactly the ascent through ergodic, weakly mixing, and mixing. This is the central insight that the dynamical hierarchy and the spectral hierarchy are the same hierarchy: an atom of $\sigma$ at $\lambda$ is an eigenfunction, a continuous $\sigma$ is the death of all eigenfunctions, and a Rajchman $\sigma$ is the termwise decay of correlations. Weak mixing is dual to discrete spectrum — the Kronecker factor of Theorem 5 is the maximal rotation quotient, and weak mixing is exactly its collapse — so the generic-transformation result is the foundational reason the subject is built around weak mixing rather than mixing: putting these together, the typical system has continuous but non-Rajchman spectrum. This is exactly the Koopman-von Neumann dichotomy made structural, and it generalises from a single transformation to the relative theory over a factor, where weak mixing becomes weak mixing *relative to* the Kronecker factor and the bridge is Furstenberg's tower of such extensions, which carries the whole apparatus forward into additive combinatorics and the spectral theory of [38.05.02].

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (strong mixing implies weak mixing implies ergodic).** For any measure-preserving system, mixing $\Rightarrow$ weak mixing $\Rightarrow$ ergodic.

*Proof.* Set $a_k(A,B)=\mu(T^{-k}A\cap B)-\mu(A)\mu(B)$. If $T$ is mixing then $a_k\to0$, so $|a_k|\to0$ and its Cesàro means vanish, giving weak mixing. If $T$ is weakly mixing then $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}|a_k|\to0$, hence $\big|\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k\big|\to0$, which is characterisation (4) of ergodicity from [38.04.02]. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Koopman-von Neumann lemma).** A bounded sequence $a_k\geq0$ has $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k\to0$ iff there is $J\subseteq\mathbb{N}$ of density one with $a_k\to0$ along $J$, iff $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k^2\to0$.

*Proof.* If the Cesàro mean vanishes, the sets $E_m=\{a_k>1/m\}$ have density zero by Markov's inequality $\frac1n|E_m\cap[0,n)|\leq m\,\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k$; a diagonal choice of thresholds $N_m$ assembles a density-one $J$ with $a_k\to0$ along $J$. Conversely a density-one $J$ with $a_k\to0$ along it forces $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}a_k\leq\frac{C}{n}|J^c\cap[0,n)|+\sup_{k\in J,k\geq M}a_k\to0$. The square equivalence is Cauchy-Schwarz one way ($\frac1n\sum a_k\leq(\frac1n\sum a_k^2)^{1/2}$) and $a_k^2\leq Ca_k$ the other. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (weak mixing $\Leftrightarrow$ no nonconstant eigenfunction).** $T$ is weakly mixing iff $U_T$ has no eigenfunction in $L^2_0(\mu)$.

*Proof.* ($\Leftarrow$) For $f\in L^2_0$ the spectral measure $\sigma_f$ (Herglotz-Bochner) satisfies $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}|\langle U_T^kf,f\rangle|^2\to\sum_z\sigma_f(\{z\})^2$. No eigenfunction means $\sigma_f$ non-atomic, so the limit is $0$; the Koopman-von Neumann lemma upgrades this to $\frac1n\sum_{k<n}|\langle U_T^kf,f\rangle|\to0$, and polarisation gives the same for $\langle U_T^kf,g\rangle$, hence weak mixing. ($\Rightarrow$) An eigenfunction $U_Tf=\lambda f$ in $L^2_0$ gives $|\langle U_T^kf,f\rangle|=|\lambda^k|\,\|f\|^2=\|f\|^2$ constant, so the absolute Cesàro average is bounded below by $\|f\|^2>0$, contradicting weak mixing. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (weak mixing $\Leftrightarrow$ $T\times T$ ergodic).** $T$ is weakly mixing iff $T\times T$ is ergodic on $(\mu\times\mu)$.

*Proof.* The product Koopman operator is $U_T\otimes U_T$ and $T\times T$ is ergodic iff its only invariant $L^2$ functions are constants. If $U_Tf=\lambda f$ with $f$ nonconstant in $L^2_0$, then $f\otimes\bar f$ is a nonconstant $U_T\otimes U_T$-invariant function, so $T\times T$ is non-ergodic. Conversely, if $U_T$ has no nonconstant eigenfunction, decompose any product-invariant $F$ along $1\otimes1$, $L^2_0\otimes1$, $1\otimes L^2_0$, $L^2_0\otimes L^2_0$; ergodicity of $T$ kills the mixed-constant parts, and on $L^2_0\otimes L^2_0$ the joint spectral measure $\sigma_f\times\sigma_g$ has no mass on $\{z\bar w=1\}$ (both factors non-atomic), so no nonzero invariant vector survives. Thus $F$ is constant and $T\times T$ is ergodic. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (Bernoulli and hyperbolic toral systems are mixing).** Bernoulli shifts and hyperbolic toral automorphisms are mixing.

*Proof.* For the Bernoulli shift on $X=\{0,\dots,r-1\}^{\mathbb{Z}}$ with product measure, cylinder sets $A,B$ depending on coordinates in $[-M,M]$ become independent under the shift once $n>2M$: $\mu(T^{-n}A\cap B)=\mu(A)\mu(B)$ exactly for $n>2M$, since the supports of $T^{-n}A$ and $B$ then occupy disjoint coordinate blocks and the measure is a product. Cylinders are dense in $L^2$, so mixing follows for all $A,B$. For the hyperbolic toral automorphism, on characters $U_T^n\chi_m=\chi_{(A^\top)^n m}$ and hyperbolicity sends $\|(A^\top)^n m\|\to\infty$ for every nonzero $m$, so $\langle U_T^n\chi_m,\chi_\ell\rangle=\delta_{(A^\top)^n m,\ell}=0$ for large $n$; density of trigonometric polynomials extends correlation decay to all of $L^2$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- Ergodicity, unique ergodicity, and equidistribution [38.04.02] is the floor of this hierarchy: ergodicity is the demand that the invariant $\sigma$-algebra be degenerate, equivalently that $1$ be a simple eigenvalue of $U_T$, and weak mixing strengthens this from "the eigenvalue $1$ is simple" to "$1$ is the only eigenvalue." The signed-Cesàro correlation criterion (4) for ergodicity proved there is exactly what the absolute-Cesàro criterion here sharpens, and the irrational rotation that is uniquely ergodic there is the canonical ergodic-not-weak-mixing example here.

- The ergodic theorems of Birkhoff, von Neumann, and Kingman [37.02.03] supply the analytic backbone: von Neumann's mean ergodic theorem is the $L^2$ projection onto the eigenvalue-$1$ eigenspace, and the Koopman-von Neumann lemma used here is the weak-mixing refinement that controls Cesàro averages of correlations along density-one sets. The pointwise theory underlies the empirical-measure arguments that detect rigidity in the weak-but-not-strong-mixing examples.

- Hilbert space and the spectral theorem [02.11.08] provide the operator-theoretic stage: the Koopman operator is an isometry (unitary in the invertible case) of $L^2(\mu)$, its eigenfunctions are the unit-modulus point spectrum, and the Herglotz-Bochner representation of the positive-definite correlation sequence furnishes the spectral measure $\sigma_f$ whose atoms-versus-continuity dichotomy is the whole content of the weak-mixing characterisation.

- The spectral theory of dynamical systems [38.05.02] is the direct successor: it develops the maximal spectral type, multiplicity, and the Halmos-von Neumann classification of discrete-spectrum systems as group rotations, recasting the mixing hierarchy proved here as the regularity ladder (point, continuous, Rajchman) of the spectral measure, and isolating the Kronecker factor whose vanishing is weak mixing.

- Hyperbolic sets and Anosov systems [38.03.01] supply the geometric source of mixing: hyperbolic toral automorphisms and Anosov diffeomorphisms are mixing (indeed Bernoulli) for their natural smooth invariant measures, the exponential expansion-contraction producing the correlation decay, and Markov partitions code them as Bernoulli shifts, tying the smooth and symbolic faces of mixing together.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The mixing condition entered ergodic theory through statistical mechanics and the work of Eberhard Hopf, whose 1937 *Ergodentheorie* [ref: TODO_REF Hopf 1937] established mixing for the geodesic flow on surfaces of negative curvature by the argument now called Hopf's method; mixing formalised Gibbs's heuristic that a stirred fluid forgets its initial coarse-grained state. The spectral viewpoint was opened by Bernard Koopman's 1931 observation that composition with a measure-preserving map is a unitary operator, and decisively by the joint 1932 *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* note of Koopman and von Neumann [ref: TODO_REF Koopman-von Neumann 1932], which introduced continuous spectrum as the operator-theoretic signature of thorough mixing and proved the density-one averaging lemma that bears their names. The recognition that weak mixing — continuous spectrum off the constants — is the precise dividing line between rotation-like (discrete spectrum) and genuinely scrambling behaviour crystallised in this circle.

The strictness of the hierarchy and the surprising genericity of its middle rung were settled in the 1940s and 1950s. Rokhlin's 1948 note [ref: TODO_REF Rokhlin 1948], titled to announce that a *general* measure-preserving transformation is not mixing, showed via the tower approximation that mixing is exceptional, and Halmos's 1956 *Lectures on Ergodic Theory* [ref: TODO_REF Halmos 1956] assembled the category-theoretic picture in which weak mixing and ergodicity are residual while mixing is meagre. Explicit weakly-but-not-strongly mixing systems were harder to exhibit by hand; Chacón's 1969 rank-one construction gave a concrete example, and the rigidity phenomenon it exploits — recurrence of the Koopman powers to the identity along a sparse sequence — became a central theme. The relative form of weak mixing, splitting off the Kronecker factor, was the structural innovation that Furstenberg turned into the ergodic-theoretic proof of Szemerédi's theorem in 1977, carrying the Koopman-von Neumann dichotomy from a classification tool into a generative principle of additive combinatorics.
