## Intuition [Beginner]

In electrostatics, the electric field is created by charges. Faraday discovered something deeper: a **changing** magnetic field creates an electric field too — even when no charges are present at all. This is **electromagnetic induction**.

The key quantity is **magnetic flux**: how much magnetic field passes through a loop. Think of it as the total number of magnetic field lines threading the loop. If that number changes — because the field gets stronger or weaker, or because the loop moves, or because the loop rotates — an **induced EMF** (voltage) appears around the loop.

Faraday's law: the induced EMF equals the rate at which the magnetic flux through the loop changes. **Lenz's law** adds a minus sign: the induced current flows in the direction that *opposes* the change in flux. If flux is increasing, the induced current creates a field that tries to decrease it. If flux is decreasing, the induced current tries to increase it. Nature resists change — and this resistance is what conserves energy.

Examples: push a bar magnet through a coil and a current flows. Change the current in one wire and a nearby wire feels a voltage. Spin a coil in a magnetic field and you get an alternating voltage — this is how generators work. Run AC through a primary coil wrapped around an iron core and a secondary coil picks up voltage — this is how transformers work.

The right-hand rule for induced current: curl your fingers around the loop in the direction of the induced current, and your thumb points in the direction of the magnetic field the induced current *creates*. Lenz's law says this field opposes whatever change is happening to the original flux.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A circular loop of wire with radius $r = 0.1$ m sits in a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ perpendicular to the loop. The field strength increases at a steady rate $dB/dt = 0.5$ T/s. Compute the induced EMF and the current if the loop has resistance $R = 2.0\,\Omega$.

**Step 1. Magnetic flux through the loop.** The field is uniform and perpendicular, so the flux equals the field strength times the area of the loop. The area of a circle of radius $r$ is $\pi r^2$:

$$
\Phi_B \;=\; B \cdot \pi r^2.
$$

**Step 2. Rate of change of flux.** The radius is fixed (the loop does not move or change size), so only $B$ is changing:

$$
\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} \;=\; \pi r^2 \,\frac{dB}{dt}.
$$

**Step 3. Induced EMF.** By Faraday's law (with Lenz's minus sign), the induced EMF has magnitude:

$$
\mathcal{E} \;=\; \pi r^2 \,\frac{dB}{dt} \;=\; \pi\,(0.1)^2\,(0.5) \;=\; \pi \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.5 \;=\; 0.005\pi \;\approx\; 0.016\;\text{V}.
$$

**Step 4. Current.** The induced current is $I = \mathcal{E}/R = 0.016/2.0 = 0.008$ A $= 8.0$ mA.

The induced EMF is about 16 millivolts, driving a current of 8 mA through the loop. By Lenz's law, the induced current flows in the direction that creates a magnetic field opposing the increase — so the induced field points opposite to $\mathbf{B}$.

### Second worked example: sliding rail generator

A conducting rod of length $\ell = 0.5$ m slides at constant speed $v = 2.0$ m/s along two frictionless parallel rails in a uniform magnetic field $B = 0.4$ T perpendicular to the plane of the rails. The rails are connected by a resistor $R = 5.0\,\Omega$ at one end.

Step 1. Compute the motional EMF. As the rod moves, the free charges inside experience the magnetic force $q\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}$, which drives current along the rod. The motional EMF is $\mathcal{E} = B\ell v = 0.4 \times 0.5 \times 2.0 = 0.4$ V.

Step 2. Compute the current: $I = \mathcal{E}/R = 0.4/5.0 = 0.08$ A.

Step 3. Compute the power dissipated: $P = I^2 R = (0.08)^2 \times 5.0 = 0.032$ W.

Step 4. What external force is needed to maintain the rod at constant speed? By Lenz's law, the induced current creates a magnetic force opposing the motion: $F_{\rm mag} = BI\ell = 0.4 \times 0.08 \times 0.5 = 0.016$ N. An external force of 0.016 N must be applied in the direction of motion to maintain constant speed. The power delivered by this force is $Fv = 0.016 \times 2.0 = 0.032$ W, exactly equal to the power dissipated in the resistor. Energy is conserved: mechanical work is converted to electrical energy and then to heat.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $S$ be an oriented surface with boundary curve $\mathcal{C} = \partial S$, and let $\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}, t)$ be a magnetic field. The **magnetic flux** through $S$ is

$$
\Phi_B \;:=\; \int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{a}.
$$

**Faraday's law** (integral form) states that the EMF around the boundary curve equals the negative rate of change of flux:

$$
\boxed{\;\;\mathcal{E} \;:=\; \oint_{\mathcal{C}} \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} \;=\; -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} \;=\; -\frac{d}{dt}\int_S \mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{a}.\;\;}
$$

The minus sign is **Lenz's law**: the induced EMF drives current in the direction that opposes the change in flux.

When the curve $\mathcal{C}$ is stationary (not moving or deforming), the time derivative passes through the integral, and applying Stokes' theorem to the left-hand side yields the **differential form**:

$$
\boxed{\;\;\nabla \times \mathbf{E} \;=\; -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}.\;\;}
$$

This is the third Maxwell equation. It says: a time-varying magnetic field is a source of curl for the electric field. In electrostatics, $\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t = 0$ and $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = 0$, recovering the conservative electric field. In electrodynamics, the electric field is no longer conservative — its curl is proportional to how fast $\mathbf{B}$ is changing.

**Motional EMF.** When the loop moves through a non-uniform field, or a conductor moves with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ through $\mathbf{B}$, the free charges inside the conductor experience a magnetic force $q\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}$ that acts as an effective electric field $\mathbf{E}_m = \mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}$. The motional EMF around a moving loop $\mathcal{C}(t)$ is

$$
\mathcal{E}_{\text{mot}} \;=\; \oint_{\mathcal{C}(t)} (\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B})\cdot d\mathbf{l}.
$$

The flux rule (Faraday's law with the total time derivative of $\Phi_B$) accounts for both the changing-field and moving-loop contributions. The combined result is

$$
\mathcal{E} \;=\; -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} \;=\; -\int_S \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\cdot d\mathbf{a} \;+\; \oint_{\mathcal{C}} (\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B})\cdot d\mathbf{l},
$$

where the first term is the **transformer EMF** (changing field, stationary loop) and the second is the **motional EMF** (moving loop in static field). The universal flux rule $\mathcal{E} = -d\Phi_B/dt$ subsumes both mechanisms into a single expression.

**Displacement current (Maxwell's correction to Ampere's law).** Ampere's law in magnetostatics reads $\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}$. Taking the divergence of both sides gives $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{B}) = 0 = \mu_0\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J}$, which contradicts charge conservation $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J} = -\partial\rho/\partial t$ when charges are moving. Maxwell's correction adds the **displacement current** $\epsilon_0\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t$:

$$
\boxed{\;\;\nabla \times \mathbf{B} \;=\; \mu_0 \mathbf{J} \;+\; \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}.\;\;}
$$

The divergence of both sides now gives $0 = \mu_0(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J} + \partial\rho/\partial t)$, using Gauss's law $\epsilon_0\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = \rho$. This is the continuity equation — charge conservation is built in.

**The complete Maxwell equations (vector form).** At this point all four equations are in hand:

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} \;=\; \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}, \qquad \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} \;=\; 0,
$$

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{E} \;=\; -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}, \qquad \nabla \times \mathbf{B} \;=\; \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0\epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}.
$$

These four equations, plus the Lorentz force law $\mathbf{F} = q(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B})$, constitute classical electrodynamics.

**Inductance.** For a coil of $N$ turns carrying current $I$, the total flux linkage is $\Lambda = N\Phi_B$. **Self-inductance** $L$ is defined by $\Lambda = LI$, and the induced EMF is $\mathcal{E} = -L\,dI/dt$. **Mutual inductance** $M$ between two circuits satisfies $\Lambda_2 = MI_1$ and $\mathcal{E}_2 = -M\,dI_1/dt$. The energy stored in the magnetic field of an inductor is $U = \tfrac{1}{2}LI^2$; more generally, the total magnetic field energy is

$$
U_B \;=\; \frac{1}{2\mu_0}\int_V |\mathbf{B}|^2\,d\tau.
$$

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The flux rule $\mathcal{E} = -d\Phi_B/dt$ applies when the loop is a single connected curve. For a conductor that **breaks** (e.g., a sliding bar that leaves the rail), the EMF still exists but you must be careful about what "the loop" is — Griffiths Example 5.11 (the Faraday disc / homopolar generator) is the standard cautionary example.
- Faraday's law $\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t$ is always true. The flux rule $\mathcal{E} = -d\Phi_B/dt$ has subtleties when the loop deforms or when the "circuit" is not well-defined. The differential form is the reliable statement; the flux rule is a computational shortcut that works in most practical situations.
- The displacement current $\epsilon_0\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t$ is not a flow of charge. It is the rate of change of the electric field, and it has the dimensions of current density. It produces magnetic fields in the same way as a real current — this is what makes electromagnetic waves possible.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Equivalence of the flux rule and the differential Faraday law for a stationary loop).** *Let $S$ be a fixed (time-independent) oriented surface with boundary $\mathcal{C} = \partial S$, and let $\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r},t)$ be a smooth magnetic field. Then*

$$
\oint_{\mathcal{C}} \mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{l} \;=\; -\frac{d}{dt}\int_S \mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{a}
$$

*if and only if $\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t$.*

**Proof.** Since $S$ is fixed, the time derivative passes through the spatial integral:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\int_S \mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{a} \;=\; \int_S \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\cdot d\mathbf{a}.
$$

By Stokes' theorem applied to the left-hand side:

$$
\oint_{\mathcal{C}} \mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{l} \;=\; \int_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{E})\cdot d\mathbf{a}.
$$

Substituting both into the flux rule gives

$$
\int_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{E})\cdot d\mathbf{a} \;=\; -\int_S \frac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\cdot d\mathbf{a},
$$

or equivalently

$$
\int_S \left(\nabla\times\mathbf{E} + \frac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\right)\cdot d\mathbf{a} \;=\; 0.
$$

Since this must hold for *every* fixed surface $S$, the integrand vanishes pointwise:

$$
\nabla\times\mathbf{E} \;=\; -\frac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t}. \qquad\qed
$$

**Corollary.** *For a moving loop $\mathcal{C}(t)$ sweeping out surface $S(t)$, the total time derivative of the flux picks up an additional boundary term that is precisely the motional EMF:*

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\int_{S(t)}\mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{a} \;=\; \int_{S(t)}\frac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\cdot d\mathbf{a} \;+\; \oint_{\mathcal{C}(t)}(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B})\cdot d\mathbf{l}.
$$

*This is the Leibniz rule (transport theorem) for flux integrals. The flux rule $\mathcal{E} = -d\Phi_B/dt$ unifies both contributions.*

### Worked example: mutual inductance of coaxial solenoids

A long solenoid of radius $a$, $n_1$ turns per unit length, carries current $I_1$. A second solenoid of radius $b > a$, $n_2$ turns per unit length, is wound coaxially around the first, over a length $\ell$.

Inside the inner solenoid the field is $B = \mu_0 n_1 I_1$ (from Ampere's law); outside it is negligible for an ideal solenoid. The flux through one turn of the outer solenoid is $\Phi_{22} = B\cdot\pi a^2 = \mu_0 n_1 I_1 \pi a^2$ (only the cross-section of the inner solenoid carries flux). The total flux linkage in the outer solenoid is $\Lambda_2 = n_2 \ell \cdot \Phi_{22} = \mu_0 n_1 n_2 \ell \pi a^2 I_1$, so

$$
M \;=\; \frac{\Lambda_2}{I_1} \;=\; \mu_0 n_1 n_2 \ell\,\pi a^2.
$$

Note that $M$ depends on the geometry (lengths, radii, turn densities) but not on the currents. Reversing the argument (driving $I_2$ through the outer solenoid and computing flux linkage in the inner) gives the same $M$ — mutual inductance is symmetric: $M_{12} = M_{21}$.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

A rectangular loop of width $w$ and height $h$ sits with its plane perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = B_0\sin(\omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$. The loop is stationary. Find the induced EMF as a function of time.

The flux is $\Phi_B = B(t) \times (\text{area})$. Differentiate.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Flux $\Phi_B = B_0\sin(\omega t)\cdot wh$. Induced EMF:

$$
\mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -whB_0\omega\cos(\omega t).
$$

The EMF oscillates at the same frequency as the field, but $90^\circ$ out of phase — when the field is at maximum, the rate of change is zero and the EMF vanishes; when the field passes through zero, the rate of change is maximum and the EMF peaks.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

A conducting rod of length $\ell$ slides along two parallel rails in a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ perpendicular to the plane of the rails. The rod moves with constant velocity $v$. Find the motional EMF and the current if the total circuit resistance is $R$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The free charges in the moving rod experience force $q\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}$. Alternatively, compute the rate of change of the area swept by the rod.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Motional EMF: $\mathcal{E} = B\ell v$ (the field strength times rod length times velocity). Current: $I = \mathcal{E}/R = B\ell v/R$. The power dissipated is $P = I^2 R = B^2\ell^2 v^2/R$, which must be supplied by whatever external force is pushing the rod — Lenz's law in action: the induced current creates a magnetic force $F = BI\ell = B^2\ell^2 v/R$ opposing the motion, so the external agent does work at rate $Fv = B^2\ell^2 v^2/R$, exactly balancing the dissipation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the displacement current term by requiring Ampere's law to be consistent with charge conservation. Start with $\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J}$, take the divergence, and use the continuity equation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The divergence of a curl is always zero. The continuity equation is $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J} = -\partial\rho/\partial t$. Use Gauss's law to relate $\rho$ to $\mathbf{E}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Taking the divergence of $\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J}$ gives $0 = \mu_0\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J}$, which requires $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J} = 0$. But the continuity equation says $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J} = -\partial\rho/\partial t$, which is nonzero whenever charge density is changing. Gauss's law gives $\rho = \epsilon_0\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}$, so $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J} = -\epsilon_0\,\partial(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E})/\partial t = -\nabla\cdot(\epsilon_0\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t)$. Moving this term to the other side:

$$
\nabla\cdot\left(\mathbf{J} + \epsilon_0\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\right) = 0.
$$

This is automatically satisfied if $\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J} + \mu_0\epsilon_0\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t$, since the divergence of any curl vanishes identically.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A long solenoid of radius $a$, $n$ turns per unit length, carries current $I(t) = I_0 e^{-t/\tau}$. Find the induced electric field at radius $r > a$ from the solenoid axis.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use Faraday's law in integral form with a circular Amperian loop of radius $r > a$. The flux through this loop is the field inside the solenoid times the solenoid's cross-sectional area (not the loop area — the field is zero outside the solenoid).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Inside the solenoid, $B = \mu_0 n I(t) = \mu_0 n I_0 e^{-t/\tau}$. Outside, $B = 0$. For a circular loop of radius $r > a$, the flux is $\Phi_B = B\cdot\pi a^2 = \mu_0 n I_0 \pi a^2 e^{-t/\tau}$ (flux only through the solenoid cross-section, not the entire loop). By symmetry $\mathbf{E}$ is tangential and constant on the loop, so Faraday's law gives

$$
\oint \mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{l} = E(2\pi r) = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = \frac{\mu_0 n I_0 \pi a^2}{\tau}\,e^{-t/\tau}.
$$

Hence $E(r) = \mu_0 n I_0 a^2/(2r\tau)\,e^{-t/\tau}$. The induced electric field exists even outside the solenoid where $B = 0$ — the changing *flux* creates the field, not the local field value.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Two coaxial circular loops of radii $a$ and $b$ ($b \ll a$) are separated by a distance $d$ along their common axis. The small loop carries current $I(t)$. Using the magnetic dipole approximation for the field of the large loop at the location of the small loop, compute the mutual inductance $M$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The field of the small loop on axis at distance $d$ is $B_z = \mu_0 I b^2 / (2(b^2 + d^2)^{3/2})$. For $b \ll d$, approximate.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The magnetic field on the axis of a circular loop of radius $b$ carrying current $I$ at axial distance $d$ is

$$
B_z = \frac{\mu_0 I b^2}{2(b^2 + d^2)^{3/2}}.
$$

The flux through the large loop (radius $a$) due to this field is approximately $B_z \cdot \pi a^2$ (uniform field over the large loop, valid when $b \ll d$). The flux linkage is $\Lambda = B_z \pi a^2$, so

$$
M = \frac{\Lambda}{I} = \frac{\mu_0 \pi a^2 b^2}{2(b^2 + d^2)^{3/2}}.
$$

For $b \ll d$: $M \approx \mu_0 \pi a^2 b^2 / (2d^3)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the self-inductance per unit length of a coaxial cable with inner radius $a$ and outer radius $b$, carrying current $I$ on the inner conductor and $-I$ on the outer.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use Ampere's law to find $B$ between the conductors ($a < r < b$). Then compute the magnetic energy per unit length and use $U = \tfrac{1}{2}LI^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Between the conductors, $B = \mu_0 I/(2\pi r)$. The magnetic energy per unit length is

$$
\frac{U}{\ell} = \frac{1}{2\mu_0}\int_a^b B^2\,2\pi r\,dr = \frac{\mu_0 I^2}{4\pi}\int_a^b \frac{dr}{r} = \frac{\mu_0 I^2}{4\pi}\ln\frac{b}{a}.
$$

Setting $U/\ell = \tfrac{1}{2}LI^2/\ell$ gives the inductance per unit length:

$$
\frac{L}{\ell} = \frac{\mu_0}{2\pi}\ln\frac{b}{a}.
$$

</details>

## Retarded potentials and gauge freedom [Master]

The Maxwell equations in the potential formulation use the scalar potential $\phi$ and vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ with $\mathbf{B} = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{E} = -\nabla\phi - \partial\mathbf{A}/\partial t$. Substituting into the two inhomogeneous Maxwell equations gives

$$
\nabla^2\phi + \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{A}) = -\frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0},
$$

$$
\nabla^2\mathbf{A} - \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{A}}{\partial t^2} - \nabla\!\left(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{A} + \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial t}\right) = -\mu_0\mathbf{J}.
$$

These are coupled and messy. **Gauge freedom** simplifies them. The potentials are not unique: the transformation

$$
\phi \;\to\; \phi' = \phi - \frac{\partial\Lambda}{\partial t}, \qquad \mathbf{A} \;\to\; \mathbf{A}' = \mathbf{A} + \nabla\Lambda,
$$

for any smooth function $\Lambda(\mathbf{r}, t)$, leaves $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ unchanged. This is because $\mathbf{B} = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ is invariant under $\mathbf{A}\to\mathbf{A}+\nabla\Lambda$ (curl of gradient is zero), and the change in $\mathbf{E}$ from the $\partial\mathbf{A}/\partial t$ term cancels the change from the $\nabla\phi$ term.

**Lorenz gauge.** Choose $\Lambda$ so that

$$
\nabla\cdot\mathbf{A} + \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial t} = 0.
$$

The two potential equations decouple into **wave equations with sources**:

$$
\boxed{\;\;\nabla^2\phi - \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial t^2} = -\frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}, \qquad \nabla^2\mathbf{A} - \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{A}}{\partial t^2} = -\mu_0\mathbf{J}.\;\;}
$$

These are solved by the **retarded potentials**:

$$
\phi(\mathbf{r}, t) \;=\; \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int \frac{\rho(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|}\,d^3 r', \qquad \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}, t) \;=\; \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int \frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|}\,d^3 r',
$$

where $t_r = t - |\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|/c$ is the **retarded time**. The fields at point $\mathbf{r}$ and time $t$ are determined by the sources at the retarded time — the information travels at speed $c$. This is the precise sense in which electromagnetic influences propagate at the speed of light: the potentials are retarded, and the fields inherit this causality.

**Coulomb gauge.** The alternative choice $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{A} = 0$ gives $\nabla^2\phi = -\rho/\epsilon_0$ (Poisson's equation — the scalar potential is instantaneous), but the vector potential satisfies a more complicated equation with an additional non-local term. The Coulomb gauge is useful in quantum mechanics (radiation problems); the Lorenz gauge is natural in relativistic formulations.

### Transformers and power transmission

A transformer consists of two coils wound on a common iron core. AC current in the primary coil creates a time-varying magnetic flux through the core; the changing flux induces an EMF in the secondary coil via Faraday's law. For $N_1$ primary turns and $N_2$ secondary turns, the voltage ratio is

$$
\frac{V_2}{V_1} = \frac{N_2}{N_1}.
$$

Step-up transformers ($N_2 > N_1$) increase voltage and decrease current; step-down transformers decrease voltage and increase current. The power $P = VI$ is conserved (minus small losses). Long-distance power transmission uses high voltage ($\sim$500 kV) to minimise $I^2R$ resistive losses in the transmission lines, then steps down to consumer voltages ($\sim$120 V or 240 V) at the destination. Without transformers, practical long-distance electrical power transmission would be impossible.

### Eddy currents and electromagnetic braking

A changing magnetic flux through a conducting body induces circulating currents called **eddy currents**. By Lenz's law, these currents create magnetic fields opposing the change, producing a drag force. This **electromagnetic braking** is used in trains (eddy-current brakes), induction furnaces (eddy currents heat the metal), and metal detectors.

The power dissipated by eddy currents in a conducting plate of thickness $d$ and conductivity $\sigma$ in a changing magnetic field of amplitude $B_0$ and frequency $\omega$ is approximately $P \propto \sigma B_0^2 \omega^2 d^2$. Laminated transformer cores (thin insulated sheets of iron rather than solid blocks) reduce eddy-current losses by breaking up the current paths — the induced EMF drives current within each lamination, but the thin cross-section limits the current and hence the power dissipation.

### Energy storage in magnetic fields

The energy stored in an inductor carrying current $I$ is $U = \tfrac{1}{2}LI^2$. This energy is stored in the magnetic field, with energy density $u_B = B^2/(2\mu_0)$. For a long solenoid ($B = \mu_0 nI$, volume $V = A\ell$):

$$
U = \frac{B^2}{2\mu_0}V = \frac{(\mu_0 nI)^2}{2\mu_0}A\ell = \frac{1}{2}\mu_0 n^2 A\ell\, I^2 = \frac{1}{2}LI^2,
$$

confirming the identification of magnetic energy with the field energy. Superconducting magnetic energy storage (SMES) systems store energy in the magnetic field of a superconducting coil, achieving energy densities of several megajoules per cubic metre with negligible resistive losses.

### RL, RC, and RLC circuits

The combination of resistance ($R$), inductance ($L$), and capacitance ($C$) produces a rich variety of transient and steady-state behaviours. An **RL circuit** responds to a step input with exponential current rise (or decay) on a timescale $\tau_L = L/R$. An **RC circuit** charges and discharges on a timescale $\tau_C = RC$. An **RLC circuit** exhibits damped oscillations at angular frequency $\omega = \sqrt{1/(LC) - (R/2L)^2}$ when the resistance is below the critical value $R_c = 2\sqrt{L/C}$; above this value the response is overdamped.

The RLC circuit is the electrical analogue of the damped harmonic oscillator: $L$ plays the role of mass (inertia opposes change in current), $1/C$ plays the role of the spring constant (the capacitor provides a restoring "force"), and $R$ plays the role of damping. The analogy is exact: the circuit equation $L\,d^2Q/dt^2 + R\,dQ/dt + Q/C = 0$ is mathematically identical to $m\ddot{x} + b\dot{x} + kx = 0$. The quality factor $Q = \omega_0 L/R = 1/(R\omega_0 C)$ (where $\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC}$) measures the sharpness of resonance — high-Q circuits ring for many cycles before the oscillation decays.

## Energy conservation: Poynting's theorem [Master]

Multiplying Faraday's law by $\mathbf{B}/\mu_0$ and Ampere's law (with displacement current) by $\epsilon_0\mathbf{E}$, then subtracting, yields **Poynting's theorem**:

$$
\boxed{\;\;\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \nabla\cdot\mathbf{S} \;=\; -\mathbf{J}\cdot\mathbf{E},\;\;}
$$

where the **electromagnetic energy density** is $u = \tfrac{1}{2}\epsilon_0|\mathbf{E}|^2 + \tfrac{1}{2\mu_0}|\mathbf{B}|^2$ and the **Poynting vector** is

$$
\mathbf{S} \;=\; \frac{1}{\mu_0}\,\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B}.
$$

Poynting's theorem is the energy conservation law for electromagnetism. The term $\mathbf{J}\cdot\mathbf{E}$ is the rate at which the field does work on the charges (Joule heating, or mechanical power). If this is zero (vacuum), then $\partial u/\partial t + \nabla\cdot\mathbf{S} = 0$ — energy is conserved locally, with $\mathbf{S}$ being the energy flux (power per unit area carried by the field). For electromagnetic waves, $\mathbf{S}$ points in the direction of propagation and has magnitude $c\,u$.

Integrating Poynting's theorem over a volume $V$ bounded by surface $\Sigma$ gives

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\int_V u\,d\tau = -\oint_\Sigma \mathbf{S}\cdot d\mathbf{a} - \int_V \mathbf{J}\cdot\mathbf{E}\,d\tau.
$$

The rate of change of field energy in $V$ equals the energy flowing in through the boundary minus the work done on charges inside. This is a local energy balance: energy does not disappear but flows from one region to another, carried by the Poynting vector. The integral form of Poynting's theorem is the starting point for understanding energy flow in waveguides, optical fibres, and antennas.

For a plane electromagnetic wave $\mathbf{E} = E_0\cos(kz - \omega t)\hat{\mathbf{x}}$, $\mathbf{B} = (E_0/c)\cos(kz - \omega t)\hat{\mathbf{y}}$, the Poynting vector is $\mathbf{S} = (E_0^2/\mu_0 c)\cos^2(kz - \omega t)\hat{\mathbf{z}}$. The time-averaged intensity is $\langle|\mathbf{S}|\rangle = E_0^2/(2\mu_0 c)$. For sunlight at Earth's surface ($\sim 1000$ W/m$^2$), the corresponding electric field amplitude is $E_0 \approx 870$ V/m and the magnetic field amplitude is $B_0 = E_0/c \approx 2.9\,\mu$T.

## The electromagnetic field tensor (preview) [Master]

The four Maxwell equations are not four independent laws — they are the coordinate expression of a single geometric object. Define the **electromagnetic field tensor** $F^{\mu\nu}$ in terms of the 4-potential $A^\mu = (\phi/c, \mathbf{A})$:

$$
F^{\mu\nu} \;:=\; \partial^\mu A^\nu - \partial^\nu A^\mu.
$$

In matrix form (with metric signature $+{-}{-}{-}$):

$$
F^{\mu\nu} \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} 0 & E_x/c & E_y/c & E_z/c \\ -E_x/c & 0 & -B_z & B_y \\ -E_y/c & B_z & 0 & -B_x \\ -E_z/c & -B_y & B_x & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

The two homogeneous Maxwell equations ($\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$ and $\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t$) are equivalent to the **Bianchi identity** $\partial_\lambda F_{\mu\nu} + \partial_\mu F_{\nu\lambda} + \partial_\nu F_{\lambda\mu} = 0$. The two inhomogeneous equations ($\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = \rho/\epsilon_0$ and $\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J} + \mu_0\epsilon_0\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t$) become

$$
\boxed{\;\;\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} \;=\; \mu_0 J^\nu,\;\;}
$$

where $J^\nu = (c\rho, \mathbf{J})$ is the four-current. This single tensor equation replaces the four vector Maxwell equations. Gauge invariance is the statement that $F^{\mu\nu}$ is unchanged under $A^\mu \to A^\mu + \partial^\mu\Lambda$ — the field tensor is gauge-invariant even though the potentials are not. The full differential-forms treatment lives in unit [10.04.01].

## Connections [Master]

- **Coulomb's law and Gauss's law [10.01.01]** provides the first and third Maxwell equations ($\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = \rho/\epsilon_0$, $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$). Faraday's law ($\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t$) is the time-dependent generalisation that makes the electric field non-conservative. Without Faraday's law, $\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = 0$ and the electric field can always be written as the gradient of a scalar potential. Faraday's law requires the additional term $-\partial\mathbf{A}/\partial t$ in the electric field: $\mathbf{E} = -\nabla\phi - \partial\mathbf{A}/\partial t$.

- **Biot-Savart and Ampere's law [10.02.01]** gives the magnetostatic Ampere's law $\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J}$. Maxwell's displacement current $\mu_0\epsilon_0\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t$ extends it to time-varying fields — the fourth equation, completing the set. The displacement current was Maxwell's boldest theoretical contribution: he postulated a term that had never been observed, purely from the requirement of mathematical consistency with charge conservation.

- **Maxwell's equations in differential forms [10.04.01]** reformulates the four vector Maxwell equations as two differential-form equations ($dF = 0$, $d{\star}F = J$). The Faraday law content is part of $dF = 0$; the displacement current is part of $d{\star}F = J$. The differential-forms language makes gauge invariance and topology (cohomology of the fields) manifest.

- **EM waves [10.04.02]** derives wave solutions from the complete Maxwell equations. The wave equation for $\mathbf{E}$ (Exercise 8) requires both Faraday's law and the displacement current — without either, electromagnetic waves do not exist. The existence of electromagnetic waves is the central prediction of the full Maxwell theory, and it rests on the coupling between the electric and magnetic fields established by Faraday's law and the displacement current.

- **Hamiltonian mechanics [09.04.02]** uses gauge transformations in the context of constrained systems (Dirac-Bergmann algorithm). The gauge freedom $\mathbf{A}\to\mathbf{A}+\nabla\Lambda$ in electromagnetism is the physical origin of gauge symmetry, and the minimal-coupling Hamiltonian $H = |\mathbf{p}-e\mathbf{A}|^2/(2m) + e\phi$ is the bridge between classical mechanics and electrodynamics.

- **Special relativity [10.05.01]** shows that the scalar and vector potentials combine into the 4-potential $A^\mu$, and $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ are different components of the same tensor $F^{\mu\nu}$. Faraday's law and the absence of magnetic monopoles are the "space-space" components of the Bianchi identity; Gauss's law and Ampere's law with displacement current are the "time-space" components of the inhomogeneous equation. The Lorentz transformation mixes $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$: a pure electric field in one frame has magnetic components in another, and the induced electric field of Faraday's law is the magnetic field of a moving charge seen from a different frame.

- **Covariant electrodynamics [10.06.01]** develops the full tensor formulation. The field tensor $F^{\mu\nu}$ and the Bianchi identity $\partial_{[\lambda}F_{\mu\nu]} = 0$ encode Faraday's law and the absence of magnetic monopoles in a manifestly Lorentz-covariant form.

- **Electrochemistry [14.11.01]** uses Faraday's law of electrolysis (distinct from Faraday's law of induction, but discovered by the same Faraday). The two laws are connected: both involve the conversion between different forms of energy via electromagnetic processes. Faradaic processes in electrochemistry involve charge transfer across an electrode-electrolyte interface, governed by the same electromagnetic principles developed here.

- **Practical technology.** Faraday's law underpins the entire electrical power industry. Generators (rotating coils in magnetic fields) convert mechanical energy to electrical energy. Transformers step voltage up for long-distance transmission and down for consumer use — their efficiency typically exceeds 99% for large power transformers. Induction motors, induction stovetops, wireless charging, and magnetic braking all rely on electromagnetic induction. The global electrical grid is an application of Faraday's 1831 discovery at industrial scale.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

### Faraday's experiments (1831–1832)

Michael Faraday discovered electromagnetic induction in August 1831 through a series of meticulous experiments at the Royal Institution in London. His apparatus consisted of an iron ring with two separate coils of wire wound around it. When he connected a battery to the first coil, a momentary deflection appeared on a galvanometer connected to the second coil — but only during the instant of connection, not while the current flowed steadily. When he disconnected the battery, another brief deflection occurred, in the opposite direction. Faraday recognised that the induced current depended not on the current itself but on the *change* in current — the change in the magnetic field threading the ring.

Over the following months, Faraday demonstrated induction in every conceivable configuration: moving a bar magnet through a coil, rotating a copper disc between the poles of a magnet (the homopolar generator), and varying the distance between two coils. He established that the induced EMF is proportional to the rate of change of the magnetic flux through the circuit, regardless of the mechanism producing that change. His results were published in the first series of his "Experimental Researches in Electricity" (1832).

Faraday had no mathematical training — he left school at fourteen and was self-taught in science — and he thought entirely in terms of field lines and "electrotonic states." His mental picture was geometric: magnetic field lines thread through space, and when the number of lines passing through a conducting loop changes, a current is induced. This picture, though qualitative, captured the essential physics. William Thomson (Lord Kelvin) later showed that Faraday's electrotonic state could be identified with what is now called the magnetic vector potential $\mathbf{A}$.

### Joseph Henry and self-induction

Joseph Henry, working independently in Albany, New York, discovered self-induction in 1832 — the phenomenon in which a current-carrying coil induces an EMF in itself when the current changes. Henry observed that breaking the circuit of a large electromagnet produced a spark far larger than the spark from breaking a simple wire circuit of the same resistance. The coil's own magnetic field, collapsing when the current was interrupted, induced a large voltage that drove the spark. Henry also discovered mutual induction independently of Faraday, but Faraday published first.

Henry's discovery of self-induction introduced the concept of inductance $L$ and the back-EMF $\mathcal{E} = -L\,dI/dt$. This has practical consequences in every electrical circuit: switching off an inductive load (motor, relay, transformer) generates a voltage spike that can damage components unless suppressed by a flyback diode or snubber circuit.

### Maxwell's synthesis (1865)

James Clerk Maxwell translated Faraday's qualitative picture into the differential equations that bear his name. Maxwell's key insight was the addition of the displacement current $\epsilon_0\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t$ to Ampere's law, motivated by the requirement of charge conservation. Without this term, Ampere's law contradicts the continuity equation $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J} = -\partial\rho/\partial t$ in time-varying situations (as shown in Exercise 3). The displacement current has no effect in magnetostatics (all currents are steady, fields are constant) but is essential for time-varying fields.

Maxwell's 1865 paper "A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field" combined Faraday's law of induction, Ampere's law with the displacement current, Gauss's law, and the absence of magnetic monopoles into a unified system of equations. From these equations he derived the wave equation, predicted electromagnetic waves travelling at $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\epsilon_0}$, and identified this speed with the measured speed of light. The displacement current was the keystone: without it, the wave equation does not emerge from the Maxwell equations, and electromagnetic waves do not exist.

### Hertz's experimental confirmation (1887–1888)

Heinrich Hertz confirmed Maxwell's wave prediction experimentally in 1887–1888. Hertz used a spark-gap oscillator to generate electromagnetic waves at a frequency of about 56 MHz (wavelength roughly 5 metres) and detected them with a resonant loop of wire at distances of several metres. He demonstrated that the waves reflected off metal surfaces, refracted through prisms of pitch, polarised, and interfered — all properties predicted by Maxwell's theory. Hertz also measured the wave speed and found it consistent with $c$.

Hertz's experiments settled the debate between action-at-a-distance theories (Weber, Neumann) and the field theory of Faraday and Maxwell. The fact that electromagnetic effects propagate at a finite speed, and that this speed equals $c$, could not be explained by instantaneous action at a distance. The field concept — disturbances propagating through space at a finite speed — was vindicated.

### The Lorenz gauge and retarded potentials

The retarded-potential formulation is due to Ludvig Lorenz (1867), not Hendrik Lorentz of the Lorentz transformation — the two are frequently confused in the literature. Lorenz showed that the potentials could be chosen to satisfy the gauge condition $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{A} + (1/c^2)\partial\phi/\partial t = 0$, and that the resulting wave equations for $\phi$ and $\mathbf{A}$ have solutions given by the retarded potentials. This formulation makes causality explicit: the fields at a point depend on the sources at earlier times, with the delay equal to the light-travel time.

The tensor formulation emerged from Minkowski (1908) after Einstein's special relativity (1905) showed that the Maxwell equations are already Lorentz-covariant. The electromagnetic field tensor $F^{\mu\nu}$ unifies the electric and magnetic fields into a single geometric object, and the four Maxwell equations reduce to two tensor equations.

### Philosophical significance: fields as real entities

Faraday's discovery resolved a deep asymmetry in the known laws. Oersted (1820) had shown that electric currents create magnetic fields. Ampere's law quantified this. But the reverse — do magnetic fields create electric fields? — was missing. Faraday's law closes the loop: changing magnetic fields create electric fields, and (via Maxwell's displacement current) changing electric fields create magnetic fields. This mutual coupling is what sustains electromagnetic waves in vacuum, requiring no charges or currents at all.

The philosophical import is that fields are real dynamical entities, not mere bookkeeping devices for forces between charges. The Poynting vector $\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B}/\mu_0$ shows that electromagnetic fields carry energy and momentum through empty space. A light beam exerts radiation pressure. The energy stored in the field between the plates of a capacitor is real — it can be converted to heat, mechanical work, or chemical energy. This was a radical departure from the Newtonian action-at-a-distance paradigm, in which forces act instantaneously between material particles with no intervening medium.

Faraday's field-line picture, though initially dismissed by the mathematical establishment (who preferred action-at-a-distance formulations), proved to be the correct physical intuition. Maxwell's equations describe fields that propagate, carry energy, and obey conservation laws — dynamical entities in their own right. This shift from particles-with-forces to fields-as-primary-objects is one of the deepest conceptual changes in the history of physics, and it paved the way for quantum field theory, in which *all* fundamental interactions are described by fields.
