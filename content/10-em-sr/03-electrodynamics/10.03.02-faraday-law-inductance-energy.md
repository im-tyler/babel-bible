## Intuition [Beginner]

Michael Faraday discovered in 1831 that a changing magnetic field creates an electric field. If you move a magnet through a coil of wire, a voltage appears across the wire — even though there is no battery. This is **electromagnetic induction**, and Faraday's law quantifies it.

The **electromotive force** (EMF) around a closed loop equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux through the loop:

$$\mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

The minus sign is **Lenz's law**: the induced EMF always opposes the change that created it. If you push a magnet toward a coil, the induced current creates its own magnetic field that pushes back. This is nature's way of resisting change — the electromagnetic equivalent of inertia.

**Mutual inductance** ($M$) measures how much flux from circuit 1 links circuit 2. A changing current in circuit 1 induces an EMF in circuit 2: $\mathcal{E}_2 = -M\,dI_1/dt$. **Self-inductance** ($L$) is the special case where circuit 1 and circuit 2 are the same: a changing current in a coil induces an EMF in itself: $\mathcal{E} = -L\,dI/dt$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A circular loop of radius $a$ sits in a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B}(t) = B_0 t\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ that increases linearly with time. Find the induced EMF.

The flux through the loop is $\Phi_B = B(t) \cdot \pi a^2 = B_0 \pi a^2 t$. Faraday's law gives $\mathcal{E} = -d\Phi_B/dt = -B_0 \pi a^2$. The induced EMF is constant (since $B$ changes at a constant rate) and drives a current that creates a field opposing the increase (Lenz's law). The induced current flows clockwise (when viewed from above) to create a downward field that opposes the increasing upward field.

Lenz's law states that the induced EMF opposes the change that created it. This is a consequence of:

**B.** If the induced current reinforced the change (instead of opposing it), it would increase the flux change, which would increase the EMF, which would increase the current further — an infinite feedback loop that creates energy from nothing. ## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Faraday's law in integral form.**

$$\mathcal{E} = \oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d}{dt}\int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{a}$$

where $C$ is a closed curve bounding the surface $S$, and the total time derivative acts on the flux (including changes in $B$ and in the geometry of $S$ if the loop moves or deforms).

**Faraday's law in differential form.** For a stationary loop:

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}$$

This follows from the integral form by Stokes' theorem: $\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) \cdot d\mathbf{a}$ and comparing with $-\int_S (\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t) \cdot d\mathbf{a}$ for arbitrary $S$.

**The flux rule (motional EMF).** For a moving loop, the total time derivative of the flux includes both the time variation of $\mathbf{B}$ and the motion of the boundary $C$:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{a} = \int_S \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\cdot d\mathbf{a} + \oint_C (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B})\cdot d\mathbf{l}$$

where $\mathbf{v}$ is the velocity of the boundary element $d\mathbf{l}$. The total EMF is:

$$\mathcal{E} = \oint_C (\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B})\cdot d\mathbf{l}$$

**Mutual inductance (Neumann formula).** The mutual inductance between two circuits $C_1$ and $C_2$ is:

$$M_{12} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\oint_{C_1}\oint_{C_2}\frac{d\mathbf{l}_1 \cdot d\mathbf{l}_2}{|\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2|}$$

This shows $M_{12} = M_{21}$ (reciprocity): the mutual inductance is a geometric property of the two circuits, independent of which carries the current.

**Self-inductance.** For a single circuit carrying current $I$:

$$L = \frac{\Phi_B}{I} = \frac{1}{I}\int_S \mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{a}$$

where $\Phi_B$ is the total flux linking the circuit. For a solenoid of length $\ell$, cross-section $A$, and $n$ turns per unit length: $L = \mu_0 n^2 A\ell$.

**Magnetic energy.** The energy stored in a system of inductors is:

$$W = \frac{1}{2}\sum_i L_i I_i^2 + \sum_{i < j} M_{ij} I_i I_j$$

In terms of the magnetic field:

$$W = \frac{1}{2\mu_0}\int |\mathbf{B}|^2\,d^3r = \frac{1}{2}\int \mathbf{H}\cdot\mathbf{B}\,d^3r$$

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (Neumann formula and reciprocity).**

**Theorem.** *The mutual inductance $M_{12}$ of circuit $C_2$ with respect to circuit $C_1$ equals $M_{21}$, and is given by the Neumann formula.*

*Proof.* When current $I_1$ flows in $C_1$, it creates a vector potential $\mathbf{A}_1(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0 I_1}{4\pi}\oint_{C_1}\frac{d\mathbf{l}_1}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_1|}$. The flux through $C_2$ is:

$$\Phi_{12} = \int_{S_2}\mathbf{B}_1\cdot d\mathbf{a}_2 = \oint_{C_2}\mathbf{A}_1\cdot d\mathbf{l}_2 = \frac{\mu_0 I_1}{4\pi}\oint_{C_1}\oint_{C_2}\frac{d\mathbf{l}_1\cdot d\mathbf{l}_2}{|\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2|}$$

So $M_{12} = \Phi_{12}/I_1 = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\oint\oint\frac{d\mathbf{l}_1\cdot d\mathbf{l}_2}{|\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2|}$. The double integral is symmetric under $1 \leftrightarrow 2$, so $M_{12} = M_{21}$. $\square$

**Bridge.** Faraday's law is the third of Maxwell's equations (after Gauss and Ampere) and provides the crucial link between electricity and magnetism: changing magnetic fields create electric fields. The foundational insight is that induction is a *local* phenomenon: the curl of $\mathbf{E}$ at each point equals minus the time derivative of $\mathbf{B}$ at that point. The central message is that inductance is a purely geometric quantity: $M$ and $L$ depend only on the shapes and positions of the circuits, not on the currents. This geometric character makes inductance a powerful tool in circuit analysis and motivates the vector potential formulation [10.02.02]. Putting these together, Faraday's law provides the time-dependent coupling between electric and magnetic fields that, together with the displacement current [10.03.04], completes the set of equations that describe all classical electromagnetic phenomena.

A square loop of side $a$ sits in a uniform field $B(t) = B_0\sin(\omega t)$ perpendicular to the loop. Find the induced EMF.

The amplitude is $B_0 a^2 \omega$ — it increases with both the field strength and the frequency.

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

A solenoid of length $\ell = 10$ cm, radius $R = 1$ cm, with $N = 1000$ turns. Find its self-inductance.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$B = \mu_0 n I$ inside the solenoid. Flux through each turn is $B \cdot \pi R^2$. Total flux linkage is $N\Phi = LI$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Turns per unit length: $n = N/\ell = 10000$ m$^{-1}$. Field inside: $B = \mu_0 n I$. Flux per turn: $\Phi = B\pi R^2 = \mu_0 n I \pi R^2$. Total flux linkage: $N\Phi = N\mu_0 n I\pi R^2$. Self-inductance: $L = N\Phi/I = \mu_0 n N \pi R^2 = \mu_0 N^2 \pi R^2/\ell$. Numerically: $L = 4\pi\times 10^{-7}\times 10^6 \times \pi\times 10^{-4}/0.1 = 4\pi^2\times 10^{-5}/0.1 \approx 3.95\times 10^{-3}$ H $\approx 4.0$ mH.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the mutual inductance of two concentric coplanar circular loops with radii $a$ (small) and $b$ (large, $b \gg a$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $M = \Phi_{12}/I_1$. For a small loop inside a large one, the field from the large loop is approximately uniform over the small loop. $\Phi_{12} = B_1 \cdot \pi a^2$ where $B_1$ is the on-axis field of the large loop at its center.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For the large loop (radius $b$, current $I_b$): the field at its center is $B = \mu_0 I_b/(2b)$. This is approximately uniform over the small loop (radius $a \ll b$). The flux through the small loop is $\Phi_{12} = B\pi a^2 = \mu_0 I_b \pi a^2/(2b)$. So $M = \Phi_{12}/I_b = \mu_0 \pi a^2/(2b)$.

By reciprocity, $M_{21} = M_{12} = \mu_0\pi a^2/(2b)$. For the reverse calculation (current in the small loop, flux through the large loop), we would use $\Phi_{21} = MI_a$, which gives $\Phi_{21}/I_a = \mu_0\pi a^2/(2b)$. The Neumann formula would give the same result (but requires evaluating an elliptic integral).
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the expression for the energy stored in a magnetic field: $W = \frac{1}{2\mu_0}\int|\mathbf{B}|^2\,d^3r$, starting from the formula $W = \frac{1}{2}LI^2$ for a single inductor.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $L\Phi/I$ and substitute $\mathbf{B} = \text{curl}\,\mathbf{A}$. Integrate by parts (vector identity).
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$W = \frac{1}{2}LI^2 = \frac{1}{2}I\Phi = \frac{1}{2}I\int_S\mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{a} = \frac{1}{2}I\oint_C\mathbf{A}\cdot d\mathbf{l}$ (Stokes). Writing $I\,d\mathbf{l} = \int \mathbf{J}\,d^3r$ (current density) and using the Coulomb gauge solution $\mathbf{A} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\mathbf{J}'}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|}\,d^3r'$:

$$W = \frac{1}{2}\int\mathbf{J}\cdot\mathbf{A}\,d^3r = \frac{1}{2}\int(\text{curl}\,\mathbf{H})\cdot\mathbf{A}\,d^3r$$

Using the vector identity $\mathbf{H}\cdot\text{curl}\,\mathbf{A} = \text{div}(\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{H}) + \mathbf{A}\cdot\text{curl}\,\mathbf{H}$ and integrating by parts:

$$W = \frac{1}{2}\int\mathbf{H}\cdot(\text{curl}\,\mathbf{A})\,d^3r = \frac{1}{2}\int\mathbf{H}\cdot\mathbf{B}\,d^3r = \frac{1}{2\mu_0}\int|\mathbf{B}|^2\,d^3r$$

The surface term vanishes at infinity. This is the magnetic energy in terms of the field.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the motional EMF formula from the Lorentz force law: a conductor element $d\mathbf{l}$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ in a field $\mathbf{B}$ experiences a force $d\mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}$ on each charge carrier. Show that the EMF around a moving loop equals $-\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The EMF is $\oint(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B})\cdot d\mathbf{l}$ where $\mathbf{E}$ is the electric field in the lab frame. Use the convective derivative of the flux.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For charges in a conductor moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$, the force per unit charge is $\mathbf{f} = \mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}$ (Lorentz force). The EMF around a closed loop of the conductor is:

$$\mathcal{E} = \oint_C (\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B})\cdot d\mathbf{l}$$

Using Faraday's law for the electric field part: $\oint_C \mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\int_S\frac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\cdot d\mathbf{a}$. For the motional part, use the identity: $\oint_C(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B})\cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\int_S \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{a})_{\text{convective}}$. Together:

$$\mathcal{E} = -\int_S\frac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\cdot d\mathbf{a} - \int_S\mathbf{v}\cdot(\text{curl}\,\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B})\cdot d\mathbf{a} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

where the total derivative includes both the changing field and the moving boundary. This is the flux rule: the EMF equals the negative total rate of change of flux, regardless of whether the change comes from a time-varying $\mathbf{B}$, a moving loop, or a deforming loop.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Two coupled circuits have self-inductances $L_1$, $L_2$ and mutual inductance $M$. Derive the coefficient of coupling $k = M/\sqrt{L_1 L_2}$ and show that $0 \leq k \leq 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The energy $W = \frac{1}{2}L_1 I_1^2 + MI_1I_2 + \frac{1}{2}L_2 I_2^2 \geq 0$ for all currents. This is a positive-definite quadratic form.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The magnetic energy of two coupled circuits is $W = \frac{1}{2}L_1 I_1^2 + MI_1I_2 + \frac{1}{2}L_2 I_2^2$. Since energy is always non-negative, this quadratic form must be positive semi-definite: $W \geq 0$ for all $I_1, I_2$. The matrix $\begin{pmatrix}L_1 & M \\ M & L_2\end{pmatrix}$ must be positive semi-definite, which requires $L_1 \geq 0$, $L_2 \geq 0$, and $L_1 L_2 - M^2 \geq 0$. The last condition gives $M^2 \leq L_1 L_2$, i.e., $|M| \leq \sqrt{L_1 L_2}$.

Defining $k = M/\sqrt{L_1 L_2}$ (the coupling coefficient): $-1 \leq k \leq 1$, and since $M$ can be taken positive by appropriate choice of current direction: $0 \leq k \leq 1$. $k = 0$: no coupling (distant circuits). $k = 1$: perfect coupling (all flux from one circuit links the other, e.g., a tightly wound transformer).
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

A conducting bar of length $\ell$ slides along two parallel rails separated by distance $\ell$, in a uniform field $\mathbf{B} = B_0\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ perpendicular to the rail plane. The bar moves with velocity $v$ to the right. Find the induced EMF and the current if the circuit has resistance $R$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The area of the circuit changes as the bar moves: $A = \ell \cdot x(t)$ where $x(t) = vt$. The flux is $\Phi = B_0 \ell v t$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The area enclosed by the circuit increases as the bar moves: $A(t) = \ell \cdot x = \ell vt$. The flux is $\Phi = B_0 A = B_0 \ell v t$. By Faraday's law: $\mathcal{E} = -d\Phi/dt = -B_0 \ell v$. The induced EMF is constant (since the bar moves at constant velocity). The current is $I = \mathcal{E}/R = B_0 \ell v/R$.

The force on the current-carrying bar in the magnetic field is $F = I\ell B_0 = B_0^2\ell^2 v/R$ (opposing the motion, by Lenz's law). The power dissipated is $P = I^2 R = B_0^2\ell^2 v^2/R$. The mechanical power input is $Fv = B_0^2\ell^2 v^2/R$. Energy is conserved: the mechanical work done pushing the bar equals the electrical energy dissipated in the resistance.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, symbolic).**

An RL circuit has resistance $R$ and inductance $L$. A battery of voltage $V_0$ is connected at $t = 0$. Find the current as a function of time.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The circuit equation is $V_0 = IR + L\,dI/dt$. This is a first-order linear ODE with initial condition $I(0) = 0$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$V_0 = IR + L\,dI/dt$. Rearranging: $dI/dt + (R/L)I = V_0/L$. The integrating factor is $e^{Rt/L}$. Multiplying: $d/dt(Ie^{Rt/L}) = (V_0/L)e^{Rt/L}$. Integrating from 0 to $t$: $I(t)e^{Rt/L} - I(0) = (V_0/R)(e^{Rt/L} - 1)$. With $I(0) = 0$: $I(t) = (V_0/R)(1 - e^{-Rt/L})$.

The time constant is $\tau = L/R$. After one time constant: $I = 0.632 \times V_0/R$. The current approaches the steady-state value $V_0/R$ exponentially. The inductor initially opposes the change in current (back-EMF $L\,dI/dt$) and eventually acts like a wire (no voltage drop at steady state).
</details>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

Mathlib has line integrals and Stokes' theorem but not Faraday's law, the concept of EMF, inductance, or magnetic energy. The time-dependent coupling between electric and magnetic fields is a physical law, not a mathematical theorem, and would require physical axioms beyond pure mathematics. `lean_status: none`.

## Advanced results [Master]

**The flux rule controversy.** The "flux rule" $\mathcal{E} = -d\Phi_B/dt$ is not universally valid. Feynman (Lectures on Physics, Vol. II, Ch. 17) identified cases where the flux rule fails: it gives wrong answers for circuits that change topology, or for situations involving both moving and stationary parts. The correct approach is to use the Lorentz force law $\mathbf{f} = \mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}$ directly, integrating around the circuit. The flux rule is a convenient heuristic that works for most practical situations but is not a fundamental law.

**Self-inductance and the Neumann formula.** The Neumann formula for self-inductance involves a divergent integral (the denominator $|\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2|$ vanishes when the two line elements coincide). This divergence is resolved by giving the wire a finite radius $a$ and computing the internal inductance separately. For a round wire of radius $a$: the internal contribution is $\mu_0\ell/(8\pi)$ (independent of $a$) and the external contribution depends logarithmically on $a$.

**Magnetic energy and force.** The force between two circuits can be computed from the energy: $F = -\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}$ where $x$ is the separation. For two coupled circuits: $F = -I_1 I_2 \frac{\partial M}{\partial x}$. This provides a convenient way to compute forces without integrating the Lorentz force density.

**Synthesis.** Faraday's law is the dynamical heart of electromagnetism: it introduces time-dependence and creates the coupling between electric and magnetic fields that makes wave propagation possible. The foundational insight is that a changing magnetic field creates a curling electric field — not a conservative field that can be written as the gradient of a potential, but a circulating field with non-zero line integral around any loop enclosing the changing flux. The central message is that inductance — both mutual and self — is a purely geometric property of circuits, determined by the Neumann formula and characterised by the coupling coefficient $k$. Putting these together, Faraday's law provides the third Maxwell equation ($\text{curl}\,\mathbf{E} = -\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t$), which together with the Ampere-Maxwell law [10.03.04] forms the dynamical pair that supports electromagnetic waves [10.04.02].

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Reciprocity of mutual inductance).** $M_{12} = M_{21}$.

*Proof.* From the Neumann formula: $M_{12} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\oint_{C_1}\oint_{C_2}\frac{d\mathbf{l}_1\cdot d\mathbf{l}_2}{|\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2|}$. The dot product and the distance are symmetric under interchange of indices 1 and 2. Therefore $M_{12} = M_{21}$. $\square$

**Proposition (Energy stored in an inductor).** The work done to establish a current $I$ in an inductor $L$ is $W = \frac{1}{2}LI^2$.

*Proof.* The EMF across the inductor is $\mathcal{E} = -L\,dI/dt$ (opposing the increase). The external source must provide voltage $V = L\,dI/dt$. The power supplied is $P = VI = LI\,dI/dt$. The total energy stored is $W = \int_0^t P\,dt' = \int_0^I LI'\,dI' = \frac{1}{2}LI^2$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Vector potential [10.02.02]** provides the Neumann formula for inductance and the link between flux and the line integral of $\mathbf{A}$.
- **Displacement current [10.03.04]** is the fourth Maxwell equation; Faraday's law is the third. Together they form the dynamical pair.
- **EM waves [10.04.02]** result from the coupling of Faraday's law and the Ampere-Maxwell law.
- **Poynting vector [10.03.05]** describes energy flow; the inductor energy $LI^2/2$ is stored in the magnetic field $B^2/(2\mu_0)$.
- **Faraday tensor [10.06.01]** encodes both $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$; Faraday's law is one component of the exterior derivative $dF = 0$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Faraday's discovery of electromagnetic induction in 1831 was one of the most important experiments in the history of physics. He found that a changing magnetic field induces an electric current, connecting electricity and magnetism for the first time. Faraday had no mathematical training and described his results in terms of "lines of force" — what we now call field lines.

Maxwell formalised Faraday's results mathematically in 1865, expressing the law in differential form as $\text{curl}\,\mathbf{E} = -\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t$. The integral form (the flux rule) is often attributed to Faraday himself, though the precise mathematical statement was formulated later.

Lenz's law (1834) provided the sign convention and was recognised as a consequence of energy conservation. The Neumann formula for mutual inductance was derived by Franz Ernst Neumann in 1845, providing the first quantitative theory of induction.

The concept of self-inductance was introduced by Joseph Henry in 1832 (independently of Faraday), and the unit of inductance (the Henry) is named after him. Henry discovered self-inductance before Faraday published his results on mutual induction, though Faraday's work was completed first.
