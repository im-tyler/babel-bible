## Intuition [Beginner]

When a charged particle accelerates, it radiates electromagnetic waves. By energy conservation, the radiated energy must come from somewhere — it comes from the particle's own kinetic energy. This means the radiation exerts a **back-reaction force** on the particle, slowing it down. This force is called the **radiation reaction** or the **Abraham-Lorentz force**.

The radiation reaction force for a non-relativistic charge is:

$$\mathbf{F}_{\rm rad} = \frac{\mu_0 q^2}{6\pi c}\dot{\mathbf{a}}$$

where $\dot{\mathbf{a}}$ is the rate of change of acceleration (the "jerk"). This is a peculiar force: it depends not on the velocity or acceleration, but on the *derivative* of the acceleration.

This leads to two famous problems. First, the **runaway solutions**: if a particle is released from rest with no external force, the Abraham-Lorentz equation $\mathbf{F}_{\rm rad} = m\mathbf{a}$ admits solutions where the acceleration grows exponentially, violating conservation of energy. Second, the **pre-acceleration problem**: to avoid runaways, one must impose a boundary condition that requires the particle to start accelerating *before* the force is applied, violating causality.

These pathologies signal that classical electrodynamics breaks down at very small distances. Quantum mechanics resolves the problems, but the classical Abraham-Lorentz force remains the correct description for macroscopic charged particles.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The radiation reaction coefficient is $\tau = \mu_0 q^2/(6\pi mc)$. For an electron:

$\tau = 4\pi\times 10^{-7}\times(1.6\times 10^{-19})^2/(6\pi\times 9.11\times 10^{-31}\times 3\times 10^8)$

$= 4\pi\times 10^{-7}\times 2.56\times 10^{-38}/(6\pi\times 2.73\times 10^{-22})$

$\approx 6.3\times 10^{-24}$ s.

The characteristic time $\tau \sim 10^{-23}$ s is the time for light to cross the classical electron radius ($r_e \sim 10^{-15}$ m). On any timescale longer than $\tau$, the radiation reaction is a small perturbation. Only at sub-atomic timescales do the pathologies of the Abraham-Lorentz equation become relevant.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**The Abraham-Lorentz equation.** The equation of motion for a non-relativistic charged particle including radiation reaction is:

$$m\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{F}_{\rm ext} + \tau m\dot{\mathbf{a}}$$

where $\tau = \mu_0 q^2/(6\pi mc)$ is the **radiation reaction time** and $\mathbf{F}_{\rm ext}$ is the external force.

**Derivation from energy conservation.** The power radiated by the Larmor formula [10.07.01] is $P = \mu_0 q^2 a^2/(6\pi c)$. The work done by the radiation reaction force must supply this energy:

$$\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\mathbf{F}_{\rm rad}\cdot\mathbf{v}\,dt = -\frac{\mu_0 q^2}{6\pi c}\int_{t_1}^{t_2}a^2\,dt$$

Integrating the right side by parts: $\int a^2\,dt = -\int\mathbf{v}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{a}}\,dt + [\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{a}]_{t_1}^{t_2}$. For periodic motion (or motion that starts and ends at rest), the boundary term vanishes, giving:

$$\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\left(\mathbf{F}_{\rm rad} - \frac{\mu_0 q^2}{6\pi c}\dot{\mathbf{a}}\right)\cdot\mathbf{v}\,dt = 0$$

Since this holds for arbitrary $\mathbf{v}(t)$: $\mathbf{F}_{\rm rad} = (\mu_0 q^2/6\pi c)\dot{\mathbf{a}} = \tau m\dot{\mathbf{a}}$.

**Runaway solutions.** For $\mathbf{F}_{\rm ext} = 0$: $m\mathbf{a} = \tau m\dot{\mathbf{a}}$, giving $\mathbf{a}(t) = \mathbf{a}_0 e^{t/\tau}$. The acceleration grows exponentially with timescale $\tau \sim 10^{-23}$ s. This is unphysical: a free charge spontaneously accelerates to infinite speed.

**Pre-acceleration (acausal) solution.** Imposing the boundary condition $\mathbf{a}(t) \to 0$ as $t \to \infty$ (no runaways at late times) gives:

$$\mathbf{a}(t) = \frac{1}{\tau m}\int_t^\infty \mathbf{F}_{\rm ext}(t')\,e^{(t-t')/\tau}\,dt'$$

The acceleration at time $t$ depends on the force at *future* times $t' > t$, weighted by $e^{-(t'-t)/\tau}$. The particle anticipates the force by a time of order $\tau \sim 10^{-23}$ s. This violates causality, but only on timescales so short that quantum mechanics dominates.

**The Lorentz-Dirac equation (relativistic).** The covariant generalisation of the Abraham-Lorentz equation is the **Lorentz-Dirac equation**:

$$m\frac{du^\mu}{d\tau_p} = F^\mu_{\rm ext} + \tau m\left(\frac{d^2 u^\mu}{d\tau_p^2} + \frac{du^\nu}{d\tau_p}\frac{du_\nu}{d\tau_p}u^\mu\right)$$

where $\tau_p$ is proper time. The second term in the radiation reaction is the **Schott term** (representing the near-field energy that is temporarily stored and then released).

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (Abraham-Lorentz force from energy conservation).**

**Theorem.** *A non-relativistic charge q subject to external force F_ext satisfies the equation of motion $m\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{F}_{\rm ext} + \tau m\dot{\mathbf{a}}$ where $\tau = \mu_0 q^2/(6\pi mc)$.*

*Proof.* By Larmor's formula, the radiated power is $P = \mu_0 q^2 a^2/(6\pi c)$. The work-energy theorem requires that the radiation reaction force $\mathbf{F}_{\rm rad}$ satisfies:

$$W_{\rm rad} = \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\mathbf{F}_{\rm rad}\cdot\mathbf{v}\,dt = -\frac{\mu_0 q^2}{6\pi c}\int_{t_1}^{t_2}a^2\,dt$$

Integrating the right side by parts (assuming periodic motion or $a(t_1) = a(t_2) = 0$):

$$\int_{t_1}^{t_2}a^2\,dt = [\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{a}]_{t_1}^{t_2} - \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\mathbf{v}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{a}}\,dt = -\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\mathbf{v}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{a}}\,dt$$

Therefore:

$$\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\left(\mathbf{F}_{\rm rad} + \frac{\mu_0 q^2}{6\pi c}\dot{\mathbf{a}}\right)\cdot\mathbf{v}\,dt = 0$$

Since this holds for all trajectories, $\mathbf{F}_{\rm rad} = -(\mu_0 q^2/6\pi c)\dot{\mathbf{a}}$ up to a total time derivative (which does not contribute to the integral). Choosing the simplest form gives the Abraham-Lorentz force. $\square$

**Bridge.** The radiation reaction problem builds toward one of the deepest unsolved problems in classical physics: the self-energy of a point charge. The foundational insight is that radiation carries energy away from the source, and this energy loss must appear as a force on the source. The central message is that the resulting equation of motion is third-order in time, leading to unphysical runaway and acausal solutions. This is exactly the signal that classical electrodynamics is incomplete at the atomic scale. Putting these together, the radiation reaction is the classical limit of the quantum vacuum polarisation effects in QED, the self-energy divergence is resolved by mass renormalisation, and the Abraham-Lorentz force determines the energy loss of particles in accelerators [10.07.05].

Compute the radiation reaction time $\tau$ for a proton ($q = e$, $m = 1.67\times 10^{-27}$ kg).

$\tau = \mu_0 q^2/(6\pi mc)$.

The proton's $\tau$ is about $10^4$ times smaller than the electron's, because the proton is $10^3$ times heavier. The radiation reaction is even less important for protons than for electrons.
</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that for a harmonic oscillator $x(t) = x_0\cos(\omega t)$, the time-averaged radiation reaction power equals the Larmor power.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\langle P_{\rm rad}\rangle = \langle\mathbf{F}_{\rm rad}\cdot\mathbf{v}\rangle$ with $\mathbf{F}_{\rm rad} = \tau m\dot{\mathbf{a}}$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $x = x_0\cos(\omega t)$: $v = -\omega x_0\sin(\omega t)$, $a = -\omega^2 x_0\cos(\omega t)$, $\dot{a} = \omega^3 x_0\sin(\omega t)$.

$F_{\rm rad}\cdot v = \tau m\omega^3 x_0\sin(\omega t)\cdot(-\omega x_0\sin(\omega t)) = -\tau m\omega^4 x_0^2\sin^2(\omega t)$.

$\langle P_{\rm rad}\rangle = -\tau m\omega^4 x_0^2/2$.

The Larmor power: $P_{\rm Larmor} = \mu_0 q^2 a^2/(6\pi c) = \tau m\omega^4 x_0^2\cos^2(\omega t)$. Time average: $\langle P_{\rm Larmor}\rangle = \tau m\omega^4 x_0^2/2$.

The radiation reaction removes energy at the same rate as Larmor predicts it is radiated: $\langle P_{\rm rad}\rangle = -\langle P_{\rm Larmor}\rangle$. Energy is conserved.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

A charge is released from rest at $t = 0$ and acted on by a constant external force $F_0$ for time $T$, then the force is removed. Solve the Abraham-Lorentz equation and show that the pre-acceleration is confined to times $|t| \lesssim \tau$ before $t = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the pre-acceleration solution $a(t) = (1/\tau m)\int_t^\infty F(t')e^{(t-t')/\tau}\,dt'$ with $F(t') = F_0$ for $0 < t' < T$ and $F(t') = 0$ otherwise.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $t < 0$: $a(t) = (F_0/\tau m)\int_0^T e^{(t-t')/\tau}\,dt' = (F_0/\tau m)e^{t/\tau}(1 - e^{-T/\tau})$.

For $0 < t < T$: $a(t) = (F_0/\tau m)\int_t^T e^{(t-t')/\tau}\,dt' = (F_0/m)(1 - e^{(t-T)/\tau})$.

For $t > T$: $a(t) = 0$ (no force, and the boundary condition prevents runaways).

At $t = 0$: $a(0) = (F_0/\tau m)(1 - e^{-T/\tau})$. For $T \gg \tau$: $a(0) \approx F_0/m$ (the expected Newtonian result).

For $t \ll 0$: $a(t) \approx (F_0/\tau m)e^{t/\tau} \to 0$ exponentially. The pre-acceleration is confined to times $|t| \lesssim \tau$ before $t = 0$, consistent with the claim that the acausality occurs on the timescale $\tau \sim 10^{-23}$ s.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the Abraham-Lorentz equation for a free particle ($F_{\rm ext} = 0$) has the general solution $\mathbf{v}(t) = \mathbf{v}_0 + \mathbf{A}e^{t/\tau}$ where $\mathbf{A}$ is an arbitrary constant. Identify the runaway mode.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The equation is $m\mathbf{a} = \tau m\dot{\mathbf{a}}$, i.e., $\dot{\mathbf{a}} = \mathbf{a}/\tau$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $F_{\rm ext} = 0$: $\mathbf{a} = \tau\dot{\mathbf{a}}$, giving $\mathbf{a}(t) = \mathbf{A}e^{t/\tau}$. Integrating: $\mathbf{v}(t) = \mathbf{v}_0 + \tau\mathbf{A}e^{t/\tau}$.

For $\mathbf{A} \neq 0$: $|\mathbf{v}| \to \infty$ as $t \to \infty$. This is the **runaway mode**: a free charge spontaneously accelerates to infinite speed with timescale $\tau \sim 10^{-23}$ s. The only non-runaway solution is $\mathbf{A} = 0$, giving $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}_0$ (uniform motion, as expected).

The runaway solution is unphysical and is eliminated by imposing the boundary condition $\mathbf{a}(t) \to 0$ as $t \to \infty$. But this boundary condition is imposed at *future* times, which leads to the pre-acceleration problem: the present acceleration depends on future forces.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

The energy radiated per revolution by an electron in a circular orbit of radius $R$ and speed $v$ is $\Delta E = (q^2/(3\epsilon_0))(v^4/c^4 R)$ per orbit. Compute this for a 1 GeV electron in a 100 m radius ring.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\Delta E = q^2\gamma^4/(3\epsilon_0 R)$ (the Larmor formula integrated over one orbit, with $a = v^2/R$ and relativistic correction $\gamma^4$). For $\gamma \approx E/(m_e c^2) = 10^9/(511\times 10^3) \approx 1957$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $E = 1$ GeV: $\gamma = E/(m_ec^2) = 10^9/(0.511\times 10^6) = 1957$.

$\Delta E = \frac{e^2\gamma^4}{3\epsilon_0 R} = \frac{(1.6\times 10^{-19})^2\times(1957)^4}{3\times 8.85\times 10^{-12}\times 100}$

$= \frac{2.56\times 10^{-38}\times 1.466\times 10^{13}}{2.655\times 10^{-9}} = \frac{3.75\times 10^{-25}}{2.655\times 10^{-9}} = 1.41\times 10^{-16}$ J $= 0.88$ keV.

The electron loses 0.88 keV per revolution to synchrotron radiation. This energy must be replaced by the RF cavities in the accelerator. For a ring of circumference $2\pi\times 100 = 628$ m, the revolution frequency is $f = c/(2\pi R) = 4.77\times 10^5$ Hz, and the total radiated power is $P = f\Delta E = 6.7\times 10^{-11}$ W per electron. For a beam of $10^{11}$ electrons: $P = 6.7$ kW — a significant power loss.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the Lorentz-Dirac equation conserves four-momentum by verifying that the radiation reaction four-force $K^\mu_{\rm rad} = \tau m(\ddot{u}^\mu + \dot{u}^\nu\dot{u}_\nu u^\mu)$ is orthogonal to the four-velocity: $K^\mu_{\rm rad}u_\mu = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate $u^\mu u_\mu = c^2$ twice in proper time and use the result to show the Schott term cancels the $\dot{u}^2$ term.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From $u^\mu u_\mu = c^2$: $\dot{u}^\mu u_\mu + u^\mu\dot{u}_\mu = 2\dot{u}^\mu u_\mu = 0$, so $\dot{u}\cdot u = 0$. Differentiating again: $\ddot{u}\cdot u + \dot{u}\cdot\dot{u} = 0$, so $\ddot{u}\cdot u = -\dot{u}\cdot\dot{u}$.

Compute $K^\mu_{\rm rad}u_\mu = \tau m(\ddot{u}^\mu u_\mu + \dot{u}^\nu\dot{u}_\nu u^\mu u_\mu) = \tau m(-\dot{u}^2 + \dot{u}^2 c^2/c^2) = \tau m(-\dot{u}^2 + \dot{u}^2) = 0$.

The orthogonality $K^\mu u_\mu = 0$ ensures that the radiation reaction preserves the mass-shell condition $p^\mu p_\mu = m^2c^2$. The first term ($\ddot{u}^\mu$, the Schott term) represents the near-field energy that is temporarily stored and then released as radiation. The second term ($\dot{u}^2 u^\mu$) is the net radiation loss.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The **classical electron radius** $r_e = e^2/(4\pi\epsilon_0 mc^2) \approx 2.82\times 10^{-15}$ m is obtained by setting the electrostatic self-energy equal to the rest energy. Show that $\tau = r_e/(2c)$ and discuss why this relation connects the radiation reaction to the electron's spatial extent.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\tau = \mu_0 e^2/(6\pi mc)$. $r_e = e^2/(4\pi\epsilon_0 mc^2)$. Use $\mu_0\epsilon_0 = 1/c^2$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\tau = \mu_0 e^2/(6\pi mc) = \mu_0\epsilon_0 c^2\cdot e^2/(6\pi\epsilon_0 mc^3) = e^2/(6\pi\epsilon_0 mc^3)$.

$r_e = e^2/(4\pi\epsilon_0 mc^2)$.

$\tau/r_e = (e^2/(6\pi\epsilon_0 mc^3))/(e^2/(4\pi\epsilon_0 mc^2)) = (4\pi)/(6\pi c) = 2/(3c)$. Wait: $\tau = r_e\cdot(2/(3c))$. More precisely: $\tau = (2/3)(r_e/c)$.

This means $\tau$ is the light-travel time across the classical electron radius (up to a factor of 2/3). The radiation reaction time is the time it takes a light signal to cross the electron. This connects the radiation reaction to the electron's spatial structure: a finite-size electron would not have the self-energy divergence, and the radiation reaction would arise from the finite propagation time of the field across the electron's extent.

In QED, the electron is treated as a point particle, and the divergences are handled by renormalisation. The classical electron radius $r_e$ is not the actual size of the electron (which is pointlike to $< 10^{-18}$ m experimentally), but a characteristic scale where classical electrodynamics breaks down.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

The **Landau-Lifshitz equation** is an approximation to the Lorentz-Dirac equation that avoids the third-order structure by treating the radiation reaction as a perturbation:

$$m\dot{u}^\mu = F^\mu_{\rm ext} + \tau(\partial_\nu F^{\mu\nu}_{\rm ext}u^\nu + F^{\mu\nu}_{\rm ext}\dot{u}_\nu)$$

Show that this is a second-order ODE (no $\ddot{u}$ term) by substituting $\dot{u}^\nu = F^\nu_{\rm ext}/m$ in the radiation reaction term.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Landau-Lifshitz approach iterates once: replace $\dot{u}$ in the radiation reaction by the zeroth-order (Lorentz force) value, eliminating the third derivative.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Starting from the Lorentz-Dirac equation: $m\dot{u}^\mu = F^\mu_{\rm ext} + \tau m(\ddot{u}^\mu + \dot{u}^2 u^\mu)$.

The key idea: for $\tau$ small, the radiation reaction is a perturbation. To first order in $\tau$, replace $\dot{u}^\mu$ by $F^\mu_{\rm ext}/m$ (the zeroth-order Lorentz force) everywhere it appears in the radiation reaction:

$$\tau m\ddot{u}^\mu \approx \tau\frac{d}{d\tau_p}F^\mu_{\rm ext} = \tau(\partial_\nu F^{\mu\nu}u^\nu + F^{\mu\nu}\dot{u}_\nu) \approx \tau(\partial_\nu F^{\mu\nu}u^\nu + F^{\mu\nu}F_{\nu}/m)$$

The result is:

$$m\dot{u}^\mu = F^\mu_{\rm ext} + \tau\left(\partial_\nu F^{\mu\nu}u^\nu + \frac{1}{m}F^{\mu\nu}F_\nu\right) + \tau\dot{u}^2 u^\mu$$

This is a **second-order** ODE: the right side contains $u^\mu$ and $F_{\rm ext}(x)$ (no derivatives of $u$ beyond first order). The Landau-Lifshitz equation is free of runaways and pre-acceleration, and agrees with the Lorentz-Dirac equation to order $\tau$. It is the preferred equation for practical calculations in accelerator physics.
</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**The self-energy and mass renormalisation.** The electrostatic self-energy of a sphere of charge $q$ and radius $R$ is $U = q^2/(8\pi\epsilon_0 R)$. For a point charge ($R \to 0$), $U \to \infty$. The total mass is $m = m_0 + U/c^2$ where $m_0$ is the "bare" mechanical mass. As $R \to 0$: $m_0$ must be adjusted (renormalised) to keep $m$ finite. This is the classical analogue of QED mass renormalisation.

**The Dirac derivation (1938).** Dirac derived the Lorentz-Dirac equation by analysing the force on a spherical shell of charge, carefully separating the retarded and advanced fields at the shell surface. The difference $F_{\rm ret} - F_{\rm adv}$ gives the radiation reaction force, free of divergences. This approach avoids the self-energy problem by never computing the self-field energy.

**Experimental tests.** The radiation reaction has been directly measured in laser-electron interactions (Cole et al., 2018; Poder et al., 2018). Ultra-intense laser pulses ($I > 10^{20}$ W/cm$^2$) collide with GeV electrons, and the radiation loss modifies the electron trajectories in a way that is consistent with the Landau-Lifshitz equation. At even higher intensities ($I > 10^{23}$ W/cm$^2$), quantum radiation reaction effects (photon emission probabilities replacing continuous energy loss) are expected to become important.

**Synthesis.** The radiation reaction problem reveals the limits of classical electrodynamics: a consistent description of a point charge interacting with its own field leads to unphysical solutions. The foundational insight is that the back-reaction from radiation is a real physical effect that modifies the motion of charged particles. The central message is that the Abraham-Lorentz and Lorentz-Dirac equations are the best classical descriptions, valid on timescales much longer than $\tau \sim 10^{-23}$ s. Putting these together, the radiation reaction determines the energy loss in particle accelerators [10.07.05], the Landau-Lifshitz approximation provides the practical computational tool, and the resolution of the pathologies requires the quantum field theory treatment where the electron is a point particle with renormalised mass.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Energy balance).** For periodic motion, the time-averaged power delivered by the Abraham-Lorentz force equals the time-averaged Larmor radiated power.

*Proof.* The radiation reaction power is $P_{\rm rad} = \mathbf{F}_{\rm rad}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \tau m\dot{\mathbf{a}}\cdot\mathbf{v}$. Time-averaging over a period $T$: $\langle\tau m\dot{\mathbf{a}}\cdot\mathbf{v}\rangle = \tau m\langle\dot{\mathbf{a}}\cdot\mathbf{v}\rangle = \tau m\langle d(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{v})/dt - a^2\rangle = -\tau m\langle a^2\rangle$ (the total derivative averages to zero for periodic motion). The Larmor power is $\langle P_{\rm Larmor}\rangle = \mu_0 q^2\langle a^2\rangle/(6\pi c) = \tau m\langle a^2\rangle$. So $\langle P_{\rm rad}\rangle = -\langle P_{\rm Larmor}\rangle$: the radiation reaction removes energy at exactly the rate predicted by Larmor. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The radiation reaction problem has a long and controversial history. The Abraham-Lorentz force was derived independently by Abraham (1904) and Lorentz (1909) using different methods. The runaway solutions were immediately recognised as a problem, and various resolutions were proposed over the following decades.

Dirac (1938) gave the most elegant derivation of the relativistic equation by using the difference between retarded and advanced fields to isolate the radiation reaction, avoiding the self-energy divergence. However, the pre-acceleration problem remained.

The Landau-Lifshitz equation (1951) provided a practical resolution by reducing the third-order equation to a second-order perturbative approximation. This is now the standard equation used in accelerator physics.

The philosophical significance is that the radiation reaction problem is the first place where classical field theory encounters a genuine inconsistency (as opposed to a mere inaccuracy). The resolution in QED through renormalisation shows that the classical theory is an effective theory that breaks down at short distances, analogous to how Newtonian mechanics breaks down at high velocities.
