## Intuition [Beginner]

The electric dipole radiation discussed in [10.07.02] is the strongest type of radiation from a small source. But it is not the only type. Just as a charge distribution can have higher multipole moments (dipole, quadrupole, octupole), the radiation it emits has higher multipole components.

The next two terms in the expansion are **magnetic dipole** (M1) and **electric quadrupole** (E2) radiation. Both are suppressed compared to electric dipole (E1) by a factor of roughly $(kd)^2$ where $d$ is the source size and $k$ is the wave number. For an atom ($d \sim 10^{-10}$ m) emitting optical light ($k \sim 10^7$ m$^{-1}$): $(kd)^2 \sim 10^{-6}$. The higher multipoles are a million times weaker than the electric dipole.

However, if the electric dipole moment is zero (by symmetry), the leading contribution comes from M1 and E2. This happens in many atomic and nuclear transitions. The "forbidden" transitions in spectroscopy are exactly those where E1 is forbidden but M1 or E2 are allowed.

Magnetic dipole radiation comes from oscillating current loops (equivalently, oscillating magnetic moments). Electric quadrupole radiation comes from charge distributions that have no net dipole but have a quadrupole moment — like two dipoles arranged in opposition.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A hydrogen atom transition from the 2s to the 1s state has zero electric dipole matrix element (by parity). The leading contribution is E2 (electric quadrupole), suppressed by $(ka_0)^2 \sim (2\pi \times 10^7 \times 10^{-10})^2 \sim 4\times 10^{-6}$.

The transition rate is reduced by this factor compared to a typical E1 transition. Instead of a lifetime of $10^{-8}$ s (typical for E1), the 2s state has a lifetime of about 0.1 s — it is **metastable**. This is why the 2s state of hydrogen is called a "forbidden" state: the E1 transition is forbidden by selection rules, and the higher multipole transitions are very slow.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Multipole expansion of the radiation field.** The retarded vector potential for a localised source of size $d \ll \lambda$ is expanded in powers of $kd$:

$$\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi r}\left[\dot{\mathbf{p}}(t_r) + \frac{1}{2c}\ddot{\mathbf{Q}}(t_r)\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}} + \ldots\right]$$

where $\mathbf{p}$ is the electric dipole moment and $\mathbf{Q}$ is the electric quadrupole moment tensor. The magnetic dipole contribution is:

$$\mathbf{A}_{M1} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi cr}\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\dot{\mathbf{m}}(t_r)$$

where $\mathbf{m} = \frac{1}{2}\int\mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}')\,d^3r'$ is the magnetic dipole moment.

**Magnetic dipole radiation.** The radiation fields for an oscillating magnetic dipole $\mathbf{m}(t) = m_0 e^{-i\omega t}\hat{\mathbf{z}}$:

$$\mathbf{B}_{M1} = \frac{\mu_0 k^2 m_0}{4\pi}\frac{e^{i(kr-\omega t)}}{r}\sin\theta\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}, \qquad \mathbf{E}_{M1} = -\frac{\mu_0 k^2\omega m_0}{4\pi}\frac{e^{i(kr-\omega t)}}{r}\sin\theta\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$$

Note the E-field is in the $\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$ direction (perpendicular to the E1 case, where E is in $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$). The total radiated power:

$$P_{M1} = \frac{\mu_0 m_0^2\omega^4}{12\pi c^3}$$

**Electric quadrupole radiation.** The electric quadrupole moment tensor is:

$$Q_{ij} = \int(3r'_ir'_j - r'^2\delta_{ij})\rho(\mathbf{r}')\,d^3r'$$

The radiated power is:

$$P_{E2} = \frac{\mu_0\omega^6}{2880\pi c^5}\sum_{i,j}|\ddot{Q}_{ij}|^2$$

**Suppression factors.** The ratio of M1 or E2 to E1:

$$\frac{P_{M1}}{P_{E1}} \sim (kd)^2, \qquad \frac{P_{E2}}{P_{E1}} \sim (kd)^2$$

where $d$ is the source size. For atoms ($d \sim 10^{-10}$ m) at optical frequencies ($k \sim 10^7$ m$^{-1}$): $(kd)^2 \sim 10^{-6}$.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (Magnetic dipole radiation from the vector potential).**

**Theorem.** *An oscillating magnetic dipole $m(t) = m_0 e^{-i\omega t}$ radiates power $P = \mu_0 m_0^2\omega^4/(12\pi c^3)$ with the same $\sin^2\theta$ angular pattern as electric dipole radiation.*

*Proof.* The vector potential for the magnetic dipole is obtained from the second term in the multipole expansion. Using the identity $\int\mathbf{J}\,d^3r' = -i\omega\mathbf{p}$ and $\frac{1}{2}\int\mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{J}\,d^3r' = -i\omega\mathbf{m}$:

$$\mathbf{A}_{M1} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi r}(-i\omega)\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\mathbf{m}(t_r)\frac{ik}{\omega} = \frac{i\mu_0 k}{4\pi r}\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\mathbf{m}_0\,e^{i(kr-\omega t)}$$

Computing $\mathbf{B} = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ in the far zone ($kr \gg 1$):

$$\mathbf{B}_{M1} = ik\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\mathbf{A}_{M1} = -\frac{\mu_0 k^2}{4\pi r}(\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times(\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\mathbf{m}_0))\,e^{i(kr-\omega t)}$$

Using $\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times(\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\mathbf{m}_0) = \hat{\mathbf{r}}(\hat{\mathbf{r}}\cdot\mathbf{m}_0) - \mathbf{m}_0$: the transverse component is $-\mathbf{m}_{0,\perp} = -m_0\sin\theta\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$. So $\mathbf{B}_{M1} = (\mu_0 k^2 m_0/4\pi r)\sin\theta\,e^{i(kr-\omega t)}\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$.

The Poynting vector: $\langle S\rangle = (\mu_0 k^4 m_0^2/(32\pi^2 c r^2))\sin^2\theta$. Integrating: $P = \mu_0 m_0^2\omega^4/(12\pi c^3)$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The multipole expansion builds toward a complete description of radiation from arbitrary charge-current distributions. The foundational insight is that each multipole order is suppressed by $(kd)^2$ relative to the previous one, creating a hierarchy of radiation channels. The central message is that when the dominant E1 channel is forbidden by symmetry, the higher multipoles M1 and E2 become the leading radiation mechanism. This is exactly the situation in forbidden atomic transitions and nuclear gamma decay. Putting these together, the multipole expansion generalises the electric dipole radiation [10.07.02] to all orders, provides the selection rules used in spectroscopy and nuclear physics, and connects to the scattering amplitudes [10.07.06] where the induced multipole moments determine the cross-section.

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

State the parity selection rules for E1, M1, and E2 transitions. Explain why the 2s-to-1s transition in hydrogen is E1-forbidden but M1-allowed.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

E1 changes parity (odd operator). M1 does not change parity (even operator). E2 does not change parity (even operator).
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Parity selection rules:**

- **E1:** Parity changes. The electric dipole operator $\mathbf{r}$ is odd under parity ($\mathbf{r} \to -\mathbf{r}$).
- **M1:** Parity does not change. The magnetic dipole operator $\mathbf{L} + 2\mathbf{S}$ is even under parity (angular momentum is an axial vector).
- **E2:** Parity does not change. The quadrupole operator $r_ir_j$ is even under parity.

**Hydrogen 2s to 1s:** Both states are s-orbitals with even parity. E1 is forbidden (would require odd-to-even parity change). M1 is allowed (even-to-even) but very weak. The dominant decay channel for 2s $\to$ 1s is actually two-photon E1E1 emission (not a single photon), with a lifetime of about 0.12 s. Single-photon M1 is further suppressed.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

The traceless quadrupole tensor for a linear charge distribution along the z-axis is $Q_{33} = 2q d^2$, $Q_{11} = Q_{22} = -qd^2$. Compute the E2 radiated power for $\rho(\mathbf{r}, t) = q[\delta(z-d) + \delta(z+d)]\cos(\omega t)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The quadrupole moment of two charges $\pm q$ at positions $\pm d\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ oscillating in and out: $Q_{33} = 2q d^2\cos(\omega t)$, $Q_{11} = Q_{22} = -qd^2\cos(\omega t)$, all others zero.

## Advanced results [Master]

**The full multipole expansion.** The radiation field can be decomposed into electric and magnetic multipoles of order $\ell = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$:

- **Electric multipole E$\ell$:** radiated power $\propto k^{2\ell+2}|Q_{E\ell}|^2$
- **Magnetic multipole M$\ell$:** radiated power $\propto k^{2\ell+2}|Q_{M\ell}|^2$

The angular distribution of the $\ell$-th multipole involves the spherical harmonics $Y_\ell^m(\theta, \phi)$. E1 ($\ell = 1$) gives $\sin^2\theta$. E2 ($\ell = 2$) gives a pattern with four lobes involving $\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta$.

**Selection rules in quantum mechanics.** The quantum mechanical transition rate for multipole radiation follows from the matrix elements of the corresponding operators:

- E1: $\Delta\ell = \pm 1$, parity changes
- M1: $\Delta\ell = 0$, parity preserved (spin-flip transitions)
- E2: $\Delta\ell = 0, \pm 2$ (not $\Delta\ell = \pm 1$), parity preserved

These selection rules determine which atomic and nuclear transitions are "allowed" (E1) or "forbidden" (higher multipoles). Forbidden transitions are critical for lasers (the metastable upper state provides population inversion) and for astrophysical diagnostics (forbidden lines identify low-density gas in nebulae).

**Synthesis.** The multipole expansion of radiation fields extends the electric dipole treatment to the complete description of radiation from any source. The foundational insight is that the radiation channels form a hierarchy suppressed by powers of $(kd)^2$, with the dominant channel determined by the symmetry of the source. The central message is that when the dominant channel is forbidden by selection rules, the subdominant channels become physically important, producing the "forbidden" transitions that are central to atomic spectroscopy, nuclear physics, and astrophysics. Putting these together, the multipole expansion generalises the dipole radiation [10.07.02], the selection rules connect to the quantum mechanical treatment of transitions, and the scattering cross-sections [10.07.06] decompose into multipole channels that match the radiation multipoles developed here.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (M1 power formula).** The total radiated power from an oscillating magnetic dipole $\mathbf{m}(t) = m_0\cos(\omega t)\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ is $P = \mu_0 m_0^2\omega^4/(12\pi c^3)$.

*Proof.* The time-averaged Poynting vector is $\langle S\rangle = (\mu_0 k^4 m_0^2)/(32\pi^2 c r^2)\sin^2\theta$. Integrating over solid angle: $\int\sin^2\theta\,d\Omega = 8\pi/3$. So $P = (\mu_0 k^4 m_0^2)/(32\pi^2 c)\cdot(8\pi/3) = \mu_0 k^4 m_0^2/(12\pi c)$. Using $k = \omega/c$: $P = \mu_0 m_0^2\omega^4/(12\pi c^3)$. Note the extra factor of $1/c^2$ compared to the E1 formula $P_{E1} = \mu_0 p_0^2\omega^4/(12\pi c)$: M1 is weaker by $\sim 1/c^2$ for comparable moments, reflecting the $(kd)^2$ suppression. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Electric dipole radiation [10.07.02]** is the leading term of the multipole expansion developed here.
- **Larmor formula [10.07.01]** is recovered from E1 for a single charge.
- **Radiation reaction [10.07.04]** involves the self-field at the source, which includes contributions from all multipoles.
- **Thomson scattering [10.07.06]** is E1 scattering; the multipole expansion provides the higher-order corrections.
- **Vector potential [10.02.02]** is the quantity expanded in the multipole series.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The multipole expansion of radiation fields was developed by Mie (1908) and Debye (1909) in the context of scattering from spherical particles. The application to atomic and nuclear transition rates was made by Weisskopf (1951) and Blatt and Weisskopf (1952), who derived the selection rules and the semi-classical transition rates used in nuclear physics.

The discovery of "forbidden" spectral lines in astrophysical nebulae (Bowen, 1928) was a landmark: these lines were identified as M1 and E2 transitions from metastable states of O$^{2+}$ and N$^{+}$ ions. The fact that these transitions are observed in nebulae but not in laboratory sources is because laboratory gas densities are too high — collisions de-excite the metastable states before they can radiate. The low density of nebulae ($n \sim 10^3$ cm$^{-3}$) allows the forbidden transitions to proceed.
