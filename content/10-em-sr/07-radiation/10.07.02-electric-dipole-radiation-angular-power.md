## Intuition [Beginner]

When an electric charge oscillates back and forth, it radiates electromagnetic waves. The simplest radiating system is the **oscillating electric dipole** — two equal and opposite charges separated by a small distance that varies sinusoidally with time.

The radiation pattern from an oscillating dipole is not uniform. The radiated power is strongest in the directions perpendicular to the oscillation (the "equator" of the dipole) and zero along the axis of oscillation (the "poles"). The angular dependence goes as $\sin^2\theta$ where $\theta$ is the angle from the dipole axis. Think of a radio antenna: it radiates most strongly broadside to the antenna and not at all along its length.

The total power radiated by an oscillating dipole with dipole moment $p = p_0\cos(\omega t)$ is:

$$P = \frac{\mu_0 p_0^2 \omega^4}{12\pi c}$$

The power scales as $\omega^4$: doubling the frequency increases the radiated power by a factor of 16. This strong frequency dependence explains why higher-frequency radiation (UV, X-rays) is produced by atomic-scale dipoles while lower-frequency radiation (radio waves) requires macroscopic antennas.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A short radio antenna of length $\ell = 1$ m carries an oscillating current $I = I_0\cos(\omega t)$ with $I_0 = 1$ A and frequency $f = 100$ MHz. Find the total radiated power.

The antenna is an electric dipole with $p_0 = I_0\ell/\omega$. (The current $I_0$ flowing over the length $\ell$ creates a dipole moment $p = q\ell$ where $dq/dt = I_0$.) The angular frequency is $\omega = 2\pi\times 10^8$ rad/s.

$p_0 = I_0\ell/\omega = 1\times 1/(2\pi\times 10^8) = 1.59\times 10^{-9}$ C$\cdot$m.

$P = \mu_0 p_0^2\omega^4/(12\pi c) = \mu_0\omega^2(I_0\ell)^2/(12\pi c)$. Substituting: $P = 4\pi\times 10^{-7}\times(2\pi\times 10^8)^2\times 1/(12\pi\times 3\times 10^8) = 4\pi\times 10^{-7}\times 3.95\times 10^{17}\times 1/(12\pi\times 3\times 10^8) \approx 4.39$ W. About 4.4 watts of radiation from a 1-meter antenna at 100 MHz with 1 amp of current.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

An **oscillating electric dipole** has dipole moment $\mathbf{p}(t) = p_0\cos(\omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ (or more generally $\mathbf{p}(t) = \mathbf{p}_0 e^{-i\omega t}$, taking the real part).

**Retarded potential approach.** In the Lorenz gauge, the retarded vector potential is:

$$\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|}\,d^3r'$$

For a source of size $d$ much smaller than the wavelength ($d \ll \lambda = 2\pi c/\omega$) and the observation distance ($d \ll r$), expand the retarded time: $t_r \approx t - r/c + \hat{\mathbf{r}}\cdot\mathbf{r}'/c$. Keeping the leading correction (the dipole term):

$$\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi r}\dot{\mathbf{p}}(t_r) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi r}(-\omega p_0\sin(\omega t_r))\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$$

where $t_r = t - r/c$ is the retarded time.

**Radiation fields** (far field, $r \gg \lambda$). The electric and magnetic fields in the radiation zone are:

$$\mathbf{E} = -\frac{\mu_0 p_0\omega^2}{4\pi}\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\cos(\omega(t - r/c))\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$$

$$\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 p_0\omega^2}{4\pi c}\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\cos(\omega(t - r/c))\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$$

Both fields fall off as $1/r$ and are transverse (perpendicular to $\hat{\mathbf{r}}$). They satisfy $\mathbf{B} = \hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\mathbf{E}/c$.

**Time-averaged Poynting vector.**

$$\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle = \frac{\mu_0 p_0^2\omega^4}{32\pi^2 c}\frac{\sin^2\theta}{r^2}\,\hat{\mathbf{r}}$$

**Total radiated power.**

$$P = \oint\langle S\rangle r^2\,d\Omega = \frac{\mu_0 p_0^2\omega^4}{12\pi c}$$

This is the **electric dipole radiation formula**. Using $p_0 = q_0 d$ (charge times separation) or $p_0 = I_0\ell/\omega$ (current times length divided by angular frequency):

$$P = \frac{\mu_0 q_0^2 d^2\omega^4}{12\pi c} = \frac{\mu_0 I_0^2\ell^2\omega^2}{12\pi c}$$

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (Electric dipole radiation fields from the retarded potentials).**

**Theorem.** *An oscillating electric dipole $\mathbf{p}(t) = p_0 e^{-i\omega t}\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ radiates fields (in the far zone $r \gg \lambda$):*

$$\mathbf{E} = \frac{k^2 p_0}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{e^{i(kr - \omega t)}}{r}\sin\theta\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}, \qquad \mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 k^2\omega p_0}{4\pi}\frac{e^{i(kr - \omega t)}}{r}\sin\theta\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$$

*The total radiated power is $P = \mu_0 p_0^2\omega^4/(12\pi c)$.*

*Proof.* For a localized source with $\mathbf{p}(t) = \int\mathbf{r}'\rho(\mathbf{r}', t)\,d^3r'$, the retarded scalar and vector potentials can be expanded in powers of $kr'$ (multipole expansion). The leading (dipole) term for the vector potential is:

$$\mathbf{A}_{\text{dip}} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi r}(-i\omega\mathbf{p}_0)\,e^{i(kr - \omega t)}$$

From $\mathbf{A}$, compute $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$. In the far zone ($kr \gg 1$), the gradient acts mainly on the phase factor $e^{ikr}$: $\nabla e^{ikr} \approx ik\hat{\mathbf{r}}\,e^{ikr}$. So:

$$\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A} \approx ik\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\mathbf{A} = \frac{\mu_0 k^2\omega p_0}{4\pi}\frac{e^{i(kr-\omega t)}}{r}\sin\theta\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$$

From Faraday's law in the frequency domain ($\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = i\omega\mathbf{B}$), or directly from $V_{\text{dip}}$ and $\mathbf{A}_{\text{dip}}$:

$$\mathbf{E} \approx -c\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\mathbf{B} = \frac{k^2 p_0}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{e^{i(kr-\omega t)}}{r}\sin\theta\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$$

The time-averaged Poynting vector is $\langle S\rangle = \frac{1}{2\mu_0}|\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B}^*| = \frac{\mu_0 p_0^2\omega^4}{32\pi^2 c}\frac{\sin^2\theta}{r^2}$. Integrating over solid angle: $\int\sin^2\theta\,d\Omega = 2\pi\int_0^\pi\sin^3\theta\,d\theta = 8\pi/3$. So $P = \frac{\mu_0 p_0^2\omega^4}{12\pi c}$. $\square$

**Bridge.** Electric dipole radiation is the dominant radiation mechanism for most radiating systems: radio antennas, oscillating atoms, and molecular transitions. The foundational insight is that the radiation fields are produced by the *acceleration* of charges (since $\ddot{\mathbf{p}} = \sum q_i\mathbf{a}_i$), which connects to the Larmor formula [10.07.01]. The central message is that the dipole radiation power scales as $\omega^4$ (or equivalently $\lambda^{-4}$), the angular distribution goes as $\sin^2\theta$, and the fields are transverse with $|\mathbf{E}| = c|\mathbf{B}|$. Putting these together, the dipole radiation formula is the leading term in the multipole expansion [10.07.03], and the higher multipoles (magnetic dipole, electric quadrupole) are suppressed by additional powers of $kd$ (where $d$ is the source size). The dipole radiation also provides the classical model for atomic transitions [10.07.06], where the oscillating charge distribution in an atom produces radiation at the transition frequency.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the angular integral $\int\sin^2\theta\,d\Omega = 8\pi/3$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$d\Omega = \sin\theta\,d\theta\,d\phi$. Integrate $\phi$ from 0 to $2\pi$ and $\theta$ from 0 to $\pi$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\int\sin^2\theta\,d\Omega = \int_0^{2\pi}d\phi\int_0^\pi\sin^3\theta\,d\theta = 2\pi\int_0^\pi\sin^3\theta\,d\theta$. Using $\sin^3\theta = \sin\theta(1-\cos^2\theta)$ and substituting $u = \cos\theta$: $\int_0^\pi\sin^3\theta\,d\theta = \int_{-1}^{1}(1-u^2)\,du = [u - u^3/3]_{-1}^{1} = (1 - 1/3) - (-1 + 1/3) = 2/3 + 2/3 = 4/3$. So the total is $2\pi\times 4/3 = 8\pi/3$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the **radiation resistance** of a short dipole antenna of length $\ell$ carrying current $I_0$. The radiation resistance is defined by $P = \frac{1}{2}R_{\rm rad}I_0^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$P = \mu_0 I_0^2\ell^2\omega^2/(12\pi c)$. Set equal to $R_{\rm rad}I_0^2/2$ and solve for $R_{\rm rad}$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$P = \frac{1}{2}R_{\rm rad}I_0^2 = \frac{\mu_0 I_0^2\ell^2\omega^2}{12\pi c}$. So $R_{\rm rad} = \frac{\mu_0\ell^2\omega^2}{6\pi c} = \frac{2\pi\ell^2}{3\lambda^2}\sqrt{\mu_0/\epsilon_0}\cdot\eta_0/\eta_0 = \frac{2\pi}{3}(\ell/\lambda)^2\times 377\,\Omega$. For a half-wave dipole ($\ell = \lambda/2$): $R_{\rm rad} \approx 73\,\Omega$ (the standard value). For a short dipole ($\ell \ll \lambda$): $R_{\rm rad} \approx 80(\ell/\lambda)^2\,\Omega$, which is very small — most of the input power is reflected rather than radiated.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the electric dipole radiation formula from the Larmor formula [10.07.01] by showing that $P_{\rm Larmor} = \mu_0 q^2 a^2/(6\pi c)$ for an oscillating charge $q$ with acceleration $a = \omega^2 d$ gives the same result.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$p = qd$, so $\ddot{p} = q\ddot{d} = qa$. The Larmor formula gives $P = \mu_0 q^2 a^2/(6\pi c) = \mu_0\ddot{p}^2/(6\pi c)$. For $p = p_0\cos(\omega t)$: time-average $\langle\ddot{p}^2\rangle = p_0^2\omega^4/2$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From the Larmor formula: $P = \mu_0 q^2 a^2/(6\pi c) = \mu_0\ddot{p}^2/(6\pi c)$ (since $\ddot{p} = qa$ for a single charge). For $p(t) = p_0\cos(\omega t)$: $\ddot{p} = -p_0\omega^2\cos(\omega t)$. The time average is $\langle\ddot{p}^2\rangle = p_0^2\omega^4/2$. So $P = \mu_0 p_0^2\omega^4/(12\pi c)$. This matches the dipole radiation formula exactly.

The connection is: the Larmor formula describes radiation from a *single* accelerating charge, while the dipole formula describes radiation from a system with oscillating dipole moment. For a two-charge system with charges $\pm q$ separated by distance $d\cos(\omega t)$, the dipole moment is $p = qd\cos(\omega t)$ and the sum of the Larmor powers of the two charges gives the dipole radiation power.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the near field of an oscillating electric dipole ($kr \ll 1$) is dominated by the static dipole field $\mathbf{E} \approx \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{3(\mathbf{p}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}})\hat{\mathbf{r}}-\mathbf{p}}{r^3}$, and the transition from near field to radiation field occurs at $r \sim \lambda/(2\pi) = 1/k$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The exact dipole fields contain terms proportional to $1/r^3$, $1/r^2$, and $1/r$. Identify which term dominates in each regime.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The exact electric field of an oscillating dipole (in the frequency domain) has three terms:

$$\mathbf{E} = \frac{p_0}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\left[(2\cos\theta\,\hat{\mathbf{r}}+\sin\theta\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})\left(\frac{1}{r^3} - \frac{ik}{r^2}\right) + \sin\theta\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\frac{k^2}{r}\right]e^{i(kr-\omega t)}$$

The three terms scale as: $1/r^3$ (static dipole), $k/r^2$ (induction), $k^2/r$ (radiation). The near field ($kr \ll 1$): $1/r^3 \gg k/r^2 \gg k^2/r$, so $\mathbf{E}$ is dominated by the static dipole pattern. The far field ($kr \gg 1$): $k^2/r \gg k/r^2 \gg 1/r^3$, so $\mathbf{E}$ is dominated by the radiation field $\propto \sin\theta/r$.

The crossover occurs at $kr \sim 1$, i.e., $r \sim 1/k = \lambda/(2\pi)$. For a 1 GHz antenna ($\lambda = 30$ cm): the near-field extends to $r \sim 5$ cm, and the radiation field dominates beyond $r \sim 50$ cm.

The magnetic field similarly has two terms: $ik/r^2$ (induction) and $k^2/r$ (radiation). In the near field, $\mathbf{B}$ is weaker than $\mathbf{E}$ by a factor of $kr$; in the far field, $|\mathbf{E}| = c|\mathbf{B}|$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the total radiated power by integrating the Poynting vector over a sphere of radius $r$: $P = \int\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}}\,r^2\,d\Omega$. Show that the result is independent of $r$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\langle S\rangle \propto 1/r^2$ and $dA = r^2\,d\Omega$, so $r^2$ cancels. The angular integral gives $8\pi/3$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The time-averaged Poynting vector is $\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle = \frac{\mu_0 p_0^2\omega^4}{32\pi^2 c}\frac{\sin^2\theta}{r^2}\,\hat{\mathbf{r}}$. The power through a sphere of radius $r$ is:

$$P = \int_0^{2\pi}d\phi\int_0^\pi\frac{\mu_0 p_0^2\omega^4}{32\pi^2 c}\frac{\sin^2\theta}{r^2}\,r^2\sin\theta\,d\theta = \frac{\mu_0 p_0^2\omega^4}{32\pi^2 c}\cdot 2\pi\cdot\frac{8\pi}{3}$$

Wait — let me redo the angular integral. $\int\sin^2\theta\,d\Omega = 2\pi\int_0^\pi\sin^3\theta\,d\theta = 2\pi\cdot 4/3 = 8\pi/3$.

$$P = \frac{\mu_0 p_0^2\omega^4}{32\pi^2 c}\cdot\frac{8\pi}{3} = \frac{\mu_0 p_0^2\omega^4}{12\pi c}$$

The $r^2$ from the area element cancels the $1/r^2$ from the Poynting vector, so $P$ is independent of $r$. This is expected: in the radiation zone, energy flows outward without loss, so the total power through any enclosing surface is the same.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

A half-wave dipole antenna ($\ell = \lambda/2$) has a different radiation pattern than the short dipole. State the radiation resistance of a half-wave dipole ($73\,\Omega$) and explain why it is larger than the short dipole's radiation resistance for the same current.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The half-wave dipole has $\ell/\lambda = 0.5$, so the $(ell/\lambda)^2$ factor is $0.25$, giving $R_{\rm rad} \approx 80\times 0.25 = 20\,\Omega$... but the actual value is $73\,\Omega$. The short-dipole formula underestimates because the current distribution is not uniform.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The short-dipole formula $R_{\rm rad} = 80(\ell/\lambda)^2\,\Omega$ assumes uniform current along the antenna. For $\ell = \lambda/2$: this gives $80\times 0.25 = 20\,\Omega$, which is wrong.

The correct value $73\,\Omega$ comes from integrating the radiation from a **sinusoidal** current distribution $I(z) = I_0\cos(kz)$ (which vanishes at the ends of the antenna, as physically required). The sinusoidal distribution has a larger effective current moment than the uniform distribution, producing more radiation. The exact calculation requires integrating the radiation from each current element with the appropriate phase delay.

The radiation resistance of $73\,\Omega$ is close to $75\,\Omega$ (standard coaxial cable impedance), making the half-wave dipole naturally impedance-matched to standard transmission lines — one reason it is the most common antenna design.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, symbolic).**

The **Rayleigh scattering** cross-section for light scattering from a molecule with polarisability $\alpha$ is $\sigma = k^4|\alpha|^2/(6\pi)$. Explain why the sky is blue and sunsets are red using the $\omega^4$ (or $\lambda^{-4}$) dependence.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Rayleigh scattering is dipole radiation from the induced dipole in a molecule. Shorter wavelengths (blue) scatter much more than longer wavelengths (red).
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The cross-section $\sigma \propto k^4 = (2\pi/\lambda)^4 \propto \lambda^{-4}$. Blue light ($\lambda \approx 450$ nm) is scattered more than red light ($\lambda \approx 700$ nm) by a factor of $(700/450)^4 \approx 5.7$. When sunlight passes through the atmosphere, the blue component is scattered sideways (making the sky blue from scattered light) while the red component passes through with less scattering (making the direct sun appear reddish at sunset when the light path through the atmosphere is longest).

At sunset, sunlight travels through much more atmosphere than at noon. The blue light is almost entirely scattered away from the direct beam, leaving only red and orange. This is the same $\omega^4$ dependence as electric dipole radiation: the scattering molecule is an induced dipole ($p = \alpha E$), and its radiated power scales as $\omega^4$.
</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**The Hertz vector.** The electric and magnetic fields of a dipole radiator can be expressed compactly in terms of the **electric Hertz vector** $\Pi_e$:

$$\mathbf{E} = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \Pi_e) - \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial^2\Pi_e}{\partial t^2}, \qquad \mathbf{B} = \mu_0\epsilon_0\nabla \times \frac{\partial\Pi_e}{\partial t}$$

For an oscillating dipole: $\Pi_e = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r}\mathbf{p}(t_r)\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$. This formulation automatically satisfies Maxwell's equations and is the basis for the multipole expansion.

**The multipole expansion.** The full radiation field is expanded in multipoles: the electric dipole (E1) is the leading term, followed by the magnetic dipole (M1) and electric quadrupole (E2), each suppressed by an additional factor of $kd$ (where $d$ is the source size). The expansion is analogous to the Taylor expansion of the retarded time.

**Radiation from an arbitrary current distribution.** For a general current $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}, t)$, the far-field radiation is:

$$\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi r}\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times(\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\ddot{\mathbf{p}}_{\perp}(t_r))$$

where $\mathbf{p}_{\perp}$ is the component of the dipole moment perpendicular to $\hat{\mathbf{r}}$. This generalises the single-dipole formula to arbitrary source distributions.

**Synthesis.** Electric dipole radiation is the universal leading-order description of electromagnetic radiation from any charge distribution. The foundational insight is that radiation is produced by the second time derivative of the dipole moment ($\ddot{\mathbf{p}}$), which corresponds to charge acceleration. The central message is that the dipole radiation pattern ($\sin^2\theta$), the $\omega^4$ power scaling, and the $1/r$ field decay are universal features that appear whenever the source is small compared to the wavelength. Putting these together, electric dipole radiation connects the microscopic (Larmor formula [10.07.01] for a single charge) to the macroscopic (antenna radiation resistance, Rayleigh scattering [10.07.06]), and provides the foundation for the full multipole expansion [10.07.03] that describes radiation from extended sources.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Dipole radiation fields).** The far-field radiation from an oscillating dipole $\mathbf{p}(t) = p_0\cos(\omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ is:

$$\mathbf{E}(r,\theta,t) = -\frac{\mu_0 p_0\omega^2}{4\pi r}\sin\theta\cos(\omega t_r)\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}, \quad \mathbf{B} = \hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\mathbf{E}/c$$

*Proof.* The retarded vector potential for the dipole is $\mathbf{A} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi r}\dot{\mathbf{p}}(t_r)$. In the far zone ($kr \gg 1$), $\nabla \times \mathbf{A} \approx ik\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\mathbf{A}$ (the gradient acts on the phase factor). This gives $\mathbf{B} = ik\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\mathbf{A}$. The electric field follows from $\mathbf{E} = -c\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\mathbf{B}$ (far-field relation from Maxwell's equations). Computing $\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\hat{\mathbf{z}} = -\sin\theta\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$ and $\hat{\mathbf{r}}\times\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} = -\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ gives the stated result. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Larmor formula [10.07.01]** is the single-charge version of dipole radiation; $P = \mu_0 q^2 a^2/(6\pi c)$.
- **Multipole radiation [10.07.03]** extends the dipole treatment to magnetic dipole and electric quadrupole terms.
- **Synchrotron radiation [10.07.05]** is produced by relativistic charges; the dipole formula is the non-relativistic limit.
- **Thomson scattering [10.07.06]** is dipole radiation from a bound charge driven by an incident wave.
- **Vector potential [10.02.02]** provides the retarded potentials from which the radiation fields are derived.
- **EM waves [10.04.02]** are the general framework; dipole radiation is a specific solution.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Hertz's experiments (1886-1888) were the first demonstration of electromagnetic radiation from an oscillating dipole. His spark-gap transmitter was essentially an oscillating electric dipole, and he measured the radiation pattern, finding the $\sin^2\theta$ angular distribution predicted by Maxwell's theory. This was the decisive confirmation of Maxwell's equations.

The connection between dipole radiation and atomic transitions was made by Lorentz (1903), who modelled atoms as oscillating electrons (the "Lorentz oscillator") and showed that they radiate at the oscillation frequency. This classical model correctly predicts the $\omega^4$ scaling but fails to explain the discrete nature of atomic spectra, which required quantum mechanics.

Rayleigh scattering (1871) was explained by Lord Rayleigh as dipole radiation from the induced polarisation of air molecules, correctly predicting the $\lambda^{-4}$ wavelength dependence that makes the sky blue. This was one of the first quantitative applications of electromagnetic theory to a natural phenomenon.
