## Intuition [Beginner]

A radio transmitter does not push current into empty space for nothing. The current sloshing up and down a metal wire accelerates the conduction electrons, and accelerating charges radiate electromagnetic waves â€” the same Larmor mechanism that makes a single shaken charge spit out light [10.07.01]. A device engineered to turn a feed current into waves that fly away is called an **antenna**. The cleaner the conversion, the better the antenna.

The same physics runs in reverse. When an electromagnetic wave sweeps over a free electron, its electric field shakes the electron, and the shaking electron re-radiates. That re-radiation is called **scattering**. A single electron is a feeble scatterer: sunlight carries about 1360 watts per square metre, but one electron scatters only about $10^{-25}$ watts of it. Stack $10^{25}$ electrons into a cubic metre of air and the reradiated light paints the sky blue, because the scattering probability rises steeply at short (blue) wavelengths.

There is a third, unsettling consequence. If a charge radiates, it loses energy. That energy must come from the charge's own motion, so the radiation exerts a tiny recoil force back on its source â€” the **radiation reaction**. For an electron the recoil is almost always negligible, but it is never quite zero, and a careful treatment exposes a famous sickness: the classical equation that describes it predicts runaway speeds unless it is repaired.

The three threads share one root. Antennas, scattering, and radiation reaction are three faces of the fact that an accelerating charge couples to a $1/r$ field that carries energy to infinity [10.07.02]. This unit assembles the three capstone applications that close out the radiation chapter.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Part A â€” a half-wave dipole.** An FM transmitter feeds a half-wave dipole at $f=100$ MHz with a peak current $I_0=1.0$ A. The wavelength is $\lambda=c/f=3\times10^8/10^8=3$ m, so the antenna is $1.5$ m long. The radiation resistance is $R_{\rm rad}=73\,\Omega$. Time-averaging the oscillation introduces a factor of one half:

$$\langle P\rangle=\tfrac{1}{2}I_0^2 R_{\rm rad}=\tfrac{1}{2}(1.0)^2(73)\approx 36.5\text{ W}.$$

About $37$ watts sail off as radio waves. The number $73\,\Omega$ is the price the feed current pays, measured in ohms, to fill space with radiation.

**Part B â€” an electron in sunlight.** The same sunlight delivers $I=1360$ W/m$^2$. Each free electron scatters a slice of that flux equal to the Thomson cross-section $\sigma_T=6.65\times10^{-29}$ m$^2$:

$$P=\sigma_T I=(6.65\times10^{-29})(1360)\approx 9.0\times10^{-26}\text{ W}.$$

A single electron reradiates about a tenth of a billionth of a billionth of a watt. The electron is a near-invisible scatterer, which is why clean air is transparent and only the immense column of air above us, holding roughly $10^{29}$ electrons per square metre, scatters enough blue light to colour the sky.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Antennas

A **linear antenna** is a thin conducting wire of length $\ell$ carrying a specified current distribution $I(z,t)=\mathrm{Re}\,[\tilde I(z)e^{-i\omega t}]$ along its axis. In the **radiation zone** ($r\gg\lambda$) the vector potential reduces to

$$\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r},t)=\frac{\mu_0 e^{ikr}}{4\pi r}\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}\int_{-\ell/2}^{\ell/2}\tilde I(z')\,e^{-ikz'\cos\theta}\,dz',$$

where $k=\omega/c$ and $\theta$ is the polar angle measured from the wire axis. The far-zone electric field is the transverse piece of $-\partial_t\mathbf{A}$:

$$\mathbf{E}_{\rm rad}(r,\theta,t)=\frac{ik\mu_0 e^{i(kr-\omega t)}}{4\pi r}\,\sin\theta\int_{-\ell/2}^{\ell/2}\tilde I(z')\,e^{-ikz'\cos\theta}\,dz'\;\hat{\boldsymbol\theta}.$$

The **radiation resistance** is the effective load presented to the feed, defined by equating radiated power to ohmic dissipation in a fictitious resistor:

$$\langle P\rangle=\tfrac{1}{2}R_{\rm rad}|\tilde I_{\rm feed}|^2.$$

For a centre-fed **half-wave dipole** ($\ell=\lambda/2$) the current is $\tilde I(z)=I_0\cos(kz)$ (vanishing at the ends, as physically required), and evaluation of the integral gives $R_{\rm rad}=73.1\,\Omega$ with directivity $1.64$ ($2.15$ dBi).

An **antenna array** of $N$ identical elements separated by spacing $d$ and fed with progressive phase shift $\alpha$ has a radiation pattern that factorises:

$$\left(\frac{dP}{d\Omega}\right)_{\rm array}=\left(\frac{dP}{d\Omega}\right)_{\rm element}\times|A(\psi)|^2,\qquad A(\psi)=\frac{\sin(N\psi/2)}{N\sin(\psi/2)},\quad \psi=kd\cos\theta+\alpha.$$

The second factor, the **array factor**, depends only on geometry and phasing â€” not on the element. Steering the beam amounts to tuning $\alpha$, which is how phased-array radars swing a beam electronically with no moving parts.

### Scattering

The **differential scattering cross-section** is defined as the time-averaged reradiated power per unit solid angle, divided by the incident intensity:

$$\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}=\frac{\langle dP_{\rm scat}/d\Omega\rangle}{\langle I_{\rm inc}\rangle}.$$

The **total cross-section** is its integral, $\sigma=\int(d\sigma/d\Omega)\,d\Omega$. For a free electron driven by a low-frequency wave, the result is **Thomson scattering** with

$$\frac{d\sigma_T}{d\Omega}=\frac{r_e^2}{2}(1+\cos^2\theta),\qquad \sigma_T=\frac{8\pi}{3}r_e^2,$$

where $r_e=e^2/(4\pi\varepsilon_0 m_ec^2)\approx2.818\times10^{-15}$ m is the **classical electron radius** and $\theta$ is the scattering angle. For a bound, sub-wavelength scatterer of static polarisability $\alpha_0$, the result is **Rayleigh scattering**,

$$\sigma_{\rm Ray}=\frac{k^4|\alpha_0|^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0^2}\propto\omega^4,$$

and the strong $\omega^4$ rise is what paints the sky blue. At photon energies approaching $m_ec^2$ the classical result is replaced by the quantum **Kleinâ€“Nishina** formula, which falls below $\sigma_T$ as energy grows.

### Radiation reaction

The **Abrahamâ€“Lorentz force** is the non-relativistic self-force that accounts for the energy carried away by a charge's own radiation:

$$\mathbf{F}_{\rm rad}=\frac{q^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3}\,\ddot{\mathbf{v}}=m\tau_0\ddot{\mathbf{v}},\qquad \tau_0=\frac{q^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 m c^3}=\frac{2r_e}{3c}.$$

For the electron $\tau_0\approx6.26\times10^{-24}$ s. The equation of motion is third-order in time,

$$m\dot{\mathbf{v}}=\mathbf{F}_{\rm ext}+m\tau_0\ddot{\mathbf{v}},$$

which is the source of the runaway and pre-acceleration pathologies. The covariant generalisation is the **Lorentzâ€“Abrahamâ€“Dirac** (LAD) equation; its perturbative reduction to a second-order equation is the **Landauâ€“Lifshitz** equation, the form actually used in accelerator and plasma codes.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Thomson cross-section from Larmor).** *A free charge $q$ of mass $m$ illuminated by a linearly polarised plane wave of peak field $E_0$ reradiates time-averaged power*

$$\langle P_{\rm scat}\rangle=\frac{q^4 E_0^2}{12\pi\varepsilon_0 m^2 c^3},$$

*so that, dividing by the incident intensity $\langle I\rangle=c\varepsilon_0 E_0^2/2$, the total cross-section is*

$$\sigma_T=\frac{8\pi}{3}\bigg(\frac{q^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 m c^2}\bigg)^2=\frac{8\pi}{3}r_e^2.$$

**Proof.** Take the incident wave to drive the charge along $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ with $E_x(t)=E_0\cos\omega t$. Below any resonance the magnetic force is negligible and the equation of motion reduces to $m\dot v_x=qE_0\cos\omega t$, giving acceleration $a(t)=(qE_0/m)\cos\omega t$. The instantaneous Larmor power [10.07.01] is

$$P(t)=\frac{q^2 a^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3}=\frac{q^4 E_0^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 m^2 c^3}\cos^2\omega t.$$

Time-averaging $\langle\cos^2\omega t\rangle=1/2$ yields the stated $\langle P_{\rm scat}\rangle$. The reradiation is exactly the dipole pattern of [10.07.02] with $p_0=qE_0/m\omega^2$: the driven charge is an oscillating dipole, and the angular distribution follows from the dipole $\sin^2\Theta$ law in the polarisation frame.

The incident intensity is the time-averaged Poynting flux,

$$\langle I\rangle=\tfrac{1}{2}c\varepsilon_0 E_0^2.$$

The cross-section is the ratio of scattered power to incident flux:

$$\sigma_T=\frac{\langle P_{\rm scat}\rangle}{\langle I\rangle}=\frac{q^4 E_0^2}{12\pi\varepsilon_0 m^2 c^3}\cdot\frac{2}{c\varepsilon_0 E_0^2}=\frac{q^4}{6\pi\varepsilon_0^2 m^2 c^4}.$$

Collecting $q^4/(6\pi\varepsilon_0^2m^2c^4)$ as $(8\pi/3)\,[q^2/(4\pi\varepsilon_0 mc^2)]^2=(8\pi/3)r_e^2$ gives the stated result. For an electron the numerical value is $\sigma_T\approx6.65\times10^{-29}$ m$^2$, independent of frequency precisely because the $E_0^2$ and $\omega$ dependencies cancel between the driven acceleration and the Larmor power. $\square$

**Bridge.** This derivation builds toward the antenna and scattering machinery developed below, where the same retarded-potential integral appears again in the half-wave dipole and in the array factor. The foundational reason a thin wire radiates at all is that the accelerating conduction electrons along it produce the $1/r$ fields of Larmor; this is exactly the dipole radiation of [10.07.02] specialised to a driven, spatially extended current. Putting these together, the retarded-potential picture generalises the single-charge Larmor formula [10.07.01] to extended sources, and the bridge is the radiation resistance $R_{\rm rad}=73\,\Omega$ that converts a feed current into watts of outgoing wave â€” the antenna is engineering, but the physics is Larmor all the way down.

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the Abrahamâ€“Lorentz equation $m\dot{\mathbf v}=\mathbf F_{\rm ext}+m\tau_0\ddot{\mathbf v}$ admits **runaway** solutions of the form $\mathbf v(t)=\mathbf v_0+\mathbf A\,e^{t/\tau_0}$ even when $\mathbf F_{\rm ext}=\mathbf 0$, and explain why these are unphysical.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Setting $\mathbf F_{\rm ext}=\mathbf0$ gives $\dot{\mathbf v}=\tau_0\ddot{\mathbf v}$, or $\ddot{\mathbf v}-(1/\tau_0)\dot{\mathbf v}=\mathbf0$. The $\mathbf A$ mode grows without bound: a charge initially at rest would spontaneously accelerate to arbitrary speed with no external agent, violating energy conservation (the kinetic energy would have to be supplied by the particle's own field). Such solutions are discarded by boundary condition; doing so consistently is the content of the Dirac and Landauâ€“Lifshitz reformulations below.
</details>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

The Landauâ€“Lifshitz reduction replaces the third-derivative term $\ddot{\mathbf v}$ in the Abrahamâ€“Lorentz force by the time derivative of the lowest-order acceleration $\dot{\mathbf v}=\mathbf F_{\rm ext}/m$. Derive the resulting **Landauâ€“Lifshitz radiation-reaction force** (non-relativistic, leading order) $\mathbf F_{\rm LL}=\tau_0\dot{\mathbf F}_{\rm ext}$, and verify explicitly that it has no runaway solutions.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

From $m\dot{\mathbf v}=\mathbf F_{\rm ext}+m\tau_0\ddot{\mathbf v}$, substitute $\ddot{\mathbf v}=\dot{\mathbf F}_{\rm ext}/m$. The resulting equation is second order in $\mathbf v$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From the zeroth-order equation $\dot{\mathbf v}=\mathbf F_{\rm ext}/m$, differentiate: $\ddot{\mathbf v}=\dot{\mathbf F}_{\rm ext}/m$. Substituting into the Abrahamâ€“Lorentz term gives $m\tau_0\ddot{\mathbf v}=\tau_0\dot{\mathbf F}_{\rm ext}$, so the Landauâ€“Lifshitz equation is

$$m\dot{\mathbf v}=\mathbf F_{\rm ext}+\tau_0\dot{\mathbf F}_{\rm ext}.$$

This is first-order in $\mathbf v$ (the radiation reaction is now an explicit functional of the external force, not of $\mathbf v$ itself), so with $\mathbf F_{\rm ext}=\mathbf0$ it reduces to $\dot{\mathbf v}=\mathbf0$: a charge at rest stays at rest, and no exponential mode exists. The pathological third-order structure has been removed by treating the recoil as a perturbation â€” justified whenever $\tau_0\omega\ll1$, which covers every terrestrial accelerator and astrophysical plasma except the extreme fields near pulsars and laser intensities approaching the Schwinger limit.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

The quantum Kleinâ€“Nishina total cross-section for Compton scattering reduces to the Thomson result in the low-energy limit $x=\hbar\omega/(m_ec^2)\ll1$. Using the leading high-energy behaviour $\sigma_{\rm KN}\sim\sigma_T\,(3/8x)(\ln 2x+\tfrac12)$ for $x\gg1$, estimate the photon energy at which the scattering probability has fallen to half of $\sigma_T$, and explain physically why high-energy gamma rays penetrate matter so effectively.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Set $\sigma_{\rm KN}/\sigma_T\approx\tfrac12$ and solve roughly for $x$; the logarithm varies slowly so a single iteration suffices.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Setting $(3/8x)(\ln 2x+\tfrac12)\approx\tfrac12$ gives $(3/4x)(\ln 2x+\tfrac12)\approx1$, i.e. $\ln 2x+\tfrac12\approx4x/3$. Trying $x=1$: left $=\ln2+0.5\approx1.19$, right $=1.33$ â€” close. Refining, $x\approx1.1$ gives left $\approx\ln2.2+0.5\approx1.24$ and right $\approx1.47$; the half-threshold sits near $x\approx1$, i.e. $\hbar\omega\approx m_ec^2=0.511$ MeV. Physically, at high energy the photon carries enough momentum to knock the electron substantially (Compton recoil), the scattering becomes inelastic, and the cross-section falls as $1/\omega$. Gamma rays above a few MeV therefore interact only weakly with electrons and penetrate thick shielding â€” the reason gamma sources require dense, high-$Z$ material such as lead rather than the light shielding that stops alpha and beta radiation.
</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The half-wave dipole in detail

For a centre-fed dipole of length $\ell=\lambda/2$ the current distribution that satisfies the boundary condition $I(\pm\lambda/4)=0$ is $\tilde I(z)=I_0\cos(kz)$. The far-field radiation integral evaluates to

$$\mathbf{E}_{\rm rad}=\frac{i\eta_0 I_0 e^{i(kr-\omega t)}}{2\pi r}\,\frac{\cos[(\pi/2)\cos\theta]}{\sin\theta}\,\hat{\boldsymbol\theta},$$

giving the angular pattern $|\cos[(\pi/2)\cos\theta]|^2/\sin^2\theta$. This is the half-wave analogue of the short-dipole $\sin^2\theta$ pattern of [10.07.02]: both vanish on axis and peak broadside, but the half-wave version is slightly narrower because the contributions from different points along the wire add with phase that reinforces the broadside direction. Integrating the Poynting vector over the sphere and dividing by $\tfrac12 I_0^2$ yields the radiation resistance

$$R_{\rm rad}=\frac{\eta_0}{2\pi}\int_0^\pi\frac{\cos^2[(\pi/2)\cos\theta]}{\sin\theta}\,d\theta\approx73.1\,\Omega,$$

a result first obtained by Carter in 1932 and now tabulated in every antenna handbook. The proximity of $73\,\Omega$ to the $75\,\Omega$ standard for coaxial cable is a happy accident of nature, not a convention: it is why centre-fed dipoles are the natural match to standard transmission lines.

### Multipole scattering and the optical theorem

A compact scatterer of size $a\ll\lambda$ has its scattering amplitude decomposed into the same electric-dipole, magnetic-dipole, and electric-quadrupole channels developed in [10.07.03]. Each multipole $E1$, $M1$, $E2$ contributes a partial cross-section suppressed by $(ka)^2$ relative to its predecessor, so the leading channel dominates unless forbidden by symmetry â€” the selection-rule logic of [10.07.03] applied to absorption and scattering. The forward scattering amplitude is tied to the total cross-section by the **optical theorem**,

$$\sigma_{\rm tot}=\frac{4\pi}{k}\,\mathrm{Im}\,f(\theta=0),$$

which expresses the physically deep fact that the wave removed from the incident beam (extinction) interferes destructively with the forward-going scattered wave. Rayleigh's $\omega^4$ law is the leading $E1$ term for a dielectric sphere, and the anomalous scattering resonances that appear near molecular absorption lines are the classical shadows of quantum transitions.

### Kleinâ€“Nishina and the quantum boundary

The Thomson formula holds for $\hbar\omega\ll m_ec^2$. When the photon energy becomes comparable to the electron rest energy, the scattering becomes inelastic (Compton scattering), the scattered photon is red-shifted by the recoil, and the total cross-section is given by the Kleinâ€“Nishina formula:

$$\sigma_{\rm KN}=\frac{3\sigma_T}{4}\bigg[\frac{1+x}{x^3}\bigg(\frac{2x(1+x)}{1+2x}-\ln(1+2x)\bigg)+\frac{\ln(1+2x)}{2x}-\frac{1+3x}{(1+2x)^2}\bigg],$$

with $x=\hbar\omega/(m_ec^2)$. In the low-energy limit this reduces to $\sigma_T$ term by term. At high energy $\sigma_{\rm KN}\sim(3\sigma_T/8x)(\ln 2x+\tfrac12)$, falling roughly as $1/\omega$: gamma rays above a few MeV interact only weakly with electrons, which is why high-energy gamma astronomy can see photons that have crossed most of the universe. In astrophysical sources such as blazars, relativistic electrons up-scatter low-energy synchrotron photons to gamma energies by **inverse Compton** scattering [10.07.05], and the same Kleinâ€“Nishina decline ultimately caps the gamma-ray spectrum.

### The Lorentzâ€“Abrahamâ€“Dirac equation and its pathologies

The covariant form of the radiation-reaction equation, derived by Dirac in 1938 by careful regularisation of the self-field, is

$$m\frac{dU^\mu}{d\tau}=F_{\rm ext}^\mu+\frac{q^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3}\bigg(\frac{d^2U^\mu}{d\tau^2}+\frac{U^\mu}{c^2}\frac{dU_\nu}{d\tau}\frac{dU^\nu}{d\tau}\bigg).$$

The second term inside the bracket keeps $U_\mu U^\mu=c^2$ conserved under the recoil. The equation is third-order in proper time and inherits the runaway solutions of the non-relativistic Abrahamâ€“Lorentz form. Eliminating the runaway by imposing the future boundary condition $dU^\mu/d\tau\to0$ as $\tau\to+\infty$ shifts the pathology to **pre-acceleration**: the charge begins to move a time $\sim\tau_0$ before the external force arrives. For the electron $\tau_0\approx6.3\times10^{-24}$ s, far below any directly resolvable timescale, so the pre-acceleration is operationally harmless â€” but it signals that classical electrodynamics with point charges is not a closed theory.

### The Landauâ€“Lifshitz equation

The standard resolution treats the self-force perturbatively. Substituting the lowest-order equation $m\,dU^\mu/d\tau=F_{\rm ext}^\mu$ back into the LAD reaction term reduces the dynamics to a regular second-order equation,

$$m\frac{dU^\mu}{d\tau}=F_{\rm ext}^\mu+\frac{q^2}{6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3}\bigg[\frac{dF_{\rm ext}^\mu}{d\tau}+\frac{U^\mu F_{\rm ext}^\nu F_{{\rm ext},\nu}}{m c^2}\bigg],$$

with no runaways and no pre-acceleration. This is the **Landauâ€“Lifshitz equation**, used universally in accelerator tracking codes and in strong-field plasma simulations. Its regime of validity is $\tau_0\omega\ll1$ and $\chi=\gamma|\mathbf E|/E_{\rm crit}\ll1$, where $E_{\rm crit}=m^2c^3/(q\hbar)\approx1.3\times10^{18}$ V/m is the Schwinger field. Only in the magnetospheres of pulsars ($B\sim10^8$ T) or in the focus of petawatt lasers does $\chi$ approach unity and the classical reaction give way to stochastic quantum radiation reaction, an active frontier of ultra-intense laser physics.

**Synthesis.** The three subjects of this unit are unified by the exchange of energy and momentum between a charge distribution and the electromagnetic field it radiates or absorbs. The foundational reason antennas, scattering, and radiation reaction belong in one chapter is that each is a different facet of the Larmor mechanism [10.07.01]: an antenna is a designed current distribution radiating coherently, scattering is the radiation re-emitted by a charge driven by an incoming wave, and radiation reaction is the self-force that very mechanism exerts on its own source. This is exactly the content of energy-momentum conservation for the coupled particle-field system. The central insight is that the same transverse $1/r$ dipole field of [10.07.02] appears in all three, and only the prescription of the source changes. Putting these together, the radiation resistance generalises Ohm's law to free space, the Thomson cross-section generalises the dipole formula to a driven oscillator, and the Landauâ€“Lifshitz force generalises Newton's second law to include the recoil of emission. The bridge is the energy balance $\langle I\rangle\sigma_T=\langle P_{\rm scat}\rangle$, which ties scattering to radiation reaction through the very Larmor power from which this whole chapter began.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Thomson differential cross-section).** *The differential cross-section for Thomson scattering of an unpolarised plane wave by a free charge is*

$$\frac{d\sigma_T}{d\Omega}=\frac{r_e^2}{2}(1+\cos^2\theta),$$

*and its integral over solid angle is $\sigma_T=(8\pi/3)r_e^2$.*

*Proof.* Take the incident wave to propagate along $\hat{\mathbf z}$ and let its electric field point along $\hat{\mathbf x}$ with peak amplitude $E_0$. The charge accelerates along $\hat{\mathbf x}$ with $a(t)=(qE_0/m)\cos\omega t$, and reradiates with the dipole pattern of [10.07.02]. For an observation direction $\hat{\mathbf n}$ making polar angle $\Theta_x$ with the acceleration axis $\hat{\mathbf x}$, the time-averaged radiated power per unit solid angle is

$$\bigg\langle\frac{dP}{d\Omega}\bigg\rangle=\frac{q^2 a_0^2}{32\pi^2\varepsilon_0 c^3}\sin^2\Theta_x=\frac{q^4 E_0^2}{32\pi^2\varepsilon_0 m^2 c^3}\sin^2\Theta_x,$$

with $a_0=qE_0/m$. Dividing by the incident intensity $\langle I\rangle=c\varepsilon_0 E_0^2/2$ gives the polarised differential cross-section

$$\bigg(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\bigg)_{\!\hat{\mathbf x}}=\frac{q^4}{16\pi^2\varepsilon_0^2 m^2 c^4}\sin^2\Theta_x=r_e^2\sin^2\Theta_x.$$

For an **unpolarised** beam we average over two orthogonal polarisations, $\hat{\mathbf x}$ and $\hat{\mathbf y}$. If the scattering direction $\hat{\mathbf n}$ makes angle $\theta$ with the incident propagation axis $\hat{\mathbf z}$, then $\cos\Theta_x=\hat{\mathbf n}\cdot\hat{\mathbf x}=n_x$ and $\cos\Theta_y=n_y$, with $n_x^2+n_y^2+n_z^2=1$ and $n_z=\cos\theta$. Averaging,

$$\langle\sin^2\Theta_x+\sin^2\Theta_y\rangle=2-(n_x^2+n_y^2)=2-(1-n_z^2)=1+\cos^2\theta.$$

So

$$\frac{d\sigma_T}{d\Omega}=\frac{r_e^2}{2}(1+\cos^2\theta).$$

Integrating,

$$\sigma_T=\frac{r_e^2}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}d\phi\int_0^\pi(1+\cos^2\theta)\sin\theta\,d\theta=\frac{r_e^2}{2}\cdot2\pi\cdot\bigg[\big(-\cos\theta\big)-\frac{\cos^3\theta}{3}\bigg]_0^\pi.$$

The bracket evaluates to $[1+\tfrac13]-[-1-\tfrac13]=8/3$, giving

$$\sigma_T=\frac{r_e^2}{2}\cdot2\pi\cdot\frac{8}{3}=\frac{8\pi}{3}r_e^2,$$

completing the proof. $\square$

**Corollary (forwardâ€“backward symmetry).** *Because $d\sigma_T/d\Omega$ depends on $\theta$ only through $\cos^2\theta$, Thomson scattering is symmetric between $\theta$ and $\pi-\theta$: a free electron scatters as much light backwards as forwards. This symmetry is broken in the quantum (Kleinâ€“Nishina) regime, where recoil preferentially suppresses backward scattering at high photon energy.*

## Connections [Master]

- **Larmor formula [10.07.01]** is the single result from which all three threads of this unit descend. The antenna radiation integral is the Larmor field summed coherently over an extended current; the Thomson cross-section is Larmor power divided by incident intensity; the Abrahamâ€“Lorentz force is the Larmor energy loss written as a recoil on the source. Whenever this unit computes a radiated or scattered power, the Larmor prefactor $q^2a^2/(6\pi\varepsilon_0 c^3)$ is doing the work.

- **Electric dipole radiation [10.07.02]** supplies the angular pattern and the retarded-potential machinery. The half-wave dipole is an extended oscillating dipole whose far field is the integral of the elementary dipole field of [10.07.02] over the sinusoidal current; Rayleigh scattering is dipole radiation from the polarisability-induced dipole $p=\alpha_0 E$; and the $\sin^2\Theta$ law is what fixes the $(1+\cos^2\theta)$ shape of the Thomson differential cross-section.

- **Multipole radiation [10.07.03]** generalises dipole radiation to higher channels and governs the corrections to both antenna patterns and scattering amplitudes. The $E2$ and $M1$ terms suppressed by $(ka)^2$ describe the deviations of a finite antenna from the ideal dipole, and the multipole decomposition of a compact scatterer reduces Rayleigh and anomalous scattering to the same selection rules that control forbidden spectral lines.

- **Synchrotron radiation [10.07.05]** is the relativistic, circular-motion limit of the Larmor mechanism, and its complement is **inverse Compton scattering**, in which the relativistic electrons of a synchrotron source up-scatter ambient low-energy photons to gamma energies via the Thomson/Kleinâ€“Nishina cross-sections of this unit. The same $\gamma^4$ beaming that makes synchrotron sources bright also makes inverse Compton the dominant gamma-ray channel in blazars and pulsar wind nebulae.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The three threads of this unit were woven between 1897 and 1962, and each carries a cautionary tale about how far a classical theory can be pushed.

Joseph Larmor published the power formula $P=2q^2a^2/(3c^3)$ (Gaussian units) in 1897 [ref: TODO_REF Larmor 1897], derived from the Hertzian dipole. Within a decade it was clear that the same result described both emission (antennas) and re-emission (scattering). Guglielmo Marconi's transatlantic transmission of 1901 used a crude antenna whose radiation resistance was a few ohms at most; the modern theory of the half-wave dipole and its $73\,\Omega$ radiation resistance was worked out by Carter (1932) once the sinusoidal current distribution was properly imposed, turning antenna design from empiricism into calculation.

Joseph John Thomson measured the scattering of X-rays by electrons in 1906 [ref: TODO_REF Thomson 1906] and showed that the cross-section, of order $10^{-29}$ m$^2$, implied a particle of radius $\sim10^{-15}$ m â€” the classical electron radius $r_e$. The Thomson cross-section was the first quantitative bridge between electrodynamics and the newly discovered electron, and its independence of frequency was a clean classical prediction that quantum mechanics would later refine via the Kleinâ€“Nishina formula (1929) once Compton's 1923 measurement of the inelastic photon recoil showed the classical picture failing above $m_ec^2$. Lord Rayleigh's earlier $\omega^4$ scattering law (1871) [ref: TODO_REF Rayleigh 1871] fell into place as the dipole limit of the same theory, explaining the blue sky two decades before the electron was discovered.

Radiation reaction has always been the uncomfortable part of the story. Max Abraham (1904â€“1905) [ref: TODO_REF Abraham 1905] and Hendrik Lorentz independently derived the self-force $\mathbf F_{\rm rad}\propto\ddot{\mathbf v}$ by computing the field of an extended charge and shrinking it to a point, exposing immediately the third-order time derivative and its runaway solutions. Paul Dirac's 1938 covariant reformulation [ref: TODO_REF Dirac 1938] made the equation Lorentz-invariant but did not remove the runaway; the future boundary condition that does remove it merely relocates the trouble to pre-acceleration. The pragmatic resolution came from Lev Landau and Evgeny Lifshitz in their 1941 *Classical Theory of Fields* [ref: TODO_REF Landau Lifshitz 1941], where the radiation reaction is treated as a perturbation and reduced to a well-posed second-order equation. Fritz Rohrlich's *Classical Charged Particles* (1965, 3rd ed. 2007) [ref: TODO_REF Rohrlich 2007] remains the canonical defence of the consistency of classical point-charge electrodynamics, while the modern frontier â€” stochastic quantum radiation reaction at $\chi\sim1$ â€” is now probed directly in petawatt-laser experiments.

The philosophical lesson is that a theory can be extraordinarily accurate and still be open at the centre: the Larmor mechanism predicts the radiation perfectly but cannot, within classical point-particle electrodynamics, consistently describe the recoil that this same radiation produces. The antenna, the scatterer, and the self-force together mark the boundary where classical electrodynamics hands the baton to quantum field theory.
