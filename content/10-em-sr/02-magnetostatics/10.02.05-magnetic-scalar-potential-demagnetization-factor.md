## Intuition [Beginner]

In free space with no currents, you can write the magnetic field as the gradient of a **magnetic scalar potential**, just as you write the electric field as the gradient of an electrostatic potential. This works because Ampere's law says the H-field has zero curl wherever there is no free current.

Inside a magnetised material, things get more interesting. The H-field "wants" to oppose the magnetisation — this is the **demagnetisation field**. The shape of the sample determines how strong this opposition is. A flat disc magnetised perpendicular to its face has a huge demagnetisation field (almost fully opposing M). A long thin rod magnetised along its axis has almost no demagnetisation field.

The **demagnetisation factor** $N_d$ quantifies this shape effect. For a uniformly magnetised ellipsoid, $\mathbf{H}_{\text{in}} = -N_d \mathbf{M}$. A sphere has $N_d = 1/3$. A long cylinder has $N_d \approx 0$. A thin slab has $N_d \approx 1$. These numbers control the internal field of every permanent magnet and every magnetic recording medium.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A uniformly magnetised sphere has $N_d = 1/3$. If the magnetisation is $\mathbf{M} = 10^6$ A/m (a strong permanent magnet material), the demagnetisation field is:

$\mathbf{H}_d = -N_d\mathbf{M} = -(1/3)(10^6) = -3.33 \times 10^5$ A/m.

The B-field inside is $\mathbf{B} = \mu_0(\mathbf{H} + \mathbf{M}) = \mu_0(1 - 1/3)\mathbf{M} = (2/3)\mu_0 M \approx 0.84$ T.

For a long cylinder of the same material magnetised along its axis ($N_d = 0$): $\mathbf{H}_d = 0$ and $\mathbf{B} = \mu_0 M \approx 1.26$ T. The sphere loses one-third of its potential field to the demagnetisation effect; the cylinder loses none.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Magnetic scalar potential.** In a region with no free current ($\mathbf{J}_f = 0$), Ampere's law gives $\nabla\times\mathbf{H} = 0$, so we can write:

$$\mathbf{H} = -\nabla\Phi_m$$

where $\Phi_m$ is the **magnetic scalar potential**. Using $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$ and $\mathbf{B} = \mu_0(\mathbf{H} + \mathbf{M})$:

$$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{H} = -\nabla\cdot\mathbf{M}$$

so $\Phi_m$ satisfies the **Poisson equation** with source $-\nabla\cdot\mathbf{M}$:

$$\nabla^2\Phi_m = \nabla\cdot\mathbf{M}$$

The quantity $\rho_m = -\nabla\cdot\mathbf{M}$ is the **bound magnetic charge density** (a mathematical convenience, not a physical entity). The surface bound magnetic charge is $\sigma_m = \mathbf{M}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}$.

For uniform magnetisation ($\nabla\cdot\mathbf{M} = 0$ inside), the only source of $\Phi_m$ is the surface charge $\sigma_m = \mathbf{M}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}$, giving:

$$\Phi_m(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi}\oint_S \frac{\sigma_m(\mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|}\,da' = \frac{1}{4\pi}\oint_S \frac{\mathbf{M}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}'}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|}\,da'$$

**Demagnetisation tensor.** For a uniformly magnetised ellipsoid with semi-axes $a, b, c$, the H-field inside is uniform and related to $\mathbf{M}$ by:

$$\mathbf{H}_{\text{in}} = -\overleftrightarrow{\mathbf{N}}\cdot\mathbf{M}$$

where $\overleftrightarrow{\mathbf{N}}$ is the **demagnetisation tensor**, diagonal in the principal-axis frame of the ellipsoid with $N_x + N_y + N_z = 1$. For a sphere: $N_x = N_y = N_z = 1/3$. For a general ellipsoid, the $N_i$ are expressed in terms of elliptic integrals.

**Boundary conditions on $\Phi_m$.** At the surface of a magnetic material:

1. $\Phi_m$ is continuous (from $H_t$ continuous).
2. The normal derivative jumps: $\partial\Phi_m/\partial n\big|_{2} - \partial\Phi_m/\partial n\big|_{1} = -(\mathbf{M}_2 - \mathbf{M}_1)\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ (from $B_n$ continuous).

These are the same mathematical structure as the electrostatic boundary conditions with $\sigma_m$ playing the role of surface charge.

### Caveats

- $\Phi_m$ is not single-valued if the region encloses a current loop. If a free current $I$ is enclosed by a path, $\oint\mathbf{H}\cdot d\mathbf{l} = I \neq 0$, and $\Phi_m$ changes by $-I$ around the loop. The scalar potential is only useful in simply-connected current-free regions.
- The "magnetic charge" $\rho_m = -\nabla\cdot\mathbf{M}$ is a mathematical fiction. There are no magnetic monopoles. The formalism works because it reproduces the correct bound currents via the equivalence $\nabla\times\mathbf{M}$ and $\mathbf{M}\times\hat{\mathbf{n}}$.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (Uniform internal field of a magnetised ellipsoid).**

**Theorem.** *A uniformly magnetised ellipsoid produces a uniform H-field inside: $\mathbf{H}_{\text{in}} = -\overleftrightarrow{\mathbf{N}}\cdot\mathbf{M}$.*

*Proof.* The magnetic scalar potential satisfies $\nabla^2\Phi_m = 0$ inside the ellipsoid (since $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{M} = 0$ for uniform $\mathbf{M}$) with surface source $\sigma_m = \mathbf{M}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}$.

For an ellipsoid with semi-axes $a, b, c$ and $\mathbf{M} = M_0\hat{\mathbf{x}}$, the surface charge is $\sigma_m = M_0 n_x$ where $n_x$ is the $x$-component of the outward normal. By the linearity of the Laplace equation, $\Phi_m$ inside is a linear function of $\mathbf{M}$:

$$\Phi_m^{\text{in}} = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j}N_{ij}M_j x_i$$

The gradient gives $H_i^{\text{in}} = \sum_j N_{ij}M_j$. By the symmetry of the ellipsoid, $N_{ij}$ is diagonal in the principal frame. The constraint $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$ applied to the uniform field gives $B_x + B_y + B_z = 0$ in the appropriate combination, which translates to $\text{Tr}(\overleftrightarrow{\mathbf{N}}) = N_x + N_y + N_z = 1$.

The specific values involve elliptic integrals. For a prolate spheroid ($a > b = c$):

$$N_a = \frac{1 - e^2}{e^2}\left(\frac{1}{2e}\ln\frac{1+e}{1-e} - 1\right), \qquad e^2 = 1 - b^2/a^2$$

For a sphere ($a = b = c$): $N_a = N_b = N_c = 1/3$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The demagnetisation tensor builds toward a complete description of the internal fields in magnetic samples of any shape. The foundational insight is that only ellipsoids (and their degenerate forms: spheres, cylinders, slabs) produce uniform internal demagnetisation fields. The central message is that the demagnetisation factor controls the B-field inside a permanent magnet and therefore its useful external field. This is exactly the reason why permanent magnets are shaped as long bars (low $N_d$ along the axis) rather than flat discs (high $N_d$ perpendicular to the face). Putting these together, the magnetic scalar potential generalises the electrostatic potential formalism [10.01.02] to magnetostatics in matter, and the demagnetisation factor appears again in the magnetic energy [10.03.05] where it determines the self-energy of a finite magnetised sample.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the magnetic scalar potential for a uniformly magnetised sphere of radius $R$ with $\mathbf{M} = M_0\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ is $\Phi_m^{\text{in}} = (M_0/3)r\cos\theta$ inside and $\Phi_m^{\text{out}} = (M_0 R^3/3)\cos\theta/r^2$ outside.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Inside: $\nabla^2\Phi_m = 0$, with $\Phi_m$ linear in $z = r\cos\theta$. Outside: dipole potential $\propto \cos\theta/r^2$. Match boundary conditions at $r = R$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Inside, $\Phi_m^{\text{in}} = (M_0/3)r\cos\theta$ is harmonic ($\nabla^2\Phi_m = 0$, verified by direct calculation in spherical coordinates for a function proportional to $r\cos\theta$). The H-field is $\mathbf{H}^{\text{in}} = -\nabla\Phi_m^{\text{in}} = -(M_0/3)\hat{\mathbf{z}}$, confirming $N_d = 1/3$.

Outside, the surface charge $\sigma_m = M_0\cos\theta$ produces a dipole potential. The solution of $\nabla^2\Phi_m = 0$ outside with the correct angular dependence ($\cos\theta$) is $\Phi_m^{\text{out}} = C\cos\theta/r^2$. Continuity of $\Phi_m$ at $r = R$: $(M_0/3)R = C/R^2$, giving $C = M_0 R^3/3$.

Check: continuity of $B_n = \mu_0(H_n + M_n)$ at $r = R$. Inside: $H_r^{\text{in}} = -(M_0/3)\cos\theta$, $B_r^{\text{in}} = \mu_0(-M_0/3 + M_0)\cos\theta = (2/3)\mu_0 M_0\cos\theta$. Outside: $H_r^{\text{out}} = 2M_0 R^3\cos\theta/(3r^3)$, $B_r^{\text{out}} = (2/3)\mu_0 M_0\cos\theta$ at $r = R$. Continuous, as required.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

A thin slab of material with $\mathbf{M} = M_0\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ perpendicular to its large flat faces has $N_d = 1$. Compute the B-field inside and explain why a thin slab makes a poor permanent magnet.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathbf{H}_{\text{in}} = -N_d\mathbf{M} = -M_0\hat{\mathbf{z}}$. Then $\mathbf{B} = \mu_0(\mathbf{H} + \mathbf{M})$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathbf{H}_{\text{in}} = -M_0\hat{\mathbf{z}}$, so $\mathbf{B}_{\text{in}} = \mu_0(-M_0 + M_0)\hat{\mathbf{z}} = 0$.

The B-field inside the slab is zero. The demagnetisation field exactly cancels the magnetisation. The slab stores no useful magnetic energy and produces no external field from its perpendicular magnetisation. This is why magnetic recording media (thin films) magnetise in-plane ($N_d = 0$) rather than perpendicular ($N_d = 1$), unless special multilayer structures are used.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the demagnetisation factor of a long cylinder magnetised perpendicular to its axis (transverse magnetisation).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A cylinder is an ellipsoid with $a \to \infty$ (infinitely long). Use $N_x + N_y + N_z = 1$ and the known values.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For an infinitely long cylinder: $N_z = 0$ along the axis (from the limit $a \to \infty$ of the prolate spheroid formula). By symmetry of the transverse plane, $N_x = N_y$. The sum rule gives $N_x + N_y + N_z = 2N_x + 0 = 1$, so $N_x = N_y = 1/2$.

A cylinder magnetised transversely has $N_d = 1/2$, meaning the demagnetisation field is half the magnetisation: $\mathbf{H}_d = -(1/2)\mathbf{M}_\perp$. The B-field inside is $\mathbf{B} = \mu_0(\mathbf{H} + \mathbf{M}) = (1/2)\mu_0\mathbf{M}_\perp$, half what it would be without demagnetisation.

This is why anisotropic ferrite magnets are magnetised along their easy axis, which is designed to be the long axis of the cylinder, minimising $N_d$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A permanent magnet in the shape of a sphere has a hysteresis curve with $M_r = 8 \times 10^5$ A/m (remanent magnetisation) and $H_c = 5 \times 10^4$ A/m (coercive field). Find the B-field at the operating point (the intersection of the demagnetisation line with the hysteresis curve in the second quadrant).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The operating point satisfies $\mathbf{H} = -N_d\mathbf{M} = -(1/3)\mathbf{M}$ (sphere). On the hysteresis curve, $B = \mu_0(H + M)$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The operating point satisfies $H = -M/3$ (demagnetisation line for a sphere). On the second-quadrant hysteresis curve, $H < 0$ and $M > 0$. The intersection gives:

$H = -M/3$, $B = \mu_0(H + M) = \mu_0(M - M/3) = (2/3)\mu_0 M$.

For the linear part of the hysteresis curve (approximation), $M \approx M_r + (H/H_c)(M_r - 0)$. At $H = -M_r/3$: $M \approx M_r(1 - M_r/(3H_c))$. With $M_r = 8 \times 10^5$ and $H_c = 5 \times 10^4$: $M_r/(3H_c) = 800/(150) = 5.33 > 1$, so the operating point is below the coercive field — the sphere cannot sustain this magnetisation.

This shows that spherical magnets are impractical for most applications: the demagnetisation factor of 1/3 is too large. Real permanent magnets are long cylinders or horseshoe shapes to minimise $N_d$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

The magnetic scalar potential satisfies $\nabla^2\Phi_m = \nabla\cdot\mathbf{M}$ with boundary condition $\partial\Phi_m/\partial n|_{\text{outside}} - \partial\Phi_m/\partial n|_{\text{inside}} = -\sigma_m$ at the surface. Show that this is the same mathematical problem as the electrostatic Poisson equation with charge density $\rho_e = -\nabla\cdot\mathbf{P}$ and surface charge $\sigma_e = \mathbf{P}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Replace $\mathbf{M}$ with $\mathbf{P}$ and $\Phi_m$ with $\Phi_e$ (with a sign). The equations are identical in form.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The electrostatic potential satisfies $\nabla^2\Phi_e = -\rho_e/\epsilon_0 = \nabla\cdot\mathbf{P}/\epsilon_0$ (from $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{D} = \rho_f$ and $\mathbf{D} = \epsilon_0\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{P}$, with $\rho_f = 0$ giving $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = -\nabla\cdot\mathbf{P}/\epsilon_0$). The boundary condition on $\Phi_e$ at the surface: $\epsilon_0(E_{2n} - E_{1n}) = \sigma_e = \mathbf{P}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}$, which gives $\partial\Phi_e/\partial n|_1 - \partial\Phi_e/\partial n|_2 = \sigma_e/\epsilon_0$.

The magnetic problem has $\nabla^2\Phi_m = \nabla\cdot\mathbf{M}$ (same form with $\mathbf{M}$ replacing $\mathbf{P}/\epsilon_0$). The boundary condition: $\partial\Phi_m/\partial n|_2 - \partial\Phi_m/\partial n|_1 = -\sigma_m = -\mathbf{M}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}$.

The mathematical structures are identical under the mapping $\mathbf{P}/\epsilon_0 \leftrightarrow \mathbf{M}$, $\Phi_e \leftrightarrow -\Phi_m$. Every solution method for the electrostatic problem (image charges, multipole expansion, Green's functions [10.01.02]) carries over directly to the magnetic problem.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Compute the demagnetisation factor $N_a$ for a prolate spheroid (cigar shape) with aspect ratio $a/b = 10$, where $a$ is the semi-axis along the symmetry axis. Use the formula:

$$N_a = \frac{1-e^2}{2e^3}\left(\ln\frac{1+e}{1-e} - 2e\right), \qquad e^2 = 1 - b^2/a^2$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $a/b = 10$: $b/a = 0.1$, $e^2 = 1 - 0.01 = 0.99$, $e = 0.995$. Then evaluate.

For $a/b = 10$: $b^2/a^2 = 0.01$, $e^2 = 0.99$, $e = \sqrt{0.99} \approx 0.995$.

$1 - e^2 = 0.01$. $2e^3 = 2(0.995)^3 \approx 1.970$.

$\ln\frac{1+e}{1-e} = \ln\frac{1.995}{0.005} = \ln(399) \approx 5.989$.

$N_a = \frac{0.01}{1.970}(5.989 - 1.990) = \frac{0.01}{1.970}\times 3.999 \approx 0.0203$.

So $N_a \approx 0.02$ — the demagnetisation along the long axis is very small. The sum rule gives $N_b = N_c = (1 - N_a)/2 \approx 0.49$.

A prolate magnet magnetised along its long axis has $N_d \approx 0.02$, meaning the demagnetisation field is only 2% of M. This is why bar magnets are long and thin: the shape minimises the self-demagnetisation.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the energy of a uniformly magnetised ellipsoid in its own field is $U = (1/2)\mu_0\mathbf{M}\cdot\overleftrightarrow{\mathbf{N}}\cdot\mathbf{M}\,V$ where $V$ is the volume. This is the **magnetostatic self-energy**.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The energy density is $u = -(1/2)\mathbf{M}\cdot\mathbf{B}_d$ where $\mathbf{B}_d = \mu_0\mathbf{H}_d = -\mu_0\overleftrightarrow{\mathbf{N}}\cdot\mathbf{M}$ is the demagnetisation field. Integrate over the volume.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The magnetostatic self-energy is the energy stored in the field produced by the magnetised body itself:

$$U = -\frac{1}{2}\int_V \mathbf{M}\cdot\mathbf{B}_d\,d\tau$$

where $\mathbf{B}_d = \mu_0\mathbf{H}_d = -\mu_0\overleftrightarrow{\mathbf{N}}\cdot\mathbf{M}$ is the demagnetisation B-field inside the ellipsoid. Since both $\mathbf{M}$ and $\mathbf{H}_d$ are uniform:

$$U = -\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{M}\cdot\mathbf{B}_d\,V = \frac{\mu_0}{2}\mathbf{M}\cdot\overleftrightarrow{\mathbf{N}}\cdot\mathbf{M}\,V$$

For a sphere: $U = (\mu_0/6)M^2 V$. For a long cylinder magnetised axially ($N_d = 0$): $U = 0$. For a thin slab magnetised perpendicularly ($N_d = 1$): $U = (\mu_0/2)M^2 V$.

The self-energy favours magnetisation along the axis with the smallest $N_d$ — the magnet "wants" to be magnetised along the long axis. This is the **shape anisotropy** energy, which competes with the magnetocrystalline anisotropy to determine the easy axis of a real magnet.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

A magnetic material has a linear hysteresis curve $M = \chi_m H$ with $\chi_m > 0$ (no saturation). The sample is a sphere. Show that the effective susceptibility $\chi_{\text{eff}} = M/H_{\text{applied}}$ is $\chi_m/(1 + \chi_m/3)$, where $H_{\text{applied}}$ is the external field far from the sphere.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Inside the sphere: $H_{\text{in}} = H_{\text{applied}} + H_d = H_{\text{applied}} - M/3$. Also $M = \chi_m H_{\text{in}}$. Solve simultaneously.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The H-field inside the sphere has two contributions: the applied field $\mathbf{H}_{\text{applied}}$ and the demagnetisation field $\mathbf{H}_d = -(1/3)\mathbf{M}$:

$$H_{\text{in}} = H_{\text{applied}} - M/3$$

The constitutive relation gives $M = \chi_m H_{\text{in}} = \chi_m(H_{\text{applied}} - M/3)$. Solving for $M$:

$$M(1 + \chi_m/3) = \chi_m H_{\text{applied}}$$
$$M = \frac{\chi_m}{1 + \chi_m/3}H_{\text{applied}}$$

The effective susceptibility is $\chi_{\text{eff}} = \chi_m/(1 + \chi_m/3)$. For $\chi_m \ll 1$: $\chi_{\text{eff}} \approx \chi_m$ (demagnetisation is negligible). For $\chi_m \gg 1$ (ferromagnet): $\chi_{\text{eff}} \approx 3$ — the sphere acts as if it has susceptibility 3 regardless of how large $\chi_m$ is.

This limiting behaviour is the **flux concentration** effect: a high-permeability sphere in a uniform field concentrates the flux, but the demagnetisation field limits the concentration factor to $1/(1 + \chi_m N_d)$ which saturates for large $\chi_m$.
</details>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The Green's function approach on ellipsoidal domains is beyond Mathlib's current scope. `lean_status: none`.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Demagnetisation factors for general ellipsoids.** The exact formulas involve incomplete elliptic integrals. For a general ellipsoid with semi-axes $a \geq b \geq c$:

$$N_a = \frac{abc}{2}\int_0^\infty \frac{ds}{(a^2 + s)\sqrt{(a^2+s)(b^2+s)(c^2+s)}}$$

with $N_b$ and $N_c$ obtained by cyclic permutation. The integral can be expressed in terms of the Legendre elliptic integrals $F(\phi, k)$ and $E(\phi, k)$. Tabulated values appear in Osborn (1945) and are reprinted in every standard EM reference.

**The magnetic circuit analogy.** In devices with high-permeability cores (transformers, inductors), the magnetic flux follows the core like current follows a wire. The **reluctance** $\mathcal{R} = \ell/(\mu A)$ (where $\ell$ is the path length and $A$ the cross-section) is the magnetic analogue of electrical resistance. Gaps in the core have reluctance $\mathcal{R}_{\text{gap}} = \ell_g/(\mu_0 A)$, which dominates because $\mu_0 \ll \mu$. This is the basis of gapped inductor and transformer design.

**Demagnetisation and magnetic recording.** In magnetic thin films used for data storage (hard drives, magnetic tape), the bits are tiny magnetised regions on a thin film. The demagnetisation field of each bit opposes its magnetisation and causes **demagnetisation noise** that limits the storage density. Modern perpendicular magnetic recording uses engineered multilayer structures that reduce the effective $N_d$ below the naive thin-film value of 1.

**Synthesis.** The magnetic scalar potential and demagnetisation factor complete the magnetostatic description of materials by providing the mathematical tools for computing internal fields in finite samples. The foundational insight is that only ellipsoidal samples produce uniform internal fields, which generalises the method of separation of variables from the Laplace equation to problems with magnetisation sources. The central message is that the demagnetisation factor $N_d$ is a purely geometric quantity that controls the B-field inside any magnetised body and therefore determines the performance of permanent magnets, inductors, and magnetic recording media. Putting these together, the magnetic scalar potential parallels the electrostatic potential [10.01.02], the demagnetisation factor controls the energy of magnetised bodies [10.03.05], and the reluctance concept extends to the magnetic circuit analogy used in engineering.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Sum rule for demagnetisation factors).** The principal demagnetisation factors of a general ellipsoid satisfy $N_x + N_y + N_z = 1$.

*Proof.* Consider a uniformly magnetised ellipsoid with $\mathbf{M} = M_0\hat{\mathbf{x}}$. The B-field inside is $B_x = \mu_0(1 - N_x)M_0$. The flux through the ellipsoid is $\Phi_x = \int B_x\,dA_x = \mu_0(1-N_x)M_0 \cdot (4\pi abc/3)$ (the cross-sectional area perpendicular to $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$, averaged).

Now compute the same flux by integrating the far-field dipole over a large enclosing surface. The total dipole moment is $\mathbf{m} = V\mathbf{M} = (4\pi abc/3)M_0\hat{\mathbf{x}}$. The far field is a pure dipole: $\mathbf{B} = (\mu_0/4\pi)(2m\cos\theta\,\hat{\mathbf{r}} - m\sin\theta\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})/r^3$. The total flux through a large sphere is $\oint B_r\,r^2\,d\Omega = 0$ for the dipole field (equal positive and negative contributions from the northern and southern hemispheres). But by Gauss's law: $\oint\mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{a} = 0$ always.

The argument proceeds by computing the integral $\int_V \nabla\cdot\mathbf{B}\,d\tau = 0$ and relating the divergence to the demagnetisation factors. The uniform field $\mathbf{B}_{\text{in}} = \mu_0(\mathbf{M} - \overleftrightarrow{\mathbf{N}}\cdot\mathbf{M})$ has zero divergence, and the matching with the external dipole field constrains $\text{Tr}(\overleftrightarrow{\mathbf{N}}) = 1$. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The concept of the demagnetisation factor was introduced by Thomson (Lord Kelvin) in the 1850s during his study of the magnetic properties of iron samples of different shapes. He recognised that the measured susceptibility of a material depends on the sample geometry, and that only the ellipsoidal shape produces a uniform internal field.

The exact formulas for general ellipsoids were derived independently by several authors. The most cited reference is Osborn (1945), who tabulated the demagnetisation factors and provided the elliptic-integral expressions. Stoner (1945) independently derived the same results in the same journal issue.

The magnetic scalar potential has an interesting philosophical aspect: it introduces "magnetic charges" $\rho_m = -\nabla\cdot\mathbf{M}$ as a computational tool, even though no magnetic monopoles exist in nature. The formalism works because the magnetisation produces the same external field as a fictitious charge distribution. This is an example of a mathematical fiction that produces correct physical results — the charges are not real, but the field they produce is.
