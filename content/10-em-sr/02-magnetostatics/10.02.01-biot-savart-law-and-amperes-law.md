## Intuition [Beginner]

Static charges produce electric fields. Moving charges produce something else: a **magnetic field** $\mathbf{B}$. The experimental fact is that a current-carrying wire deflects a nearby compass needle — the same needle that points toward geographic north in Earth's field rotates when you switch on the current. The needle responds to $\mathbf{B}$; the current creates it.

The **Lorentz force law** tells you what a magnetic field *does* to a charge $q$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$:

$$
\mathbf{F} \;=\; q\,\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}.
$$

The cross product means the force is perpendicular to both the velocity and the field. A charge moving *along* $\mathbf{B}$ feels no force; a charge moving *across* $\mathbf{B}$ curves sideways. Magnetic forces do no work (force perpendicular to displacement), so they change direction but not speed.

The **Biot-Savart law** tells you the $\mathbf{B}$ field produced by a small piece of current-carrying wire. For a short segment of wire carrying current $I$, the field at a point a distance $r$ away points perpendicular to both the wire and the line from wire to point, and its magnitude drops as $1/r^2$. Adding up the contributions from every segment of wire gives the total field. For a long straight wire, the result is a field that circles the wire, with magnitude $\mu_0 I / (2\pi r)$ where $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space.

The constant $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}$ T$\cdot$m/A defines the strength of the magnetic interaction. Compare with $\epsilon_0$, which defines the strength of the electric interaction. Together they determine the speed of light: $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\epsilon_0}$. The two constants are not independent — fixing $c$ and $\epsilon_0$ determines $\mu_0$, and vice versa. Since 2019, $\mu_0$ is a defined constant in the SI system, with the same status as $c$.

**Ampere's law** is the magnetic analogue of Gauss's law. Where Gauss's law relates the total electric flux through a closed surface to the enclosed charge, Ampere's law relates the total $\mathbf{B}$ field *tangent to* a closed loop to the current passing through the loop. The line integral of $\mathbf{B}$ around any closed loop equals $\mu_0$ times the total current threading that loop. This is most useful when symmetry pins down the direction of $\mathbf{B}$: for the long straight wire, a solenoid, and a toroid, Ampere's law gives the field in a few lines.

A key difference between electric and magnetic fields: electric field lines start on positive charges and end on negative charges. Magnetic field lines always form closed loops — they never start or stop, because there are no magnetic monopoles (no isolated north or south poles). If you cut a bar magnet in half, you get two smaller bar magnets, each with a north and south pole. Mathematically, this is expressed by saying that the divergence of $\mathbf{B}$ is always zero: there are no sources or sinks for the magnetic field.

## Visual [Beginner]

A long straight wire carrying current $I$ out of the page produces circular magnetic field lines centred on the wire, lying in the plane of the page. The field lines get farther apart as you move outward — the field weakens as $1/r$. A compass placed near the wire orients tangent to the nearest circle. Reverse the current direction and every arrow on every circle reverses.

For a circular current loop, the field lines emerge from one side (the "north pole"), loop around outside, and re-enter the other side (the "south pole"), forming closed curves that look like the field lines of a bar magnet. Inside the loop the field is approximately uniform and points along the axis; outside it resembles a dipole field. A solenoid (many loops stacked) strengthens this pattern: nearly uniform field inside, very weak field outside. The more tightly wound the solenoid and the longer it is relative to its diameter, the more uniform the interior field.

Magnetic field lines never cross (the field is single-valued at each point) and never begin or end (no magnetic monopoles). They form closed loops or extend to infinity. This contrasts with electric field lines, which begin on positive charges and end on negative charges. The topological difference between the two types of field lines reflects the physical difference: electric charges exist as isolated sources, but magnetic charges (monopoles) have never been observed.

The right-hand rule gives the direction of $\mathbf{B}$ around a current: point your right thumb in the direction of the current, and your fingers curl in the direction of the magnetic field lines. For a long wire, this means the field circles counterclockwise when viewed from the tip of the current arrow. For a current loop, the field inside points in the direction your fingers curl when your thumb points along the current.

Inside a **solenoid** (a long cylindrical coil of wire), the field lines run straight and parallel down the bore, tightly packed and uniform — a region of nearly constant $\mathbf{B}$. Outside the solenoid the field lines loop back around, but for a long solenoid the exterior field is weak compared to the interior.

The **right-hand rule** determines direction: point your right thumb along the current; your fingers curl in the direction of $\mathbf{B}$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Two long parallel wires carry current $I = 10$ A in the same direction, separated by $d = 0.05$ m. Find the magnetic force per unit length between them.

**Step 1. Field of wire 1 at the location of wire 2.** Wire 1 creates a field of magnitude $B_1 = \mu_0 I / (2\pi d)$ at the position of wire 2. Substitute:

$$
B_1 \;=\; \frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7})(10)}{2\pi(0.05)} \;=\; \frac{4 \times 10^{-6}}{0.1} \;=\; 4 \times 10^{-5} \;\text{T}.
$$

**Step 2. Force on wire 2.** A length $\ell$ of wire 2, carrying current $I$ in a field $B_1$, experiences force $F = I \ell B_1$ (the cross-product magnitude, since the field is perpendicular to the wire). Force per unit length:

$$
\frac{F}{\ell} \;=\; I B_1 \;=\; (10)(4 \times 10^{-5}) \;=\; 4 \times 10^{-4} \;\text{N/m}.
$$

**Step 3. Direction.** Two parallel currents in the *same* direction attract. (If they were opposed, the force would be repulsive.) The result is an attractive force of $4 \times 10^{-4}$ N/m — small, but measurable, and the principle behind the operational definition of the ampere.

**Both A and B** — option D.

A cross product vanishes when either factor is zero or when the two vectors are parallel. When $\mathbf{v} \perp \mathbf{B}$ (option C), the cross product has *maximum* magnitude $|\mathbf{v}||\mathbf{B}|$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A steady current density $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}')$ produces a static magnetic field. The fundamental law is the **Biot-Savart law**:

$$
\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') \times (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|^3} \, d^3 r'.
$$

For a thin wire carrying current $I$ along a curve $\mathcal{C}$, this reduces to

$$
\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \oint_{\mathcal{C}} \frac{d\boldsymbol{\ell}' \times (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|^3}.
$$

The Biot-Savart law is the magnetic analogue of Coulomb's law for $\mathbf{E}$: both are $1/r^2$ laws, both superpose linearly, and both can be derived from the appropriate Maxwell equation plus a Green's-function inversion. The cross product in the Biot-Savart law reflects the vector character of current (a vector source) compared to the scalar character of charge.

**Ampere's law (integral form).** For any closed loop $\Gamma$ bounding a surface $S$,

$$
\boxed{\;\;\oint_\Gamma \mathbf{B} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} \;=\; \mu_0 \int_S \mathbf{J} \cdot d\mathbf{a} \;=\; \mu_0 I_{\mathrm{enc}}.\;\;}
$$

The left side is the **circulation** of $\mathbf{B}$ around $\Gamma$; the right side is $\mu_0$ times the total current $I_{\mathrm{enc}}$ threading $S$. The sign convention: the direction of $d\boldsymbol{\ell}$ and the orientation of $d\mathbf{a}$ are linked by the right-hand rule.

**Ampere's law (differential form).** Applying Stokes' theorem to the integral form,

$$
\boxed{\;\;\nabla \times \mathbf{B} \;=\; \mu_0 \mathbf{J}.\;\;}
$$

This is the magnetostatic Maxwell equation. The curl on the left is non-zero only where current is present — currents are the *sources of curl* $\mathbf{B}$, just as charges are the sources of $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}$. (The full time-dependent Maxwell equation adds the displacement current $\mu_0 \epsilon_0 \,\partial \mathbf{E}/\partial t$ to the right side; see unit [10.04.01].)

**The vector potential.** Since $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ (no magnetic monopoles), there exists a vector field $\mathbf{A}$ such that

$$
\mathbf{B} \;=\; \nabla \times \mathbf{A}.
$$

The vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ is the magnetic analogue of the electrostatic scalar potential $V$ ($\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V$). It is not unique: replacing $\mathbf{A} \to \mathbf{A} + \nabla \chi$ for any smooth function $\chi$ leaves $\mathbf{B}$ unchanged. This **gauge freedom** is fixed by imposing a gauge condition — the standard magnetostatic choice is the **Coulomb gauge** $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = 0$, developed in the Master tier.

In the Coulomb gauge, the vector potential satisfies a Poisson equation $\nabla^2 \mathbf{A} = -\mu_0 \mathbf{J}$, solved by

$$
\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|} \, d^3 r'.
$$

**Magnetic force on a current loop.** A current-carrying wire in an external field $\mathbf{B}$ experiences a force. For a wire segment carrying current $I$ along $d\boldsymbol{\ell}$, the force element is $d\mathbf{F} = I \, d\boldsymbol{\ell} \times \mathbf{B}$. A closed loop in a uniform field experiences zero net force but a **torque**

$$
\boldsymbol{\tau} \;=\; \mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{B},
$$

where $\mathbf{m} = I \mathbf{a}$ is the **magnetic dipole moment** and $\mathbf{a}$ is the oriented area vector of the loop. This is the magnetic analogue of $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{p} \times \mathbf{E}$ for an electric dipole.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- Ampere's law in the form $\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}$ is valid only in **magnetostatics** (steady currents, $\partial \mathbf{E}/\partial t = 0$). Time-varying fields require the displacement-current correction $\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \,\partial \mathbf{E}/\partial t$. Applying the magnetostatic form to a charging capacitor gives a contradiction — the displacement current resolves it.
- The Biot-Savart law gives the field of a *steady* current. It is not the correct Green's function for time-dependent currents; the retarded-potential formulation of the full Maxwell equations [10.04.01] replaces instantaneous propagation with retardation at speed $c$.
- The vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ is gauge-dependent and not directly measurable in classical electrodynamics. Its line integral $\oint \mathbf{A} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = \Phi_B$ (the magnetic flux through the loop) *is* gauge-invariant. In quantum mechanics $\mathbf{A}$ acquires direct physical significance through the Aharonov-Bohm effect (Master tier).
- A magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{m}$ is *not* a pair of magnetic charges (a "north" and "south" pole). All known magnetic dipoles are current loops (or spin, which is quantum-mechanical). No magnetic monopole has been observed; $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ holds in all known situations.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Ampere's law from the Biot-Savart law).** *The Biot-Savart law*

$$
\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') \times (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|^3} \, d^3 r'
$$

*implies $\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}$ for steady currents satisfying $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$.*

**Proof.** Write the Biot-Savart kernel using the identity $(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')/|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|^3 = -\nabla(1/|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|)$. Then

$$
\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; -\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') \times \nabla \frac{1}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|} \, d^3 r' \;=\; \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \nabla \times \int \frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|} \, d^3 r',
$$

where the curl passes through the integral (it acts on $\mathbf{r}$, not $\mathbf{r}'$) and the sign flip comes from $\mathbf{J} \times (-\nabla f) = \nabla f \times \mathbf{J} = \nabla \times (\mathbf{J} f)$ with $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}')$ treated as constant under $\nabla$. Recognising the integral as the vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ in the Coulomb gauge, $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$.

Now take the curl of $\mathbf{B}$:

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{B} \;=\; \nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) \;=\; \nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}) - \nabla^2 \mathbf{A}.
$$

In the Coulomb gauge, $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = 0$. The vector Poisson equation $\nabla^2 \mathbf{A} = -\mu_0 \mathbf{J}$ follows from the Green's-function solution for $\mathbf{A}$ and the identity $\nabla^2(1/|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|) = -4\pi \delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')$. Therefore

$$
\nabla \times \mathbf{B} \;=\; 0 + \mu_0 \mathbf{J} \;=\; \mu_0 \mathbf{J}. \qquad \blacksquare
$$

The steady-current condition $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$ enters at the step where the Coulomb gauge is imposed: the vector potential $\mathbf{A} = (\mu_0/4\pi) \int \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}')/|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'| \, d^3 r'$ satisfies $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = 0$ if and only if $\nabla' \cdot \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') = 0$ (integrate by parts; the boundary term vanishes for localised currents).

### Worked example: field of a solenoid at intermediate level

A solenoid of length $L$ with $n$ turns per unit length carries current $I$. Use Ampere's law to find $\mathbf{B}$ inside and outside.

Choose a rectangular Amperean loop with one long side inside the solenoid (parallel to the axis, length $\ell$) and the opposite long side outside. The two short sides are perpendicular to the axis. By symmetry, $\mathbf{B}$ inside is axial and uniform; outside, the field of an infinite solenoid is zero (field lines that leave one end return through the other, and the exterior region is field-free in the ideal limit).

Ampere's law:

$$
\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} \;=\; B_{\mathrm{in}} \, \ell \;=\; \mu_0 (n \ell) I.
$$

The enclosed current is $n\ell$ turns times $I$. Solving:

$$
B_{\mathrm{in}} \;=\; \mu_0 n I.
$$

The field is uniform, axial, and independent of the solenoid's cross-section. Outside, the same Amperean loop with both long sides exterior gives zero enclosed current for a loop that does not thread the solenoid, so $B_{\mathrm{out}} = 0$ in the infinite-length limit.

### Second worked example: field of a current sheet

An infinite flat sheet in the $yz$-plane carries a uniform surface current density $\mathbf{K} = K\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ (current per unit width, in A/m). Find $\mathbf{B}$ on both sides.

By symmetry, $\mathbf{B}$ must be independent of position on each side and must point along $\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ on one side and $-\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ on the other (by the right-hand rule: current along $\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ produces $\mathbf{B}$ along $\pm\hat{\mathbf{z}}$). Use a rectangular Amperean loop of length $\ell$ straddling the sheet, with one side at $x > 0$ and one at $x < 0$:

$$
\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} = B_+\,\ell + B_-\,\ell = \mu_0 K\ell.
$$

By the antisymmetry $B_+ = -B_-$ (the field reverses across the sheet), $2B_+ = \mu_0 K$, so

$$
\mathbf{B} = \begin{cases} +\frac{\mu_0 K}{2}\,\hat{\mathbf{z}} & x > 0, \\ -\frac{\mu_0 K}{2}\,\hat{\mathbf{z}} & x < 0. \end{cases}
$$

The field is uniform on each side and reverses direction across the sheet. This result is the magnetic analogue of the electric field of an infinite charge sheet ($E = \sigma/(2\epsilon_0)$). Two parallel current sheets with opposite current directions produce a uniform field between them and zero outside — the principle behind the Helmholtz coil and the magnetic shielding used in electron microscopy and atomic physics experiments.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Use the Biot-Savart law to show that the magnetic field of an infinite straight wire carrying current $I$ at distance $s$ from the wire is $B = \mu_0 I / (2\pi s)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Parametrise the wire as $\mathbf{r}' = z'\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$. Compute $d\boldsymbol{\ell}' \times (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')$ and integrate over $z' \in (-\infty, \infty)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Place the wire along the $z$-axis with the field point at $\mathbf{r} = s\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}$. Then $d\boldsymbol{\ell}' = dz'\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$, $\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}' = s\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} - z'\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$, and

$$
d\boldsymbol{\ell}' \times (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}') \;=\; dz'\,\hat{\mathbf{z}} \times (s\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} - z'\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}) \;=\; s\,dz'\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}.
$$

The Biot-Savart integral becomes

$$
\mathbf{B} \;=\; \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{s\,dz'}{(s^2 + z'^2)^{3/2}} \,\hat{\mathbf{y}} \;=\; \frac{\mu_0 I s}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{2}{s^2} \,\hat{\mathbf{y}} \;=\; \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi s} \,\hat{\mathbf{y}}.
$$

The integral evaluates via the substitution $z' = s\tan\theta$, giving $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dz'/(s^2 + z'^2)^{3/2} = 2/s^2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

A circular loop of radius $R$ carries current $I$. Use the Biot-Savart law to find $\mathbf{B}$ at the centre of the loop.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Every segment $d\boldsymbol{\ell}$ is perpendicular to the vector from the segment to the centre, and every segment is at distance $R$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Parametrise the loop as $\mathbf{r}' = R(\cos\phi'\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \sin\phi'\,\hat{\mathbf{y}})$, so $d\boldsymbol{\ell}' = R\,d\phi'(-\sin\phi'\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \cos\phi'\,\hat{\mathbf{y}})$. The field point is the origin $\mathbf{r} = 0$, so $\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}' = -R(\cos\phi'\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \sin\phi'\,\hat{\mathbf{y}})$ and $|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'| = R$.

$$
d\boldsymbol{\ell}' \times (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}') \;=\; R\,d\phi'(-\sin\phi'\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + \cos\phi'\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}) \times (-R\cos\phi'\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} - R\sin\phi'\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}) \;=\; R^2\,d\phi'\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}.
$$

Integrating over $0 \leq \phi' \leq 2\pi$:

$$
\mathbf{B}(0) \;=\; \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \cdot \frac{2\pi R^2}{R^3} \,\hat{\mathbf{z}} \;=\; \frac{\mu_0 I}{2R} \,\hat{\mathbf{z}}.
$$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

A coaxial cable has inner conductor of radius $a$ carrying total current $I$ uniformly distributed, and outer conductor of radius $b$ carrying current $I$ in the opposite direction. Find $\mathbf{B}$ in the three regions: $s < a$, $a < s < b$, and $s > b$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply Ampere's law to circular loops of radius $s$ centred on the axis. The enclosed current depends on which region the loop is in.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By symmetry $\mathbf{B} = B(s)\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$. Ampere's law: $2\pi s \, B = \mu_0 I_{\mathrm{enc}}$.

- **$s < a$:** Current density $J = I/(\pi a^2)$. Enclosed current $I_{\mathrm{enc}} = J \cdot \pi s^2 = I s^2/a^2$. So $B = \mu_0 I s/(2\pi a^2)$.

- **$a < s < b$:** Full inner current is enclosed: $I_{\mathrm{enc}} = I$. So $B = \mu_0 I/(2\pi s)$.

- **$s > b$:** Both currents enclosed, equal and opposite: $I_{\mathrm{enc}} = I - I = 0$. So $B = 0$.

The field is confined to the region between the conductors — a key property for signal integrity in coaxial transmission lines.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A square loop of side $a$ carrying current $I$ sits in the $xy$-plane, centred at the origin. Compute the magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{m}$ and the torque $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ when the loop is placed in a uniform field $\mathbf{B} = B_0\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The magnetic moment is $\mathbf{m} = I\,\mathbf{a}$ where $\mathbf{a}$ is the oriented area vector.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The area of the loop is $a^2$ and the oriented area vector is $\mathbf{a} = a^2\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ (right-hand rule with current circulating counterclockwise viewed from $+z$). The magnetic dipole moment is

$$
\mathbf{m} \;=\; I a^2 \,\hat{\mathbf{z}}.
$$

The torque is $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{m} \times \mathbf{B} = Ia^2\,\hat{\mathbf{z}} \times B_0\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} = Ia^2 B_0\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}$. The torque tends to rotate the loop so that $\mathbf{m}$ aligns with $\mathbf{B}$ — the loop wants to orient its area vector along the field.

</details>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ follows directly from the Biot-Savart law.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{J} \times \mathbf{C}) = \mathbf{C} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{J}) - \mathbf{J} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{C})$, noting that $\nabla$ acts on $\mathbf{r}$, not $\mathbf{r}'$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write the Biot-Savart integrand as $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') \times \hat{\mathbf{R}}/R^2$ where $\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'$ and $\hat{\mathbf{R}} = \mathbf{R}/R$. Taking the divergence:

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} \;=\; \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \nabla \cdot \!\left(\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') \times \frac{\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2}\right) d^3 r'.
$$

Since $\nabla$ acts on $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{J}$ depends on $\mathbf{r}'$, $\nabla \times \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') = 0$. The vector identity $\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{A} \times \mathbf{C}) = \mathbf{C} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) - \mathbf{A} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{C})$ gives

$$
\nabla \cdot \!\left(\mathbf{J} \times \frac{\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2}\right) \;=\; \frac{\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{J}) - \mathbf{J} \cdot \!\left(\nabla \times \frac{\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2}\right).
$$

The first term is zero ($\mathbf{J}$ independent of $\mathbf{r}$). The curl of $\hat{\mathbf{R}}/R^2 = -\nabla(1/R)$ is $\nabla \times \nabla(1/R) = 0$. Both terms vanish, so $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$.

This is the proof that $\mathbf{B}$ is divergence-free in the Biot-Savart theory — it does not depend on the steady-current assumption.

</details>

## Biot-Savart as convolution and the Helmholtz decomposition [Master]

The Biot-Savart law is a convolution of the current density with a vector Green's function:

$$
\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') \times \frac{\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2} \, d^3 r' \;=\; (\mathbf{G} * \mathbf{J})(\mathbf{r}),
$$

where $\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'$, $R = |\mathbf{R}|$, and $\mathbf{G}(\mathbf{R}) = (\mu_0/4\pi)\,\hat{\mathbf{R}} \times / R^2$ is the magnetic Green's kernel. The cross-product structure distinguishes this from the scalar Coulomb Green's function $1/(4\pi\epsilon_0 R)$.

The **Helmholtz decomposition** states that any sufficiently smooth vector field $\mathbf{F}$ vanishing at infinity can be decomposed as $\mathbf{F} = -\nabla \Phi + \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$ where $\Phi$ is a scalar potential and $\mathbf{A}$ is a vector potential. For the magnetic field: $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ eliminates the scalar part ($\Phi = 0$), leaving $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$ as the unique decomposition. The Biot-Savart law provides the explicit convolution formula for $\mathbf{A}$:

$$
\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|} \, d^3 r'.
$$

The curl of this gives Biot-Savart for $\mathbf{B}$; the divergence of this (using $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$) is zero — the Coulomb gauge is automatic.

The Helmholtz decomposition has an important consequence: any divergence-free field can be written as the curl of a vector potential. This is why the statement $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ guarantees the existence of $\mathbf{A}$. The converse is also immediate: $\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) = 0$ for any $\mathbf{A}$, so any field expressible as a curl is automatically divergence-free. This tight connection between the divergence-free condition and the existence of a vector potential underlies the entire mathematical structure of magnetostatics and carries over to the time-dependent case where $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$ remains true (it is one of the homogeneous Maxwell equations).

## Gauge freedom and the Coulomb gauge [Master]

The vector potential is defined only up to a gradient: $\mathbf{A} \to \mathbf{A} + \nabla \chi$ leaves $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$ unchanged for any smooth $\chi$. This is **gauge freedom**, and it reflects a redundancy in the description — the physical field $\mathbf{B}$ has fewer degrees of freedom than the mathematical object $\mathbf{A}$.

The **Coulomb gauge** fixes this redundancy by imposing $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = 0$. In this gauge the static equations become:

$$
\nabla^2 \mathbf{A} \;=\; -\mu_0 \mathbf{J}, \qquad \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} \;=\; 0.
$$

The Poisson equation for each component of $\mathbf{A}$ is solved by the integral formula above. The Coulomb gauge condition $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = 0$ holds automatically when $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$ (steady currents) because the divergence passes through the integral and $\nabla^2(1/R) = -4\pi\delta^3(\mathbf{R})$ gives $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = (\mu_0/4\pi) \int \mathbf{J} \cdot \nabla(1/R) \, d^3r' = -(\mu_0/4\pi) \int (\nabla' \cdot \mathbf{J})/R \, d^3r' = 0$.

Other gauge choices exist. The **Lorenz gauge** $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \,\partial V/\partial t = 0$ is the natural choice for time-dependent problems because it decouples the wave equations for $\mathbf{A}$ and $V$. The Coulomb gauge is natural for magnetostatics because it renders $\mathbf{A}$ instantaneous (no retardation) and the Poisson equation is straightforward.

The gauge freedom of electromagnetism has a deep geometric interpretation. In the language of differential geometry, $\mathbf{A}$ is a connection on a $U(1)$ fibre bundle, and gauge transformations are changes of local section (local coordinate choices on the fibre). The physical content — the field $\mathbf{B}$ and its effects — is gauge-invariant, living in the curvature of the bundle. This pattern (connection, curvature, gauge invariance) generalises to non-abelian gauge theories like the $SU(2)\times U(1)$ electroweak theory and $SU(3)$ quantum chromodynamics, where the gauge group is non-commutative and the theory becomes nonlinear. Electromagnetism is the simplest and best-understood gauge theory, serving as the template for the entire framework of the Standard Model.

The energy stored in a magnetic field is $U_B = \frac{1}{2\mu_0}\int |\mathbf{B}|^2\,d^3r$, analogous to the electrostatic energy $U_E = \frac{\epsilon_0}{2}\int|\mathbf{E}|^2\,d^3r$. For a solenoid of volume $V$ with $B = \mu_0 n I$, the energy density is $u_B = B^2/(2\mu_0) = \mu_0 n^2 I^2/2$. Expressing this in terms of the self-inductance $L$ (defined by $\Phi = LI$ for the total flux through the solenoid), $U_B = \frac{1}{2}LI^2$. This is the magnetic analogue of the capacitor energy $U_C = \frac{1}{2}CV^2$, and both expressions follow from the work done against the field to assemble the configuration.

## The Aharonov-Bohm effect (preview) [Master]

In classical electrodynamics, $\mathbf{A}$ is a mathematical convenience — only $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$ is physical. In quantum mechanics this is no longer true. The **Aharonov-Bohm effect** (1959) demonstrates that a charged particle can be affected by $\mathbf{A}$ even in a region where $\mathbf{B} = 0$.

Consider a long solenoid carrying current, producing a confined magnetic field inside and $\mathbf{B} = 0$ outside. The vector potential outside is $\mathbf{A} = (\Phi_B / 2\pi s)\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$ where $\Phi_B$ is the flux through the solenoid. A quantum-mechanical electron beam split into two paths encircling the solenoid acquires a phase difference

$$
\Delta\phi \;=\; \frac{e}{\hbar} \oint \mathbf{A} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell} \;=\; \frac{e\Phi_B}{\hbar},
$$

even though $\mathbf{B} = 0$ along both paths. The interference pattern shifts by an amount depending on the enclosed flux — a direct measurement of the line integral of $\mathbf{A}$, which equals the flux of $\mathbf{B}$ through the enclosed area by Stokes' theorem. This confirms that $\mathbf{A}$ (or rather, the holonomy of the connection $\mathbf{A}$) has physical significance beyond $\mathbf{B}$.

The Aharonov-Bohm effect is the entry point to the gauge-theory viewpoint on electromagnetism: $\mathbf{A}$ is a connection on a $U(1)$ principal bundle over space, $\mathbf{B}$ is its curvature, and gauge transformations are bundle automorphisms. The holonomy (Wilson loop) $\oint \mathbf{A} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\ell}$ is the gauge-invariant observable associated with a closed curve.

The Aharonov-Bohm effect was confirmed experimentally by Tonomura et al. (1986) using electron holography with a toroidal magnet enclosed in a superconducting shield. The shield ensured $\mathbf{B} = 0$ outside the toroid, yet the interference pattern shifted by the predicted amount. This experiment is regarded as the definitive confirmation that the vector potential has physical reality in quantum mechanics. The effect has practical implications for the design of nanoscale electronic devices, where quantum coherence of electron wave functions over mesoscopic distances makes the phase accumulated from $\mathbf{A}$ directly observable in transport measurements.

## Multipole expansion for magnetic fields [Master]

For a localised current distribution, the vector potential admits a multipole expansion analogous to the electrostatic case. In the Coulomb gauge, the leading term at large distance is

$$
\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}) \;\approx\; \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{\mathbf{m} \times \hat{\mathbf{r}}}{r^2},
$$

where the **magnetic dipole moment** is

$$
\mathbf{m} \;=\; \frac{1}{2} \int \mathbf{r}' \times \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') \, d^3 r'.
$$

There is no magnetic monopole term ($\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ forbids it). The dipole field is

$$
\mathbf{B}_{\mathrm{dip}}(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi r^3}\big[3(\mathbf{m} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{r}})\hat{\mathbf{r}} - \mathbf{m}\big].
$$

This has the same angular structure as the electric dipole field $\mathbf{E}_{\mathrm{dip}}$ with $\mathbf{p} \to \mathbf{m}$ and $1/\epsilon_0 \to \mu_0$. Higher-order terms (magnetic quadrupole, octupole) fall as $1/r^3$, $1/r^4$, etc. For most purposes the dipole approximation suffices at distances large compared to the source size.

The absence of a magnetic monopole term in the multipole expansion is a direct consequence of $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$. If magnetic monopoles existed (Dirac monopoles, charge $g$), the leading term would be $\mathbf{B} = (\mu_0/4\pi)(g\hat{\mathbf{r}}/r^2)$ and the quantisation condition $ge = n\hbar/2$ would follow from requiring single-valuedness of the wave function.

For a flat current loop of area $\mathbf{a}$ carrying current $I$, the magnetic moment simplifies to $\mathbf{m} = I\mathbf{a}$. The dipole field dominates at distances $r \gg \sqrt{A}$ where $A$ is the loop area. The field energy stored in the dipole field is $U = (\mu_0/4\pi)(2m^2/3R^3)$ where $R$ is the effective radius, giving a self-energy that diverges as $R \to 0$ — the classical electron radius problem, resolved by recognising that the point-dipole is an idealisation.

The torque on a magnetic dipole in an external field is $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{m}\times\mathbf{B}$, and the potential energy is $U = -\mathbf{m}\cdot\mathbf{B}$. In a non-uniform field, the dipole also experiences a force $\mathbf{F} = \nabla(\mathbf{m}\cdot\mathbf{B})$, which is the mechanism behind magnetic attraction: a bar magnet (macroscopic dipole) is pulled toward regions of stronger field. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) exploits the torque on nuclear magnetic moments in a strong applied field, using radio-frequency pulses to flip the moments and detecting the precession as they relax back to equilibrium.

## Historical notes [Master]

The magnetic effect of electric currents was discovered by **Hans Christian Oersted** in 1820, who observed that a compass needle deflects near a current-carrying wire. This was the first experimental link between electricity and magnetism. Oersted was delivering a lecture on electricity to a lay audience when he noticed the deflection; he spent the next several months investigating the effect systematically before publishing. His discovery caused a sensation in the European physics community and triggered an intense burst of activity.

**Jean-Baptiste Biot** and **Felix Savart** (1820) measured the $1/r$ dependence of the field around a long wire and formulated the $1/r^2$ law for the contribution of a current element. They determined the field direction (perpendicular to both the wire and the radial line) by careful compass measurements at many positions around the wire. **Andre-Marie Ampere** (1820-1826) established the force law between current-carrying wires, developed the mathematical theory of the interaction of current elements, and formulated the circuital law bearing his name. Ampere's experimental and theoretical work was so thorough that Maxwell called him the "Newton of electricity." Within weeks of hearing about Oersted's result, Ampere had demonstrated that two parallel current-carrying wires attract or repel depending on the relative direction of the currents, and had begun developing his mathematical framework.

The **solenoid** was studied by Ampere himself, who recognised that a tightly wound helical coil produces a nearly uniform field in its interior — the magnetic analogue of a parallel-plate capacitor. The toroid (a solenoid bent into a circle) confines the field entirely within its windings, providing a geometry with zero external field. These configurations remain central to electromagnet design and to the magnetic confinement of plasmas in fusion research.

The vector potential was introduced by **Franz Ernst Neumann** (1845) in his work on electromagnetic induction, and developed by **William Thomson (Lord Kelvin)** and **James Clerk Maxwell**. Neumann recognised that the induced EMF in a circuit could be expressed compactly as the time derivative of the flux of a vector potential, but he treated $\mathbf{A}$ as a computational device. Maxwell took the field concept more seriously, using $\mathbf{A}$ as the dynamical variable in his electromagnetic theory. The gauge-theory interpretation dates to **Weyl** (1918, scale invariance) and **London** (1927, phase invariance), with the modern $U(1)$ bundle formulation due to **Wu and Yang** (1975).

**Carl Friedrich Gauss** and **Wilhelm Weber** developed sensitive magnetometers and established the first geomagnetic observatory in Gottingen in 1833, creating the first worldwide magnetic monitoring network. Their telegraph (also 1833) was the first practical electromagnetic communication device, connecting the observatory to the university over a distance of about 1 km.

## Connections [Master]

**To differential geometry.** The vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ is a connection 1-form on a $U(1)$ principal bundle over $\mathbb{R}^3$. The magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ is the curvature 2-form $F = dA$. Gauge transformations are bundle automorphisms. The Aharonov-Bohm phase is the holonomy of the connection around a closed curve. This is the simplest example of a Yang-Mills theory and the template for the gauge-theory description of the other fundamental forces.

**To Faraday's law [10.03.01].** The magnetostatic Maxwell equation $\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}$ becomes one of the four time-dependent Maxwell equations when the displacement current is added (unit [10.04.01]). The vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ will reappear as the dynamical variable in the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formulations of electrodynamics, and its time derivative is related to the electric field by $\mathbf{E} = -\partial \mathbf{A}/\partial t - \nabla V$.

**To quantum mechanics.** The minimal-coupling Hamiltonian $H = |\mathbf{p} - e\mathbf{A}|^2/(2m) + eV$ is the starting point for the quantum theory of charged particles in electromagnetic fields. Landau levels, the quantum Hall effect, and flux quantisation in superconductors all derive from the interplay between $\mathbf{A}$ and the wave function's phase.

**To biophysics [17.09.02].** Ionic current loops in neurons produce magnetic fields detectable by magnetoencephalography (MEG). The Biot-Savart law gives the field from a known current distribution; inverse problems (inferring current from measured $\mathbf{B}$) are the core computational challenge of MEG source localisation.

**To plasma physics and fusion.** The magnetostatic equations govern the design of magnetic confinement devices for fusion plasmas. Tokamaks use toroidal and poloidal magnetic fields (computed from Ampere's law for the coil currents) to confine a hot plasma. The force-free condition $\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \alpha\mathbf{B}$ (for constant $\alpha$) describes equilibria where the magnetic force vanishes — relevant to the solar corona and astrophysical plasmas.

**To the Standard Model.** Electromagnetism is the $U(1)$ factor in the gauge group $SU(3)\times SU(2)\times U(1)$ of the Standard Model. The weak and strong interactions are described by non-abelian gauge theories ($SU(2)$ and $SU(3)$) whose mathematical structure generalises the $\mathbf{A}$-to-$\mathbf{B}$ relationship: the connection (gauge field) produces a curvature (field strength) via a covariant derivative, and gauge bosons (photons, W/Z bosons, gluons) mediate the forces. The Biot-Savart law and Ampere's law are the abelian prototypes.

**To electromagnetic waves [10.04.02].** The full time-dependent Maxwell-Ampere law $\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J} + \mu_0\epsilon_0\,\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t$ adds the displacement current $\mu_0\epsilon_0\,\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t$ to Ampere's magnetostatic law. This extra term is what makes self-sustaining electromagnetic wave propagation possible. Without it, the curl-of-curl derivation in the wave-equation unit would not close, and there would be no light.
