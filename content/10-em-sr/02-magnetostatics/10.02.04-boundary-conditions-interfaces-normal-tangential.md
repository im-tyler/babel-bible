## Intuition [Beginner]

When an electromagnetic field crosses from one material to another (say, from glass to air), the field changes abruptly at the boundary. But not all components change in the same way. The key principle is:

- **Normal components** (perpendicular to the surface) of **D** and **B** are **continuous** across the boundary (unless there is surface charge or surface monopoles).
- **Tangential components** (parallel to the surface) of **E** and **H** are **continuous** across the boundary (unless there is surface current).

Think of it this way: the field lines of **D** (representing free charge effects) bend at the interface but do not break — they continue across, just at a different angle. Similarly, the field lines of **B** never have endpoints (no magnetic monopoles), so the normal component of **B** must be continuous.

For the tangential components, the electric field **E** along the surface cannot jump, because that would require infinite energy (it would mean a voltage difference over zero distance). The same argument applies to **H** unless there is a surface current flowing on the boundary.

| Tangential $H_t$ | Continuous | Jump if surface current $K_f$ present |

## Worked example [Beginner]

A uniform electric field $\mathbf{E}_0 = E_0\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ exists in vacuum. A large flat dielectric slab ($\epsilon_r = 4$) is placed perpendicular to the field. Find the fields inside the dielectric.

The field is entirely normal to the surface (the $z$-direction is perpendicular to the slab). The boundary condition for the normal component of $\mathbf{D}$ is: $D_1^n = D_2^n$ (no free surface charge). In vacuum, $D_1 = \epsilon_0 E_0$. Inside the dielectric, $D_2 = \epsilon E_2 = 4\epsilon_0 E_2$. Setting them equal: $\epsilon_0 E_0 = 4\epsilon_0 E_2$, so $E_2 = E_0/4$. The electric field is reduced by a factor of 4 inside the dielectric. The bound surface charge on the dielectric creates an opposing field that partially cancels the applied field.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

At an interface between media 1 and 2 with unit normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ pointing from 1 to 2, the four boundary conditions are:

$$\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot (\mathbf{D}_2 - \mathbf{D}_1) = \sigma_f$$

$$\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot (\mathbf{B}_2 - \mathbf{B}_1) = 0$$

$$\hat{\mathbf{n}} \times (\mathbf{E}_2 - \mathbf{E}_1) = 0$$

$$\hat{\mathbf{n}} \times (\mathbf{H}_2 - \mathbf{H}_1) = \mathbf{K}_f$$

where $\sigma_f$ is the free surface charge density and $\mathbf{K}_f$ is the free surface current density.

**Derivation (pillbox method for normal D).** Construct a Gaussian pillbox of area $\Delta a$ and vanishing height straddling the interface. Apply Gauss's law: $\oint \mathbf{D} \cdot d\mathbf{a} = Q_{f,\text{enc}}$. As the height shrinks to zero, the side contribution vanishes, leaving $D_2^n \Delta a - D_1^n \Delta a = \sigma_f \Delta a$, hence $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot (\mathbf{D}_2 - \mathbf{D}_1) = \sigma_f$.

**Derivation (amperian loop for tangential E).** Construct a rectangular loop of length $\Delta l$ and vanishing height straddling the interface. Apply Faraday's law: $\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -d\Phi_B/dt$. As the height shrinks to zero, the area vanishes, so the flux term vanishes, leaving $(E_2^t - E_1^t)\Delta l = 0$, hence $E_1^t = E_2^t$.

**In linear media.** For linear, isotropic media with $\mathbf{D} = \epsilon\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B} = \mu\mathbf{H}$, the boundary conditions become:

$$\epsilon_1 E_1^n = \epsilon_2 E_2^n \quad (\text{if } \sigma_f = 0)$$

$$B_1^n = B_2^n$$

$$E_1^t = E_2^t$$

$$\frac{B_1^t}{\mu_1} = \frac{B_2^t}{\mu_2} \quad (\text{if } \mathbf{K}_f = 0)$$

The field lines refract at the interface. For $\mathbf{E}$, the angles $\theta_1, \theta_2$ with respect to the normal satisfy $\epsilon_1 \tan\theta_2 = \epsilon_2 \tan\theta_1$. For $\mathbf{B}$, the analogous relation is $\mu_1 \tan\theta_2 = \mu_2 \tan\theta_1$.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (Refraction of field lines at a dielectric interface).**

Consider an interface between two linear dielectrics with permittivities $\epsilon_1$ and $\epsilon_2$. The electric field in each medium has normal and tangential components. From the boundary conditions: $E_1^t = E_2^t$ and $\epsilon_1 E_1^n = \epsilon_2 E_2^n$.

Let $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ be the angles the fields make with the normal. Then $E_1^t = E_1 \sin\theta_1$, $E_1^n = E_1 \cos\theta_1$, and similarly for medium 2. From the tangential condition: $E_1 \sin\theta_1 = E_2 \sin\theta_2$. From the normal condition: $\epsilon_1 E_1 \cos\theta_1 = \epsilon_2 E_2 \cos\theta_2$. Dividing:

$$\frac{\tan\theta_1}{\epsilon_1} = \frac{\tan\theta_2}{\epsilon_2}$$

This is the **law of refraction for electric field lines**. When $\epsilon_2 > \epsilon_1$, the field bends toward the normal (like light entering a denser medium). The field lines crowd together in the higher-$\epsilon$ medium because $D$ is continuous (same number of lines) but $E$ is smaller.

**Bridge.** The boundary conditions are the foundation for solving every electromagnetic problem involving material interfaces: dielectric interfaces [10.04.03], conductor boundaries [10.01.03], waveguide walls [10.04.04], and cavity surfaces [10.04.05]. The foundational insight is that Maxwell's equations in differential form are valid only in regions where the material properties are smooth; at interfaces, the differential equations must be supplemented by the boundary (jump) conditions. The central message is that the boundary conditions are not additional postulates but direct consequences of the integral form of Maxwell's equations applied to infinitesimal volumes and loops straddling the interface. Putting these together, the four conditions (two for normal components, two for tangential) provide exactly the constraints needed to determine the field on both sides of the interface given the source distribution, and they generalise to time-dependent problems without change (the time-derivative terms vanish as the pillbox or loop height goes to zero).

At the surface of a perfect conductor in electrostatics, the electric field inside is zero. State the boundary condition for $\mathbf{D}$ just outside and find the surface charge density.

Inside the conductor $E = 0$, so $D = 0$. The boundary condition is $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot \mathbf{D}_{\text{out}} = \sigma_f$.

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Two dielectrics with $\epsilon_1 = 2\epsilon_0$ and $\epsilon_2 = 5\epsilon_0$ share a flat interface. The field in medium 1 is $\mathbf{E}_1 = 3\hat{\mathbf{n}} + 4\hat{\mathbf{t}}$ (units of V/m). Find $\mathbf{E}_2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Tangential: $E_1^t = E_2^t$. Normal: $\epsilon_1 E_1^n = \epsilon_2 E_2^n$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Tangential component: $E_2^t = E_1^t = 4$ V/m. Normal component: $\epsilon_1 E_1^n = \epsilon_2 E_2^n$, so $2\epsilon_0 \cdot 3 = 5\epsilon_0 \cdot E_2^n$, giving $E_2^n = 6/5 = 1.2$ V/m. Therefore $\mathbf{E}_2 = 1.2\,\hat{\mathbf{n}} + 4\,\hat{\mathbf{t}}$ V/m. The normal component decreased (higher $\epsilon$ means smaller $E$ for the same $D$) while the tangential component stayed the same.
</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the boundary condition for the tangential component of $\mathbf{H}$ from Ampere's law applied to an amperian loop straddling the interface.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply $\oint \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = I_{f,\text{enc}}$ to a rectangle of length $\ell$ and vanishing height. The enclosed free current is $K_f \ell$ where $K_f$ is the surface current density.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Construct a rectangular loop of length $\ell$ straddling the interface, with sides parallel and perpendicular to the surface. As the height goes to zero: $\oint \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = H_2^t \ell - H_1^t \ell = I_{f,\text{enc}}$. The enclosed free current is $K_f \ell$ (surface current times length), where $K_f$ is the component of surface current perpendicular to the loop. In vector form: $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \times (\mathbf{H}_2 - \mathbf{H}_1) = \mathbf{K}_f$.

If there is no free surface current ($\mathbf{K}_f = 0$), then $H_1^t = H_2^t$: the tangential component of $\mathbf{H}$ is continuous. In linear media with $\mathbf{B} = \mu\mathbf{H}$: $B_1^t/\mu_1 = B_2^t/\mu_2$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B}_0 = B_0\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ makes a 45-degree angle with the normal to a flat interface between two magnetic materials with $\mu_1 = 2\mu_0$ and $\mu_2 = 6\mu_0$. Find the direction and magnitude of $\mathbf{B}_2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The angle with the normal satisfies $\mu_1 \tan\theta_2 = \mu_2 \tan\theta_1$. The normal component of $B$ is continuous; the tangential component of $H$ is continuous.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\theta_1 = 45^\circ$, so $\tan\theta_1 = 1$. From the refraction law: $\mu_1 \tan\theta_2 = \mu_2 \tan\theta_1$, giving $2\mu_0 \tan\theta_2 = 6\mu_0 \cdot 1$, so $\tan\theta_2 = 3$, $\theta_2 = \arctan 3 \approx 71.6^\circ$. The field bends toward the surface in the higher-$\mu$ medium.

Normal component: $B_1^n = B_0 \cos 45^\circ = B_0/\sqrt{2}$. Since $B_n$ is continuous: $B_2^n = B_0/\sqrt{2}$. Then $B_2^t = B_2^n \tan\theta_2 = 3B_0/\sqrt{2}$. So $B_2 = B_0/\sqrt{2}\sqrt{1 + 9} = B_0\sqrt{10/2} = B_0\sqrt{5}$, directed at $71.6^\circ$ from the normal. The field magnitude increased because $H$ is continuous tangentially and $\mu_2 > \mu_1$.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

A surface current $\mathbf{K} = K\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ flows on the flat $x$-$y$ plane ($z = 0$). Find the magnetic field above and below the current sheet using boundary conditions. Assume vacuum on both sides.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

By symmetry and Ampere's law, $B_x$ is the only component and it jumps by $\mu_0 K$ across the sheet. Use $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \times (\mathbf{B}_2/\mu_0 - \mathbf{B}_1/\mu_0) = \mathbf{K}$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For an infinite current sheet in the $x$-$y$ plane with $\mathbf{K} = K\hat{\mathbf{y}}$: by the right-hand rule and symmetry, $\mathbf{B}$ is in the $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ direction, with $B_x > 0$ for $z < 0$ and $B_x < 0$ for $z > 0$ (or vice versa). The normal component ($B_z$) is continuous and equals zero by symmetry. The tangential boundary condition: $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \times (\mathbf{B}_2/\mu_0 - \mathbf{B}_1/\mu_0) = \mathbf{K}$, with $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \hat{\mathbf{z}}$. So $\hat{\mathbf{z}} \times (\mathbf{B}_2 - \mathbf{B}_1)/\mu_0 = K\hat{\mathbf{y}}$. If $\mathbf{B}_1 = B_1\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ (below, $z < 0$) and $\mathbf{B}_2 = B_2\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ (above, $z > 0$): $\hat{\mathbf{z}} \times (B_2 - B_1)\hat{\mathbf{x}}/\mu_0 = (B_2 - B_1)\hat{\mathbf{y}}/\mu_0 = K\hat{\mathbf{y}}$. So $B_2 - B_1 = \mu_0 K$. By symmetry, $B_1 = -B_2$, giving $2B_2 = \mu_0 K$, $B_2 = \mu_0 K/2$ and $B_1 = -\mu_0 K/2$. Above: $\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mu_0 K}{2}\hat{\mathbf{x}}$, below: $\mathbf{B} = -\frac{\mu_0 K}{2}\hat{\mathbf{x}}$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the boundary conditions are equivalent to the requirement that Maxwell's equations hold in the weak (distributional) sense. Explain the connection to the pillbox and loop constructions.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In the weak formulation, the fields are distributions and the derivatives include delta-function contributions at interfaces. The pillbox and loop constructions extract the coefficients of these delta functions.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In the distributional formulation, a field that is smooth except at an interface $S$ has derivatives that contain delta-function terms on $S$. For example, if $\mathbf{D}$ has a jump $[\mathbf{D}] = \mathbf{D}_2 - \mathbf{D}_1$ across $S$, then $\text{div}\,\mathbf{D}$ (in the distributional sense) equals the smooth divergence plus $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot [\mathbf{D}]\,\delta_S$ where $\delta_S$ is the surface delta function.

Gauss's law in the distributional sense: $\text{div}\,\mathbf{D} = \rho_f$ (distributionally). Matching the delta-function terms: $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot [\mathbf{D}]\,\delta_S = \sigma_f\,\delta_S$, giving $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot [\mathbf{D}] = \sigma_f$. This is exactly the pillbox boundary condition.

Similarly, $\text{curl}\,\mathbf{E} = -\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t$ distributionally. The curl of a field with a tangential jump contains $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \times [\mathbf{E}]\,\delta_S$. Since $\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t$ has no delta-function part (B has no normal jump), we get $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \times [\mathbf{E}] = 0$: the tangential E boundary condition from the amperian loop.

The pillbox and loop constructions are simply the physical realisations of extracting the distributional (delta-function) content of Maxwell's equations at discontinuities.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

A dielectric sphere of radius $R$ and permittivity $\epsilon$ is placed in a uniform external field $\mathbf{E}_0$. State the boundary conditions at $r = R$ and explain how they determine the field inside.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

At $r = R$: $E_\theta$ is continuous (tangential), $\epsilon E_r$ is continuous (normal D, no free charge). These two conditions determine the two unknown coefficients in the interior solution $E_{\text{in}} = E_0 3/(\epsilon/\epsilon_0 + 2)$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Tangential condition: $E_\theta^{\text{out}}(R) = E_\theta^{\text{in}}(R)$. Normal condition: $\epsilon_0 E_r^{\text{out}}(R) = \epsilon E_r^{\text{in}}(R)$ (no free surface charge).

The exterior field is $\mathbf{E}_{\text{out}} = \mathbf{E}_0 + \mathbf{E}_{\text{dipole}}$ where the dipole field depends on the polarisation. Applying both conditions simultaneously gives $E_{\text{in}} = 3\epsilon_0/(\epsilon + 2\epsilon_0) \cdot E_0$. For $\epsilon \to \epsilon_0$ (no sphere): $E_{\text{in}} = E_0$. For $\epsilon \to \infty$ (conducting sphere): $E_{\text{in}} = 0$.

The two boundary conditions provide exactly the right number of constraints to determine the two unknown quantities: the interior field strength and the induced dipole moment.
</details>

</aside>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

Mathlib has differential forms and Sobolev space theory but not the electromagnetic boundary conditions, the pillbox/loop constructions, or the distributional interpretation of interface conditions. The surface charge and surface current sources as distributions are absent. `lean_status: none`.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Boundary conditions in the differential-forms language.** Maxwell's equations in the language of differential forms are $dF = 0$ and $d*F = J$ where $F$ is the field 2-form and $J$ is the current 3-form. At a material interface, these equations must hold distributionally. The condition $dF = 0$ implies that the tangential components of $E$ and $H$ (the pullback of $F$ to the interface) are continuous. The condition $d*F = J$ implies that the normal components of $D$ and $B$ (the pullback of $*F$) jump by the surface sources. This formulation is coordinate-free and manifestly Lorentz-invariant.

**Interface conditions for nonlinear and anisotropic media.** For nonlinear media ($\mathbf{D}$ depends nonlinearly on $\mathbf{E}$), the boundary conditions still hold at the level of $\mathbf{D}$ and $\mathbf{E}$: $D_n$ and $E_t$ are continuous. However, the constitutive relation $\mathbf{D}(\mathbf{E})$ is different on each side, so the field on one side is a nonlinear function of the field on the other. For anisotropic media ($\mathbf{D} = \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \cdot \mathbf{E}$ with tensor $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$), the refraction law becomes more complex: $\epsilon_{ij} E_j n_i$ is continuous (normal $D$) and $E_i - (E_j n_j)n_i$ is continuous (tangential $E$).

**Leontovich boundary conditions (impedance boundary conditions).** For a good (but not perfect) conductor, the fields penetrate a skin depth $\delta = \sqrt{2/(\mu\sigma\omega)}$. The Leontovich condition approximates the conductor by a surface impedance: $\mathbf{E}_t = Z_s\, \hat{\mathbf{n}} \times \mathbf{H}_t$ where $Z_s = (1+i)/(\sigma\delta) = \sqrt{\mu\omega/(2\sigma)}(1+i)$ is the surface impedance. This avoids solving for the fields inside the conductor.

**Synthesis.** The boundary conditions at material interfaces are the essential bridge between Maxwell's equations in bulk matter and the solution of practical electromagnetic problems. The foundational insight is that the four boundary conditions (two normal, two tangential) are direct consequences of the integral form of Maxwell's equations and provide exactly the constraints needed to match solutions across interfaces. The central message is that the boundary conditions are universal: they hold for static and time-dependent fields, for linear and nonlinear media, and in any coordinate system. Putting these together, the boundary conditions are the load-bearing structure for every electromagnetic boundary-value problem: dielectrics [10.04.03], conductors [10.01.03], waveguides [10.04.04], cavities [10.04.05], and scattering problems [10.07.06].

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (The four boundary conditions).** The boundary conditions at a material interface are necessary and sufficient for Maxwell's equations to hold in the presence of discontinuities.

*Proof (necessity).* Each boundary condition is derived by applying the corresponding integral law to an infinitesimal volume or loop straddling the interface:

1. $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot [\mathbf{D}] = \sigma_f$: Apply $\oint_S \mathbf{D} \cdot d\mathbf{a} = Q_{f,\text{enc}}$ to a pillbox of vanishing height.
2. $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot [\mathbf{B}] = 0$: Apply $\oint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{a} = 0$ to a pillbox of vanishing height.
3. $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \times [\mathbf{E}] = 0$: Apply $\oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -d/dt\int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{a}$ to a loop of vanishing height (the flux term vanishes).
4. $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \times [\mathbf{H}] = \mathbf{K}_f$: Apply $\oint_C \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = I_{f,\text{enc}} + d/dt\int_S \mathbf{D} \cdot d\mathbf{a}$ to a loop of vanishing height (the displacement current term vanishes).

*Sufficiency.* The boundary conditions ensure that Maxwell's equations hold in the weak (distributional) sense at the interface, which together with the equations in the bulk gives a well-posed problem. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Electrostatics [10.01.03]** uses the E and D boundary conditions to solve conductor and dielectric problems.
- **Dielectrics [10.01.04]** relate D and E through the permittivity; the boundary conditions link fields across interfaces.
- **Plane waves [10.04.03]** use all four boundary conditions to derive Fresnel reflection/transmission coefficients.
- **Waveguides [10.04.04]** require the tangential E and normal B conditions at conducting walls.
- **Magnetic materials [10.02.03]** add the B-H relation and the boundary conditions for H.
- **Covariant EM [10.06.01]** expresses the boundary conditions in a manifestly Lorentz-invariant form.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The boundary conditions were implicit in Maxwell's original formulation (1865) but were first stated explicitly by Oliver Heaviside in the 1880s as part of his vector-calculus reformulation of Maxwell's theory. Heaviside recognised that the differential form of Maxwell's equations requires smooth material properties, and that the interface conditions must be stated separately.

The pillbox and amperian loop constructions became standard pedagogical tools in the 20th century, appearing in every EM textbook from Abraham and Becker (1932) through Griffiths (2017). The distributional interpretation (boundary conditions as delta-function terms in the differential equations) was made precise by Laurent Schwartz in his theory of distributions (1950).

The Leontovich impedance boundary condition (1940s) was an important practical advance: it allowed engineers to treat conducting surfaces (like the walls of a waveguide) without solving for the fields inside the conductor, greatly simplifying antenna and waveguide design.
