## Intuition [Beginner]

The **Green's function** is the solution to a differential equation with a point source. For the wave equation, the Green's function tells us how a disturbance at one point and time propagates to another point at a later time.

For electromagnetic waves, the key result is the **retarded Green's function**: a flash of light at position $\mathbf{r}'$ at time $t'$ arrives at position $\mathbf{r}$ at time $t = t' + |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|/c$. The signal travels at the speed of light, delayed by the travel time. This is the **causal** Green's function: the effect (observation at $\mathbf{r}$) comes after the cause (source at $\mathbf{r}'$).

There is also an **advanced Green's function** where the signal arrives *before* it is sent: $t = t' - |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|/c$. This violates causality and is unphysical, but it is mathematically valid as a solution. The physical solution uses only the retarded Green's function.

The **Feynman propagator** is the average of the retarded and advanced Green's functions (divided by 2). It plays a central role in quantum electrodynamics, where time ordering of events is handled by the propagator rather than by explicit causal selection.

## Visual [Beginner]

| Feynman: $(G_{\rm ret} + G_{\rm adv})/2$ | Time-symmetric | Only in quantum field theory |

## Worked example [Beginner]

A point charge $q$ at the origin suddenly appears at $t = 0$. The retarded potential at distance $r$ is:

$$V(r, t) = \frac{q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r}\Theta(t - r/c)$$

where $\Theta$ is the Heaviside step function. Before time $t = r/c$, the signal has not arrived yet, so $V = 0$. At exactly $t = r/c$, the potential jumps to $q/(4\pi\epsilon_0 r)$. The information that the charge exists propagates outward at speed $c$.

The advanced potential would be $V(r, t) = q\,\Theta(t + r/c)/(4\pi\epsilon_0 r)$, which is nonzero for $t > -r/c$ — the potential exists *before* the charge appears. This is unphysical.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The **Green's function** $G(\mathbf{r}, t; \mathbf{r}', t')$ for the wave equation satisfies:

$$\left(\nabla^2 - \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial t^2}\right)G(\mathbf{r}, t; \mathbf{r}', t') = -4\pi\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')\delta(t - t')$$

**Retarded Green's function.**

$$G_{\rm ret}(\mathbf{r}, t; \mathbf{r}', t') = \frac{\delta(t - t' - |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|/c)}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|}$$

This is nonzero only on the **forward light cone** $t - t' = |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|/c$ (the signal travels at speed $c$).

**Advanced Green's function.**

$$G_{\rm adv}(\mathbf{r}, t; \mathbf{r}', t') = \frac{\delta(t - t' + |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|/c)}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|}$$

This is nonzero on the **backward light cone** $t - t' = -|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|/c$.

**Retarded potentials.** Using $G_{\rm ret}$, the solutions for $V$ and $\mathbf{A}$ are:

$$V(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\frac{\rho(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|}\,d^3r'$$

$$\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|}\,d^3r'$$

where $t_r = t - |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|/c$ is the **retarded time**. The field at $(\mathbf{r}, t)$ depends on the source at the *earlier* time $t_r$.

**Kirchhoff integral.** The field at any point can be expressed as an integral over a closed surface (the Kirchhoff representation):

$$V(\mathbf{r}, t) = -\frac{1}{4\pi}\oint_S\left[\frac{1}{R}\frac{\partial V}{\partial n} - V\frac{\partial}{\partial n}\frac{1}{R} + \frac{1}{cR}\frac{\partial V}{\partial t}\frac{\partial R}{\partial n}\right]_{t_r}\,da$$

This is the basis for diffraction theory [10.08.03].

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (The retarded Green's function for the wave equation).**

**Theorem.** *The retarded Green's function $G_{\rm ret}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \delta(t - r/c)/(4\pi r)$ satisfies the inhomogeneous wave equation with a point source.*

*Proof.* We need to show $\Box G_{\rm ret} = -4\pi\delta^3(\mathbf{r})\delta(t)$ where $\Box = \nabla^2 - c^{-2}\partial_t^2$.

Away from the origin ($r \neq 0$), $G_{\rm ret}$ is a distribution supported on the sphere $t = r/c$ (the forward light cone). For $r > 0$ and $t \neq r/c$: $G_{\rm ret} = 0$, and the delta function vanishes, so the equation is satisfied.

Near the light cone, integrate $\Box G_{\rm ret}$ over a small 4-volume enclosing the origin. By Gauss's theorem in 4D:

$$\int_V \Box G_{\rm ret}\,d^4x = \oint_{\partial V}\left(\nabla G_{\rm ret}\cdot d\mathbf{a} - \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial G_{\rm ret}}{\partial t}\,c\,dt\,dA\right)$$

The integral of $G_{\rm ret}$ over the light cone picks up the delta function, and the $1/r$ factor provides the correct normalisation. Evaluating the integral on a small cylinder around the origin: the radial and time derivatives of $G_{\rm ret}$ contribute surface terms that evaluate to $-4\pi$. This confirms $\Box G_{\rm ret} = -4\pi\delta^3(\mathbf{r})\delta(t)$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The retarded Green's function is the fundamental solution of the wave equation and the basis for all radiation calculations in classical electrodynamics. The foundational insight is that causality selects the retarded (not advanced) Green's function: the field at a point depends on the source at earlier times, with the delay determined by the light travel time. The central message is that the retarded potentials automatically incorporate the finite speed of light, making causality a built-in feature of the solution. Putting these together, the Green's function method provides the systematic framework for computing the electromagnetic fields from arbitrary source distributions [10.08.02], and the Kirchhoff integral provides the basis for diffraction theory [10.08.03]. The Feynman propagator, which is the quantum field theory analogue, generalises this to the quantum regime where time ordering replaces classical causality.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

A point charge $q$ moves along the $z$-axis with constant velocity $v$. Write the retarded time condition for an observer at position $\mathbf{r}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The retarded time $t_r$ satisfies $t - t_r = |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'(t_r)|/c$ where $\mathbf{r}'(t_r) = v t_r\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The source position at time $t_r$ is $\mathbf{r}'(t_r) = v t_r\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$. The retarded time condition is:

$$t - t_r = \frac{1}{c}|\mathbf{r} - vt_r\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}| = \frac{1}{c}\sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + (z - vt_r)^2}$$

This is a quadratic equation for $t_r$ (the unknown retarded time). Squaring: $c^2(t - t_r)^2 = x^2 + y^2 + (z - vt_r)^2$. Expanding and solving: this gives the Lienard-Wiechert potentials, which are the retarded potentials for a moving point charge.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

For the static case ($\partial/\partial t = 0$), show that the retarded potential reduces to the Coulomb potential $V = q/(4\pi\epsilon_0 r)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

When $\rho$ is time-independent, $\rho(\mathbf{r}', t_r) = \rho(\mathbf{r}')$. The time delay drops out.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a static charge distribution: $\rho(\mathbf{r}', t_r) = \rho(\mathbf{r}')$ (the charge density does not depend on time, so the retarded time is irrelevant). The retarded potential becomes:

$$V(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\frac{\rho(\mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|}\,d^3r'$$

This is the standard Coulomb potential, identical to the electrostatic result. The retardation has no effect for static sources because the information "charge is here" has always been available — there was never a time when the field had to wait for the signal to arrive.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the retarded potential of a point charge $q$ moving along the $z$-axis with constant velocity $v$. State the result in terms of the instantaneous position.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

This is the Lienard-Wiechert potential. The result involves the factor $1/(1 - \hat{\mathbf{R}}\cdot\mathbf{v}/c)$ where $\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'(t_r)$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Lienard-Wiechert potential for a charge moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ at constant speed is:

$$V(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{q}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{1}{R(1 - \hat{\mathbf{R}}\cdot\mathbf{v}/c)}\bigg|_{t_r}$$

where $\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{v}t_r$ is the vector from the retarded position to the observation point, $R = |\mathbf{R}|$, and $\hat{\mathbf{R}} = \mathbf{R}/R$. The factor $(1 - \hat{\mathbf{R}}\cdot\mathbf{v}/c)^{-1}$ accounts for the Doppler-like compression or expansion of the field lines due to the motion. When the charge moves toward the observer ($\hat{\mathbf{R}}\cdot\mathbf{v} > 0$), the potential is enhanced. When it moves away, the potential is reduced.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

The **Sommerfeld radiation condition** requires that the far-field behaves as $f(\theta,\phi)e^{ikr}/r$ with $\lim_{r\to\infty}r(\frac{\partial f}{\partial r} - ikf) = 0$. Explain why this selects the retarded (outgoing) solution.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Outgoing waves have $e^{ikr}$ dependence (phase increasing with $r$); incoming waves have $e^{-ikr}$. The condition picks outgoing.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In the frequency domain, the wave equation has two spherical solutions: $e^{ikr}/r$ (outgoing, propagating away from the source) and $e^{-ikr}/r$ (incoming, propagating toward the source). The general solution is a linear combination.

The Sommerfeld condition $\lim_{r\to\infty}r(\frac{\partial f}{\partial r} - ikf) = 0$ selects the outgoing wave. For $f = Ae^{ikr}/r$: $\frac{\partial f}{\partial r} = A(ikr - 1)e^{ikr}/r^2 \approx ikAe^{ikr}/r$ for large $r$. Then $r(\frac{\partial f}{\partial r} - ikf) \approx r(ikAe^{ikr}/r - ikAe^{ikr}/r) = 0$. This satisfies the condition.

For $f = Be^{-ikr}/r$: $\frac{\partial f}{\partial r} = B(-ikr-1)e^{-ikr}/r^2 \approx -ikBe^{-ikr}/r$. Then $r(\frac{\partial f}{\partial r} - ikf) \approx r(-ikBe^{-ikr}/r - ikBe^{-ikr}/r) = -2ikBe^{-ikr} \neq 0$. This violates the condition.

The Sommerfeld condition is the mathematical expression of causality: only outgoing waves are physical at infinity.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

Compute the retarded Green's function in the frequency domain (the Helmholtz Green's function) by Fourier-transforming $G_{\rm ret}(\mathbf{r}, t)$. Show that $G_{\rm ret}(\mathbf{r}, \omega) = e^{ikr}/(4\pi r)$ where $k = \omega/c$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Fourier transform: $G(\mathbf{r}, \omega) = \int G(\mathbf{r}, t)e^{i\omega t}\,dt$. The delta function $\delta(t - r/c)$ makes the integral straightforward.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$$G_{\rm ret}(\mathbf{r}, \omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{\delta(t - r/c)}{4\pi r}\,e^{i\omega t}\,dt = \frac{e^{i\omega r/c}}{4\pi r} = \frac{e^{ikr}}{4\pi r}$$

where $k = \omega/c$. The Fourier transform converts the retarded delta function into an outgoing spherical wave. The Helmholtz equation $(\nabla^2 + k^2)G = -4\pi\delta^3(\mathbf{r})$ is satisfied by $e^{ikr}/(4\pi r)$.

The advanced Green's function in the frequency domain is $G_{\rm adv}(\mathbf{r}, \omega) = e^{-ikr}/(4\pi r)$ (incoming spherical wave). The Feynman propagator is the average: $(e^{ikr} + e^{-ikr})/(8\pi r) = \cos(kr)/(4\pi r)$, which is real and symmetric. In quantum field theory, the Feynman propagator includes an additional prescription for handling the pole at $\omega = 0$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

State the **Wheeler-Feynman absorber theory** and explain how it uses both the retarded and advanced Green's functions to resolve the radiation reaction problem.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The theory postulates that the physical field is the average of retarded and advanced fields: $F = (F_{\rm ret} + F_{\rm adv})/2$. Radiation reaction arises from the response of the absorbing universe.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Wheeler-Feynman absorber theory (1945).** The theory postulates:

1. Every charged particle interacts with other charges via the **time-symmetric** (half-retarded + half-advanced) field: $F = (F_{\rm ret} + F_{\rm adv})/2$.

2. An accelerated charge radiates outward via the retarded field. This radiation is absorbed by distant charges (the "absorber"), which re-radiate via their advanced field. The advanced re-radiation converges on the original charge at the moment of its acceleration, producing the radiation reaction force.

3. The net effect is that the original charge experiences: (a) the time-symmetric field from all other charges (which is purely retarded in the presence of a complete absorber) and (b) a self-force that reproduces the Abraham-Lorentz radiation reaction [10.07.04].

The theory resolves the self-energy problem (the infinite self-force of a point charge on itself) by eliminating the self-field entirely: charges interact only with other charges. The apparent causality violation (advanced fields) is cancelled by the response of the absorber, restoring macroscopic causality.

The theory is mathematically consistent but requires a complete absorber (the entire universe), and its physical status is debated. It influenced the development of quantum electrodynamics and the Feynman propagator.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

The Poisson equation $\nabla^2 G = -4\pi\delta^3(\mathbf{r})$ has the Green's function $G = 1/r$. Show this is the static limit ($\omega \to 0$) of the Helmholtz Green's function $e^{ikr}/(4\pi r)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

As $\omega \to 0$, $k = \omega/c \to 0$, so $e^{ikr} \to 1$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Helmholtz Green's function is $G(\mathbf{r}, \omega) = e^{ikr}/(4\pi r)$ with $k = \omega/c$. In the static limit $\omega \to 0$: $k \to 0$, so $e^{ikr} \to e^0 = 1$. Therefore $G \to 1/(4\pi r)$. The inhomogeneous Helmholtz equation $(\nabla^2 + k^2)G = -\delta^3(\mathbf{r})$ reduces to Poisson's equation $\nabla^2 G = -\delta^3(\mathbf{r})$ as $k \to 0$, and the Coulomb potential $1/(4\pi r)$ is recovered. This confirms that electrostatics is the zero-frequency limit of electrodynamics.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, symbolic).**

For a harmonically oscillating source $\rho(\mathbf{r}, t) = \rho(\mathbf{r})e^{-i\omega t}$, write the retarded potential in terms of the Helmholtz Green's function. Identify the multipole expansion structure.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$V(\mathbf{r})e^{-i\omega t} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\frac{\rho(\mathbf{r}')e^{ik|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|}}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|}\,d^3r'\cdot e^{-i\omega t}$. Expand $e^{ik|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|}/|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|$ in powers of $kr'$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $\rho(\mathbf{r}, t) = \rho(\mathbf{r})e^{-i\omega t}$, the retarded potential is:

$$V(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\frac{\rho(\mathbf{r}')e^{ik|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|}}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|}\,d^3r'$$

The factor $e^{ik|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|}/|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|$ is the Helmholtz Green's function evaluated at $|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|$.

**Multipole expansion:** For $r \gg r'$ (source much smaller than the observation distance), expand $|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'| \approx r - \hat{\mathbf{r}}\cdot\mathbf{r}'$ in the phase and $|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'| \approx r$ in the amplitude:

$$\frac{e^{ik|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|}}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|} \approx \frac{e^{ikr}}{r}\,e^{-ik\hat{\mathbf{r}}\cdot\mathbf{r}'}$$

Expanding $e^{-ik\hat{\mathbf{r}}\cdot\mathbf{r}'} \approx 1 - ik\hat{\mathbf{r}}\cdot\mathbf{r}' + \frac{1}{2}(ik\hat{\mathbf{r}}\cdot\mathbf{r}')^2 - \ldots$:

- **Monopole** ($\ell = 0$): $\int\rho\,d^3r' = q$ (total charge). Gives the Coulomb term $qe^{ikr}/(4\pi\epsilon_0 r)$.
- **Dipole** ($\ell = 1$): $-ik\int\mathbf{r}'\rho\,d^3r' = -ik\mathbf{p}$. Gives the dipole radiation field [10.07.02].
- **Quadrupole** ($\ell = 2$): proportional to $k^2$. Gives the quadrupole radiation [10.07.03].

The expansion in powers of $kr'$ (or equivalently $kd$ where $d$ is the source size) is the multipole expansion.
</details>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

## Advanced results [Master]

**The Kirchhoff representation theorem.** The field at any interior point can be expressed as an integral over the boundary surface, using the retarded Green's function. This is the mathematical foundation for diffraction theory [10.08.03]:

$$V(\mathbf{r}, t) = -\frac{1}{4\pi}\oint_S\left[G_{\rm ret}\frac{\partial V}{\partial n} - V\frac{\partial G_{\rm ret}}{\partial n}\right]\,da$$

In the frequency domain, this becomes the Helmholtz-Kirchhoff integral, which is the basis for Huygens' principle and the Fresnel-Kirchhoff diffraction formula.

**The Feynman propagator in quantum electrodynamics.** The Feynman propagator $D_F(x - x') = \theta(t - t')G_{\rm ret} + \theta(t' - t)G_{\rm adv}$ is time-ordered: it uses the retarded Green's function for positive time differences and the advanced for negative. In momentum space: $D_F(k) = 1/(k^2 + i\epsilon)$. This is the Green's function that appears in Feynman diagrams and encodes the propagation of virtual photons.

**The Jefimenko approach.** Jefimenko (1989) showed that the fields $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ can be written directly in terms of the retarded sources without computing the potentials first. These are the Jefimenko equations [10.08.02], which follow from substituting the retarded potentials into $\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V - \partial\mathbf{A}/\partial t$ and $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$.

**Synthesis.** The Green's function method is the universal framework for solving the wave equation in electrodynamics: it reduces the problem of finding fields from arbitrary sources to a quadrature (integration over the source with the Green's function kernel). The foundational insight is that the retarded Green's function encodes both the wave equation and the causality condition in a single mathematical object. The central message is that the retarded potentials $V$ and $\mathbf{A}$ are obtained by convolving the source ($\rho$, $\mathbf{J}$) with the Green's function, and the fields are then computed from the potentials. Putting these together, the Green's function provides the direct path from sources to fields, which is the basis for radiation calculations [10.07.02], diffraction theory [10.08.03], and the quantum field theory of photons.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Uniqueness of the retarded solution).** The retarded Green's function is the unique solution of the inhomogeneous wave equation that satisfies: (1) $G = 0$ for $t < t'$ (causality), and (2) $G \to 0$ as $r \to \infty$ (Sommerfeld radiation condition).

*Proof.* Suppose $G_1$ and $G_2$ both satisfy the wave equation with the same source and the two conditions. Then $D = G_1 - G_2$ satisfies the homogeneous wave equation with $D = 0$ for $t < t'$ and $D \to 0$ at infinity. By the domain of dependence theorem for the wave equation, the solution in the forward light cone is uniquely determined by the initial data on the $t = t'$ surface (where $D = 0$) and the boundary conditions at infinity (where $D = 0$). Therefore $D = 0$ everywhere, and $G_1 = G_2$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Vector potential [10.02.02]** is the quantity solved for by the Green's function; the retarded vector potential is $\mathbf{A} = \mu_0\int G_{\rm ret} * \mathbf{J}$.
- **Jefimenko's equations [10.08.02]** express $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ directly in terms of the retarded sources.
- **Diffraction [10.08.03]** uses the Kirchhoff integral (derived from the Green's function).
- **Dipole radiation [10.07.02]** is obtained by expanding the retarded potential in powers of $kd$.
- **Radiation reaction [10.07.04]** involves the self-field, which is computed using the Green's function with the source being the charge's own current.
- **Faraday tensor [10.06.01]** provides the covariant formulation; the retarded Green's function generalises to the covariant propagator on Minkowski space.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The concept of the Green's function was introduced by George Green in 1828 (for the Poisson equation). The extension to the wave equation was developed by Kirchhoff (1882), who derived the integral representation theorem and showed that Huygens' principle follows from the wave equation.

The retarded and advanced Green's functions were distinguished by Sommerfeld (1912), who formulated the radiation condition that selects the retarded solution. The Wheeler-Feynman absorber theory (1945) attempted to use both Green's functions symmetrically, eliminating the self-energy problem by postulating direct particle-particle interaction.

The Feynman propagator was introduced by Feynman in 1949 as part of his formulation of quantum electrodynamics. It is the Green's function that appears in Feynman diagrams and represents the amplitude for a virtual photon to propagate from one spacetime point to another. The causal structure (retarded vs advanced) is handled by the $i\epsilon$ prescription in the momentum-space representation.
