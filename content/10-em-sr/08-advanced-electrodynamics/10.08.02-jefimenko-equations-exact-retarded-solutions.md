## Intuition [Beginner]

Suppose you know exactly where every charge and current was, at every moment in the past. Can you figure out what the electric field $\mathbf{E}$ and the magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ are right now?

**Jefimenko's equations** answer yes. They give you exact formulas for $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ at any point in space and any moment in time, provided you know the charge density $\rho$ and the current density $\mathbf{J}$ everywhere at their respective **retarded times** — the times in the past when the signal leaving each source region would arrive at your observation point *now*, travelling at the speed of light.

The key physical idea is **causality with delay**. A change in the charge or current at position $\mathbf{r}'$ at some past moment does not affect the field at position $\mathbf{r}$ until a time $|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|/c$ later — the light-travel time between the two points. The "retarded time" for each source point is:

$$t_r = t - \frac{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|}{c}$$

This says: the field you measure *now* (at time $t$) depends on what the sources were doing at the earlier time $t_r$, and the farther away a source is, the further back in time you have to look.

Jefimenko's equations are **exact**. They make no assumptions about the sources being small, slow, oscillating, or far away. They are the full, causal, time-dependent generalization of the static Coulomb and Biot-Savart laws.

**What makes them different from Maxwell's equations?** Maxwell's equations are differential equations: they tell you how the fields change from point to point and from moment to moment, but they do not directly give you the fields. To get $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ from Maxwell's equations, you need to solve a boundary-value problem. Jefimenko's equations, by contrast, are **integral equations**: they give you $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ directly, provided you know the sources everywhere.

The price is that you need complete knowledge of the source distribution over all of space and time (up to the past light cone). In practice, this is often unavailable, which is why Maxwell's differential equations — which can be solved with local boundary conditions — remain the primary working tool.

**The relationship to potentials.** Jefimenko's equations are derived from the retarded scalar and vector potentials $V$ and $\mathbf{A}$ [10.08.01]. The potentials are computed first, then differentiated to obtain $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$. Jefimenko's contribution was to carry out the differentiation once and for all, producing explicit formulas for the fields. This eliminates the intermediate step of computing potentials and makes the causal relationship between sources and fields fully explicit.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A point charge $q$ sits at the origin and has been there forever. What does Jefimenko's equation for $\mathbf{E}$ give?

The charge density is $\rho(\mathbf{r}') = q\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r}')$ (a point charge at the origin). Because the charge has always been there, $\dot{\rho} = 0$ (the charge density is not changing) and $\dot{\mathbf{J}} = 0$ (there is no current). The only surviving term in Jefimenko's equation for $\mathbf{E}$ is the one proportional to $\rho$ itself.

Evaluating at the retarded time makes no difference here because $\rho$ is constant in time — looking back in time gives the same value. The result is the familiar Coulomb field:

$$\mathbf{E} = \frac{q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r^2}\hat{\mathbf{r}}$$

This confirms that Jefimenko's equations reproduce the static laws when nothing is changing.

**Second example: a current that suddenly turns on.** Suppose a steady current $\mathbf{J}_0$ in a wire is turned on at $t = 0$ (it was zero before that). At time $t$, which parts of the wire contribute to the magnetic field at position $\mathbf{r}$?

Only the parts of the wire with retarded time $t_r \geq 0$ contribute — that is, only source points satisfying $t - R/c \geq 0$, or equivalently $R \leq ct$. This means the magnetic field at $\mathbf{r}$ at time $t$ is determined by all current elements within a sphere of radius $ct$ centered at $\mathbf{r}$. Outside this sphere, the signal has not arrived yet. The sphere expands at the speed of light, gradually encompassing more of the wire until the entire current contributes.

This is the physical content of Jefimenko's equations in action: the field builds up causally, with information arriving from progressively more distant source elements as time passes. No signal arrives faster than light.

**Why the equations have three terms for $\mathbf{E}$.** The electric field depends on three things: where the charges are ($\rho$), how the charges are changing ($\dot{\rho}$), and how the currents are changing ($\dot{\mathbf{J}}$). The first term is the familiar charge-creates-field effect. The second term is a correction that appears when the amount of charge is varying — the field needs to "catch up" with the change. The third term is the most physically distinct: a changing current creates an electric field even where there are no charges. This is Faraday induction at its most fundamental level, and it is the origin of electromagnetic radiation.

For the magnetic field, there are only two terms. The first is the current-creates-field effect (analogous to Biot-Savart). The second is the correction from changing currents. There is no term involving $\dot{\rho}$ in the $\mathbf{B}$ equation — a changing charge density does not directly produce a magnetic field. (It does so only indirectly through the current that accompanies the charge redistribution, via the continuity equation.)

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Jefimenko's equations** give the electric and magnetic fields produced by arbitrary time-dependent charge and current distributions:

$$\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\left[\frac{\rho(\mathbf{r}', t_r)\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2} + \frac{\dot{\rho}(\mathbf{r}', t_r)\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{cR} - \frac{\dot{\mathbf{J}}(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{c^2 R}\right]d^3r'$$

$$\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\left[\frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}', t_r) \times \hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2} + \frac{\dot{\mathbf{J}}(\mathbf{r}', t_r) \times \hat{\mathbf{R}}}{cR}\right]d^3r'$$

where:

- $\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'$ is the vector from the source point to the field point
- $R = |\mathbf{R}|$ and $\hat{\mathbf{R}} = \mathbf{R}/R$
- $t_r = t - R/c$ is the retarded time
- Dots denote time derivatives: $\dot{\rho} = \partial\rho/\partial t$, $\dot{\mathbf{J}} = \partial\mathbf{J}/\partial t$, all evaluated at the retarded time

Each equation contains terms that fall off as $1/R^2$ (near-field or induction terms) and terms that fall off as $1/R$ (radiation terms). The $1/R$ terms are the ones responsible for electromagnetic radiation [10.07.02].

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (Jefimenko's equations from the retarded potentials).**

The retarded potentials [10.08.01] are:

$$V(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\frac{\rho(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{R}\,d^3r'$$

$$\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{R}\,d^3r'$$

The fields are obtained from $\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V - \partial\mathbf{A}/\partial t$ and $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$.

**The challenge** is that the retardation condition $t_r = t - R/c$ couples space and time: the retarded time depends on $\mathbf{r}$ through $R = |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|$. Taking the spatial gradient or time derivative of a retarded quantity produces additional terms.

**Lemma (Derivatives of retarded quantities).** For any function $f(\mathbf{r}', t_r)$:

$$\nabla f(\mathbf{r}', t_r) = -\frac{\dot{f}(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{c}\,\nabla R = \frac{\dot{f}(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{c}\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}$$

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial t}f(\mathbf{r}', t_r) = \dot{f}(\mathbf{r}', t_r)$$

The first result follows because $\nabla t_r = -\nabla R / c = \hat{\mathbf{R}}/c$ (holding $\mathbf{r}'$ fixed). The gradient "reaches back" through the retardation and picks up a time derivative multiplied by the spatial variation of the delay.

**Step 1: Compute $\nabla V$.** Applying the gradient to $V$:

$$\nabla V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\nabla\!\left(\frac{\rho(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{R}\right)d^3r'$$

Using the product rule and the lemma:

$$\nabla\!\left(\frac{\rho}{R}\right) = \frac{1}{R}\,\nabla\rho + \rho\,\nabla\!\left(\frac{1}{R}\right) = \frac{1}{R}\cdot\frac{\dot{\rho}}{c}\,\hat{\mathbf{R}} - \frac{\rho\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2}$$

So:

$$\nabla V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\left[-\frac{\rho\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2} + \frac{\dot{\rho}\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{cR}\right]d^3r'$$

**Step 2: Compute $\partial\mathbf{A}/\partial t$.**

$$\frac{\partial\mathbf{A}}{\partial t} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\dot{\mathbf{J}}(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{R}\,d^3r'$$

**Step 3: Combine for $\mathbf{E}$.**

$$\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V - \frac{\partial\mathbf{A}}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\left[\frac{\rho\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2} - \frac{\dot{\rho}\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{cR}\right]d^3r' - \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\dot{\mathbf{J}}}{R}\,d^3r'$$

Using $\mu_0 = 1/(\epsilon_0 c^2)$:

$$\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\left[\frac{\rho\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2} + \frac{\dot{\rho}\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{cR} - \frac{\dot{\mathbf{J}}}{c^2 R}\right]d^3r'$$

This is Jefimenko's equation for $\mathbf{E}$.

**Step 4: Compute $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$.**

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{A} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\nabla \times \!\left(\frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}', t_r)}{R}\right)d^3r'$$

Using $\nabla \times (f\mathbf{J}) = f\,(\nabla \times \mathbf{J}) + (\nabla f) \times \mathbf{J}$ and noting that $\nabla$ acts on $\mathbf{r}$ (not $\mathbf{r}'$), so $\nabla \times \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}', t_r) = \nabla t_r \times \dot{\mathbf{J}} = (\hat{\mathbf{R}}/c) \times \dot{\mathbf{J}}$:

$$\nabla \times \!\left(\frac{\mathbf{J}}{R}\right) = \frac{1}{R}\cdot\frac{\hat{\mathbf{R}} \times \dot{\mathbf{J}}}{c} - \frac{\hat{\mathbf{R}} \times \mathbf{J}}{R^2}$$

Therefore:

$$\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\left[\frac{\mathbf{J} \times \hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2} + \frac{\dot{\mathbf{J}} \times \hat{\mathbf{R}}}{cR}\right]d^3r'$$

This is Jefimenko's equation for $\mathbf{B}$. $\square$

**Bridge.** Jefimenko's equations are the culmination of the retarded potential framework [10.08.01]: they bypass the potentials entirely and express the fields directly in terms of the sources. The foundational insight is that causality manifests as the retarded time $t_r = t - R/c$ inside every integral, and each time derivative of the source ($\dot{\rho}$, $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$) produces a new physical effect — the $1/R$ radiation terms that carry energy to infinity. The central message is that Maxwell's equations, combined with causality, yield unique, exact expressions for $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ for any source distribution. Putting these together, Jefimenko's equations unify electrostatics, magnetostatics, induction, and radiation in a single causal framework, reducing to Coulomb and Biot-Savart in the static limit and to radiation fields in the far zone.

## Comparison with Coulomb and Biot-Savart [Intermediate+]

**Coulomb's law** gives $\mathbf{E}$ from a static charge distribution:

$$\mathbf{E}_{\rm Coulomb}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\frac{\rho(\mathbf{r}')\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2}\,d^3r'$$

This is the first term in Jefimenko's $\mathbf{E}$ equation with $\rho$ evaluated at time $t$ (not $t_r$), and with $\dot{\rho} = 0$ and $\dot{\mathbf{J}} = 0$. Coulomb's law is an **instantaneous** approximation: it assumes the field responds to the charge distribution at the same instant, with no light-travel delay. This is correct only for truly static distributions.

**Biot-Savart law** gives $\mathbf{B}$ from a steady current:

$$\mathbf{B}_{\rm Biot\text{-}Savart}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}') \times \hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2}\,d^3r'$$

This is the first term in Jefimenko's $\mathbf{B}$ equation with $\mathbf{J}$ evaluated at time $t$ and $\dot{\mathbf{J}} = 0$.

**What Jefimenko adds:**

1. **Retardation.** Every source quantity is evaluated at the retarded time $t_r$, not the present time $t$. For sources close to the observer (small $R$), the retardation is negligible and Coulomb/Biot-Savart are good approximations. For distant or rapidly varying sources, retardation is essential.

2. **Time-derivative terms.** The $\dot{\rho}/R$ and $\dot{\mathbf{J}}/R$ terms have no counterpart in the static laws. These $1/R$ terms are the **radiation fields** — they carry energy away from the source and dominate at large distances.

3. **The $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$ term in $\mathbf{E}$.** A changing current produces an electric field even in the absence of charge. This is the mechanism behind electromagnetic waves: accelerating charges generate time-varying fields that propagate outward.

**The continuity equation and the coupling between $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$.** Charge conservation, expressed as $\dot{\rho} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$, links the $\dot{\rho}$ term in $\mathbf{E}$ to the current $\mathbf{J}$. This is why the $\dot{\rho}$ and $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$ terms in the radiation electric field combine to give a transverse (perpendicular to $\hat{\mathbf{R}}$) radiation field: the continuity equation ensures that the longitudinal component cancels. Physically, the radiation field is a joint effect of charge conservation and time-varying currents — you cannot have one without the other.

**When are Coulomb and Biot-Savart good approximations?** The retarded time is $t_r = t - R/c$. For a source at distance $R$, the retardation (the delay) is $R/c$. If the source varies on a timescale $T$ (say, the period of an oscillation), then retardation is negligible when $R/c \ll T$, or equivalently $R \ll cT = \lambda$ (the wavelength). In this regime, $\rho(\mathbf{r}', t_r) \approx \rho(\mathbf{r}', t)$ and the static formulas are accurate. But for $R \gtrsim \lambda$, the retardation is significant and the full Jefimenko equations must be used.

This has a practical implication: in electrical circuits operating at 60 Hz ($\lambda \approx 5000$ km), retardation is negligible for any laboratory-scale setup, and the quasi-static (Coulomb + Biot-Savart) approximation is excellent. At microwave frequencies ($\lambda \sim$ cm), retardation is essential even across a circuit board.

## The structure of causality [Intermediate+]

Jefimenko's equations reveal a precise causal hierarchy:

1. **The $1/R^2$ terms** ($\rho/R^2$ in $\mathbf{E}$, $\mathbf{J}/R^2$ in $\mathbf{B}$) are the **retarded static fields**. They are present whenever there are charges or currents, regardless of whether anything is changing. They dominate near the source (the near zone). These fields carry energy that oscillates back and forth between $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ but does not propagate to infinity.

2. **The $1/R$ terms involving $\dot{\rho}$ and $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$** are the **radiation fields**. They exist only when sources are changing in time, and they dominate far from the source (the radiation zone). They form transverse electromagnetic waves that carry energy outward at the speed of light. The radiated power is proportional to $|\dot{\mathbf{J}}|^2$, confirming that radiation requires acceleration.

3. **The time ordering is rigid.** A source event at $(\mathbf{r}', t')$ influences the field at $(\mathbf{r}, t)$ if and only if $t - t' = |\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|/c$. Events outside the past light cone of the observation point have no influence. This is the content of retarded causality, encoded in the $t_r = t - R/c$ condition.

This hierarchy shows why radiation is inherently an effect of time-varying sources: static sources produce only $1/R^2$ fields, which do not radiate. Only when sources accelerate (producing $\dot{\mathbf{J}} \neq 0$) does the $1/R$ radiation term appear, carrying energy to infinity.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that Jefimenko's equation for $\mathbf{E}$ reduces to Coulomb's law for a time-independent charge distribution ($\dot{\rho} = 0$, $\mathbf{J} = 0$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

When $\dot{\rho} = 0$ and $\mathbf{J} = 0$, the second and third terms vanish. The retarded time has no effect on $\rho$ because $\rho$ is constant in time.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a static charge distribution: $\dot{\rho} = 0$ (charge density does not change), $\mathbf{J} = 0$ (no current), and $\dot{\mathbf{J}} = 0$. The second and third terms in Jefimenko's $\mathbf{E}$ equation vanish, leaving:

$$\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\frac{\rho(\mathbf{r}', t_r)\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2}\,d^3r'$$

Since $\rho$ is time-independent, $\rho(\mathbf{r}', t_r) = \rho(\mathbf{r}')$ regardless of $t_r$. The result is exactly Coulomb's law.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that Jefimenko's equation for $\mathbf{B}$ reduces to the Biot-Savart law for a steady current ($\dot{\mathbf{J}} = 0$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The second term (the $1/R$ radiation term) vanishes when $\dot{\mathbf{J}} = 0$. The retarded time has no effect on $\mathbf{J}$ because the current is steady.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a steady current: $\dot{\mathbf{J}} = 0$. The second term in Jefimenko's $\mathbf{B}$ equation vanishes, leaving:

$$\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}', t_r) \times \hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2}\,d^3r'$$

Since $\mathbf{J}$ is time-independent, $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}', t_r) = \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}')$. This is the Biot-Savart law.
</details>

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

A charge distribution has $\rho(\mathbf{r}, t) = \rho_0(\mathbf{r})\cos(\omega t)$ and $\mathbf{J} = 0$. Identify which terms in Jefimenko's $\mathbf{E}$ equation survive, and describe the physical character of each.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\dot{\rho} = -\omega\rho_0(\mathbf{r})\sin(\omega t)$. The $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$ term vanishes because $\mathbf{J} = 0$ always. Both the $\rho$ and $\dot{\rho}$ terms survive, evaluated at the retarded time.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $\mathbf{J} = 0$, we have $\dot{\mathbf{J}} = 0$, so the third term vanishes. The charge density and its time derivative at the retarded time are:

$$\rho(\mathbf{r}', t_r) = \rho_0(\mathbf{r}')\cos[\omega(t - R/c)] = \rho_0(\mathbf{r}')\cos(\omega t - kR)$$

$$\dot{\rho}(\mathbf{r}', t_r) = -\omega\rho_0(\mathbf{r}')\sin[\omega(t - R/c)] = -\omega\rho_0(\mathbf{r}')\sin(\omega t - kR)$$

where $k = \omega/c$. The surviving terms are:

$$\mathbf{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\left[\frac{\rho_0(\mathbf{r}')\cos(\omega t - kR)\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{R^2} - \frac{\omega\rho_0(\mathbf{r}')\sin(\omega t - kR)\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{cR}\right]d^3r'$$

The first term ($1/R^2$) is the near-field: it resembles a delayed Coulomb field with oscillating amplitude. The second term ($1/R$) is a radiation field: it propagates outward as an electromagnetic wave. At large distances ($R \gg c/\omega$, i.e., $kR \gg 1$), the $1/R$ term dominates and carries energy to infinity [10.07.02].
</details>

</aside>

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The $1/R$ term in Jefimenko's $\mathbf{B}$ equation is:

$$\mathbf{B}_{\rm rad} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\dot{\mathbf{J}}(\mathbf{r}', t_r) \times \hat{\mathbf{R}}}{cR}\,d^3r'$$

For the oscillating dipole: $\dot{\mathbf{J}}(\mathbf{r}', t_r) = -\omega^2\mathbf{p}_0\cos(\omega t_r)\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r}')$. Substituting and integrating over the delta function (which sets $\mathbf{r}' = 0$, so $R = r$ and $\hat{\mathbf{R}} = \hat{\mathbf{r}}$):

$$\mathbf{B}_{\rm rad} = -\frac{\mu_0\omega^2}{4\pi c r}\cos[\omega(t - r/c)]\,(\mathbf{p}_0 \times \hat{\mathbf{r}})$$

This is the radiation magnetic field of an oscillating electric dipole [10.07.02]. It falls off as $1/r$, propagates as a spherical wave with phase $\omega t - kr$, and has the characteristic $\sin\theta$ angular dependence (where $\theta$ is the angle between $\mathbf{p}_0$ and $\hat{\mathbf{r}}$).
</details>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

A charge $q$ moves with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ along the $x$-axis. The current density is $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}, t) = q\mathbf{v}\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{v}t)$. Compute $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$ for (a) constant velocity and (b) constant acceleration $\mathbf{a}$. Explain which case produces radiation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\dot{\mathbf{J}}$ involves the time derivative of the velocity (for constant velocity, $\dot{\mathbf{v}} = 0$) and the time derivative of the moving delta function. The radiation terms in Jefimenko's equations are proportional to $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**(a) Constant velocity.** With $\mathbf{v}$ constant, $\dot{\mathbf{v}} = 0$. The current density changes only because the charge moves: $\dot{\mathbf{J}} = q\dot{\mathbf{v}}\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{v}t) + q\mathbf{v}\,\frac{d}{dt}\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{v}t)$. The first term vanishes. The second term involves the convective derivative of the delta function and produces the Lienard-Wiechert velocity field — a rearrangement of the static field, not radiation.

**(b) Constant acceleration.** With $\dot{\mathbf{v}} = \mathbf{a}$ constant: $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$ now contains the term $q\mathbf{a}\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{v}t)$ which contributes to the $\dot{\mathbf{J}}/R$ terms in Jefimenko's equations. These are the radiation terms, proportional to the acceleration $\mathbf{a}$.

The physical conclusion: a charge moving at constant velocity does not radiate (it merely carries its Coulomb field along). A charge undergoing acceleration radiates electromagnetic waves. This is the Larmor result [10.07.01]: the radiated power is proportional to $a^2$.
</details>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Starting from Jefimenko's equation for $\mathbf{E}$, take the far-field limit ($R \gg d$ where $d$ is the source size and $R \gg c/\omega$ where $\omega$ is a characteristic frequency) and show that only the $\dot{\mathbf{J}}/R$ term survives. Identify the transversality condition $\hat{\mathbf{R}} \cdot \mathbf{E}_{\rm rad} = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In the far field, $1/R^2 \ll 1/R$, so the $1/R^2$ terms are negligible. The surviving $1/R$ terms are the $\dot{\rho}$ and $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$ terms. Use the continuity equation $\dot{\rho} = -\nabla' \cdot \mathbf{J}$ to relate the $\dot{\rho}$ term to the $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$ term.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In the far field ($R \to \infty$), the $1/R^2$ terms (the Coulomb-like $\rho/R^2$ term) are negligible compared to the $1/R$ terms. The surviving terms are:

$$\mathbf{E}_{\rm rad} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\left[\frac{\dot{\rho}\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{cR} - \frac{\dot{\mathbf{J}}}{c^2 R}\right]d^3r'$$

Using the continuity equation $\dot{\rho} = -\nabla' \cdot \mathbf{J}$, the $\dot{\rho}$ term becomes:

$$\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\frac{-(\nabla' \cdot \mathbf{J})\,\hat{\mathbf{R}}}{cR}\,d^3r'$$

Integrating by parts (moving $\nabla'$ onto $1/R$ which gives $\hat{\mathbf{R}}/R^2$, but in the far field this contributes at higher order) and keeping only the leading $1/R$ contribution, the $\dot{\rho}$ term cancels the component of $\dot{\mathbf{J}}$ along $\hat{\mathbf{R}}$. The result is:

$$\mathbf{E}_{\rm rad} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0 c^2 R}\int\left[\hat{\mathbf{R}} \times (\hat{\mathbf{R}} \times \dot{\mathbf{J}})\right]d^3r'$$

Using the vector identity $\hat{\mathbf{R}} \times (\hat{\mathbf{R}} \times \dot{\mathbf{J}}) = \hat{\mathbf{R}}(\hat{\mathbf{R}} \cdot \dot{\mathbf{J}}) - \dot{\mathbf{J}}$, we see $\hat{\mathbf{R}} \cdot \mathbf{E}_{\rm rad} = 0$: the radiation electric field is transverse (perpendicular to the direction of propagation $\hat{\mathbf{R}}$).

Similarly, the far-field $\mathbf{B}$ is:

$$\mathbf{B}_{\rm rad} = \frac{1}{c}\,\hat{\mathbf{R}} \times \mathbf{E}_{\rm rad}$$

confirming the plane-wave relationship between $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ in the radiation zone.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

**The Darwin approximation.** For sources with $v \ll c$ (non-relativistic motion), expand the retarded potentials to order $1/c^2$ (neglecting radiation) and derive the Darwin Hamiltonian for a system of charges. Show that the inter-particle force includes a velocity-dependent magnetic correction.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand $t_r = t - R/c$ in a Taylor series: $f(\mathbf{r}', t_r) = f(\mathbf{r}', t) - (R/c)\dot{f}(\mathbf{r}', t) + (R^2/2c^2)\ddot{f}(\mathbf{r}', t) - \ldots$. Keep terms through $1/c^2$. The radiation terms enter at order $1/c^3$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Expand the retarded source quantities in powers of $1/c$:

$$f(\mathbf{r}', t_r) = f(\mathbf{r}', t) - \frac{R}{c}\dot{f}(\mathbf{r}', t) + \frac{R^2}{2c^2}\ddot{f}(\mathbf{r}', t) + \ldots$$

For the scalar potential $V$:

$$V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\frac{1}{R}\left[\rho - \frac{R}{c}\dot{\rho} + \frac{R^2}{2c^2}\ddot{\rho}\right]d^3r'$$

The $\dot{\rho}$ term can be integrated by parts using the continuity equation to give a $v^2/c^2$ correction. Similarly for $\mathbf{A}$:

$$\mathbf{A} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\mathbf{J}}{R}\,d^3r' + O(1/c^3)$$

For a system of point charges $q_a$ at positions $\mathbf{r}_a$ with velocities $\mathbf{v}_a$, the Darwin Hamiltonian through $O(1/c^2)$ is:

$$H_{\rm Darwin} = \sum_a \frac{1}{2}m_a v_a^2 + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{a \neq b}\frac{q_a q_b}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r_{ab}}\left[1 - \frac{\mathbf{v}_a \cdot \mathbf{v}_b + (\mathbf{v}_a \cdot \hat{\mathbf{r}}_{ab})(\mathbf{v}_b \cdot \hat{\mathbf{r}}_{ab})}{2c^2}\right]$$

The $1/c^2$ correction includes: the relativistic kinetic energy correction, the magnetic interaction between moving charges, and the retardation correction to the Coulomb potential. The velocity-dependent term is the magnetic force — it is the Darwin approximation to the full retarded interaction, valid for non-relativistic particles and neglecting radiation.
</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that Jefimenko's equations satisfy Maxwell's equations by computing $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}$ and $\nabla \times \mathbf{B} - (1/c^2)\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the fact that the retarded potentials satisfy the Lorenz gauge wave equations. Since $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ are derived from $V$ and $\mathbf{A}$ which satisfy the Lorenz condition, the resulting fields automatically satisfy Maxwell's equations. Direct verification requires careful tracking of retardation derivatives.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Strategy.** Rather than differentiate Jefimenko's equations directly (which involves commuting spatial derivatives with retarded-time integrals), we use the potential representation. The retarded potentials satisfy:

$$\Box V = -\rho/\epsilon_0, \quad \Box\mathbf{A} = -\mu_0\mathbf{J}$$

with the Lorenz gauge condition $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} + (1/c^2)\partial V/\partial t = 0$.

**Gauss's law** ($\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \rho/\epsilon_0$):

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = -\nabla^2 V - \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}) = -\nabla^2 V + \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial t^2} = -\Box V = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}$$

where the second equality uses the Lorenz condition and the third uses the wave equation for $V$.

**Ampere-Maxwell law** ($\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J} + \mu_0\epsilon_0\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t$):

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}) - \nabla^2\mathbf{A}$$

Using the Lorenz condition for $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A}$ and the wave equation $\nabla^2\mathbf{A} - (1/c^2)\partial^2\mathbf{A}/\partial t^2 = -\mu_0\mathbf{J}$:

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = -\frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{A}}{\partial t^2} + \mu_0\mathbf{J} - \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{A}}{\partial t^2} + \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{A}}{\partial t^2}$$

Combining with $\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t = -\nabla(\partial V/\partial t) - \partial^2\mathbf{A}/\partial t^2$ and the Lorenz condition, we recover:

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J} + \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$$

The far more demanding direct verification — differentiating under the integral sign in Jefimenko's equations — reproduces the same result but requires the identity $\nabla^2(1/R) = -4\pi\delta^3(\mathbf{R})$ and careful handling of the retarded time derivatives.
</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Connection to Lienard-Wiechert potentials.** For a point charge $q$ following trajectory $\mathbf{r}_0(t)$, the source distributions are $\rho = q\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0(t))$ and $\mathbf{J} = q\mathbf{v}(t)\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0(t))$. Substituting into Jefimenko's equations and integrating over the delta functions yields the **Lienard-Wiechert fields**:

$$\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \frac{q}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{R}{(\mathbf{R}\cdot\mathbf{u})^3}\left[(c^2 - v^2)\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{R} \times (\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{a})\right]\bigg|_{t_r}$$

where $\mathbf{u} = c\hat{\mathbf{R}} - \mathbf{v}$, $\mathbf{a} = \dot{\mathbf{v}}$, and all quantities are evaluated at the retarded time $t_r$. The first bracket gives the **velocity field** (falls off as $1/R^2$, present even for uniform motion) and the second gives the **acceleration field** (falls off as $1/R$, the radiation field).

The Lienard-Wiechert result can be derived from Jefimenko's equations by performing the spatial integration over the delta function and accounting for the Jacobian factor arising from the retardation condition (the factor $\mathbf{R}\cdot\mathbf{u}/(cR)$ in the integration measure).

**Near fields and far fields.** Jefimenko's equations naturally separate into:

- **Near zone** ($R \ll \lambda = 2\pi c/\omega$): The $1/R^2$ terms dominate. The fields are quasi-static: they resemble the instantaneous Coulomb and Biot-Savart fields, slightly modified by retardation. Energy oscillates between $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ but does not propagate outward on average.

- **Radiation zone** ($R \gg \lambda$): The $1/R$ terms dominate. The fields form transverse electromagnetic waves with $\mathbf{E}_{\rm rad}$, $\mathbf{B}_{\rm rad}$, and $\hat{\mathbf{R}}$ mutually perpendicular, with $\mathbf{B}_{\rm rad} = \hat{\mathbf{R}} \times \mathbf{E}_{\rm rad}/c$. The radiated power per unit solid angle is $dP/d\Omega = R^2|\mathbf{E}_{\rm rad}|^2/(c\mu_0)$ [10.07.02].

- **Intermediate zone** ($R \sim \lambda$): All terms contribute. The fields have complicated spatial structure, and the transition from near-field to far-field behavior occurs.

**The Darwin approximation.** The Darwin approximation (derived from the retarded potentials expanded to $O(1/c^2)$, neglecting radiation) gives an effective Hamiltonian for non-relativistic charged particles:

$$H_{\rm Darwin} = \sum_a\left(\frac{p_a^2}{2m_a} - \frac{p_a^4}{8m_a^3c^2}\right) + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{a \neq b}\frac{q_aq_b}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r_{ab}}\left[1 - \frac{\mathbf{v}_a \cdot \mathbf{v}_b + (\mathbf{v}_a \cdot \hat{\mathbf{r}}_{ab})(\mathbf{v}_b \cdot \hat{\mathbf{r}}_{ab})}{2c^2}\right]$$

This includes relativistic kinetic energy corrections, retardation corrections to the Coulomb potential, and the magnetic interaction between moving charges. It is the standard approximation used in atomic and molecular physics when radiation can be neglected but relativistic corrections to the inter-particle forces are needed.

The Darwin approximation is obtained by Taylor-expanding the retarded time: $f(t_r) = f(t - R/c) = f(t) - (R/c)\dot{f}(t) + (R^2/2c^2)\ddot{f}(t) - \ldots$ and keeping terms through $O(1/c^2)$. The $O(1/c^3)$ terms, which are the radiation terms, are dropped. This is justified when the particle velocities satisfy $v/c \ll 1$ (non-relativistic regime) and the radiation reaction force is negligible. The Darwin Hamiltonian is widely used in quantum chemistry as the basis for Breit-Pauli corrections to the non-relativistic Hamiltonian.

**The role of the continuity equation.** The continuity equation $\dot{\rho} + \nabla' \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$ plays a central role in Jefimenko's equations. It connects the $\dot{\rho}$ term in $\mathbf{E}$ to the spatial variation of $\mathbf{J}$, ensuring that the radiation field is transverse. Without the continuity equation, the $\dot{\rho}\hat{\mathbf{R}}/(cR)$ term would represent a longitudinal radiation component — a field pointing along the direction of propagation — which does not exist in nature. The continuity equation enforces charge conservation and eliminates this unphysical component, leaving only the transverse radiation field.

This is a deep connection: charge conservation (a physical principle) guarantees transversality of electromagnetic radiation (a geometric property of the radiation field). The continuity equation is the bridge between the source dynamics and the wave character of the fields.

**Synthesis.** Jefimenko's equations are the most direct expression of the causal relationship between electromagnetic sources and fields. The foundational insight is that the fields at any spacetime point are determined entirely by the source distribution on the past light cone of that point — no other source information is relevant. The central message is that the three-tier structure of the equations ($\rho/R^2$, $\dot{\rho}/R$, $\dot{\mathbf{J}}/R$ for $\mathbf{E}$; $\mathbf{J}/R^2$, $\dot{\mathbf{J}}/R$ for $\mathbf{B}$) maps directly onto the three physical regimes: static fields, induction fields, and radiation fields. Putting these together, Jefimenko's equations provide the complete, exact, causal solution for Maxwell's equations with arbitrary prescribed sources, serving as the bridge between the retarded potentials [10.08.01] and every downstream application in radiation theory, antenna design, optics, and relativistic electrodynamics.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Jefimenko's equations are the unique causal solution).** Given source distributions $\rho(\mathbf{r}, t)$ and $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}, t)$ satisfying the continuity equation, Jefimenko's equations give the unique electromagnetic fields satisfying Maxwell's equations and the causality condition ($\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{B} = 0$ before the sources are turned on).

*Proof.* The retarded potentials $V$ and $\mathbf{A}$ are the unique solutions of the Lorenz-gauge wave equations satisfying the causality condition (proven in [10.08.01] via the retarded Green's function). The fields $\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V - \partial\mathbf{A}/\partial t$ and $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$ derived from these potentials are therefore the unique causal fields. Jefimenko's equations are obtained by carrying out these derivatives explicitly, so they represent the same unique solution in a different form. Uniqueness follows from the uniqueness of the retarded potentials. $\square$

**Proposition (Static limits).** For time-independent sources, Jefimenko's equations reduce to the Coulomb and Biot-Savart laws.

*Proof.* For static sources: $\dot{\rho} = 0$, $\dot{\mathbf{J}} = 0$, and $\rho(\mathbf{r}', t_r) = \rho(\mathbf{r}')$, $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}', t_r) = \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}')$. In Jefimenko's $\mathbf{E}$ equation, the second and third terms vanish, leaving the Coulomb integral. In the $\mathbf{B}$ equation, the second term vanishes, leaving the Biot-Savart integral. The retarded time has no effect because the sources do not change. $\square$

**Proposition (Transversality of the radiation field).** The far-field radiation electric field satisfies $\hat{\mathbf{R}} \cdot \mathbf{E}_{\rm rad} = 0$.

*Proof.* In the far field, only the $1/R$ terms survive. Using the continuity equation $\dot{\rho} = -\nabla' \cdot \mathbf{J}$ and integrating by parts, the $\dot{\rho}\hat{\mathbf{R}}/(cR)$ term combines with the $\dot{\mathbf{J}}/(c^2R)$ term to produce:

$$\mathbf{E}_{\rm rad} \propto \hat{\mathbf{R}} \times (\hat{\mathbf{R}} \times \dot{\mathbf{J}}) = \hat{\mathbf{R}}(\hat{\mathbf{R}} \cdot \dot{\mathbf{J}}) - \dot{\mathbf{J}}$$

This is manifestly perpendicular to $\hat{\mathbf{R}}$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Retarded potentials [10.08.01]** are the starting point for deriving Jefimenko's equations; the potentials are differentiated to obtain the fields.
- **Coulomb's law and Biot-Savart law [10.04.01]** are the static limits of Jefimenko's equations (instantaneous, $1/R^2$ terms only).
- **Dipole radiation [10.07.02]** is obtained by evaluating the $1/R$ terms in Jefimenko's equations for an oscillating dipole source.
- **Larmor formula [10.07.01]** for the radiated power of an accelerated charge follows from the $1/R$ terms in Jefimenko's $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$.
- **Diffraction theory [10.08.03]** extends the retarded field framework to field propagation through apertures.
- **Faraday tensor [10.06.01]** provides the covariant (relativistic) formulation; Jefimenko's equations are the non-covariant component form.
- **Lienard-Wiechert potentials [10.08.01]** are the point-charge specialisation of the retarded potentials; Jefimenko's equations yield the Lienard-Wiechert fields in this limit.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

**Origins.** The retarded potentials for the scalar and vector potentials were derived by Ludvig Lorenz in 1867 (independently of Maxwell) and later systematized by Lorentz. However, the direct expressions for $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ in terms of retarded sources — bypassing the potentials — were not written down in their modern form until much later.

**Jefimenko (1966).** Oleg Jefimenko published these equations in his textbook *Electricity and Magnetism* (1966), though the underlying mathematics had been available since the late 19th century. Jefimenko's contribution was pedagogical and conceptual: he emphasized that these equations show causality directly — the fields depend on the sources at the retarded time, with no reference to potentials, gauge choices, or auxiliary fields. This perspective makes the causal structure of electromagnetism particularly transparent.

**Earlier results.** The Lienard-Wiechert potentials for point charges were derived independently by Alfred-Marie Lienard (1898) and Emil Wiechert (1900). These are the point-particle specialisation of the retarded potentials. The generalisation to continuous source distributions and the direct field expressions (Jefimenko's equations) are straightforward extensions of the same formalism, but the explicit form was rarely written out in early treatments. Heaviside (1888) and Lorentz (1909) both worked with retarded potentials and understood the causal implications, but they typically computed the fields from the potentials on a case-by-case basis rather than writing the general field formulas explicitly.

**Jefimenko's broader programme.** Oleg Jefimenko (1922–2009) spent much of his career advocating for direct causal formulations of electromagnetism. In addition to the equations that bear his name, he developed causal expressions for the gravitational field in analogy with electromagnetism (Jefimenko's gravitodynamics), arguing that the causal structure of electromagnetism should serve as a template for all field theories. While his gravitational work remains outside the mainstream, the electromagnetic equations are universally accepted as correct and pedagogically valuable.

**The role of Lorenz gauge.** It is worth noting that Jefimenko's equations depend on the Lorenz gauge condition $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} + (1/c^2)\partial V/\partial t = 0$. The retarded potentials are derived in the Lorenz gauge, and the cancellation of the longitudinal radiation component (via the continuity equation) relies on this gauge choice. In other gauges, the retarded potentials take different forms and the field expressions look different, though the resulting $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ are gauge-invariant and hence identical. Jefimenko's equations are the simplest form of the retarded field expressions precisely because the Lorenz gauge respects the causal structure of the wave equation.

**Philosophical significance.** Jefimenko's equations demonstrate that the electromagnetic fields are entirely determined by the past history of the sources, with a delay determined by the speed of light. There is no action at a distance, no instantaneous influence, and no ambiguity. The fields are causal responses to source distributions on the past light cone. This stands in contrast to the Coulomb and Biot-Savart laws, which appear to give instantaneous (acausal) relationships between sources and fields — a limitation that disappears once the full time-dependent equations are used.

**Pedagogical importance.** Griffiths (2017) and Zangwill (2013) both emphasize Jefimenko's equations as the natural endpoint of the retarded potential framework: they show that the fields can be computed directly from the sources without solving a differential equation, provided the source distribution is known everywhere in space and time. The price is that the integrals are over the entire past light cone, which is computationally demanding but physically transparent.
