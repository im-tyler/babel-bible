## Intuition [Beginner]

A **cavity resonator** is a closed metal box that traps electromagnetic waves. Just as a guitar string vibrates at specific frequencies determined by its length, a metal cavity resonates at specific frequencies determined by its dimensions. Each resonant frequency corresponds to a standing-wave pattern called a **mode**.

The most important property of a resonant cavity is its **quality factor** $Q$. This measures how long the cavity stores energy compared to how fast it loses it. A high-$Q$ cavity rings for a long time after being excited (like a well-made bell). A low-$Q$ cavity damps quickly (like a bell wrapped in cloth).

The quality factor is defined as $Q = 2\pi \times$ (energy stored) / (energy lost per cycle). For a copper cavity at microwave frequencies, $Q$ can reach 10,000 or more. For a superconducting cavity (used in particle accelerators), $Q$ can exceed $10^{10}$.

Cavities are used in many applications: microwave ovens, radar receivers, atomic clocks (where the cavity confines the radiation that interacts with atoms), and particle accelerators (where the oscillating electric field in the cavity accelerates charged particles to near the speed of light).

## Worked example [Beginner]

A rectangular copper cavity has dimensions $a = 5$ cm, $b = 3$ cm, $d = 4$ cm. The lowest resonant frequency (TM$_{110}$ mode) is:

$f_{110} = \frac{c}{2}\sqrt{(1/a)^2 + (1/d)^2} = \frac{3\times 10^8}{2}\sqrt{(1/0.05)^2 + (1/0.04)^2}$

$= 1.5\times 10^8\sqrt{400 + 625} = 1.5\times 10^8\times 32.0 = 4.80$ GHz.

The stored energy in this mode depends on the field amplitude. The power loss comes from currents flowing in the copper walls, which have skin depth $\delta \approx 1\,\mu$m at 5 GHz. The quality factor is approximately:

$Q \approx \frac{V}{\delta S}\cdot\frac{1}{\text{geometric factor}} \approx \frac{60\text{ cm}^3}{10^{-4}\text{ cm}\times 94\text{ cm}^2}\cdot 1 \approx 6400$

where $V = abd$ is the volume and $S = 2(ab + ad + bd)$ is the surface area. This is a high-$Q$ resonator: it rings for about $Q/(2\pi) \approx 1000$ cycles before the energy drops to $1/e$ of its initial value.

**B.** The quality factor is $Q = \omega U / P_{\rm loss}$, which equals the stored energy divided by the energy lost per radian. The power loss scales as $P \propto R_s E^2 S \propto R_s E^2\lambda^2$ (surface area). So $Q = \omega U/P \propto \lambda/\delta \propto \lambda^{1/2}$ (for fixed skin depth). ## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Resonant frequencies.** For a rectangular cavity of dimensions $a\times b\times d$ (with $a > b$, $a > d$), the resonant frequencies are:

$$\omega_{mnp} = c\pi\sqrt{\left(\frac{m}{a}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{n}{b}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{p}{d}\right)^2}$$

For **TM modes** ($B_z = 0$): $m, n \geq 1$, $p \geq 0$. The lowest is TM$_{110}$ with $\omega_{110} = c\pi\sqrt{1/a^2 + 1/d^2}$.

For **TE modes** ($E_z = 0$): the indices follow different rules, and the TE$_{101}$ mode ($\omega_{101} = c\pi\sqrt{1/a^2 + 1/d^2}$) may be lower than TM$_{110}$ depending on dimensions.

**Quality factor.** The quality factor of mode $\mu$ is:

$$Q_\mu = \frac{\omega_\mu U_\mu}{P_{\mu}}$$

where $U_\mu$ is the time-averaged stored energy and $P_\mu$ is the time-averaged power dissipated in the walls:

$$U_\mu = \frac{1}{4}\int_V\left(\epsilon_0|\mathbf{E}_\mu|^2 + \frac{|\mathbf{B}_\mu|^2}{\mu_0}\right)\,d\tau$$

$$P_\mu = \frac{R_s}{2}\oint_S |\mathbf{H}_{t,\mu}|^2\,da$$

where $R_s = 1/(\sigma\delta) = \sqrt{\mu_0\omega/(2\sigma)}$ is the surface resistance and $\mathbf{H}_{t}$ is the tangential magnetic field at the wall.

**Normal-mode expansion.** Any field in the cavity can be expanded in the resonant modes:

$$\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \sum_\mu a_\mu(t)\,\mathbf{E}_\mu(\mathbf{r}), \qquad \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \sum_\mu b_\mu(t)\,\mathbf{B}_\mu(\mathbf{r})$$

The mode amplitudes satisfy driven harmonic oscillator equations:

$$\ddot{a}_\mu + \frac{\omega_\mu}{Q_\mu}\dot{a}_\mu + \omega_\mu^2 a_\mu = f_\mu(t)$$

where $f_\mu(t)$ is the driving term from the source current.

**Energy decay.** In an undriven cavity, the mode amplitude decays as $a_\mu(t) = a_\mu(0)e^{-\omega_\mu t/(2Q_\mu)}\cos(\omega_\mu t + \phi)$. The stored energy decays as $U(t) = U(0)e^{-\omega_\mu t/Q_\mu}$.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (Q factor of a rectangular cavity).**

**Theorem.** *The quality factor of the TM$_{110}$ mode in a rectangular cavity of dimensions $a\times b\times d$ with wall conductivity $\sigma$ is:*

$$Q_{110} = \frac{\omega_{110}\mu_0\sigma\delta\,V}{2S}\cdot\frac{1}{1 + (a/d)^2(2d/(a+d))\cdot(1/3)}$$

*where $V = abd$ is the volume and $S$ is the total surface area.*

*Proof.* The stored energy in the TM$_{110}$ mode is:

$$U = \frac{\epsilon_0}{2}\int E_0^2\sin^2\frac{\pi x}{a}\sin^2\frac{\pi z}{d}\,d\tau = \frac{\epsilon_0 E_0^2}{8}\,abd$$

The power dissipated in the walls comes from the tangential H-field on each of the six faces. On the face $x = 0$: $H_y = -(E_0/\omega\mu_0)(\pi/a)\sin(\pi z/d)$. The contribution from all six faces is:

$$P = \frac{R_s}{2}\oint |H_t|^2\,da = \frac{R_s E_0^2\pi^2}{4\omega^2\mu_0^2}\left(\frac{bd}{a^2} + \frac{ab}{d^2} + \frac{2bd}{a^2} + \frac{2ab}{d^2} + \frac{bd}{2a^2} + \frac{ab}{2d^2}\right)$$

After simplifying and substituting $\omega^2 = c^2\pi^2(1/a^2 + 1/d^2)$ and $R_s = 1/(\sigma\delta)$:

$$Q = \frac{\omega U}{P} = \frac{\omega\mu_0\sigma\delta\,V}{2S}\cdot\Gamma$$

where $\Gamma$ is a geometric factor of order unity depending on the aspect ratio. For a cube ($a = b = d$): $\Gamma = 1/2$ exactly. $\square$

**Bridge.** The cavity quality factor builds toward the practical design of all resonant electromagnetic devices. The foundational insight is that the Q factor is determined by the ratio of volume to surface area — stored energy lives in the volume while losses occur on the surface. The central message is that $Q$ is a geometric property of the mode and the wall material, independent of the field amplitude. This is exactly what makes cavities useful as frequency standards: a high-Q cavity has a very narrow resonance linewidth $\Delta f = f/Q$, providing precise frequency discrimination. Putting these together, the cavity modes are the standing-wave counterparts of waveguide travelling-wave modes [10.04.04], the wall losses are governed by the skin depth [10.04.03], and the stored energy relates to the Poynting vector energy flow [10.03.05].

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

A cubic cavity of side 10 cm. Compute the TM$_{110}$ resonant frequency.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\omega_{110} = c\pi\sqrt{1/a^2 + 1/d^2}$. For a cube, $a = d$, so $\omega_{110} = c\pi\sqrt{2}/a$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$f_{110} = \frac{c}{2}\sqrt{2/a^2} = \frac{c\sqrt{2}}{2a} = \frac{3\times 10^8\times 1.414}{2\times 0.1} = 2.12$ GHz.

The cube cavity resonates at 2.12 GHz. The next mode (TE$_{101}$ or TE$_{011}$) has the same frequency (degenerate), so the cube has poor mode separation. Practical cavities use non-cubic aspect ratios to separate the mode frequencies.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

A cavity has $Q = 10,000$ and resonant frequency $f = 10$ GHz. What is the resonance linewidth $\Delta f$ and the ring-down time $\tau$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\Delta f = f/Q$. The ring-down time $\tau = Q/(\pi f)$ (time for the amplitude to decay by $1/e$).
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\Delta f = f/Q = 10^{10}/10^4 = 10^6$ Hz = 1 MHz. The cavity has a bandwidth of 1 MHz centred at 10 GHz — a relative bandwidth of $10^{-4}$.

The energy ring-down time: $\tau_E = Q/\omega = Q/(2\pi f) = 10^4/(2\pi\times 10^{10}) = 1.59\times 10^{-7}$ s $\approx 160$ ns. The amplitude ring-down time is twice this: $\tau = 2Q/\omega = 320$ ns. The cavity rings for about 3200 cycles (at 10 GHz) before the amplitude drops to $1/e$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that for a cubic cavity, the TM$_{110}$, TE$_{101}$, and TE$_{011}$ modes are degenerate (all have the same resonant frequency).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For a cube, $a = b = d$. The resonant frequency depends on $\sqrt{m^2 + n^2 + p^2}$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The resonant frequencies are $\omega_{mnp} = (c\pi/a)\sqrt{m^2 + n^2 + p^2}$ (for a cube $a = b = d$).

TM$_{110}$: $\sqrt{1+1+0} = \sqrt{2}$. TE$_{101}$: $\sqrt{1+0+1} = \sqrt{2}$. TE$_{011}$: $\sqrt{0+1+1} = \sqrt{2}$.

All three have the same frequency $f = c\sqrt{2}/(2a)$. This triple degeneracy comes from the cubic symmetry. Any imperfection in the manufacturing (unequal sides) lifts the degeneracy, splitting the resonance into three nearby frequencies. This is why precision cavities are deliberately made non-cubic.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A superconducting niobium cavity at 1.3 GHz has $Q = 10^{10}$. Compute the linewidth and the number of cycles before the energy drops to $1/e$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\Delta f = f/Q$ and $\tau_E = Q/\omega$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\Delta f = 1.3\times 10^9/10^{10} = 0.13$ Hz. The linewidth is a fraction of a hertz — extraordinary frequency precision.

$\tau_E = 10^{10}/(2\pi\times 1.3\times 10^9) = 1.22$ s. The cavity rings for over a second before the energy decays by $1/e$.

The number of cycles: $N = Q/(2\pi) = 1.6\times 10^9$ cycles. This extreme $Q$ is why superconducting cavities are used in particle accelerators like the LHC and ILC: the precise resonance allows efficient energy transfer to the accelerated beam. The superconducting surface resistance ($R_s \sim 10$ n$\Omega$) is about $10^7$ times smaller than copper at the same frequency.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

The stored energy in a cavity mode satisfies the equation of motion $\ddot{a} + (\omega/Q)\dot{a} + \omega^2 a = f(t)$. For a sinusoidal drive $f(t) = f_0 e^{-i\omega' t}$, find the steady-state amplitude and show that the response has a Lorentzian lineshape.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Try $a(t) = A e^{-i\omega' t}$ and solve for $A$. The response is $|A(\omega')|^2 \propto 1/((\omega'^2 - \omega^2)^2 + \omega^2\omega'^2/Q^2)$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Substituting $a = Ae^{-i\omega' t}$: $(-\omega'^2 - i\omega\omega'/Q + \omega^2)A = f_0$, so:

$$A(\omega') = \frac{f_0}{\omega^2 - \omega'^2 - i\omega\omega'/Q}$$

The power absorption (proportional to $|A|^2$) is:

$$|A|^2 = \frac{f_0^2}{(\omega^2 - \omega'^2)^2 + \omega^2\omega'^2/Q^2}$$

Near resonance ($\omega' \approx \omega$): $\omega^2 - \omega'^2 \approx 2\omega(\omega - \omega')$ and:

$$|A|^2 \approx \frac{f_0^2}{4\omega^2(\omega - \omega')^2 + \omega^4/Q^2} = \frac{f_0^2 Q^2}{\omega^4}\cdot\frac{1}{1 + 4Q^2(\omega - \omega')^2/\omega^2}$$

This is a **Lorentzian** with full width at half maximum (FWHM) $\Delta\omega = \omega/Q$. The lineshape is the same as for a damped driven harmonic oscillator.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

**Cavity perturbation theory.** A small dielectric bead of volume $\delta V$ and permittivity $\epsilon$ is introduced into a cavity at position $\mathbf{r}_0$. Show that the frequency shift is $\delta\omega/\omega = -(\epsilon - \epsilon_0)|\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}_0)|^2\delta V / (4U)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The additional energy stored in the bead is $\delta U = (\epsilon - \epsilon_0)|\mathbf{E}|^2\delta V/4$. The frequency shift follows from the perturbation formula $\delta\omega/\omega = -\delta U / U$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The additional energy stored in the dielectric bead (time-averaged):

$$\delta U = \frac{1}{4}(\epsilon - \epsilon_0)|\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}_0)|^2\,\delta V$$

By Slater's perturbation theorem, the frequency shift of a cavity mode is:

$$\frac{\delta\omega}{\omega} = -\frac{\delta U}{U} = -\frac{(\epsilon - \epsilon_0)|\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}_0)|^2\,\delta V}{4U}$$

This is the basis of **bead-pull measurements**: a small dielectric bead is moved through the cavity on a thin thread, and the frequency shift at each position maps out $|\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r})|^2$. This technique is used to measure the electric field distribution in accelerator cavities with high precision. A metallic bead ($\epsilon \to \infty$) measures $|\mathbf{B}|^2$ instead (through the diamagnetic perturbation).
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the mode fields of a cavity satisfy the orthogonality relation $\int_V \mathbf{E}_\mu\cdot\mathbf{E}_\nu\,d\tau = 0$ for $\mu \neq \nu$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Both modes satisfy the vector Helmholtz equation with PEC boundary conditions. Apply Green's identity.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Each mode satisfies $\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}_\mu) = k_\mu^2\mathbf{E}_\mu$ and $\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}_\nu) = k_\nu^2\mathbf{E}_\nu$. Taking the dot product of the first with $\mathbf{E}_\nu$ and the second with $\mathbf{E}_\mu$, subtracting, and integrating:

$$(k_\mu^2 - k_\nu^2)\int_V \mathbf{E}_\mu\cdot\mathbf{E}_\nu\,d\tau = \int_V [\mathbf{E}_\nu\cdot\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}_\mu) - \mathbf{E}_\mu\cdot\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}_\nu)]\,d\tau$$

By vector identities, the right side equals a surface integral that vanishes because $\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times\mathbf{E}_\mu = \hat{\mathbf{n}}\times\mathbf{E}_\nu = 0$ on the PEC walls. For $k_\mu \neq k_\nu$: $\int \mathbf{E}_\mu\cdot\mathbf{E}_\nu\,d\tau = 0$.

For degenerate modes ($k_\mu = k_\nu$), one can always choose linear combinations that are orthogonal. This orthogonality is the basis for the normal-mode expansion.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

A microwave cavity is coupled to an external waveguide through a small iris (aperture). Derive the **coupling parameter** $\beta_c = Q_0/Q_e$ where $Q_0$ is the unloaded Q and $Q_e$ is the external Q (due to power radiated through the iris).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The loaded Q is $1/Q_L = 1/Q_0 + 1/Q_e$. Critical coupling ($\beta_c = 1$) means all incident power is absorbed.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The cavity has two loss mechanisms: wall losses ($Q_0$) and radiation through the iris ($Q_e$). The total (loaded) Q is:

$$\frac{1}{Q_L} = \frac{1}{Q_0} + \frac{1}{Q_e} = \frac{P_{\rm wall} + P_{\rm iris}}{\omega U}$$

The coupling parameter is $\beta_c = Q_0/Q_e = P_{\rm iris}/P_{\rm wall}$.

- **Undercoupled** ($\beta_c < 1$): wall losses dominate. Increasing the iris size increases $\beta_c$.
- **Critically coupled** ($\beta_c = 1$): $P_{\rm iris} = P_{\rm wall}$. All incident power is absorbed (reflected power is zero at resonance). This is the ideal operating point for a cavity filter.
- **Overcoupled** ($\beta_c > 1$): iris radiation dominates. The cavity is well-connected to the external circuit.

The external Q can be estimated from the polarisability of the iris: $Q_e \approx (3V)/(4\pi\alpha k^3)$ where $\alpha$ is the electric polarisability of the aperture and $k = \omega/c$. The coupling is adjusted by changing the iris size.
</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Spherical cavities.** A spherical cavity of radius $R$ supports TM and TE modes based on spherical Bessel functions. The lowest TM mode (TM$_{01}$) has $k_{01}R = 2.744$ and $Q \approx R/(2\delta)$. The lowest TE mode (TE$_{01}$) has $k_{01}R = 4.511$ and $Q \approx R/\delta$ — about twice the TM value because the TE$_{01}$ mode has no surface currents on the poles.

**Coupled cavities.** Two identical cavities coupled through a small aperture have split resonant frequencies $\omega_\pm = \omega_0(1 \pm \kappa)$ where $\kappa$ is the coupling coefficient. This is the electromagnetic analogue of coupled oscillators (two pendulums connected by a spring). The symmetric mode ($E$ in phase) has a lower frequency than the antisymmetric mode ($E$ out of phase).

**Accelerator cavities.** In a linear particle accelerator, the TM$_{010}$ mode of a cylindrical cavity provides a longitudinal electric field that accelerates charged particles passing through the centre. The accelerating gradient is limited by the peak surface field (breakdown) and the power dissipation. Superconducting niobium cavities achieve gradients of 20-40 MV/m, compared to 1-5 MV/m for copper.

**Synthesis.** Cavity resonators are the electromagnetic analogue of musical instruments: they confine electromagnetic energy in standing-wave patterns with discrete resonant frequencies. The foundational insight is that the mode structure is determined entirely by the cavity geometry and the boundary conditions. The central message is that the quality factor Q quantifies the trade-off between energy storage and dissipation, and it appears again in every resonant system from mechanical oscillators to atomic transitions. Putting these together, the cavity modes are the standing-wave counterparts of the travelling-wave modes in waveguides [10.04.04], the wall losses are governed by the skin depth [10.04.03], and the normal-mode expansion provides the mathematical framework for analysing arbitrary field distributions in enclosed spaces.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Resonant frequencies of a rectangular cavity).** The resonant frequencies of a rectangular cavity of dimensions $a\times b\times d$ with perfectly conducting walls are $\omega_{mnp} = c\pi\sqrt{(m/a)^2 + (n/b)^2 + (p/d)^2}$ for non-negative integers $m, n, p$ (not all zero), subject to the TM and TE index restrictions.

*Proof.* The fields satisfy the vector Helmholtz equation $(\nabla^2 + k^2)\mathbf{E} = 0$ with $\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times\mathbf{E} = 0$ on all six faces. Separation of variables in Cartesian coordinates gives $E_i = X(x)Y(y)Z(z)$ where each factor satisfies the one-dimensional Helmholtz equation with the appropriate boundary condition. For the TM modes ($B_z = 0$), the longitudinal component $E_z = E_0\sin(m\pi x/a)\sin(n\pi y/b)\cos(p\pi z/d)$ (with $m, n \geq 1$, $p \geq 0$). Substituting into the Helmholtz equation: $k^2 = (m\pi/a)^2 + (n\pi/b)^2 + (p\pi/d)^2$, giving $\omega_{mnp} = ck$. The transverse fields are determined by the Maxwell equations relating $E_z$ to $E_x$, $E_y$, $B_x$, $B_y$. A similar construction gives the TE modes. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Waveguides [10.04.04]** provide the travelling-wave modes that become standing waves when the guide is closed at both ends.
- **Skin depth [10.04.03]** determines the surface resistance and hence the cavity Q.
- **Poynting vector [10.03.05]** measures the energy flow that is stored in the cavity mode.
- **Dipole radiation [10.07.02]** can be analysed in a cavity context, where the mode density modifies the radiation rate.
- **Fourier analysis** is the mathematical tool underlying the normal-mode expansion.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The first microwave cavity resonator was built by Rayleigh in 1897, who analysed the acoustic modes of a rectangular room and recognised the analogy with electromagnetic modes. The practical development of cavity resonators began with the development of radar in the 1930s-40s, where cavity magnetrons (cylindrical cavities with rotating electron beams) generated high-power microwave radiation.

The klystron (Varian and Varian, 1939) uses a cavity to modulate an electron beam, and the travelling-wave tube (Kompfner, 1943) uses a slow-wave structure to amplify microwave signals. The hydrogen maser (Gordon, Zeiger, and Townes, 1954) uses a hydrogen atom inside a high-Q cavity to produce an extremely stable oscillation at 1.42 GHz, with a stability of one part in $10^{15}$ — the basis of the hydrogen maser atomic clock.

Superconducting cavities were developed in the 1970s for particle accelerators. The TESLA (TeV Energy Superconducting Linear Accelerator) collaboration demonstrated 9-cell niobium cavities achieving $Q > 10^{10}$ at 1.3 GHz, which became the basis for the European XFEL and the planned International Linear Collider.
