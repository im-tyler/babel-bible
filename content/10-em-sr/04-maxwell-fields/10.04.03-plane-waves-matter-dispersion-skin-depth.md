## Intuition [Beginner]

When an electromagnetic wave enters a material, three things can happen: it slows down, it gets absorbed, and different frequencies travel at different speeds. These three effects are described by the **refractive index** $n$, the **absorption coefficient**, and the **dispersion relation**.

In vacuum, all electromagnetic waves travel at the speed of light $c$. In a transparent material like glass, the wave slows down to $v = c/n$ where $n$ is the refractive index (about 1.5 for glass). This slowing is what bends light at the surface of a lens — the basis of all optics.

In a conductor like copper, the wave does not just slow down — it gets absorbed. The field decays exponentially with distance from the surface, over a characteristic length called the **skin depth**. For copper at 60 Hz, the skin depth is about 8.5 mm; at 1 GHz, it is only 2 micrometres. This is why high-frequency circuits need very thin conductors: the current only flows in a thin skin at the surface.

**Dispersion** means that different frequencies travel at different speeds. Red light and blue light have slightly different refractive indices in glass, so a prism separates white light into a rainbow. Dispersion is what makes a pulse of light spread out as it travels through a fiber optic cable — different frequency components arrive at different times.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The skin depth in copper ($\sigma = 5.8 \times 10^7$ S/m) at frequency $f = 1$ MHz:

$\delta = \sqrt{2/(\omega\mu_0\sigma)} = \sqrt{2/(2\pi\times 10^6\times 4\pi\times 10^{-7}\times 5.8\times 10^7)}$

$= \sqrt{2/(4.59\times 10^8)} = \sqrt{4.36\times 10^{-9}} = 6.6 \times 10^{-5}$ m $= 66\,\mu$m.

At 1 MHz, electromagnetic waves only penetrate 66 micrometres into copper. This is why AM radio signals (around 1 MHz) do not penetrate metal buildings, and why the shielding on coaxial cables only needs to be a thin layer of copper.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Plane wave in a dielectric.** In a linear, isotropic, non-conducting medium with permittivity $\epsilon$ and permeability $\mu$, the wave equation for $\mathbf{E}$ is:

$$\nabla^2\mathbf{E} = \mu\epsilon\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}$$

The plane-wave solution $\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{E}_0 e^{i(kz - \omega t)}$ has the **dispersion relation**:

$$k^2 = \mu\epsilon\omega^2 \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad k = n\frac{\omega}{c}$$

where $n = c\sqrt{\mu\epsilon} = \sqrt{\mu_r\epsilon_r}$ is the **refractive index** and $v = c/n = 1/\sqrt{\mu\epsilon}$ is the **phase velocity**.

**Complex wave number in a conductor.** In a conducting medium ($\sigma > 0$), the Maxwell equations give the modified dispersion relation:

$$\tilde{k}^2 = \mu\epsilon\omega^2 + i\mu\sigma\omega$$

The complex wave number $\tilde{k} = k + i\kappa$ where:

$$k = \omega\sqrt{\frac{\mu\epsilon}{2}}\left(\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\sigma}{\omega\epsilon}\right)^2} + 1\right)^{1/2}$$

$$\kappa = \omega\sqrt{\frac{\mu\epsilon}{2}}\left(\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\sigma}{\omega\epsilon}\right)^2} - 1\right)^{1/2}$$

For a **good conductor** ($\sigma \gg \omega\epsilon$):

$$k \approx \kappa \approx \sqrt{\frac{\mu\sigma\omega}{2}}$$

and the **skin depth** is:

$$\delta = \frac{1}{\kappa} = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\mu\sigma\omega}}$$

The field inside the conductor decays as $e^{-z/\delta}$.

**Complex refractive index.** Define $\tilde{n} = n + i\kappa$ (complex refractive index, where $\kappa$ is the **extinction coefficient**). Then $\tilde{k} = \tilde{n}\omega/c$ and:

$$\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{E}_0 e^{i(\tilde{k}z - \omega t)} = \mathbf{E}_0 e^{-\kappa\omega z/c}\,e^{i(n\omega z/c - \omega t)}$$

The real part $n$ determines the phase velocity $v_p = c/n$. The imaginary part $\kappa$ determines the attenuation length $\ell = c/(\kappa\omega)$.

**Group velocity.** When the refractive index depends on frequency ($n = n(\omega)$), a wave packet travels at the **group velocity**:

$$v_g = \frac{d\omega}{dk} = \frac{c}{n + \omega\,dn/d\omega}$$

Information and energy propagate at $v_g$, not $v_p$. In regions of **normal dispersion** ($dn/d\omega > 0$), $v_g < v_p < c$. In regions of **anomalous dispersion** ($dn/d\omega < 0$), $v_g$ can exceed $c$ but no information travels faster than light.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (Skin depth in a good conductor).**

**Theorem.** *For a good conductor ($\sigma \gg \omega\epsilon$), a plane wave of frequency $\omega$ decays exponentially with skin depth $\delta = \sqrt{2/(\mu\sigma\omega)}$. The wave number is $k \approx 1/\delta$ and the phase velocity is $v_p = \omega\delta = \sqrt{2\omega/(\mu\sigma)}$.*

*Proof.* In a conductor, Maxwell's equations with Ohm's law ($\mathbf{J} = \sigma\mathbf{E}$) give:

$$\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu\sigma\mathbf{E} + \mu\epsilon\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$$

For a plane wave $\mathbf{E} = E_0 e^{i(kz - \omega t)}\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}$, the curl equation gives $ik B_y = \mu(\sigma - i\omega\epsilon)E_x$. From Faraday's law: $ikE_x = i\omega B_y$. Combining:

$$k^2 = \mu\omega(\sigma - i\omega\epsilon) \cdot \omega = \mu\epsilon\omega^2 + i\mu\sigma\omega$$

For $\sigma \gg \omega\epsilon$: $k^2 \approx i\mu\sigma\omega$. Writing $i = e^{i\pi/2}$: $k \approx \sqrt{\mu\sigma\omega}\,e^{i\pi/4} = (1+i)\sqrt{\mu\sigma\omega/2}$. So $k = \kappa = \sqrt{\mu\sigma\omega/2}$ and the skin depth is $\delta = 1/\kappa = \sqrt{2/(\mu\sigma\omega)}$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The skin depth formula builds toward the design of all high-frequency electromagnetic devices. The foundational insight is that in a good conductor the displacement current is negligible compared to the conduction current, so the wave equation reduces to a diffusion equation. The central message is that the skin depth decreases as $1/\sqrt{\omega}$, confining high-frequency currents to thin surface layers. This is exactly the reason why radio-frequency shielding works and why microwave circuits use thin conductors. Putting these together, the skin depth determines the wall losses in waveguides [10.04.04], the complex refractive index generalises to the Kramers-Kronig relations [10.08.05], and the dispersion relation in matter modifies the propagation of all electromagnetic signals through material media.

Seawater has conductivity $\sigma = 4$ S/m and permittivity $\epsilon_r = 80$ at frequency $f = 1$ kHz. Is seawater a good or poor conductor at this frequency? Compute the skin depth.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the general expression for the phase velocity in a conductor: $v_p = \omega/k$ where $k$ is given by the full expression (not the good-conductor approximation).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$v_p = \omega/\text{Re}(\tilde{k})$. Use the exact formula for $k$ from the formal definition section.

The phase velocity is $v_p = \omega/k$ where:

$$k = \omega\sqrt{\frac{\mu\epsilon}{2}}\left(\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\sigma}{\omega\epsilon}\right)^2} + 1\right)^{1/2}$$

So:

$$v_p = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu\epsilon/2}}\left(\sqrt{1 + (\sigma/\omega\epsilon)^2} + 1\right)^{-1/2}$$

In the insulator limit ($\sigma = 0$): $v_p = 1/\sqrt{\mu\epsilon} = c/n$, the usual phase velocity. In the good conductor limit ($\sigma \gg \omega\epsilon$): $v_p \approx \omega\delta = \sqrt{2\omega/(\mu\sigma)}$, which is much less than $c$ and increases with frequency.

The phase velocity in a conductor depends on frequency — conductors are inherently dispersive.

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

The **Fresnel equations** for normal incidence give the reflection coefficient $r = (n_1 - n_2)/(n_1 + n_2)$ for the electric field. Compute the fraction of power reflected when light goes from air ($n_1 = 1$) into glass ($n_2 = 1.5$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The power reflection coefficient (reflectance) is $R = |r|^2$.
</details>

$r = (1 - 1.5)/(1 + 1.5) = -0.5/2.5 = -0.2$. The reflectance is $R = |r|^2 = 0.04 = 4\%$.

Four percent of the light is reflected at the air-glass interface. For a lens with two surfaces, about 8% is reflected total (ignoring multiple reflections). Anti-reflection coatings work by creating destructive interference in the reflected wave, reducing $R$ to near zero at a specific wavelength.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

A dielectric has the Sellmeier equation $n^2(\lambda) = 1 + B\lambda^2/(\lambda^2 - C)$ where $B = 1.0$ and $C = 0.01\,\mu\text{m}^2$. Compute the group velocity dispersion $d v_g/d\omega$ at $\lambda = 0.5\,\mu$m and explain its significance for pulse propagation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The group velocity dispersion (GVD) is $d^2k/d\omega^2$. Compute $dn/d\lambda$ from the Sellmeier equation, then use $v_g = c/(n - \lambda\,dn/d\lambda)$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From $n^2 = 1 + B\lambda^2/(\lambda^2 - C)$: differentiating, $2n\,dn/d\lambda = -2BC\lambda/(\lambda^2 - C)^2$. At $\lambda = 0.5\,\mu$m: $\lambda^2 - C = 0.25 - 0.01 = 0.24$.

$n^2 = 1 + 0.25/0.24 = 2.042$, so $n = 1.429$.

$dn/d\lambda = -2\times 1.0\times 0.01\times 0.5/(2\times 1.429\times 0.24^2) = -0.01/(0.3287) = -0.0304\,\mu\text{m}^{-1}$.

The group velocity: $v_g = c/(n - \lambda\,dn/d\lambda) = c/(1.429 - 0.5\times(-0.0304)) = c/(1.429 + 0.0152) = c/1.444$.

GVD causes different frequency components of a pulse to travel at different speeds, broadening the pulse. In fiber optic communications, GVD limits the data rate over long distances. Dispersion-compensating fibers are designed with negative GVD to undo the broadening.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that in a good conductor the electric and magnetic fields are 45 degrees out of phase. Specifically, $\mathbf{B}$ lags $\mathbf{E}$ by $\pi/4$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In a good conductor, $k = \kappa = (1+i)/\delta$. Use Faraday's law $ikE_0 = i\omega B_0$ to relate $E_0$ and $B_0$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a good conductor: $\tilde{k} = (1+i)/\delta$. Faraday's law for a plane wave: $\tilde{k}E_0 = \omega B_0$, so:

$$B_0 = \frac{\tilde{k}}{\omega}E_0 = \frac{(1+i)}{\omega\delta}E_0 = \frac{\sqrt{2}\,e^{i\pi/4}}{\omega\delta}E_0$$

The complex ratio $B_0/E_0 = (1+i)/(\omega\delta)$ has argument $\pi/4$, meaning $B_0$ is shifted in phase by $+45^\circ$ relative to $E_0$. The B-field leads the E-field by 45 degrees.

In vacuum (or a lossless dielectric), $\tilde{k}$ is real, so $B_0/E_0 = k/\omega = 1/v_p$ is real and the fields are in phase. The phase shift is a direct consequence of the attenuation: the conductor converts field energy into heat, and the phase lag between E and B measures the rate of this conversion.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The **Drude model** gives the frequency-dependent conductivity of a metal as $\sigma(\omega) = \sigma_0/(1 - i\omega\tau)$ where $\sigma_0 = n_e e^2\tau/m$ and $\tau$ is the mean free time. Show that in the high-frequency limit ($\omega\tau \gg 1$), the metal becomes transparent with refractive index $n \approx \sqrt{1 - \omega_p^2/\omega^2}$ where $\omega_p = \sqrt{n_e e^2/(m\epsilon_0)}$ is the **plasma frequency**.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In the Drude model, the effective permittivity is $\epsilon(\omega) = \epsilon_0(1 - \omega_p^2/(\omega(\omega - i/\tau)))$. For $\omega\tau \gg 1$: $\epsilon \approx \epsilon_0(1 - \omega_p^2/\omega^2)$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Drude model gives the effective permittivity $\epsilon_{\rm eff}(\omega) = \epsilon_0 + i\sigma(\omega)/\omega = \epsilon_0 + i\sigma_0/(\omega(1 - i\omega\tau))$. Using $\sigma_0 = n_e e^2\tau/m$ and $\omega_p^2 = n_e e^2/(m\epsilon_0)$:

$$\epsilon_{\rm eff} = \epsilon_0 + \frac{in_e e^2\tau}{m\omega(1 - i\omega\tau)} = \epsilon_0\left(1 + \frac{i\omega_p^2\tau}{\omega(1 - i\omega\tau)}\right)$$

For $\omega\tau \gg 1$: $1 - i\omega\tau \approx -i\omega\tau$, so $\epsilon_{\rm eff} \approx \epsilon_0(1 - \omega_p^2/\omega^2)$. The refractive index is:

$$n = \sqrt{\epsilon_r} = \sqrt{1 - \omega_p^2/\omega^2}$$

For $\omega > \omega_p$: $n$ is real and the metal is transparent (e.g., alkali metals are transparent to UV). For $\omega < \omega_p$: $n$ is imaginary and the wave is evanescent (the metal reflects). The plasma frequency separates the reflective and transparent regimes.

For aluminium: $\omega_p \approx 2.4 \times 10^{16}$ rad/s ($\lambda_p \approx 80$ nm). Aluminium reflects visible and UV light but becomes transparent in the extreme UV.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the power dissipated per unit area in a conductor with skin depth $\delta$ carrying surface current $K = H_0$ is $P/A = H_0^2/(2\sigma\delta)$. This is the **surface resistance** formula used in waveguide loss calculations.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The current density inside the conductor is $J(z) = \sigma E_0 e^{-z/\delta}e^{iz/\delta}$. Integrate $|J|^2/(2\sigma)$ from $z = 0$ to $\infty$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The electric field just inside the conductor surface is $E(z) = E_0 e^{-(1+i)z/\delta}$. The current density is $J(z) = \sigma E(z)$. The time-averaged power dissipated per unit volume is $\langle J\cdot E\rangle = |J|^2/(2\sigma) = \sigma|E_0|^2 e^{-2z/\delta}/2$.

Integrating over depth:

$$\frac{P}{A} = \int_0^\infty \frac{\sigma|E_0|^2}{2}e^{-2z/\delta}\,dz = \frac{\sigma|E_0|^2\delta}{4}$$

The surface current is $K = \int_0^\infty J\,dz = \sigma E_0\delta/(1+i)$. The magnitude is $|K| = \sigma|E_0|\delta/\sqrt{2}$. The tangential H-field at the surface equals $K$: $H_0 = |K| = \sigma|E_0|\delta/\sqrt{2}$.

Substituting $|E_0| = \sqrt{2}H_0/(\sigma\delta)$:

$$\frac{P}{A} = \frac{\sigma\delta}{4}\cdot\frac{2H_0^2}{\sigma^2\delta^2} = \frac{H_0^2}{2\sigma\delta} = \frac{R_s H_0^2}{2}$$

where $R_s = 1/(\sigma\delta)$ is the **surface resistance**. This formula is used to compute the power lost in waveguide walls [10.04.04] and cavity surfaces [10.04.05].
</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**The Fresnel equations at oblique incidence.** For a plane wave incident at angle $\theta_i$ on a boundary between media with indices $n_1$ and $n_2$, the reflection and transmission coefficients depend on the polarisation:

$$r_s = \frac{n_1\cos\theta_i - n_2\cos\theta_t}{n_1\cos\theta_i + n_2\cos\theta_t}, \qquad r_p = \frac{n_2\cos\theta_i - n_1\cos\theta_t}{n_2\cos\theta_i + n_1\cos\theta_t}$$

where $s$ and $p$ denote the two linear polarisations (TE and TM respectively). At the **Brewster angle** $\theta_B = \arctan(n_2/n_1)$, $r_p = 0$ and the p-polarised wave is fully transmitted. At the **critical angle** $\theta_c = \arcsin(n_2/n_1)$ (for $n_1 > n_2$), total internal reflection occurs.

**Anomalous dispersion and resonant absorption.** Near an atomic resonance at frequency $\omega_0$, the permittivity has a resonant structure:

$$\epsilon(\omega) = \epsilon_0\left(1 + \frac{\omega_p^2}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2 - i\gamma\omega}\right)$$

where $\gamma$ is the damping rate. Below resonance, $n$ increases with $\omega$ (normal dispersion). Above resonance, $n$ decreases with $\omega$ (anomalous dispersion). At resonance, the absorption is maximum (the imaginary part of $n$ peaks). The Kramers-Kronig relations [10.08.05] connect the real and imaginary parts of $\epsilon(\omega)$ through a causal convolution integral.

**Metamaterials and negative refraction.** Materials with both $\epsilon < 0$ and $\mu < 0$ simultaneously have a negative refractive index $n = -\sqrt{|\epsilon\mu|}/(\epsilon_0\mu_0)$. In such materials, the phase velocity is opposite to the group velocity, and Snell's law gives a negative refraction angle. Veselago (1968) predicted this, and Pendry (2000) showed that a flat slab of $n = -1$ material acts as a perfect lens. The first experimental demonstration was by Shelby, Smith, and Schultz (2001) using a metamaterial structure.

**Synthesis.** The theory of plane waves in matter unifies the propagation, attenuation, and dispersion of electromagnetic waves in all material media. The foundational insight is that the complex wave number $\tilde{k}$ encodes all three effects: its real part gives the wavelength and phase velocity, its imaginary part gives the attenuation (skin depth), and its frequency dependence gives the dispersion. The central message is that the material response ($\epsilon$, $\mu$, $\sigma$) modifies the vacuum wave equation in a way captured entirely by the complex refractive index. Putting these together, the skin depth determines the losses in waveguides [10.04.04] and cavities [10.04.05], the dispersion relation constrains signal propagation in optical fibers, and the Kramers-Kronig relations [10.08.05] provide the fundamental link between dispersion and absorption through causality.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Wave equation in a conducting medium).** In a linear, isotropic medium with conductivity $\sigma$, the electric field satisfies the damped wave equation:

$$\nabla^2\mathbf{E} = \mu\epsilon\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2} + \mu\sigma\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$$

*Proof.* Start from the Maxwell-Ampere law in a conductor: $\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu\sigma\mathbf{E} + \mu\epsilon\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t$. Take the curl of Faraday's law $\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t$:

$$\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}) = -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla\times\mathbf{B}) = -\mu\sigma\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t} - \mu\epsilon\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}$$

Using the vector identity $\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}) = \nabla(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}) - \nabla^2\mathbf{E}$ and Gauss's law ($\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = 0$ for a neutral conductor with no free charge):

$$\nabla^2\mathbf{E} = \mu\epsilon\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2} + \mu\sigma\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \qquad \square$$

The $\sigma$ term is the damping term that converts field energy into Joule heat, producing the exponential attenuation characterised by the skin depth.

## Connections [Master]

- **EM waves in vacuum [10.04.02]** are the special case $\sigma = 0$, $\epsilon = \epsilon_0$, $\mu = \mu_0$.
- **Maxwell equations in matter [10.03.04]** provide the constitutive relations ($\mathbf{D} = \epsilon\mathbf{E}$, $\mathbf{J} = \sigma\mathbf{E}$) used to derive the wave equation.
- **Waveguides [10.04.04]** use the skin depth to calculate wall losses and the refractive index contrast for confinement.
- **Cavities [10.04.05]** have quality factors limited by the skin-depth losses on the cavity walls.
- **Kramers-Kronig relations [10.08.05]** connect the real and imaginary parts of the refractive index through a causal integral transform.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The concept of dispersion was studied experimentally by Cauchy (1836), who derived the empirical formula $n(\lambda) = A + B/\lambda^2 + C/\lambda^4$ describing the wavelength dependence of the refractive index in transparent materials. The Sellmeier equation (1871) provided a more accurate form based on a resonance model, correctly predicting anomalous dispersion near absorption lines.

The skin effect was first observed by Hughes (1885) and explained theoretically by Rayleigh (1886), who derived the exponential decay of AC currents in conductors. The practical importance grew with the development of radio technology in the early 20th century, where skin depth determines the efficiency of antennas and transmission lines.

The Drude model (1900) was one of the first applications of the newly discovered electron to explain optical properties of metals. It correctly predicted that metals become transparent above the plasma frequency, a result confirmed experimentally in the 1930s for alkali metals.

The discovery of negative refraction (Shelby et al., 2001) opened the field of metamaterials and transformation optics, demonstrating that engineered materials can achieve electromagnetic properties not found in nature.
