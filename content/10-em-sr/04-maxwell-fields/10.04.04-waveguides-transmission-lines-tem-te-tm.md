## Intuition [Beginner]

A **waveguide** is a hollow metal tube that guides electromagnetic waves from one end to the other. Unlike an antenna that radiates in all directions, a waveguide confines the wave to travel along the tube. The most common shape is a rectangular waveguide — a metal pipe with a rectangular cross-section.

Not all frequencies can travel through a waveguide. Each waveguide has a **cutoff frequency** $f_c$ below which the wave is reflected back and cannot propagate. The cutoff depends on the width of the guide: wider guides support lower frequencies. For a rectangular waveguide of width $a$, the cutoff for the lowest mode is $f_c = c/(2a)$.

There are three types of waveguide modes, classified by which field components are transverse (perpendicular to the direction of propagation):

- **TEM** (Transverse ElectroMagnetic): both E and B are transverse. Found in coaxial cables and two-wire lines, not in hollow pipes.
- **TE** (Transverse Electric): E is entirely transverse, B has a longitudinal component. The dominant mode in rectangular waveguides.
- **TM** (Transverse Magnetic): B is entirely transverse, E has a longitudinal component.

A coaxial cable carrying TV signals uses the TEM mode — the simplest and most broadband. A microwave oven feed uses the TE mode of a rectangular waveguide. Waveguides are essential for radar, satellite communications, and particle accelerators.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A rectangular waveguide has width $a = 2.3$ cm and height $b = 1.0$ cm. The cutoff frequency for the dominant TE$_{10}$ mode is:

$f_c = c/(2a) = 3\times 10^8/(2\times 0.023) = 6.52$ GHz.

Frequencies below 6.52 GHz cannot propagate. The waveguide is used for X-band radar (8-12 GHz), which is above the cutoff.

At $f = 10$ GHz, the guide wavelength is:

$\lambda_g = \lambda/\sqrt{1 - (f_c/f)^2} = (3\times 10^{-2})/\sqrt{1 - 0.652^2} = 0.03/0.758 = 3.96$ cm.

The guide wavelength (4.0 cm) is longer than the free-space wavelength (3.0 cm) because the wave bounces off the walls, zigzagging down the guide.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Waveguide geometry.** A waveguide is a cylindrical metal tube of arbitrary cross-section extending in the $z$-direction. The walls are perfect electric conductors (PEC). For a rectangular waveguide: cross-section $a \times b$ with $a > b$.

**Mode decomposition.** The fields in a waveguide are decomposed into modes with definite $z$-dependence $e^{i(\beta z - \omega t)}$ where $\beta$ is the **propagation constant**. The transverse fields satisfy the two-dimensional Helmholtz equation:

$$\left(\nabla_t^2 + k_c^2\right)\psi = 0$$

where $k_c$ is the **cutoff wave number** and $\psi$ is either $E_z$ (TM modes) or $B_z$ (TE modes). The boundary conditions are:

- TM: $\psi = 0$ on the wall (Dirichlet)
- TE: $\partial\psi/\partial n = 0$ on the wall (Neumann)

**Dispersion relation.** The propagation constant is:

$$\beta = \sqrt{k^2 - k_c^2} = k\sqrt{1 - (f_c/f)^2}$$

where $k = \omega/c$ and $f_c = ck_c/(2\pi)$ is the cutoff frequency. For $f > f_c$: $\beta$ is real and the mode propagates. For $f < f_c$: $\beta$ is imaginary and the mode is **evanescent** (exponentially decaying).

**Phase and group velocities.**

$$v_p = \frac{\omega}{\beta} = \frac{c}{\sqrt{1 - (f_c/f)^2}} > c$$

$$v_g = \frac{d\omega}{d\beta} = c\sqrt{1 - (f_c/f)^2} < c$$

The product $v_p v_g = c^2$. The phase velocity exceeds $c$ (the wavefronts move faster than light), but the group velocity (which carries information and energy) is always less than $c$.

**Rectangular waveguide modes.** For a rectangular guide of dimensions $a \times b$:

$$k_{c,mn} = \pi\sqrt{(m/a)^2 + (n/b)^2}$$

The TE$_{10}$ mode ($m = 1$, $n = 0$) has the lowest cutoff: $f_c = c/(2a)$.

**TEM mode.** The TEM mode requires $k_c = 0$ (zero cutoff frequency) and exists only in structures with two conductors. The fields satisfy Laplace's equation in the transverse plane. The voltage and current on the transmission line satisfy the **telegrapher's equations**:

$$\frac{\partial V}{\partial z} = -L'\frac{\partial I}{\partial t}, \qquad \frac{\partial I}{\partial z} = -C'\frac{\partial V}{\partial t}$$

where $L'$ and $C'$ are the inductance and capacitance per unit length. The characteristic impedance is $Z_0 = \sqrt{L'/C'}$ and the wave speed is $v = 1/\sqrt{L'C'}$.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (TE$_{10}$ mode of a rectangular waveguide).**

**Theorem.** *The dominant TE$_{10}$ mode of a rectangular waveguide of dimensions $a \times b$ has cutoff frequency $f_c = c/(2a)$, field components $E_y = E_0\sin(\pi x/a)\cos(\omega t - \beta z)$, and guide wavelength $\lambda_g = \lambda_0/\sqrt{1-(f_c/f)^2}$.*

*Proof.* For TE modes, $E_z = 0$ and $B_z = B_0\cos(m\pi x/a)\cos(n\pi y/b)\,e^{i(\beta z - \omega t)}$. The TE$_{10}$ mode has $m = 1$, $n = 0$: $B_z = B_0\cos(\pi x/a)$.

The transverse fields are obtained from $B_z$ using the standard TE relations:

$$E_x = 0, \quad E_y = \frac{i\omega}{k_c^2}\frac{\partial B_z}{\partial x} = -\frac{i\omega\pi}{k_c^2 a}B_0\sin\frac{\pi x}{a}$$

The cutoff wave number is $k_c = \pi/a$ and $\beta = \sqrt{k^2 - \pi^2/a^2}$. The cutoff frequency is $\omega_c = c\pi/a$, i.e., $f_c = c/(2a)$.

The guide wavelength is $\lambda_g = 2\pi/\beta = 2\pi/(k\sqrt{1-f_c^2/f^2}) = \lambda_0/\sqrt{1-(f_c/f)^2}$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The waveguide mode theory builds toward a complete description of guided electromagnetic wave propagation. The foundational insight is that the boundary conditions on the conducting walls quantise the allowed modes, producing discrete cutoff frequencies. The central message is that each mode behaves like a separate transmission channel with its own dispersion relation, phase velocity, and group velocity. This is exactly the same mathematical structure as quantum mechanical wavefunctions in a box. Putting these together, the waveguide modes generalise to cavity resonators [10.04.05] (standing waves in a closed box), the attenuation is determined by the skin depth [10.04.03] on the guide walls, and the TEM mode provides the basis for transmission line theory used in all RF engineering.

A rectangular waveguide has $a = 4$ cm. Find the cutoff frequency of the TE$_{10}$ mode.

$f_c = c/(2a)$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the phase velocity and group velocity of a waveguide mode satisfy $v_p v_g = c^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$v_p = \omega/\beta$ and $v_g = d\omega/d\beta$. Use the dispersion relation $\beta^2 = k^2 - k_c^2 = \omega^2/c^2 - k_c^2$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From $\beta^2 = \omega^2/c^2 - k_c^2$: $2\beta\,d\beta = 2\omega\,d\omega/c^2$, so $d\omega/d\beta = \beta c^2/\omega$. Therefore $v_g = \beta c^2/\omega = c^2/v_p$, giving $v_p v_g = c^2$.

Since $v_p > c$ always (the phase fronts move faster than $c$ in a waveguide), we have $v_g < c$ always. No information or energy travels faster than light, consistent with special relativity. The "superluminal" phase velocity is a geometric effect of the zigzag path the wave takes as it bounces between the walls.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the attenuation constant $\alpha$ of a rectangular waveguide due to wall losses for the TE$_{10}$ mode, given surface resistance $R_s$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The power dissipated per unit length is $dP/dz = -2\alpha P$ where $P$ is the power flow. The dissipation comes from the surface currents on the four walls.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The attenuation constant is $\alpha = P_{\rm loss}/(2P)$ where $P_{\rm loss}$ is the power dissipated per unit length and $P$ is the power flow. For TE$_{10}$:

$$\alpha = \frac{R_s}{a^3 b\beta k\eta}\left(2b\pi^2 + a^3 k^2\right)$$

where $\eta = \sqrt{\mu_0/\epsilon_0} \approx 377\,\Omega$. The dominant contribution comes from the broad walls (top and bottom). Near cutoff ($f \to f_c^+$), $\beta \to 0$ and $\alpha \to \infty$ — the losses diverge at cutoff because the group velocity vanishes and the wave spends infinite time in the guide.

For copper walls at 10 GHz ($R_s \approx 0.026\,\Omega$) in a WR-90 guide ($a = 2.286$ cm, $b = 1.016$ cm): $\alpha \approx 0.011$ Np/m $\approx 0.096$ dB/m. A 10-metre run loses about 1 dB.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A coaxial cable has inner radius $a$ and outer radius $b$. The dielectric between has $\epsilon_r = 2.3$. Show that the characteristic impedance is $Z_0 = (60/\sqrt{\epsilon_r})\ln(b/a)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$L' = (\mu_0/2\pi)\ln(b/a)$ and $C' = (2\pi\epsilon)/\ln(b/a)$. Then $Z_0 = \sqrt{L'/C'}$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The inductance per unit length of a coaxial cable: $L' = (\mu_0/2\pi)\ln(b/a)$.

The capacitance per unit length: $C' = 2\pi\epsilon/\ln(b/a)$ where $\epsilon = \epsilon_r\epsilon_0$.

The characteristic impedance is $Z_0 = \sqrt{L'/C'} = \sqrt{\mu_0\ln(b/a)/(2\pi) \cdot \ln(b/a)/(2\pi\epsilon)} = \frac{\ln(b/a)}{2\pi}\sqrt{\mu_0/\epsilon}$.

Using $\sqrt{\mu_0/\epsilon_0} = 377\,\Omega$ and $\sqrt{\mu_0/\epsilon} = 377/\sqrt{\epsilon_r}$:

$Z_0 = \frac{377}{2\pi\sqrt{\epsilon_r}}\ln(b/a) = \frac{60}{\sqrt{\epsilon_r}}\ln(b/a)$

For a 50-ohm cable with $\epsilon_r = 2.3$: $\ln(b/a) = 50\sqrt{2.3}/60 = 1.267$, so $b/a \approx 3.55$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that a TEM mode cannot exist in a hollow conducting waveguide (single conductor).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A TEM mode requires $k_c = 0$, so the transverse fields satisfy Laplace's equation. The boundary condition on the single conductor means the potential is constant on the boundary.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a TEM mode, $E_z = 0$ and $B_z = 0$. The transverse E-field satisfies $\nabla_t^2\mathbf{E}_t = 0$ (Laplace's equation with $k_c = 0$). The potential $\Phi$ satisfies $\nabla_t^2\Phi = 0$ with $\Phi = $ constant on the conducting boundary.

By the maximum principle for harmonic functions, $\Phi$ is constant everywhere in the cross-section (since it is constant on the boundary and harmonic inside). Therefore $\mathbf{E}_t = -\nabla_t\Phi = 0$ everywhere, and there is no field.

A TEM mode requires an inner conductor (a second boundary) to sustain a nonzero potential difference. Coaxial cables have two conductors at different potentials; hollow pipes have only one.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the complete set of TE$_{mn}$ and TM$_{mn}$ cutoff frequencies for a rectangular waveguide of dimensions $a \times b$ with $a > b$. Show that the TE$_{10}$ mode always has the lowest cutoff.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$f_{c,mn} = (c/2)\sqrt{(m/a)^2 + (n/b)^2}$. For TM modes, $m \geq 1$ and $n \geq 1$. For TE modes, $m$ and $n$ cannot both be zero.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The cutoff frequencies are $f_{c,mn} = (c/2)\sqrt{(m/a)^2 + (n/b)^2}$ for integers $m, n$.

**TM modes:** Both $m \geq 1$ and $n \geq 1$ (because $E_z = 0$ if either index vanishes). The lowest TM mode is TM$_{11}$: $f_{c,11} = (c/2)\sqrt{1/a^2 + 1/b^2}$.

**TE modes:** At least one of $m, n$ must be nonzero. TE$_{10}$: $f_{c,10} = c/(2a)$. TE$_{01}$: $f_{c,01} = c/(2b)$. Since $a > b$: $f_{c,10} < f_{c,01} < f_{c,11}$.

TE$_{10}$ has the lowest cutoff: $f_{c,10} = c/(2a)$. The bandwidth of single-mode operation (TE$_{10}$ only) extends from $c/(2a)$ to $c/(2b)$. For $a = 2b$: the single-mode bandwidth is one octave ($f_{c,01}/f_{c,10} = a/b = 2$). Most practical waveguides are designed with $a \approx 2b$ for maximum single-mode bandwidth.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The **evanescent mode** of a waveguide below cutoff has $\beta = i\alpha$ where $\alpha = k_c\sqrt{1 - f^2/f_c^2}$. Show that a section of below-cutoff waveguide of length $L$ acts as a high-pass filter with attenuation $e^{-\alpha L}$ and that no power is dissipated in the walls (for a lossless guide).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Below cutoff, the fields decay exponentially but no energy flows. The Poynting vector averaged over a cross-section is zero.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Below cutoff ($f < f_c$): $\beta = i\alpha$ with $\alpha = (2\pi/c)\sqrt{f_c^2 - f^2}$. The fields decay as $e^{-\alpha z}$. After length $L$: the field is attenuated by $e^{-\alpha L}$.

The Poynting vector has components proportional to $e^{-2\alpha z}$. But the transverse and longitudinal components of $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{H}$ are 90 degrees out of phase for an evanescent mode, so $\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle = (1/2)\text{Re}(\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{H}^*) = 0$. No net power flows.

The wave does not transport energy — it is a **reactive** (stored) field, not a propagating one. The guide acts as an attenuator that absorbs no power: the attenuation is purely reactive. This property is used in **evanescent-mode filters**, where a below-cutoff section provides frequency-selective attenuation without dissipating power.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

For a coaxial cable with inner radius $a$, outer radius $b$, and dielectric $\epsilon$, derive the power handling capacity $P_{\max}$ in terms of the dielectric breakdown field $E_{\rm bd}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The maximum E-field in a coaxial cable occurs at the inner conductor surface: $E_{\max} = V/(a\ln(b/a))$. The power flow is $P = V^2/(2Z_0)$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The E-field in a coaxial TEM mode: $E_r = V/(r\ln(b/a))$. The maximum occurs at $r = a$: $E_{\max} = V/(a\ln(b/a))$. Setting $E_{\max} = E_{\rm bd}$: $V_{\max} = E_{\rm bd} a\ln(b/a)$.

The power flow: $P = V^2/(2Z_0) = V^2\sqrt{\epsilon_r}/(120\ln(b/a))$. At the maximum voltage:

$$P_{\max} = \frac{E_{\rm bd}^2 a^2\ln(b/a)\sqrt{\epsilon_r}}{120}$$

For an air-filled cable ($\epsilon_r = 1$, $E_{\rm bd} = 3$ MV/m) with $a = 1$ mm, $b = 3.55$ mm: $P_{\max} = (9\times 10^{12}\times 10^{-6}\times 1.267)/120 = 9.5\times 10^4$ W $\approx 95$ kW. The inner conductor limits the power handling because the field is strongest there.
</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Circular waveguides.** A circular waveguide of radius $R$ supports modes based on Bessel functions. The TE$_{11}$ mode (cutoff $f_c = 1.841c/(2\pi R)$) is the dominant mode, followed by TM$_{01}$ ($f_c = 2.405c/(2\pi R)$). The TE$_{01}$ mode has the special property that its attenuation *decreases* with increasing frequency — making it attractive for long-distance millimetre-wave transmission.

**Mode coupling.** Imperfections in a waveguide (bends, twists, discontinuities) couple energy between modes. The coupled-mode theory describes the power transfer:

$$\frac{dA_m}{dz} = -i\sum_n \kappa_{mn} A_n\,e^{i(\beta_m - \beta_n)z}$$

where $A_m$ is the amplitude of mode $m$ and $\kappa_{mn}$ is the coupling coefficient. Phase-matched coupling ($\beta_m \approx \beta_n$) transfers power efficiently between modes.

**Optical fibers.** An optical fiber is a dielectric waveguide: a core of index $n_1$ surrounded by cladding of index $n_2 < n_1$. The modes are hybrid (HE and EH modes, with both $E_z$ and $B_z$ nonzero). Single-mode fibers have a core diameter small enough that only the HE$_{11}$ mode propagates, eliminating intermodal dispersion and enabling long-distance communication at terabit data rates.

**Synthesis.** Waveguide theory provides the mathematical framework for understanding all guided electromagnetic wave propagation, from microwave radar to optical fiber communications. The foundational insight is that the conducting (or dielectric) boundary quantises the allowed field patterns into discrete modes, each with its own cutoff frequency and dispersion relation. The central message is that the mode decomposition reduces a complex three-dimensional wave problem to a set of independent one-dimensional propagation problems, one per mode. Putting these together, the waveguide mode theory generalises to cavity resonators [10.04.05], the skin depth [10.04.03] determines the wall losses, and the TEM mode provides the foundation for transmission line theory in all RF and microwave engineering.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Completeness of waveguide modes).** The TE$_{mn}$ and TM$_{mn}$ modes of a rectangular waveguide form a complete set: any field distribution in the waveguide can be expanded as a superposition of modes.

*Proof.* The transverse field components of each mode satisfy the Helmholtz equation $\nabla_t^2\psi_{mn} + k_{c,mn}^2\psi_{mn} = 0$ with either Dirichlet (TM) or Neumann (TE) boundary conditions on the rectangle $[0,a]\times[0,b]$. The eigenfunctions $\psi_{mn}(x,y) = \cos(m\pi x/a)\cos(n\pi y/b)$ (for TE) or $\sin(m\pi x/a)\sin(n\pi y/b)$ (for TM) form orthogonal sets:

$$\int_0^a\int_0^b \psi_{mn}\psi_{m'n'}\,dx\,dy = \frac{ab}{4}\delta_{mm'}\delta_{nn'}$$

By the spectral theorem for the self-adjoint operator $-\nabla_t^2$ on the rectangle with PEC boundary conditions, these eigenfunctions form a complete orthogonal basis for $L^2([0,a]\times[0,b])$. Any square-integrable transverse field can be expanded in this basis. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **EM waves [10.04.02]** are the building blocks; waveguide modes are constrained superpositions of plane waves.
- **Plane waves in matter [10.04.03]** provide the skin depth that determines waveguide wall losses.
- **Cavities [10.04.05]** are waveguides closed at both ends, forming standing-wave resonators.
- **Maxwell equations [10.04.01]** are the governing equations from which all mode properties are derived.
- **Green's functions [10.08.01]** can be expanded in waveguide modes for boundary-value problems.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Waveguide theory was developed independently by several groups during the 1930s. George C. Southworth at Bell Labs and Wilmer L. Barrow at MIT independently demonstrated waveguide propagation in 1936. The theoretical framework was developed by Chu (1938), Schelkunoff (1943), and others during the rapid development of radar technology in World War II.

The distinction between phase velocity and group velocity in waveguides caused considerable confusion. Sommerfeld and Brillouin (1914) had already resolved the apparent paradox of superluminal phase velocities in dispersive media, showing that the front velocity (not the phase or group velocity) determines the signal speed and always satisfies $v_{\rm front} \leq c$.

Optical fiber waveguides were proposed by Kao and Hockham (1966) and first demonstrated with losses below 20 dB/km by Corning in 1970. Single-mode fiber (1983) revolutionised telecommunications by eliminating modal dispersion.
