## Intuition [Beginner]

Maxwell's equations [10.04.01] contain a feedback loop. Faraday's law says a changing magnetic field produces an electric field. The Maxwell-Ampere law says a changing electric field produces a magnetic field. In empty space — no charges, no currents — the two feed each other. A disturbance in $E$ creates a disturbance in $B$, which creates a new disturbance in $E$, and so on. This self-sustaining cycle propagates outward through space as an **electromagnetic wave**.

The wave travels at a fixed speed determined by two constants of nature: the electric constant $\epsilon_0$ and the magnetic constant $\mu_0$. That speed is $c = 1/\sqrt{\epsilon_0 \mu_0}$. Plugging in the measured values gives approximately $3 \times 10^8$ m/s — the speed of light. Maxwell's equations predict that light **is** an electromagnetic wave.

Different frequencies of oscillation correspond to different parts of the electromagnetic spectrum: radio waves (low frequency), microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, and gamma rays (high frequency). They are all the same physical phenomenon — propagating $E$ and $B$ fields — distinguished only by how rapidly they oscillate.

In an electromagnetic wave the electric field $E$ and magnetic field $B$ oscillate perpendicular to each other and both are perpendicular to the direction the wave travels. If the wave moves in the $z$-direction, $E$ might point along $x$ and $B$ along $y$ — all three at right angles.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a wave crest moving to the right along the $z$-axis. The electric field $E$ oscillates up and down along the $x$-axis — a sinusoidal ripple in the vertical plane. The magnetic field $B$ oscillates in and out along the $y$-axis — a sinusoidal ripple in the horizontal plane. The two ripples are in phase: $E$ is at its peak exactly where $B$ is at its peak, and both are zero at the same points.

The distance between successive crests is the **wavelength** $\lambda$. The number of complete oscillations per second at any fixed point is the **frequency** $f$. They are related by $\lambda f = c$ — higher frequency means shorter wavelength. A snapshot of the wave at one instant looks like two perpendicular sine curves, one in the $xz$-plane and one in the $yz$-plane, both marching along $z$.

![Electromagnetic wave: E-field oscillates along x (vertical plane), B-field oscillates along y (horizontal plane), wave propagates along z. The two fields are in phase, perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation.](/img/10.04.02-em-wave-planes-placeholder.svg)

## Worked example [Beginner]

A radio station broadcasts at frequency $f = 100$ MHz ($= 100 \times 10^6$ Hz). What is the wavelength?

Use the relation $\lambda = c / f$:

$$
\lambda \;=\; \frac{3 \times 10^8 \; \text{m/s}}{100 \times 10^6 \; \text{Hz}} \;=\; \frac{3 \times 10^8}{10^8} \;=\; 3 \; \text{m}.
$$

A 100 MHz radio wave has a wavelength of 3 metres — roughly the height of a room. This is why FM radio antennas are about 1.5 m long (half a wavelength is an efficient antenna length).

By the same formula, visible light at frequency $f \approx 5 \times 10^{14}$ Hz has wavelength $\lambda \approx 600$ nm — about a thousand times thinner than a human hair. X-rays at $f \approx 3 \times 10^{18}$ Hz have $\lambda \approx 0.1$ nm, comparable to atomic spacing in a crystal.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

In vacuum ($\rho = 0$, $\mathbf{J} = 0$), Maxwell's equations [10.04.01] reduce to

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 0, \qquad \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t},
$$

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0, \qquad \nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}.
$$

Take the curl of Faraday's law: $\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) = -\partial(\nabla \times \mathbf{B})/\partial t$. Substitute the Maxwell-Ampere law for $\nabla \times \mathbf{B}$ on the right:

$$
\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) \;=\; -\mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}.
$$

The vector identity $\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}) - \nabla^2 \mathbf{E}$, together with $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 0$, gives

$$
\nabla^2 \mathbf{E} \;=\; \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}.
$$

The same calculation starting from the curl of the Maxwell-Ampere law produces the identical equation for $\mathbf{B}$:

$$
\nabla^2 \mathbf{B} \;=\; \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{B}}{\partial t^2}.
$$

Each component of $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ satisfies the **wave equation** with propagation speed $v = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0} = c$. This is the central prediction: vacuum Maxwell equations imply that electromagnetic disturbances propagate as waves at speed $c$.

Defining the **d'Alembertian** operator $\Box := \nabla^2 - \mu_0\epsilon_0\,\partial^2/\partial t^2 = \nabla^2 - c^{-2}\,\partial^2/\partial t^2$, the vacuum equations read

$$
\boxed{\;\;\Box\,\mathbf{E} = 0, \qquad \Box\,\mathbf{B} = 0, \qquad \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 0, \qquad \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0.\;\;}
$$

The divergence-free conditions are constraints: not every solution of the scalar wave equation for each component gives a valid electromagnetic wave. The components must combine into divergence-free vector fields.

**Plane-wave solutions.** A monochromatic plane wave propagating in the direction of the unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{k}}$ with angular frequency $\omega$ and wave vector $\mathbf{k} = (\omega/c)\hat{\mathbf{k}}$ has the form

$$
\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t) \;=\; \mathbf{E}_0\, e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r} - \omega t)}, \qquad \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}, t) \;=\; \mathbf{B}_0\, e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r} - \omega t)},
$$

where $\mathbf{E}_0$ and $\mathbf{B}_0$ are constant complex amplitudes (physical fields are the real parts). Substituting into the divergence conditions forces $\hat{\mathbf{k}} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 = 0$ and $\hat{\mathbf{k}} \cdot \mathbf{B}_0 = 0$ — the fields are **transverse**. Faraday's law relates the amplitudes:

$$
\mathbf{B}_0 \;=\; \frac{1}{c}\,\hat{\mathbf{k}} \times \mathbf{E}_0, \qquad |\mathbf{B}_0| = \frac{|\mathbf{E}_0|}{c}.
$$

The $E$ and $B$ fields of a plane wave have equal magnitudes (up to the factor $1/c$), are perpendicular to each other, and are both perpendicular to the propagation direction.

### Polarization

For a plane wave propagating along $\hat{\mathbf{z}}$, the transversality condition restricts $\mathbf{E}$ to the $xy$-plane. Write $\mathbf{E}_0 = E_x\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + E_y\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ where $E_x, E_y$ are complex.

**Linear polarization.** If $E_x$ and $E_y$ have the same phase (their ratio is real), the electric field oscillates along a fixed direction in the $xy$-plane. At any fixed point the tip of $\mathbf{E}$ traces a line segment.

**Circular polarization.** If $E_x$ and $E_y$ have equal magnitude and a phase difference of $\pm\pi/2$ — for instance $E_x = E_0$, $E_y = \pm i E_0$ — the tip of $\mathbf{E}$ traces a circle. The sign determines the handedness: $E_y = +iE_0$ gives **right-hand circular** polarization (the field rotates clockwise as viewed facing the oncoming wave in the standard optics convention; note that the physics and optics conventions for handedness differ), and $E_y = -iE_0$ gives **left-hand circular**.

**General (elliptical) polarization.** For arbitrary complex $E_x, E_y$, the tip of $\mathbf{E}$ traces an ellipse. Linear and circular are the degenerate special cases.

### Energy and momentum

The **energy density** stored in the electromagnetic field is

$$
u \;=\; \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0|\mathbf{E}|^2 + \frac{1}{2\mu_0}|\mathbf{B}|^2.
$$

For a plane wave with $|\mathbf{B}| = |\mathbf{E}|/c$ and $c^2 = 1/(\mu_0\epsilon_0)$, the electric and magnetic contributions are equal: $u = \epsilon_0|\mathbf{E}|^2$.

The **Poynting vector**

$$
\mathbf{S} \;=\; \frac{1}{\mu_0}\,\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{B}
$$

gives the energy flux (power per unit area carried by the wave). For a plane wave, $\mathbf{S} = u\,c\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}$ — energy flows in the propagation direction at speed $c$.

The **intensity** (time-averaged power per unit area) of a monochromatic plane wave with electric-field amplitude $E_0$ is

$$
I \;=\; \langle |\mathbf{S}| \rangle \;=\; \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 c\, E_0^2.
$$

The factor $1/2$ comes from time-averaging $\cos^2(\omega t)$.

Electromagnetic waves also carry **momentum**. The momentum density is $\mathbf{g} = \mathbf{S}/c^2 = \epsilon_0\,\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B}$, and the radiation pressure on a perfectly absorbing surface is $P = I/c$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Wave equation from Maxwell's equations in vacuum).** *In a region of space free of charges and currents, each Cartesian component of $\mathbf{E}$ and of $\mathbf{B}$ satisfies the wave equation $\nabla^2 \psi = \mu_0\epsilon_0\,\partial^2\psi/\partial t^2$, with propagation speed $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\epsilon_0}$. Furthermore, the transversality conditions $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = 0$ and $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$ force the physical solutions to be transverse to the propagation direction.*

**Proof.** Start with Maxwell's equations in vacuum:

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 0 \qquad \text{(i)}, \qquad \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \qquad \text{(ii)},
$$

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0 \qquad \text{(iii)}, \qquad \nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \qquad \text{(iv)}.
$$

Step 1. Take the curl of (ii): $\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}) = -\partial(\nabla\times\mathbf{B})/\partial t$.

Step 2. Apply the vector identity $\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{V}) = \nabla(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{V}) - \nabla^2\mathbf{V}$ to the left-hand side and substitute (i) and (iv):

$$
\nabla(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}) - \nabla^2\mathbf{E} \;=\; -\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\Big(\mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\Big).
$$

The first term vanishes by (i), giving

$$
\nabla^2\mathbf{E} \;=\; \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}.
$$

Step 3. The identical argument starting from the curl of (iv) and using (iii) and (ii) produces $\nabla^2\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\epsilon_0\,\partial^2\mathbf{B}/\partial t^2$.

Step 4. For a monochromatic plane wave $\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{E}_0 e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}-\omega t)}$, the divergence condition $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = 0$ becomes $i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{E}_0 = 0$, so $\mathbf{E}_0 \perp \hat{\mathbf{k}}$. The dispersion relation from the wave equation is $|\mathbf{k}|^2 = \omega^2/c^2$. From Faraday's law, $\mathbf{B}_0 = (1/c)\hat{\mathbf{k}}\times\mathbf{E}_0$, which is automatically perpendicular to both $\hat{\mathbf{k}}$ and $\mathbf{E}_0$. $\square$

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The wave equation alone does **not** guarantee a valid electromagnetic wave. Any function satisfying $\nabla^2\psi = c^{-2}\partial^2\psi/\partial t^2$ for each component is a wave-equation solution, but only divergence-free combinations satisfying Faraday's law and the Maxwell-Ampere law are physical EM waves. A longitudinal wave ($\mathbf{E}$ parallel to $\hat{\mathbf{k}}$) satisfies the scalar wave equation componentwise but violates $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = 0$.
- The relation $|\mathbf{B}| = |\mathbf{E}|/c$ holds for plane waves, not for arbitrary field configurations. Near an antenna or in a standing wave the instantaneous ratio $|\mathbf{E}|/|\mathbf{B}|$ can differ from $c$.
- Polarization is defined relative to the propagation direction. For a wave reflecting off a surface, the reflected wave has a different propagation direction and therefore a different transverse plane; polarization states do not transfer unchanged across reflections without applying the Fresnel equations.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Verify that $\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r},t) = E_0 \cos(kz - \omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ satisfies the vacuum wave equation $\nabla^2\mathbf{E} = \mu_0\epsilon_0\,\partial^2\mathbf{E}/\partial t^2$ provided $\omega = ck$ and $c^2 = 1/(\mu_0\epsilon_0)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\nabla^2\mathbf{E}$ (only the $z$-derivative is nonzero since $\mathbf{E}$ points along $x$ but depends on $z$) and $\partial^2\mathbf{E}/\partial t^2$, then compare.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\partial^2\mathbf{E}/\partial z^2 = -k^2 E_0\cos(kz - \omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}$, so $\nabla^2\mathbf{E} = -k^2\mathbf{E}$. Meanwhile $\partial^2\mathbf{E}/\partial t^2 = -\omega^2\mathbf{E}$. The wave equation demands $-k^2\mathbf{E} = -\mu_0\epsilon_0\omega^2\mathbf{E}$, i.e., $k^2 = \mu_0\epsilon_0\omega^2 = \omega^2/c^2$, which gives $\omega = ck$. This is the **dispersion relation** for EM waves in vacuum.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

For the plane wave of Exercise 1, use Faraday's law $\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t$ to find $\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r},t)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\nabla\times\mathbf{E}$ has a single nonzero component. Integrate in time to get $\mathbf{B}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = \partial E_x/\partial z\,\hat{\mathbf{y}} = -kE_0\sin(kz - \omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}$. Faraday's law gives $\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t = kE_0\sin(kz-\omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}$. Integrating: $\mathbf{B} = (k/\omega)E_0\cos(kz-\omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{y}} = (E_0/c)\cos(kz-\omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}$. The magnetic field is perpendicular to both $E$ and the propagation direction, with amplitude $E_0/c$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, symbolic).**

A plane wave has electric field $\mathbf{E} = E_0\cos(kz - \omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}$. Compute the Poynting vector $\mathbf{S} = \mu_0^{-1}\,\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B}$ and the time-averaged intensity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the $\mathbf{B}$ from Exercise 2. The time average of $\cos^2$ over one period is $1/2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathbf{S} = \mu_0^{-1}(E_0\cos\phi\,\hat{\mathbf{x}})\times((E_0/c)\cos\phi\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}) = (E_0^2/(\mu_0 c))\cos^2\phi\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ where $\phi = kz - \omega t$. The instantaneous flux is $(E_0^2/(\mu_0 c))\cos^2\phi$. Time-averaging: $\langle\cos^2\phi\rangle = 1/2$, so $I = E_0^2/(2\mu_0 c) = \tfrac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 c\,E_0^2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the general monochromatic plane wave $\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{E}_0\,e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r} - \omega t)}$ in vacuum satisfies $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = 0$ if and only if $\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{E}_0 = 0$, and that Faraday's law gives $\mathbf{B}_0 = (1/\omega)\,\mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{E}_0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Under the plane-wave ansatz, $\nabla \to i\mathbf{k}$ and $\partial/\partial t \to -i\omega$. Apply these substitutions to the divergence and curl operators.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{E}_0\,e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}-\omega t)} = 0$ requires $\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{E}_0 = 0$. Faraday's law: $\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = i\mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{E}_0\,e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}-\omega t)} = -\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t = i\omega\mathbf{B}_0\,e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}-\omega t)}$. Equating: $i\mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{E}_0 = i\omega\mathbf{B}_0$, so $\mathbf{B}_0 = (1/\omega)\mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{E}_0 = (1/c)\hat{\mathbf{k}}\times\mathbf{E}_0$. This relation automatically ensures $\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{B}_0 = 0$ (since $\mathbf{k}\cdot(\mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{E}_0) = 0$ identically).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Two plane waves with the same frequency and propagation direction $\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ have electric fields $\mathbf{E}_1 = E_0\cos(kz-\omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ and $\mathbf{E}_2 = E_0\cos(kz-\omega t + \pi/2)\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}$. Describe the polarization state of the total field $\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{E}_1 + \mathbf{E}_2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write the $y$-component as a sine using the phase shift. Then track the tip of $\mathbf{E}$ at a fixed $z$ as a function of time.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

At $z = 0$: $E_x = E_0\cos(\omega t)$, $E_y = E_0\cos(\omega t - \pi/2) = E_0\sin(\omega t)$. The tip of $\mathbf{E}$ traces $E_x^2 + E_y^2 = E_0^2$ — a circle of radius $E_0$. The field rotates counterclockwise (in the $xy$-plane as viewed from the $+z$ direction). This is **left-hand circular polarization** (using the physics convention where the handedness is defined by the right-hand rule relative to the propagation direction).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Starting from Maxwell's equations in vacuum, show that the electromagnetic energy density $u = \tfrac{1}{2}\epsilon_0|\mathbf{E}|^2 + \tfrac{1}{2\mu_0}|\mathbf{B}|^2$ satisfies the continuity equation $\partial u/\partial t + \nabla\cdot\mathbf{S} = 0$ where $\mathbf{S} = \mu_0^{-1}\,\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate $u$ in time, substitute the time-derivatives of $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ from Maxwell's equations, and use a vector identity on the resulting terms.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\partial u/\partial t = \epsilon_0\,\mathbf{E}\cdot\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t + \mu_0^{-1}\,\mathbf{B}\cdot\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t$. From Maxwell: $\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t = (\mu_0\epsilon_0)^{-1}\nabla\times\mathbf{B}$ and $\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t = -\nabla\times\mathbf{E}$. Substituting:

$$
\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} \;=\; \frac{1}{\mu_0}\Big(\mathbf{E}\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{B}) - \mathbf{B}\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{E})\Big).
$$

The vector identity $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B}) = \mathbf{B}\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}) - \mathbf{E}\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{B})$ gives $\mathbf{E}\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{B}) - \mathbf{B}\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}) = -\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B})$. Therefore

$$
\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} \;=\; -\frac{1}{\mu_0}\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B}) \;=\; -\nabla\cdot\mathbf{S}.
$$

This is Poynting's theorem in vacuum: energy is locally conserved, with $\mathbf{S}$ as the energy current.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

A laser beam with wavelength $\lambda = 500$ nm and power $P = 1$ mW has a circular cross-section of diameter $d = 1$ mm. Compute the intensity $I$ and the electric-field amplitude $E_0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Intensity is power per unit area: $I = P/A$. Then use $I = \tfrac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 c\,E_0^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Area $A = \pi(d/2)^2 = \pi(0.5\times10^{-3})^2 \approx 7.85\times10^{-7}$ m$^2$. Intensity $I = P/A = 10^{-3}/(7.85\times10^{-7}) \approx 1273$ W/m$^2$. Solving $I = \tfrac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 c\,E_0^2$:

$$
E_0 = \sqrt{\frac{2I}{\epsilon_0 c}} = \sqrt{\frac{2\times1273}{8.85\times10^{-12}\times3\times10^8}} \approx 980 \; \text{V/m}.
$$

For comparison, the electric field near a charged comb is a few kV/m — a 1 mW laser is comparable, concentrated into a tiny beam.

</details>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the wave equation for $\mathbf{E}$ in a **conducting medium** with conductivity $\sigma$ (so $\mathbf{J} = \sigma\mathbf{E}$). Show that the resulting equation is a **damped** wave equation and find the **skin depth** $\delta$ — the distance over which the amplitude decays by $1/e$ — in the high-frequency limit $\omega \gg \sigma/(\epsilon_0)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In a conductor the Maxwell-Ampere law gains a $\mathbf{J}$ term: $\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J} + \mu_0\epsilon_0\,\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t = \mu_0\sigma\mathbf{E} + \mu_0\epsilon_0\,\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t$. Repeat the curl-of-curl derivation with this extra term.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $\mathbf{J} = \sigma\mathbf{E}$, taking the curl of Faraday's law and substituting as before yields

$$
\nabla^2\mathbf{E} \;=\; \mu_0\sigma\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t} + \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}.
$$

The first term is a damping/attenuation term. For a plane-wave ansatz $\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{E}_0\,e^{i(kz-\omega t)}$, substitution gives $-k^2 = -i\mu_0\sigma\omega - \mu_0\epsilon_0\omega^2$, so $k^2 = \mu_0\epsilon_0\omega^2(1 + i\sigma/(\epsilon_0\omega))$. Writing $k = k_R + ik_I$ with $k_I > 0$, the wave amplitude decays as $e^{-k_I z}$, giving skin depth $\delta = 1/k_I$. In the high-frequency limit $\sigma/(\epsilon_0\omega) \ll 1$:

$$
k \approx \frac{\omega}{c}\Big(1 + \frac{i\sigma}{2\epsilon_0\omega}\Big), \qquad k_I \approx \frac{\sigma}{2}\sqrt{\frac{\mu_0}{\epsilon_0}} \cdot \frac{1}{c} = \frac{\sigma}{2}\,\sqrt{\frac{\mu_0}{\epsilon_0}}\,\frac{1}{c}.
$$

More precisely: $\delta = 1/k_I \approx 2\epsilon_0 c/(\sigma\sqrt{\mu_0/\epsilon_0}\cdot c) = 2/(\sigma\sqrt{\mu_0/\epsilon_0}) = 2/(\sigma\,\mu_0 c)$. In the low-frequency limit (the usual skin-depth regime for metals), $k \approx (1+i)/\delta_0$ with $\delta_0 = \sqrt{2/(\mu_0\sigma\omega)}$, the standard skin-depth formula.

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that for any vacuum electromagnetic wave (not necessarily a plane wave), the time-averaged Poynting vector equals the time-averaged energy density times $c$ times the propagation direction, provided the wave is **monochromatic** — that is, show $\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle = \langle u\rangle\,c\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}$ for a monochromatic wave propagating in the $\hat{\mathbf{k}}$ direction.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the complex representation and the relation $\mathbf{B}_0 = (1/c)\hat{\mathbf{k}}\times\mathbf{E}_0$. For time averages, use $\langle \text{Re}(A e^{-i\omega t})\,\text{Re}(B e^{-i\omega t})\rangle = \tfrac{1}{2}\text{Re}(AB^*)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $\mathbf{E} = \text{Re}(\tilde{\mathbf{E}}\,e^{-i\omega t})$ and $\mathbf{B} = \text{Re}(\tilde{\mathbf{B}}\,e^{-i\omega t})$ where $\tilde{\mathbf{E}} = \mathbf{E}_0 e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}}$ and $\tilde{\mathbf{B}} = \mathbf{B}_0 e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}}$. Using the time-average identity:

$$
\langle u \rangle \;=\; \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0|\mathbf{E}_0|^2 + \frac{1}{2\mu_0}|\mathbf{B}_0|^2 = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0|\mathbf{E}_0|^2 + \frac{1}{2\mu_0}\frac{|\mathbf{E}_0|^2}{c^2} = \epsilon_0|\mathbf{E}_0|^2.
$$

$$
\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle \;=\; \frac{1}{2\mu_0}\text{Re}(\tilde{\mathbf{E}}\times\tilde{\mathbf{B}}^*) \;=\; \frac{1}{2\mu_0}\text{Re}(\mathbf{E}_0\times\mathbf{B}_0^*).
$$

Since $\mathbf{B}_0 = (1/c)\hat{\mathbf{k}}\times\mathbf{E}_0$ and $\hat{\mathbf{k}}\times\mathbf{E}_0$ is real for linear polarization (and the relation holds for general polarization), $\mathbf{E}_0\times\mathbf{B}_0^* = (1/c)\mathbf{E}_0\times(\hat{\mathbf{k}}\times\mathbf{E}_0^*) = (1/c)(|\mathbf{E}_0|^2\hat{\mathbf{k}} - (\mathbf{E}_0^*\cdot\hat{\mathbf{k}})\mathbf{E}_0)$. By transversality $\hat{\mathbf{k}}\cdot\mathbf{E}_0 = 0$, this simplifies to $(|\mathbf{E}_0|^2/c)\hat{\mathbf{k}}$. Therefore $\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle = |\mathbf{E}_0|^2\hat{\mathbf{k}}/(2\mu_0 c) = \tfrac{1}{2}\epsilon_0|\mathbf{E}_0|^2 c\,\hat{\mathbf{k}} = \langle u\rangle\,c\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}$.

</details>

**Exercise 10 (hard, symbolic).**

A standing wave is formed by the superposition of two counter-propagating plane waves: $\mathbf{E} = E_0[\cos(kz - \omega t) + \cos(kz + \omega t)]\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}$. Simplify using a trigonometric identity. Show that the resulting Poynting vector has **zero time-average**, and explain why.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $\cos A + \cos B = 2\cos((A+B)/2)\cos((A-B)/2)$. Compute $\mathbf{B}$ for each traveling wave, add them, and form $\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\cos(kz-\omega t) + \cos(kz+\omega t) = 2\cos(kz)\cos(\omega t)$. So $\mathbf{E} = 2E_0\cos(kz)\cos(\omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}$.

The forward wave has $\mathbf{B}_+ = (E_0/c)\cos(kz-\omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}$; the backward wave has $\mathbf{B}_- = -(E_0/c)\cos(kz+\omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}$. Adding: $\mathbf{B} = (E_0/c)[\cos(kz-\omega t) - \cos(kz+\omega t)]\,\hat{\mathbf{y}} = (2E_0/c)\sin(kz)\sin(\omega t)\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}$.

$$

Time-averaging $\sin(2\omega t)$ over a period gives zero. This contrasts with a traveling wave where $\langle\mathbf{S}\rangle \neq 0$.

## Complex representation and wave packets [Master]

The complex plane-wave ansatz $\mathbf{E} = \mathbf{E}_0 e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r} - \omega t)}$ with the physical field as the real part is a bookkeeping device for linear operations. Products (energy density, Poynting vector) require care: for two complex fields $A$ and $B$ representing real quantities, $\text{Re}(A)\,\text{Re}(B) \neq \text{Re}(AB)$ in general. The correct product rule for time averages of monochromatic fields is $\langle \text{Re}(A e^{-i\omega t})\,\text{Re}(B e^{-i\omega t})\rangle = \tfrac{1}{2}\text{Re}(AB^*)$.

**Wave packets.** A monochromatic plane wave extends over all space and carries infinite energy. Physical waves are **wave packets**: superpositions of plane waves centered on a central wave vector $\mathbf{k}_0$ with a spread $\Delta k$. Write

$$
\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t) \;=\; \int \tilde{\mathbf{E}}(\mathbf{k})\,e^{i(\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r} - \omega(\mathbf{k}) t)}\,d^3k,
$$

where $\omega(\mathbf{k})$ is the dispersion relation. In vacuum $\omega = c|\mathbf{k}|$, which is **linear**: the phase velocity $v_p = \omega/k = c$ equals the **group velocity** $v_g = \partial\omega/\partial k = c$. A vacuum wave packet propagates without distortion — all frequency components travel at the same speed.

In a medium the dispersion relation becomes nonlinear ($\omega = \omega(\mathbf{k})$ depends on the material's electromagnetic response), and $v_p \neq v_g$ in general. The packet envelope moves at the group velocity $v_g = \nabla_{\mathbf{k}}\omega$, while the individual crests move at the phase velocity $v_p = \omega/k$. Group velocity is the signal velocity (energy and information transport) in normal-dispersion regions; near resonances the picture is subtler and the front velocity, not the group velocity, is the causally relevant quantity.

## Dispersion in media [Master]

In a linear, isotropic medium characterized by frequency-dependent permittivity $\epsilon(\omega)$ and permeability $\mu(\omega)$, the wave equation becomes

$$
\nabla^2 \mathbf{E} \;=\; \mu(\omega)\epsilon(\omega)\,\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2},
$$

with refractive index $n(\omega) = c\sqrt{\mu(\omega)\epsilon(\omega)}$ and dispersion relation $k = n(\omega)\omega/c$. The frequency dependence of $n$ is **dispersion**: different frequencies travel at different speeds, causing pulse broadening.

The ** Sellmeier equation** and **Cauchy formula** are empirical models for $n(\omega)$ in transparent regions away from absorption resonances. Near resonances, $n(\omega)$ develops an imaginary part $\kappa(\omega)$ — the **extinction coefficient** — and the refractive index becomes complex: $\hat{n} = n + i\kappa$. The wave attenuates as $e^{-\kappa\omega z/c}$.

**Normal dispersion** ($dn/d\omega > 0$, the typical situation away from resonances) gives $v_g < v_p < c$. **Anomalous dispersion** ($dn/d\omega < 0$, near absorption lines) can give $v_g > c$ or even $v_g < 0$, but this does not violate causality: the front velocity and the information velocity never exceed $c$.

## Fresnel equations [Master]

At a planar interface between two media with refractive indices $n_1$ and $n_2$, Maxwell's equations plus boundary conditions (continuity of the tangential components of $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{H}$, continuity of the normal components of $\mathbf{D}$ and $\mathbf{B}$) determine the reflected and transmitted amplitudes.

For a wave incident at angle $\theta_i$ from medium 1, Snell's law gives the refraction angle: $n_1\sin\theta_i = n_2\sin\theta_t$. The **Fresnel equations** for the amplitude reflection and transmission coefficients depend on the polarization of the incident wave relative to the plane of incidence:

**s-polarization** ($\mathbf{E}$ perpendicular to the plane of incidence):

$$
r_s = \frac{n_1\cos\theta_i - n_2\cos\theta_t}{n_1\cos\theta_i + n_2\cos\theta_t}, \qquad t_s = \frac{2n_1\cos\theta_i}{n_1\cos\theta_i + n_2\cos\theta_t}.
$$

**p-polarization** ($\mathbf{E}$ in the plane of incidence):

$$
r_p = \frac{n_2\cos\theta_i - n_1\cos\theta_t}{n_2\cos\theta_i + n_1\cos\theta_t}, \qquad t_p = \frac{2n_1\cos\theta_i}{n_2\cos\theta_i + n_1\cos\theta_t}.
$$

At the **Brewster angle** $\theta_B = \arctan(n_2/n_1)$, $r_p = 0$: p-polarized light is fully transmitted with no reflection. This is why reflected light from a dielectric surface is predominantly s-polarized — the principle behind polarizing sunglasses.

At **normal incidence** ($\theta_i = 0$), both reduce to $r = (n_1 - n_2)/(n_1 + n_2)$ and $t = 2n_1/(n_1 + n_2)$. The **reflectance** (power fraction reflected) is $R = |r|^2 = (n_1 - n_2)^2/(n_1 + n_2)^2$. For glass ($n \approx 1.5$) in air, $R \approx 4\%$ per surface.

## Waveguides [Master]

A **waveguide** is a hollow conducting tube (typically rectangular or circular cross-section) that confines and guides electromagnetic waves. The conducting walls impose boundary conditions ($\mathbf{E}_{\parallel} = 0$ and $\mathbf{B}_{\perp} = 0$ at the walls) that quantize the allowed transverse wave vectors.

For a rectangular waveguide with dimensions $a > b$ (width, height), the modes separate into **TE modes** (transverse electric: $E_z = 0$) and **TM modes** (transverse magnetic: $B_z = 0$). Each mode has a **cutoff frequency**

$$
\omega_{mn} = c\sqrt{(m\pi/a)^2 + (n\pi/b)^2},
$$

below which the mode does not propagate (the wave vector becomes imaginary, yielding exponential attenuation). The dominant mode (lowest cutoff) is TE$_{10}$ with $\omega_{10} = c\pi/a$.

Above cutoff the propagation constant is $\beta = \sqrt{(\omega/c)^2 - (\omega_{mn}/c)^2}$, giving phase velocity $v_p = \omega/\beta > c$ and group velocity $v_g = d\omega/d\beta = c^2/v_p < c$. The product $v_p v_g = c^2$ — neither velocity exceeds the constraint imposed by causality; the superluminal phase velocity is an artifact of the modal structure and does not carry information.

## Causality and the Kramers-Kronig relations [Master]

The frequency-dependent response functions $\epsilon(\omega)$ and $\mu(\omega)$ are not arbitrary: causality (the medium cannot respond before the field arrives) imposes powerful constraints. Writing the electric susceptibility $\chi_e(\omega) = \epsilon(\omega)/\epsilon_0 - 1$, its Fourier transform $\chi_e(t)$ must vanish for $t < 0$.

If $\chi_e(\omega)$ is analytic in the upper half-plane (no poles there, which causality enforces), then the real and imaginary parts of $\chi_e(\omega) = \chi_e'(\omega) + i\chi_e''(\omega)$ are related by the **Kramers-Kronig relations**:

$$
\chi_e'(\omega) \;=\; \frac{1}{\pi}\,\mathcal{P}\!\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\chi_e''(\omega')}{\omega' - \omega}\,d\omega',
$$

$$
\chi_e''(\omega) \;=\; -\frac{1}{\pi}\,\mathcal{P}\!\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\chi_e'(\omega')}{\omega' - \omega}\,d\omega',
$$

where $\mathcal{P}$ denotes the Cauchy principal value. These are **Hilbert transform** pairs. The physical content: absorption ($\chi_e'' > 0$) and dispersion ($\chi_e'$ varying with $\omega$) are not independent — you cannot have one without the other. Any medium that absorbs at some frequencies must disperse at nearby frequencies. This is why anomalous dispersion always accompanies absorption lines.

The Kramers-Kronig relations follow from the analyticity of $\chi_e(\omega)$ in the upper half $\omega$-plane, which is itself a consequence of causality. Their proof uses contour integration: the function $\chi_e(\omega')/(\omega' - \omega)$ integrated around a semicircular contour in the upper half-plane (closed above, since $\chi_e$ is analytic there) picks up only the pole at $\omega' = \omega$, and the principal-value prescription handles this pole. The semicircle at infinity contributes zero if $\chi_e(\omega) \to 0$ as $|\omega| \to \infty$.

## Connections [Master]

- **Maxwell's equations in differential form [10.04.01]** are the starting point for everything in this unit. The vacuum subset ($\rho = 0$, $\mathbf{J} = 0$) is what produces the wave equation; the full equations with sources produce the retarded-potential solutions needed for radiation [10.07.01].

- **Faraday's law [10.03.01]** provides half the feedback loop — changing $\mathbf{B}$ generates $\mathbf{E}$ — that makes self-sustaining wave propagation possible. The other half comes from the displacement-current term in the Maxwell-Ampere law.

- **Radiation [10.07.01]** (pending) picks up where this unit leaves off. The plane-wave solutions derived here are the far-field limit of the radiation fields produced by time-varying charge and current distributions. The retarded-potential framework for radiation takes the wave equation with sources as its starting point.

- **Spectroscopy [14.12.01]** (pending) in chemistry measures absorption of electromagnetic waves at frequencies matching molecular energy-level transitions. The wave equation, polarization states, and the Poynting vector developed here are the classical substrate that the quantum treatment of light-matter interaction refines.

- **The d'Alembertian operator and special relativity** connect through the observation that $\Box = \nabla^2 - c^{-2}\partial^2/\partial t^2$ is the Lorentz-invariant wave operator on Minkowski spacetime. The wave equation $\Box\psi = 0$ is preserved under Lorentz transformations, which is the first hint that Maxwell's equations are relativistic — a point developed systematically in the relativistic-electrodynamics chapter.

- **Group velocity and wave packets** reappear in quantum mechanics [12.02.01] (pending): the de Broglie relations assign a wave vector and frequency to each momentum-energy eigenstate, and wave-packet propagation at the group velocity is how quantum-mechanical probability densities move. The mathematical framework is identical; the physical interpretation changes.

- **Kramers-Kronig relations** are a special case of the causality constraints that appear throughout physics: the Titchmarsh theorem in signal processing, the optical theorem in scattering theory, and the dispersion relations of high-energy physics all have the same Hilbert-transform structure, all originating from the analyticity enforced by causality.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Maxwell's 1865 paper "A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field" derived the wave equation from his equations and computed the propagation speed $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\epsilon_0}$. The agreement with Fizeau's measured speed of light ($\approx 3.15 \times 10^8$ m/s) was the first quantitative evidence that light is electromagnetic. Maxwell wrote: "We have strong reason to conclude that light itself — including radiant heat and other radiations — is an electromagnetic disturbance in the form of waves propagated through the electromagnetic field."

Heinrich Hertz's experiments (1886–1888) provided the direct experimental confirmation. Hertz generated electromagnetic waves with a spark-gap oscillator, detected them at a distance, verified their reflection, refraction, polarization, and standing-wave interference, and measured their speed as approximately $c$. The wavelength of Hertz's waves was about 1 m — what we now call radio waves. Hertz's apparatus was the first radio transmitter and receiver; his experiments transformed Maxwell's theoretical prediction into a demonstrated physical phenomenon.

The wave-equation derivation carries a philosophical lesson: the speed of light is not a free parameter of nature but a *consequence* of the electromagnetic constants $\epsilon_0$ and $\mu_0$. This means that $c$ is determined by the properties of the electromagnetic field itself, not by the motion of any medium. The failure of attempts to detect motion relative to a luminiferous ether (Michelson-Morley, 1887) confirmed this independence and set the stage for Einstein's 1905 identification of $c$ as a universal speed limit in special relativity. The wave equation, derived from Maxwell, was the first physical law to demand a relativistic spacetime structure.
