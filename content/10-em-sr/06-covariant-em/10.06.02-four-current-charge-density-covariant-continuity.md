## Intuition [Beginner]

Special relativity unifies space and time into a single 4-dimensional framework. The charge density $\rho$ and current density $\mathbf{J}$ are similarly unified into a single four-component object called the **four-current**:

$$j^\mu = (c\rho,\, J_x,\, J_y,\, J_z)$$

The time component is the charge density (times $c$), and the spatial components are the current density. Just as the position four-vector $(ct, x, y, z)$ combines space and time, the four-current combines charge and current.

The **continuity equation** (charge conservation) becomes beautifully simple in four-vector notation:

$$\text{div}_4(j) = 0$$

where $\text{div}_4$ denotes the four-dimensional divergence.

This single equation says: charge is conserved. In any reference frame, at any point in spacetime, the four-divergence of the current vanishes. The fact that it can be written as a single four-vector equation means it is automatically **Lorentz-invariant**: if charge is conserved in one frame, it is conserved in all frames.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A uniform line charge with density $\lambda$ lies along the $x$-axis in frame S (at rest). In this frame, $\rho = \lambda\,\delta(y)\delta(z)$ and $\mathbf{J} = 0$. The four-current is $j^\mu = (c\lambda\,\delta(y)\delta(z),\, 0,\, 0,\, 0)$.

Now boost to frame S' moving with velocity $v$ in the $x$-direction. The Lorentz transformation mixes the time and $x$ components:

$$j'^0 = \gamma(j^0 - \beta j^1) = \gamma c\lambda\,\delta(y')\delta(z')$$

$$j'^1 = \gamma(j^1 - \beta j^0) = -\gamma\beta c\lambda\,\delta(y')\delta(z') = -\gamma v\lambda\,\delta(y')\delta(z')$$

So in the boosted frame, there is both charge density $\rho' = \gamma\lambda\,\delta(y')\delta(z')$ (enhanced by length contraction) and current density $J'_x = -\gamma v\lambda\,\delta(y')\delta(z')$ (the line charge is now a current). The four-current correctly captures both effects.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The **four-current density** is defined as:

$$j^\mu = (c\rho,\, \mathbf{J})$$

where $\rho(\mathbf{r}, t)$ is the charge density and $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}, t)$ is the current density. Under a Lorentz boost with velocity $\mathbf{v}$:

$$j'^\mu = \Lambda^\mu{}_\nu\, j^\nu$$

Explicitly, for a boost along $x$: $j'^0 = \gamma(j^0 - \beta j^1)$, $j'^1 = \gamma(j^1 - \beta j^0)$, $j'^2 = j^2$, $j'^3 = j^3$.

**The continuity equation in covariant form.**

$$\partial_\mu j^\mu = \frac{1}{c}\frac{\partial j^0}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = \frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$$

This is the local statement of **charge conservation**. The integral form is $\frac{dQ}{dt} = -\oint\mathbf{J}\cdot d\mathbf{a}$.

**The inhomogeneous Maxwell equations in covariant form.** Using the Faraday tensor $F^{\mu\nu}$ [10.06.01]:

$$\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 j^\nu$$

This single tensor equation encodes both Gauss's law ($\nu = 0$) and the Ampere-Maxwell law ($\nu = 1, 2, 3$). The continuity equation follows by taking the divergence: $\partial_\nu\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0\partial_\nu j^\nu$. Since $F^{\mu\nu}$ is antisymmetric, $\partial_\nu\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = 0$, giving $\partial_\nu j^\nu = 0$.

**Transformation of charge density.** For a boost along $x$:

$$\rho' = \gamma\left(\rho - \frac{v}{c^2}J_x\right), \qquad J'_x = \gamma(J_x - v\rho)$$

A pure charge density ($\mathbf{J} = 0$) in one frame becomes both charge and current in a boosted frame. The total charge $Q = \int\rho\,d^3r$ is a Lorentz scalar: $Q' = Q$.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (Covariant continuity equation from Maxwell's equations).**

**Theorem.** *The continuity equation $\partial_\mu j^\mu = 0$ follows from the inhomogeneous Maxwell equations $\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 j^\nu$.*

*Proof.* Take the four-divergence of both sides:

$$\partial_\nu\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0\partial_\nu j^\nu$$

The left side involves the contraction of the symmetric tensor $\partial_\nu\partial_\mu$ with the antisymmetric tensor $F^{\mu\nu}$. For any symmetric $S_{\mu\nu}$ and antisymmetric $A^{\mu\nu}$: $S_{\mu\nu}A^{\mu\nu} = 0$ (each term cancels with its transposed partner: $S_{12}A^{12} + S_{21}A^{21} = S_{12}A^{12} + S_{12}(-A^{12}) = 0$). Therefore $\partial_\nu\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = 0$, and:

$$\partial_\nu j^\nu = 0$$

This is the continuity equation, derived as a *consequence* of Maxwell's equations, not an additional postulate. $\square$

**Bridge.** The four-current is the source term in the covariant Maxwell equations, playing the same role as $\rho$ and $\mathbf{J}$ in the 3D formulation but with manifest Lorentz invariance. The foundational insight is that charge conservation ($\partial_\mu j^\mu = 0$) is not an independent law but a *consequence* of the inhomogeneous Maxwell equations and the antisymmetry of $F^{\mu\nu}$. The central message is that the four-current transforms as a four-vector, ensuring that charge conservation holds in all reference frames automatically. Putting these together, the four-current provides the source for the Faraday tensor [10.06.01], and the stress-energy tensor [10.06.03] is built from both $F^{\mu\nu}$ and $j^\mu$. The Lorentz transformation of E and B fields [10.06.04] follows from the transformation of $F^{\mu\nu}$, which is sourced by $j^\mu$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Write the four-current for a point charge $q$ at position $\mathbf{r}_0(t)$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\rho = q\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0(t))$ and $\mathbf{J} = q\mathbf{v}\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0(t))$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a point charge: $\rho = q\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0(t))$ and $\mathbf{J} = q\mathbf{v}\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0(t))$. The four-current is:

$$j^\mu = q\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0(t))\,(c,\, \mathbf{v}) = q\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0(t))\,\frac{dx^\mu}{dt}$$

The four-velocity is $u^\mu = \gamma(c, \mathbf{v})$, so $j^\mu = q\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0(t))\,u^\mu/\gamma$. In covariant notation: $j^\mu(x) = q\int u^\mu\,\delta^4(x - x(\tau))\,d\tau$ where the integral is over the worldline of the charge.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Verify the continuity equation for a point charge: show that $\partial\rho/\partial t + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$ when $\rho = q\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0(t))$ and $\mathbf{J} = q\dot{\mathbf{r}}_0\,\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0(t))$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the chain rule: $\partial_t\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0) = -\dot{\mathbf{r}}_0\cdot\nabla\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0)$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t} = q\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0) = -q\dot{\mathbf{r}}_0\cdot\nabla_{\mathbf{r}}\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0)$ (chain rule: $\partial_t$ acts on $\mathbf{r}_0$, $\nabla$ acts on $\mathbf{r}$, with opposite signs).

$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = q\dot{\mathbf{r}}_0\cdot\nabla\delta^3(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0)$ (the velocity $\dot{\mathbf{r}}_0$ is constant with respect to $\nabla_{\mathbf{r}}$).

Adding: $\partial\rho/\partial t + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = -q\dot{\mathbf{r}}_0\cdot\nabla\delta^3 + q\dot{\mathbf{r}}_0\cdot\nabla\delta^3 = 0$. Charge is conserved for a moving point charge.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the total charge $Q = \int\rho\,d^3r$ is a Lorentz invariant (scalar).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$Q = \frac{1}{c}\int j^0\,d^3r$. Write as an integral over a spacelike hypersurface and show it is invariant under Lorentz transformations.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The total charge is $Q = \int\rho\,d^3r = \frac{1}{c}\int j^0\,d^3x$. In covariant notation, this is an integral over a spacelike hypersurface $\Sigma$:

$$Q = \frac{1}{c}\int_\Sigma j^\mu\,d\Sigma_\mu$$

where $d\Sigma_\mu = n_\mu\,d^3x$ is the oriented surface element ($n_\mu$ is the unit normal to the hypersurface). For a constant-time surface: $n_\mu = (1, 0, 0, 0)$ and $d\Sigma_0 = d^3x$, giving $Q = \frac{1}{c}\int j^0\,d^3x$.

Under a Lorentz transformation, both $j^\mu$ and $d\Sigma_\mu$ transform (they are dual to each other), so the integral $Q = \frac{1}{c}\int j^\mu\,d\Sigma_\mu$ is a Lorentz scalar. Equivalently, from the continuity equation: $dQ/dt = \int\partial\rho/\partial t\,d^3r = -\int\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}\,d^3r = -\oint\mathbf{J}\cdot d\mathbf{a} = 0$ (if $\mathbf{J} = 0$ at infinity). So $Q$ is conserved in every frame, and being a conserved quantity associated with a Lorentz-invariant law, it is frame-independent.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A current-carrying wire has $\mathbf{J} = J_0\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ (along the wire) and $\rho = 0$ (the wire is neutral in its rest frame). Find $\rho'$ and $\mathbf{J}'$ in a frame boosted along the wire direction.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$j^\mu = (0, 0, 0, J_0)$. Boost along $z$: $j'^0 = \gamma(0 - \beta J_0) = -\gamma\beta J_0$, $j'^3 = \gamma(J_0 - \beta\cdot 0) = \gamma J_0$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In the rest frame: $j^\mu = (0, 0, 0, J_0)$. Boost along $z$ with velocity $v$:

$j'^0 = \gamma(j^0 - \beta j^3) = \gamma(0 - \beta J_0) = -\gamma\beta J_0$

$j'^1 = j^1 = 0$, $j'^2 = j^2 = 0$

$j'^3 = \gamma(j^3 - \beta j^0) = \gamma J_0$

So $\rho' = j'^0/c = -\gamma\beta J_0/c = -\gamma v J_0/c^2$ and $J'_z = \gamma J_0$.

The neutral current-carrying wire appears *charged* in a boosted frame! The charge density is proportional to $-vJ_0/c^2$, which is very small for everyday velocities but is the origin of the magnetic force in the covariant description: what appears as a magnetic force in one frame appears as an electric force in another.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the inhomogeneous Maxwell equation $\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 j^\nu$ is equivalent to Gauss's law and the Ampere-Maxwell law.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write out $F^{\mu\nu}$ in matrix form and compute $\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu}$ for $\nu = 0$ and $\nu = 1, 2, 3$ separately.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Faraday tensor is $F^{\mu\nu} = \begin{pmatrix}0 & -E_x/c & -E_y/c & -E_z/c \\ E_x/c & 0 & -B_z & B_y \\ E_y/c & B_z & 0 & -B_x \\ E_z/c & -B_y & B_x & 0\end{pmatrix}$.

For $\nu = 0$: $\partial_\mu F^{\mu 0} = \partial_1 F^{10} + \partial_2 F^{20} + \partial_3 F^{30} = \frac{1}{c}(\partial_x E_x + \partial_y E_y + \partial_z E_z) = \frac{1}{c}\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}$. Setting equal to $\mu_0 j^0 = \mu_0 c\rho$: $\frac{1}{c}\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \mu_0 c\rho$, giving $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \mu_0 c^2\rho = \rho/\epsilon_0$. This is Gauss's law.

For $\nu = 1$: $\partial_\mu F^{\mu 1} = \partial_0 F^{01} + \partial_2 F^{21} + \partial_3 F^{31} = -\frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial E_x}{\partial t} - \partial_y B_z + \partial_z B_y$. Setting equal to $\mu_0 J_x$: $-\frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial E_x}{\partial t} + (\nabla \times \mathbf{B})_x = \mu_0 J_x$. Using $c^2 = 1/(\mu_0\epsilon_0)$: $(\nabla \times \mathbf{B})_x = \mu_0 J_x + \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial E_x}{\partial t}$. This is the $x$-component of the Ampere-Maxwell law. Similarly for $\nu = 2, 3$.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

The four-current for a charged fluid with proper charge density $\rho_0$ and four-velocity $u^\mu$ is $j^\mu = \rho_0 u^\mu$. Show that the continuity equation $\partial_\mu j^\mu = 0$ reduces to $\frac{d\rho_0}{d\tau} + \rho_0\,\partial_\mu u^\mu = 0$ where $\tau$ is proper time.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\partial_\mu(\rho_0 u^\mu) = u^\mu\partial_\mu\rho_0 + \rho_0\partial_\mu u^\mu$. The first term is the convective derivative $d\rho_0/d\tau$ divided by the proper time element.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$j^\mu = \rho_0 u^\mu$. The continuity equation:

$$\partial_\mu j^\mu = \partial_\mu(\rho_0 u^\mu) = (\partial_\mu\rho_0)u^\mu + \rho_0\partial_\mu u^\mu = 0$$

The first term: $(\partial_\mu\rho_0)u^\mu = \frac{\partial\rho_0}{\partial t}\frac{dt}{d\tau}\frac{1}{\gamma} + \nabla\rho_0\cdot\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{d\tau}\frac{1}{\gamma}$. Actually, $u^\mu = \gamma(c, \mathbf{v})$ and $u^\mu\partial_\mu = \gamma(c\,\partial_t + \mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla) = \gamma\,d/dt$. But $d/d\tau = \gamma\,d/dt$, so $u^\mu\partial_\mu = \frac{d}{d\tau}\cdot c^{-1}\cdot c = d/d\tau$... Let me be more careful.

$u^\mu\partial_\mu = u^0\partial_0 + u^i\partial_i = \gamma c\frac{1}{c}\frac{\partial}{\partial t} + \gamma v^i\frac{\partial}{\partial x^i} = \gamma(\partial_t + \mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla) = \gamma\frac{d}{dt} = \frac{d}{d\tau}$.

So $(\partial_\mu\rho_0)u^\mu = d\rho_0/d\tau$ (the derivative following the fluid element). The continuity equation becomes:

$$\frac{d\rho_0}{d\tau} + \rho_0\,\partial_\mu u^\mu = 0$$

If the four-velocity is divergence-free ($\partial_\mu u^\mu = 0$, incompressible flow), then $d\rho_0/d\tau = 0$: the proper charge density is constant along each streamline. This is the relativistic analogue of the incompressibility condition.
</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**The gauge-covariant derivative.** In the presence of an electromagnetic field, the ordinary derivative is replaced by the gauge-covariant derivative $D_\mu = \partial_\mu + iqA_\mu/\hbar$ (where $q$ is the charge). The four-current for a charged field $\psi$ is $j^\mu = iq(\psi^* D^\mu\psi - \psi(D^\mu\psi)^*)$. This expression is automatically conserved ($\partial_\mu j^\mu = 0$) by the equations of motion for $\psi$.

**Charge conservation as a Noether current.** The continuity equation $\partial_\mu j^\mu = 0$ is the Noether current associated with the global $U(1)$ symmetry of the Lagrangian: $\psi \to e^{i\alpha}\psi$ (global phase rotation). The conserved charge is $Q = \int j^0\,d^3x$, which is the total electric charge. This connects electromagnetism to the gauge principle: local gauge invariance ($\alpha \to \alpha(x)$) requires the existence of the electromagnetic field $A_\mu$.

**The charge-current tensor.** For a system of charged particles, the total angular momentum is conserved only if the current satisfies additional conditions. The full description involves the **energy-momentum tensor** $T^{\mu\nu}$ [10.06.03] and the **angular momentum tensor** $J^{\mu\nu\lambda} = x^\mu T^{\nu\lambda} - x^\nu T^{\mu\lambda}$.

**Synthesis.** The four-current is the covariant source for the electromagnetic field, unifying charge density and current density into a single geometric object. The foundational insight is that charge conservation ($\partial_\mu j^\mu = 0$) is not an additional law but a *consequence* of the antisymmetry of $F^{\mu\nu}$, and being a four-vector equation, it is automatically Lorentz-invariant. The central message is that the four-current transforms as a four-vector under Lorentz boosts, mixing charge and current in exactly the way that space and time mix. Putting these together, the four-current provides the source for the inhomogeneous Maxwell equations [10.06.01], drives the stress-energy tensor [10.06.03], and determines the transformation of fields between frames [10.06.04].

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Lorentz invariance of the continuity equation).** If $\partial_\mu j^\mu = 0$ holds in one frame, it holds in all Lorentz-transformed frames.

*Proof.* The continuity equation is $\partial_\mu j^\mu = 0$, a Lorentz scalar. Under a Lorentz transformation $x'^\mu = \Lambda^\mu{}_\nu x^\nu$: $\partial'_\mu = \Lambda_\mu{}^\nu\partial_\nu$ and $j'^\mu = \Lambda^\mu{}_\nu j^\nu$. So $\partial'_\mu j'^\mu = \Lambda_\mu{}^\nu\Lambda^\mu{}_\rho\partial_\nu j^\rho = \delta^\nu_\rho\partial_\nu j^\rho = \partial_\nu j^\nu = 0$. The contraction $\Lambda_\mu{}^\nu\Lambda^\mu{}_\rho = \delta^\nu_\rho$ follows from the defining property of Lorentz transformations: $\Lambda^\mu{}_\nu\eta_{\mu\rho}\Lambda^\rho{}_\sigma = \eta_{\nu\sigma}$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Faraday tensor [10.06.01]** is the field quantity; the four-current is the source: $\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 j^\nu$.
- **Four-vectors [10.05.03]** provide the framework; the four-current is a specific four-vector.
- **Stress-energy tensor [10.06.03]** involves both $F^{\mu\nu}$ and $j^\mu$ in the Lorentz force equation.
- **Displacement current [10.03.04]** is part of the inhomogeneous equation $\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 j^\nu$.
- **Field transformations [10.06.04]** follow from the Lorentz transformation of $F^{\mu\nu}$, which is sourced by $j^\mu$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The four-current was introduced by Minkowski in 1908 as part of his geometric formulation of special relativity. The identification of $j^\mu = (c\rho, \mathbf{J})$ as a four-vector was a key insight: it showed that charge and current are the same physical quantity viewed from different reference frames, just as space and time are aspects of a single spacetime.

The proof that charge conservation follows from Maxwell's equations (rather than being an independent principle) is sometimes attributed to Minkowski, though the continuity equation itself was known to Maxwell. The covariant formulation made the proof transparent: the antisymmetry of $F^{\mu\nu}$ forces $\partial_\nu j^\nu = 0$.

The deep connection between charge conservation and gauge invariance was discovered by Weyl (1918) and formalised by Noether (1918): global phase symmetry $\psi \to e^{i\alpha}\psi$ implies a conserved current, and local gauge symmetry $\alpha \to \alpha(x)$ requires the existence of the electromagnetic field. This gauge principle is the foundation of the Standard Model of particle physics.
