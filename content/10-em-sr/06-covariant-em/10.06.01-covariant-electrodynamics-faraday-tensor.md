## Intuition [Beginner]

Maxwell's equations are four vector equations in the electric field $\mathbf{E}$ and the magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$. They look like four separate laws governing two distinct physical fields. Special relativity reveals this appearance is an illusion: $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ are not two independent fields. They are different parts of a single unified object, seen from different viewpoints.

A charge sitting at rest feels only an electric force. The same charge, if you run past it, also feels a magnetic force. The field did not change. Your perspective did. What looks like a pure $\mathbf{E}$ field in one frame has $\mathbf{B}$ components in another, the same way a coin seen edge-on looks like a line but seen face-on looks like a circle.

That single object is the **electromagnetic field tensor** $F$, a $4 \times 4$ antisymmetric matrix whose components encode the six field quantities $(E_x, E_y, E_z, B_x, B_y, B_z)$. Under a Lorentz boost, the entries of $F$ mix — the way time and space coordinates mix under a change of inertial frame — and $\mathbf{E}$ rotates into $\mathbf{B}$.

With $F$ in hand, Maxwell's four separate vector equations collapse into two compact tensor equations. The payoff is a dramatic simplification: one geometric object, two equations, instead of two vector fields and four equations.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a point charge at rest, surrounded by radial electric field lines pointing outward — the familiar Coulomb-field picture. No magnetic field exists anywhere.

Now run past the charge at speed $v$. The field lines compress in the direction of motion (Lorentz contraction) and the charge becomes a moving current. A moving current produces a $\mathbf{B}$ field circling the line of motion.

The field lines in the two frames are related by a hyperbolic rotation in spacetime — mixing the time coordinate with the spatial coordinate along the direction of motion. The $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ components tilt into each other, just as $t$ and $x$ mix under a boost.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A point charge $q$ sits at the origin of frame $S$. The electric field is the Coulomb field $\mathbf{E} = (q/4\pi\epsilon_0)\hat{\mathbf{r}}/r^2$, and $\mathbf{B} = 0$.

Now consider frame $S'$ moving at velocity $v$ in the $x$-direction relative to $S$. In this frame the charge moves at speed $-v$ along the $x$-axis. What fields does an observer in $S'$ measure?

The Lorentz transformation of the fields gives, for $v \ll c$:

$$
\mathbf{E}' \approx \mathbf{E}, \qquad \mathbf{B}' \approx -\frac{\mathbf{v}}{c} \times \mathbf{E}.
$$

A magnetic field has appeared. If $\mathbf{v}$ points along $x$ and $\mathbf{E}$ is radial, then $\mathbf{B}'$ circles the $x$-axis, exactly like the field of a current-carrying wire. The moving charge *is* a current.

At higher speeds the full result includes a factor of $\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}$, and $\mathbf{E}'$ compresses along the direction of motion. The key point stands: $\mathbf{B}$ is not an independent field. It is the part of $F$ that becomes visible when you change frames.

### Second worked example: crossed fields

In frame $S$, the fields are $\mathbf{E} = (0, E_0, 0)$ and $\mathbf{B} = (0, 0, B_0)$ with $E_0 = cB_0$. Find a frame $S'$ where the magnetic field vanishes.

Since $\mathbf{E}\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$ (the fields are perpendicular) and $E = cB$ (equal magnitudes in the appropriate sense), the invariant $\mathcal{I}_1 = 2(B^2 - E^2/c^2) = 2(B_0^2 - E_0^2/c^2) = 2(B_0^2 - B_0^2) = 0$. Wait — this means the field is null, and no frame exists where $\mathbf{B}' = 0$ for a null field.

Let us instead take $E_0 = 2cB_0$. Then $\mathcal{I}_1 = 2(B_0^2 - 4B_0^2) = -6B_0^2 < 0$: electric-dominant. The fields are perpendicular, so $\mathcal{I}_2 = 0$. A frame with $\mathbf{B}' = 0$ exists.

The boost must be along $\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B} = E_0\hat{\mathbf{y}}\times B_0\hat{\mathbf{z}} = E_0 B_0\hat{\mathbf{x}}$, so the boost is along $x$. The transformation gives $B'_y = \gamma(B_y + (v/c^2)E_z) = 0$ and $B'_z = \gamma(B_z - (v/c^2)E_y) = \gamma(B_0 - (v/c^2)E_0) = 0$. Solving: $v = B_0 c^2/E_0 = c^2 B_0/(2cB_0) = c/2$. At $v = c/2$, $\gamma = 1/\sqrt{3/4} = 2/\sqrt{3}$. The remaining electric field is $E'_y = \gamma(E_y - vB_z) = (2/\sqrt{3})(E_0 - (c/2)B_0) = (2/\sqrt{3})(2cB_0 - cB_0/2) = (2/\sqrt{3})(3cB_0/2) = \sqrt{3}cB_0$. The magnetic field has been eliminated by the boost.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout this unit we adopt the metric signature $(+,-,-,-)$ and SI units. Greek indices $\mu, \nu, \ldots$ run over $0, 1, 2, 3$; Latin indices $i, j, \ldots$ run over $1, 2, 3$. The four-position is $x^\mu = (ct, x, y, z)$ and the four-derivative is $\partial_\mu = \frac{1}{c}\frac{\partial}{\partial t}, -\nabla$.

### The four-potential

The **electromagnetic four-potential** is

$$
A^\mu = \left(\frac{\phi}{c},\; \mathbf{A}\right),
$$

where $\phi$ is the scalar potential and $\mathbf{A}$ is the vector potential [10.01.01]. The corresponding covariant vector is $A_\mu = g_{\mu\nu}A^\nu = (\phi/c, -\mathbf{A})$.

### The Faraday tensor

The **electromagnetic field tensor** (Faraday tensor) is the rank-2 antisymmetric tensor

$$
\boxed{\;F_{\mu\nu} \;:=\; \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu.\;}
$$

In matrix form, with $\mu$ as row index and $\nu$ as column index:

$$
F_{\mu\nu} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & E_x/c & E_y/c & E_z/c \\ -E_x/c & 0 & -B_z & B_y \\ -E_y/c & B_z & 0 & -B_x \\ -E_z/c & -B_y & B_x & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

The raised-index version $F^{\mu\nu} = g^{\mu\alpha}g^{\nu\beta}F_{\alpha\beta}$ flips the sign on the electric-field entries:

$$
F^{\mu\nu} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -E_x/c & -E_y/c & -E_z/c \\ E_x/c & 0 & -B_z & B_y \\ E_y/c & B_z & 0 & -B_x \\ E_z/c & -B_y & B_x & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

The six independent components of $F_{\mu\nu}$ are exactly the three components of $\mathbf{E}$ and the three components of $\mathbf{B}$. The four-current is $J^\mu = (c\rho,\, \mathbf{J})$, where $\rho$ is the charge density and $\mathbf{J}$ is the current density.

### Covariant Maxwell equations

Maxwell's four vector equations [10.04.01] reduce to two tensor equations. The **inhomogeneous pair** (sources present) is

$$
\boxed{\;\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 J^\nu.\;}
$$

The **homogeneous pair** (no sources — the Bianchi identity) is

$$
\boxed{\;\partial_\lambda F_{\mu\nu} + \partial_\mu F_{\nu\lambda} + \partial_\nu F_{\lambda\mu} = 0,\quad\text{equivalently } \partial_{[\lambda} F_{\mu\nu]} = 0.\;}
$$

The bracket $[\lambda\mu\nu]$ denotes total antisymmetrisation over the three indices.

### Gauge invariance

The Faraday tensor is invariant under the **gauge transformation**

$$
A_\mu \;\longrightarrow\; A_\mu + \partial_\mu \lambda
$$

for any smooth function $\lambda(x)$. This follows immediately: $F_{\mu\nu} \to \partial_\mu(A_\nu + \partial_\nu\lambda) - \partial_\nu(A_\mu + \partial_\mu\lambda) = F_{\mu\nu} + (\partial_\mu\partial_\nu - \partial_\nu\partial_\mu)\lambda = F_{\mu\nu}$, since partial derivatives commute.

### Covariant Lorentz force

The equation of motion for a particle of charge $q$ and rest mass $m$ is

$$
\frac{dp^\mu}{d\tau} = q\, F^{\mu\nu}\, u_\nu,
$$

where $p^\mu = m u^\mu$ is the four-momentum, $u^\mu = dx^\mu/d\tau$ is the four-velocity, and $\tau$ is proper time. The spatial components ($\mu = 1,2,3$) reproduce the Lorentz force law $\mathbf{F} = q(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B})$; the time component ($\mu = 0$) reproduces the power equation $dE/dt = q\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{E}$.

### The electromagnetic stress-energy tensor

The symmetric rank-2 tensor

$$
T^{\mu\nu} \;=\; \frac{1}{\mu_0}\!\left(F^{\mu\alpha}F^{\nu}{}_{\alpha} - \frac{1}{4}g^{\mu\nu}F_{\alpha\beta}F^{\alpha\beta}\right)
$$

encodes the energy density ($T^{00} = \frac{1}{2}(\epsilon_0 E^2 + B^2/\mu_0)$), the Poynting vector ($T^{0i} = c(\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B})_i/\mu_0$), and the Maxwell stress tensor ($T^{ij}$). Energy-momentum conservation is $\partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = -F^{\nu\lambda}J_\lambda$, where the right-hand side is the Lorentz-force density exerted by the field on the sources.

### Sign conventions

The sign choices above follow Jackson (3e) and Griffiths (4e): metric $(+,-,-,-)$, $A^\mu = (\phi/c, \mathbf{A})$, and $F_{0i} = E_i/c$. The opposite metric $(-,+,+,+)$ (used by Landau-Lifshitz and much of the particle-physics literature) flips the signs of $F_{0i}$ and $F^{0i}$. State the convention before computing.

### Lorentz transformation of the fields

The field tensor transforms as a rank-2 tensor under Lorentz boosts: $F'^{\mu\nu} = \Lambda^\mu{}_\alpha \Lambda^\nu{}_\beta F^{\alpha\beta}$. For a boost along the $x$-axis with velocity $v$, the electric and magnetic fields transform as

$$
E'_x = E_x, \qquad E'_y = \gamma(E_y - vB_z), \qquad E'_z = \gamma(E_z + vB_y),
$$

$$
B'_x = B_x, \qquad B'_y = \gamma\!\left(B_y + \frac{v}{c^2}E_z\right), \qquad B'_z = \gamma\!\left(B_z - \frac{v}{c^2}E_y\right).
$$

The components parallel to the boost direction are unchanged; the perpendicular components mix $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$. The inverse transformation is obtained by replacing $v$ with $-v$. These transformation laws follow directly from the tensor transformation rule applied to $F^{\mu\nu}$ and the boost matrix $\Lambda^\mu{}_\nu$.

Two important special cases illustrate the physics. First, if $\mathbf{B} = 0$ in frame $S$ (a pure electric field), the boosted frame has $\mathbf{B}' \neq 0$: a magnetic field appears. Second, if $\mathbf{E} = 0$ in $S$ (a pure magnetic field), the boosted frame has $\mathbf{E}' \neq 0$: an electric field appears. These are not separate phenomena — they are different views of the same field tensor $F$.

### Invariants of the electromagnetic field

The two Lorentz invariants constructed from $F$ are

$$
\mathcal{I}_1 = F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu} = 2\!\left(B^2 - \frac{E^2}{c^2}\right), \qquad \mathcal{I}_2 = F_{\mu\nu}\tilde{F}^{\mu\nu} = -\frac{4}{c}\,\mathbf{E}\cdot\mathbf{B},
$$

where $\tilde{F}^{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon^{\mu\nu\alpha\beta}F_{\alpha\beta}$ is the **dual tensor**. Both are the same in every frame. They classify the field:

- If $\mathcal{I}_1 > 0$ (i.e., $B > E/c$), the field is **magnetic-dominant** and a frame exists where $\mathbf{E}' = 0$.
- If $\mathcal{I}_1 < 0$ (i.e., $E > cB$), the field is **electric-dominant** and a frame exists where $\mathbf{B}' = 0$.
- If $\mathcal{I}_1 = 0$ and $\mathcal{I}_2 = 0$, the field is **null** (a plane electromagnetic wave: $E = cB$ and $\mathbf{E}\perp\mathbf{B}$). No frame exists where either field vanishes.

For a plane wave propagating in the $z$-direction: $\mathbf{E} = E_0\hat{\mathbf{x}}$, $\mathbf{B} = (E_0/c)\hat{\mathbf{y}}$. Then $\mathcal{I}_1 = 2(E_0^2/c^2 - E_0^2/c^2) = 0$ and $\mathcal{I}_2 = -4E_0(E_0/c)\hat{\mathbf{x}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{y}}/c = 0$. Both invariants vanish — the electromagnetic field of a plane wave is a Lorentz invariant null field.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Equivalence of covariant and vector Maxwell equations).** *The two covariant equations $\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 J^\nu$ and $\partial_{[\lambda}F_{\mu\nu]} = 0$, with $F_{\mu\nu}$ and $F^{\mu\nu}$ as defined above, are equivalent to the four standard vector Maxwell equations in SI units.*

**Proof.** We treat the two covariant equations in turn.

*Part I: Inhomogeneous equation.* Write $\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 J^\nu$ for each value of $\nu$.

**$\nu = 0$:** $\partial_\mu F^{\mu 0} = \partial_i F^{i0} = \nabla \cdot (\mathbf{E}/c)$, since $F^{00} = 0$ and $F^{i0} = -F^{0i} = E_i/c$. The right side is $\mu_0 J^0 = \mu_0 c\rho$. So $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}/c = \mu_0 c\rho$, i.e., $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \mu_0 c^2 \rho = \rho/\epsilon_0$. This is **Gauss's law**.

**$\nu = j$ (spatial):** $\partial_\mu F^{\mu j} = \partial_0 F^{0j} + \partial_i F^{ij}$. Here $F^{0j} = -E_j/c$, so $\partial_0 F^{0j} = -\frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial E_j}{\partial t}$. For the spatial divergence, write $F^{ij}$ in terms of $\mathbf{B}$: for $j=1$, $\partial_i F^{i1} = \partial_2 F^{21} + \partial_3 F^{31} = \partial_y B_z - \partial_z B_y = (\nabla \times \mathbf{B})_1$. The same pattern holds for $j=2,3$, giving $\partial_i F^{ij} = (\nabla \times \mathbf{B})_j$. Setting the sum equal to $\mu_0 J^j$:

$$
(\nabla \times \mathbf{B})_j - \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial E_j}{\partial t} = \mu_0 J_j.
$$

Since $1/c^2 = \mu_0\epsilon_0$, this is $\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0\epsilon_0\,\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t$. This is the **Ampere-Maxwell law**.

*Part II: Homogeneous equation.* Expand $\partial_{[\lambda} F_{\mu\nu]} = 0$, i.e., $\partial_\lambda F_{\mu\nu} + \partial_\mu F_{\nu\lambda} + \partial_\nu F_{\lambda\mu} = 0$.

**All indices spatial** ($\lambda = 1, \mu = 2, \nu = 3$): $\partial_1 F_{23} + \partial_2 F_{31} + \partial_3 F_{12} = -\partial_x B_x - \partial_y B_y - \partial_z B_z = -\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$. This is **no magnetic monopoles**: $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$.

**One index temporal** ($\lambda = 0, \mu = 1, \nu = 2$): $\partial_0 F_{12} + \partial_1 F_{20} + \partial_2 F_{01} = 0$. Substituting $F_{12} = -B_z$, $F_{20} = -E_y/c$, $F_{01} = E_x/c$:

$$
-\frac{1}{c}\frac{\partial B_z}{\partial t} - \frac{1}{c}\partial_x E_y + \frac{1}{c}\partial_y E_x = 0.
$$

This is $(\nabla \times \mathbf{E})_z = -\partial B_z/\partial t$. Cyclic permutations of the indices give the other two components, yielding $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\partial\mathbf{B}/\partial t$. This is **Faraday's law**. The remaining index choices ($0,1,3$ and $0,2,3$) reproduce the other components of Faraday's law. $\square$

**Corollary (Charge conservation).** *The antisymmetry $F^{\mu\nu} = -F^{\nu\mu}$ implies $\partial_\nu\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = 0$ identically. Applying $\partial_\nu$ to the inhomogeneous equation gives $\partial_\nu J^\nu = 0$, the continuity equation $\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$.*

This corollary has deep significance. Charge conservation is not an independent law — it is a *consequence* of the field equations, guaranteed by the antisymmetry of $F^{\mu\nu}$. In the Lagrangian framework, this follows from Noether's theorem applied to the $U(1)$ gauge symmetry. The connection between symmetry and conservation law (Noether's theorem) and the connection between antisymmetry of the field tensor and charge conservation are two sides of the same coin: gauge invariance of the action implies charge conservation, and the antisymmetric structure of $F^{\mu\nu}$ encodes this invariance at the level of the field equations.

### Worked example: the field of an infinite current sheet

An infinite sheet in the $xy$-plane carries uniform surface current density $\mathbf{K} = K\hat{\mathbf{x}}$. The four-current is $J^\mu = (0, K\,\delta(z), 0, 0)$. In the rest frame $S$, the magnetic field above and below the sheet is $\mathbf{B} = \pm(\mu_0 K/2)\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ (from Ampere's law), and $\mathbf{E} = 0$.

Now boost to frame $S'$ moving at velocity $v$ along the $x$-axis. The fields transform as:

$$
E'_y = \gamma(E_y - vB_z) = 0, \qquad E'_z = \gamma(E_z + vB_y) = \pm\gamma v(\mu_0 K/2),
$$

$$
B'_y = \gamma\!\left(B_y + \frac{v}{c^2}E_z\right) = \pm\gamma(\mu_0 K/2), \qquad B'_z = 0.
$$

In $S'$, the current sheet moves at speed $v$ and carries both a surface current $K' = \gamma K$ and a surface charge density $\sigma' = -\gamma v K/c^2$. The electric field in $S'$ is produced by this surface charge; the magnetic field is produced by the surface current. Both fields are enhanced by the factor $\gamma$. This illustrates the unification: the pure magnetic field in $S$ acquires an electric component in $S'$ because the current sheet, which is electrically neutral in $S$, develops a surface charge in $S'$. The field tensor $F^{\mu\nu}$ is the same geometric object in both frames; only its components change under the Lorentz boost.

F_{\mu\nu} \to \partial_\mu(A_\nu + \partial_\nu\lambda) - \partial_\nu(A_\mu + \partial_\mu\lambda) = F_{\mu\nu} + (\partial_\mu\partial_\nu\lambda - \partial_\nu\partial_\mu\lambda).
$$

Since $\partial_\mu\partial_\nu = \partial_\nu\partial_\mu$ for smooth $\lambda$, the second term vanishes and $F_{\mu\nu}$ is unchanged. This is the mathematical content of gauge invariance: the observable fields depend only on $F$, not on the particular choice of $A$.

</details>

## Differential-forms formulation [Master]

On Minkowski spacetime $(\mathbb{R}^{1,3}, \eta)$, let $A = A_\mu\,dx^\mu$ be the electromagnetic potential 1-form. The Faraday tensor is the exterior derivative

$$
F = dA.
$$

This is a 2-form. The identity $d^2 = 0$ gives $dF = ddA = 0$, which is the homogeneous Maxwell equation $dF = 0$ in differential-forms language. This equation is *topological*: it depends only on $d^2 = 0$, not on the metric.

The **Hodge dual** of $F$ (with respect to the Minkowski metric) is

$$
{\star F}_{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_{\mu\nu\alpha\beta}F^{\alpha\beta},
$$

where $\epsilon_{\mu\nu\alpha\beta}$ is the Levi-Civita symbol with $\epsilon_{0123} = +1$. The inhomogeneous Maxwell equation becomes

$$
d{\star F} = \mu_0\,J,
$$

where $J = J_\mu\,dx^\mu$ is the current 3-form (equivalently $J = {\star j}$ where $j^\mu$ is the four-current vector). This equation *does* depend on the metric, through the Hodge star.

Maxwell's equations in this language are:

$$
\boxed{\;dF = 0, \qquad d{\star F} = \mu_0\,J.\;}
$$

The split is sharp: $dF = 0$ is metric-independent and topological (it encodes the statement that $F$ is locally exact, $F = dA$, guaranteed by the Poincare lemma on contractible domains). The equation $d{\star F} = \mu_0 J$ contains the dynamics — the coupling of the field to the metric and to the sources.

**Topological vs metric content.** The homogeneous equation $dF = 0$ has solutions $F = dA$ on any contractible region. Gauge freedom is the statement that $A$ and $A + d\lambda$ give the same $F$. On non-simply-connected topology (e.g., the Aharonov-Bohm configuration, where spacetime has a hole), $F = dA$ holds only locally and the global information is carried by the connection $A$ itself. This distinction — between the local field strength $F$ and the global gauge potential $A$ — is what makes the Aharonov-Bohm effect possible and is the prototype for the topological structure of all gauge theories.

### Hodge duality and electric-magnetic duality

The Hodge star operator maps $k$-forms to $(4-k)$-forms on four-dimensional spacetime. Applied to the 2-form $F$, it produces the dual 2-form ${\star F}$. In components:

$$
{\star F}_{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_{\mu\nu\alpha\beta}F^{\alpha\beta}.
$$

The dual tensor exchanges $\mathbf{E}$ and $c\mathbf{B}$: ${\star F}_{0i} = -B_i$ and ${\star F}_{ij} = \epsilon_{ijk}E_k/c$. Under this exchange, the homogeneous equation $dF = 0$ maps to $d{\star F} = 0$ (vacuum inhomogeneous equation) and vice versa. This is **electric-magnetic duality**: in vacuum, the Maxwell equations are symmetric under the exchange $\mathbf{E} \to c\mathbf{B}$, $\mathbf{B} \to -\mathbf{E}/c$.

If magnetic monopoles existed with magnetic charge density $\rho_m$ and current $\mathbf{J}_m$, the homogeneous equation would acquire a source: $dF = \mu_0 J_m$. The duality would become exact: the four equations would split into two pairs related by the exchange $(\mathbf{E}, \mathbf{B}, \rho_e, \mathbf{J}_e) \leftrightarrow (c\mathbf{B}, -\mathbf{E}/c, \rho_m/c, \mathbf{J}_m/c)$. Dirac showed that the existence of a single magnetic monopole anywhere in the universe would require electric charge to be quantised in units of $e = n\hbar/(2g)$ where $g$ is the magnetic charge. No magnetic monopoles have been observed, but many grand unified theories predict their existence.

The differential-forms language also makes the relation to de Rham cohomology manifest. The equation $dF = 0$ says that $F$ is closed. On a contractible region, $F = dA$ (exact) by the Poincare lemma. On a topology with holes (torus, sphere with punctures), closed forms need not be exact — the cohomology class of $F$ carries topological information. This is the mathematical framework underlying the Aharonov-Bohm effect, the Dirac monopole, and the topological classification of gauge fields (instanton number, Chern class).

## Lagrangian and gauge structure [Master]

The electromagnetic Lagrangian density is

$$
\mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{4\mu_0}F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu} - J^\mu A_\mu.
$$

Varying with respect to $A_\mu$ (with appropriate boundary conditions) yields the Euler-Lagrange equation $\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 J^\nu$, i.e., the inhomogeneous Maxwell equation. The homogeneous equation $dF = 0$ is not an equation of motion here — it is the identity $F = dA$.

**Noether current and charge conservation.** The Lagrangian is invariant under the global $U(1)$ transformation $A_\mu \to A_\mu$ (constant shift) or, more precisely, the matter sector has a $U(1)$ symmetry $\psi \to e^{i\alpha}\psi$ whose Noether current is $J^\mu$. The conservation law $\partial_\mu J^\mu = 0$ follows either from Noether's theorem applied to the global phase symmetry, or directly from the antisymmetry of $F^{\mu\nu}$ as shown in the corollary above.

**$U(1)$ gauge invariance.** The Lagrangian is invariant under the *local* gauge transformation $A_\mu \to A_\mu + \partial_\mu\lambda$, $\psi \to e^{i\lambda}\psi$. This local symmetry is the prototype of all gauge symmetries in fundamental physics. The promotion from a global $U(1)$ symmetry to a local one *requires* the introduction of the gauge field $A_\mu$ and the replacement of ordinary derivatives by covariant derivatives $D_\mu = \partial_\mu + ieA_\mu$ — the **minimal coupling** prescription.

The gauge principle can be stated as a requirement: if the global phase symmetry $\psi \to e^{i\alpha}\psi$ (which gives charge conservation by Noether's theorem) is promoted to a *local* symmetry $\psi \to e^{i\lambda(x)}\psi$, then the derivative $\partial_\mu\psi$ must be replaced by $D_\mu\psi = (\partial_\mu + ieA_\mu)\psi$ to maintain invariance. The gauge field $A_\mu$ is the price of local phase invariance, and its field strength $F_{\mu\nu}$ is the observable consequence. This is the "gauge argument" (also called the "gauge principle" or "minimal coupling principle") and it is the conceptual foundation of the entire Standard Model.

**Connection to Yang-Mills theories.** Electromagnetism is the $U(1)$ gauge theory. The non-Abelian generalisation replaces $U(1)$ by $SU(N)$: the gauge field becomes matrix-valued ($A_\mu \in \mathfrak{su}(N)$), the field strength acquires a commutator term $F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu + ig[A_\mu, A_\nu]$, and the Lagrangian becomes $\mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{4}\mathrm{Tr}(F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu})$. The commutator term makes the gauge field self-interacting — photons do not interact with each other, but gluons (the $SU(3)$ gauge bosons) do, leading to colour confinement. The entire apparatus of covariant derivatives, gauge-covariant field strengths, and Yang-Mills Lagrangians is a direct non-Abelian generalisation of the structure developed in this unit.

### Derivation of the field equations from the Lagrangian

The action for the electromagnetic field coupled to a conserved current is

$$
S = \int d^4x\left(-\frac{1}{4\mu_0}F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu} - J^\mu A_\mu\right).
$$

Varying with respect to $A_\mu$ (holding the current fixed):

$$
\delta S = \int d^4x\left(-\frac{1}{2\mu_0}F^{\mu\nu}\delta F_{\mu\nu} - J^\mu\delta A_\mu\right) = \int d^4x\left(-\frac{1}{\mu_0}F^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu\delta A_\nu - J^\mu\delta A_\mu\right).
$$

Integrating by parts (discarding boundary terms):

$$
\delta S = \int d^4x\left(\frac{1}{\mu_0}\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} - J^\nu\right)\delta A_\nu.
$$

Since $\delta A_\nu$ is arbitrary, the Euler-Lagrange equation is $\partial_\mu F^{\mu\nu} = \mu_0 J^\nu$. This is the inhomogeneous Maxwell equation, derived from an action principle. The homogeneous equation $\partial_{[\lambda}F_{\mu\nu]} = 0$ is not an equation of motion — it is the identity $F = dA$, built into the definition of $F$ in terms of $A$.

The electromagnetic Lagrangian is the simplest Lorentz-invariant, gauge-invariant, quadratic action that can be built from $A_\mu$. Any other Lorentz scalar constructed from $F$ (such as $F_{\mu\nu}F^{\nu\lambda}F_\lambda{}^\mu$) would give higher-derivative equations of motion and is excluded by the requirement that the field equations be second order. This uniqueness argument is the classical precursor of the renormalisability constraint in quantum field theory.

## Connections to other fields [Master]

The Faraday tensor is the simplest non-degenerate example of several structures that appear throughout theoretical physics.

**Riemannian geometry and GR [13.02.01].** The Riemann curvature tensor $R^\mu{}_{\nu\alpha\beta}$ is a rank-4 tensor with the same antisymmetry-in-pairs structure as $F_{\mu\nu}$ (in fact $R_{\mu\nu\alpha\beta} = -R_{\nu\mu\alpha\beta} = -R_{\mu\nu\beta\alpha}$). The Faraday tensor is a 2-form on spacetime; the Riemann tensor is a 2-form-valued endomorphism of the tangent bundle. The Bianchi identity $dF = 0$ is the linearised version of the second Bianchi identity $\nabla_{[\lambda}R_{\mu\nu]\alpha\beta} = 0$. The Einstein-Maxwell system couples the Einstein equation to the Maxwell equation through the EM stress-energy tensor defined above. In the Schwarzschild solution of GR, a charged mass produces the Reissner-Nordstrom metric, where the electromagnetic stress-energy tensor modifies the spacetime geometry.

**Quantum mechanics and the Dirac equation [12.11.01].** The minimal-coupling prescription $p_\mu \to p_\mu - eA_\mu$ (or $\partial_\mu \to D_\mu = \partial_\mu + ieA_\mu$ in natural units) is what couples the Dirac field to the electromagnetic field. The gauge-covariant derivative and the $U(1)$ gauge principle established here are the direct input to the Dirac equation and to QED. The Aharonov-Bohm effect demonstrates that the vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ has physical significance beyond the field tensor $F$: the phase $e\oint\mathbf{A}\cdot d\mathbf{l}/\hbar$ acquired by an electron wavefunction around a solenoid is measurable even in regions where $\mathbf{B} = 0$.

**Special relativity [10.05.01].** The Lorentz transformation of the fields (derived above from the tensor transformation rule) is a direct application of the Lorentz group developed in unit 10.05.01. The field tensor $F$ transforms under the 6-dimensional representation of the Lorentz group (the antisymmetric rank-2 tensor representation), which decomposes the six generators into three rotations and three boosts — the same decomposition that gives $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ from $F$.

**Radiation from accelerating charges [10.07.01].** The Larmor formula for radiation from an accelerating charge requires the covariant framework developed here. The relativistic generalisation involves the four-acceleration contracted with itself, and the power radiated is proportional to the Lorentz invariant $-\alpha_\mu\alpha^\mu$. The radiation fields are computed from the retarded potentials using the field tensor formalism.

**Electromagnetic waves [10.04.02].** The wave solutions of Maxwell's equations are null field configurations: $F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu} = 0$ and $F_{\mu\nu}\tilde{F}^{\mu\nu} = 0$. The covariant formulation makes it immediate that plane electromagnetic waves are Lorentz invariant in character — no boost can make $\mathbf{E}$ or $\mathbf{B}$ vanish for a plane wave.

**Topology and the Aharonov-Bohm effect.** In a region where $F = dA = 0$ but $A \neq 0$ (a non-contractible loop around a solenoid), the phase $e\oint A \cdot dl$ is physically measurable. This shows that $A$, not $F$, is the fundamental object — a lesson that generalises to the Wilson-loop observables of non-Abelian gauge theories and the Chern-Simons invariants of topological field theories. The Aharonov-Bohm effect is the simplest example of a topological effect in gauge theory and has no classical analogue.

**Continuum mechanics.** The stress-energy tensor $T^{\mu\nu}$ is the electromagnetic instance of the general symmetric rank-2 tensor that appears in fluid dynamics (the perfect-fluid stress-energy tensor $T^{\mu\nu} = (\rho + p)u^\mu u^\nu - pg^{\mu\nu}$) and in elasticity theory. The conservation law $\partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0$ (in source-free regions) is the continuum-physics statement of energy-momentum conservation. The Maxwell stress tensor $T^{ij}$ describes the pressure and tension exerted by the electromagnetic field — electric field lines are under tension (they pull along their length) and repel each other laterally, while magnetic field lines behave similarly. This stress is responsible for the pinch effect in current-carrying conductors and the magnetic pressure that confines plasmas in fusion devices.

**Condensed matter physics.** In superconductors, the London equations relate the current to the vector potential by $\mathbf{J} = -n_se^2\mathbf{A}/m$, leading to the Meissner effect (expulsion of magnetic fields) and the quantisation of magnetic flux through a superconducting loop in units of $\Phi_0 = h/(2e)$. The London penetration depth $\lambda_L = \sqrt{m/(\mu_0 n_s e^2)}$ characterises the distance over which the magnetic field decays inside a superconductor. These phenomena are direct applications of the covariant framework: the Meissner effect follows from the Proca-like equation that replaces the wave equation for $A_\mu$ when the photon acquires an effective mass inside the superconductor.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

The unification of electricity and magnetism into a single tensor is one of the great reductions in physics.

### Minkowski and the birth of four-dimensional physics (1908)

Hermann Minkowski, in his lecture "Space and Time" delivered to the 80th Assembly of German Natural Scientists and Physicians in Cologne on 21 September 1908, introduced the four-dimensional spacetime formalism and wrote down the electromagnetic field tensor for the first time. Minkowski showed that the Lorentz transformations are rotations in a four-dimensional space with indefinite metric, and that Maxwell's equations take a particularly compact form in this language. His opening words — "Henceforth space by itself, and time by itself, are doomed to fade away into mere shadows, and only a kind of union of the two will preserve an independent reality" — captured the conceptual shift.

Minkowski had been Einstein's mathematics teacher at the Zurich Polytechnikum. Einstein had been an unremarkable student — Minkowski reportedly described him as a "lazy dog" — but Minkowski recognised the importance of Einstein's 1905 paper immediately. His geometrical reformulation elevated special relativity from a kinematical curiosity to a fundamental restructuring of the framework of physics. Einstein initially regarded the four-dimensional formalism as unnecessary abstraction, but within a few years the tensor formalism became the indispensable language of general relativity. Minkowski died unexpectedly in 1909, at the age of 44, from appendicitis — he did not live to see his formalism become the standard language of relativistic physics.

The field tensor $F_{\mu\nu}$ was Minkowski's central contribution to electrodynamics. By arranging the six components of $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ into a single antisymmetric $4\times 4$ matrix, Minkowski showed that the four Maxwell equations are not four independent laws but the coordinate expression of two geometric equations on a rank-2 tensor. The distinction between electric and magnetic fields is frame-dependent — a purely electric field in one frame has magnetic components in another — and this frame-dependence is not a physical effect but a feature of the coordinate description, exactly as the decomposition of a vector into components depends on the choice of basis.

### Weyl and the gauge principle (1918–1929)

Hermann Weyl attempted to unify gravity and electromagnetism by allowing the spacetime metric to change by a conformal factor under parallel transport. Einstein objected that this would make the size of objects path-dependent, contradicting experiment. Weyl's idea was rescued by the quantum-mechanical identification of the gauge phase with the electromagnetic vector potential: local $U(1)$ phase invariance, not conformal rescaling, is the correct gauge symmetry. The mathematical apparatus Weyl developed — the gauge connection, the field strength as the curvature of the connection — became the standard language of all gauge theories.

Weyl's key insight was that the electromagnetic potential $A_\mu$ plays the role of a connection on a $U(1)$ principal bundle over spacetime. The field strength $F_{\mu\nu}$ is the curvature of this connection. Gauge transformations correspond to changes of section in the bundle. This geometric perspective, fully developed by Yang and Mills (1954) for non-Abelian groups and by Wu and Yang (1975) for the topological classification of gauge fields, is the foundation of modern gauge theory.

### Yang, Mills, and the Standard Model (1954)

Chen-Ning Yang and Robert Mills generalised the $U(1)$ gauge principle to non-Abelian groups ($SU(2)$ in their original paper, motivated by isospin symmetry of the nucleon). The resulting Yang-Mills equations are the non-Abelian generalisation of Maxwell's equations, with self-interacting gauge fields. The key difference from electromagnetism is that the gauge field itself carries charge (in the non-Abelian case), leading to self-interactions: gluons (the $SU(3)$ gauge bosons) interact with each other, producing confinement and asymptotic freedom.

The Standard Model of particle physics is a Yang-Mills theory with gauge group $SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)$, where the $U(1)$ factor is precisely the electromagnetic gauge group studied in this unit. The electroweak unification (Glashow, Weinberg, Salam, 1960s) combines the electromagnetic $U(1)$ with the weak $SU(2)$ into a single $SU(2)\times U(1)$ gauge theory, with spontaneous symmetry breaking via the Higgs mechanism giving the observed disparity between electromagnetic and weak interactions. The entire theoretical structure of the Standard Model — gauge fields, covariant derivatives, field-strength tensors, gauge invariance — is a direct generalisation of the $U(1)$ framework developed in this unit.

### Philosophical note: unification and frame-dependence

The Faraday tensor illustrates a general pattern in theoretical physics: what appears as multiple distinct phenomena in one frame (here, $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$) can be revealed as different aspects of a single entity when viewed from a more general perspective (here, the spacetime perspective). The pattern repeats: the electric and magnetic fields unify into $F_{\mu\nu}$; the electric and magnetic charges would unify if magnetic monopoles existed (the Dirac monopole quantisation condition $eg = n\hbar/2$ would make duality between $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ exact); and at higher energies the electromagnetic and weak forces unify into the electroweak $SU(2)\times U(1)$ theory.

The frame-dependence of $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ raises a philosophical question: are $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ "real" or are they merely projections of the tensor $F$? In the tensor view, $F$ is the fundamental object and the splitting into $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ depends on the observer's state of motion — just as the splitting of spacetime into space and time depends on the choice of inertial frame. This does not make $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ unreal (they produce measurable forces), but it does make them frame-dependent descriptions of a frame-independent reality. The situation is analogous to the decomposition of a vector into components: the components depend on the basis, but the vector itself does not.
