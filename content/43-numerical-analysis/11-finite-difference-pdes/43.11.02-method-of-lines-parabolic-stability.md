## Intuition [Beginner]

Heat spreads. Drop a hot spot into a cold metal bar and the warmth flows toward the cold, smoothing every bump until the whole bar settles. The continuous rule that governs this smoothing is the heat equation from [02.13.03], and like every partial differential equation it asks for a value at every point of the bar and at every instant of time. A computer cannot store infinitely many points, so we make the problem finite in two separate stages, one for space and one for time.

The first stage handles space. Lay a row of grid points along the bar and keep only the temperatures there. The bending of the temperature profile at a point â€” how far it sits below the average of its left and right neighbours â€” is exactly what the spatial part of the elliptic unit [43.11.01] already taught us to measure. Replacing the continuous spatial bending by this neighbour comparison turns the single partial differential equation into a large bundle of ordinary differential equations: one equation for how each grid temperature changes in time. That step is called the **method of lines**, because each grid point traces a line through time.

The second stage handles time. We now have an ordinary-differential-equation system, and the earlier numerical-ODE units give us integrators for exactly that. The simplest one marches forward: the new temperatures are the old ones plus a small time step times the current rate of change. This explicit march is cheap, but it has a catch. If the time step is too large relative to the grid spacing, the computed temperatures do not smooth â€” they oscillate and explode, the opposite of how heat behaves.

That catch is the heart of this unit. The fine spatial grid makes the ODE system **stiff**, in the sense of [43.10.05]: fast modes that the true solution barely notices still dictate how big a step the explicit method may take. The cure is to integrate in time with a method built for stiffness, one that stays calm at any step size. Those are the implicit schemes, and they cost one tridiagonal solve per step in exchange for removing the restriction entirely.

## Visual [Beginner]

The picture has two axes: space across the bar and time upward. Space is already chopped into grid points by the method of lines; the question is how big a time step the marching scheme may take before it misbehaves.

The table below contrasts the three standard time-marching choices. The explicit one is cheap per step but capped: the time step must stay below half the squared grid spacing. The two implicit ones cost a small solve each step but accept any time step the accuracy allows.

```
 time
  ^      explicit (forward) step:                implicit step:
  |      uses only known values below            ties new values together,
  |        U^{n+1}  <- U^n                         solve a tridiagonal system
  |          o                                       o---o---o   (all unknown)
  |         /|\                                       |   |   |
  |        o o o   (known)                          o   o   o   (known)
  |
  +-------------------------------------------------------------> space
```

| scheme | per-step cost | time-step rule | order in time |
|---|---|---|---|
| forward Euler (explicit, FTCS) | one multiply | step $\le$ half the squared spacing | first |
| backward Euler (implicit) | one tridiagonal solve | any step | first |
| Crank-Nicolson (implicit) | one tridiagonal solve | any step | second |

The takeaway: the method of lines splits the job into a space step and a time step, and the whole stability story lives in the time step. Explicit marching is fast but leashed to a tiny step on a fine grid; implicit marching pays a small solve to run off the leash.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Let us watch the explicit scheme stay calm and then blow up, on a tiny grid, so the stability limit becomes concrete. Take a bar of length $1$ with the two ends held at temperature $0$. Put down grid points at spacing $h = 1/4$, so the interior has three unknown temperatures, called $U_1, U_2, U_3$ from left to right.

The spatial rule says each grid temperature changes at a rate equal to its left neighbour plus its right neighbour minus twice itself, all divided by the squared spacing $h^2 = 1/16$. The explicit march updates each temperature by adding the time step $k$ times that rate. Collecting the factor, the new middle temperature is $U_2 + r\,(U_1 - 2U_2 + U_3)$, where $r = k/h^2$ is the single number that controls everything. The stability limit, derived in the formal part, is $r \le 1/2$.

Step 1. Start from a simple spike: $U_1 = 0$, $U_2 = 1$, $U_3 = 0$, with the ends fixed at $0$.

Step 2. Take a safe step, $r = 1/2$ (so $k = h^2/2 = 1/32$). The new middle value is $1 + \tfrac12(0 - 2 + 0) = 1 - 1 = 0$. The new left value is $0 + \tfrac12(0 - 0 + 1) = 1/2$, and by symmetry the new right value is $1/2$. The spike of height $1$ has spread into two half-height shoulders. The values stay between $0$ and $1$ â€” smoothing, as heat should.

Step 3. Take an unsafe step, $r = 1$ (so $k = h^2 = 1/16$, twice the limit). The new middle value is $1 + 1\,(0 - 2 + 0) = 1 - 2 = -1$. The new shoulders are $0 + 1\,(0 - 0 + 1) = 1$. The profile is now $1, -1, 1$: it did not smooth, it flipped sign and kept its size.

Step 4. Repeat the unsafe step once more from $1, -1, 1$. The new middle is $-1 + 1\,(1 + 1 - 2(-1)) = -1 + 4 = 3$. The middle value has grown from $1$ to $-1$ to $3$, swinging wider each step. The computed temperatures explode while real heat would have faded.

What this tells us: the same explicit scheme is trustworthy at $r = 1/2$ and ruinous at $r = 1$. The dividing line is the parabolic step restriction $k \le h^2/2$. Because the limit involves the *square* of the spacing, halving the grid forces the time step down by a factor of four â€” the practical pain that pushes people toward the implicit schemes.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Consider the one-dimensional heat (diffusion) equation [02.13.03] on the interval $(0,1)$ with homogeneous Dirichlet data,
$$
u_t = u_{xx} \ \text{ in } (0,1)\times(0,T], \qquad u(0,t) = u(1,t) = 0, \qquad u(x,0) = u_0(x),
$$
whose classical solvability and smoothing are supplied by the continuous theory and are not reproved here. Fix an integer $m \ge 1$, set the spatial mesh width $h = 1/(m+1)$ and grid points $x_j = jh$ for $0 \le j \le m+1$, and let $U_j(t) \approx u(x_j, t)$ for the interior indices $1 \le j \le m$, with $U_0 = U_{m+1} = 0$.

**Semidiscretisation: the method of lines.** Replace the spatial derivative $u_{xx}$ by the second-difference operator $D^2$ of [43.11.01] at each interior node, leaving time continuous:
$$
\frac{dU_j}{dt} = \frac{U_{j-1}(t) - 2U_j(t) + U_{j+1}(t)}{h^2}, \qquad 1 \le j \le m.
$$
Collecting the interior values into a vector $U(t) \in \mathbb{R}^m$, this is the linear, constant-coefficient, stiff ODE system
$$
U'(t) = A_h\,U(t), \qquad A_h = \frac{1}{h^2}\,\mathrm{tridiag}(1,-2,1) \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times m},
$$
the *semidiscrete* system. Here $A_h$ is the negative of the symmetric positive-definite operator $T = \tfrac1{h^2}\mathrm{tridiag}(-1,2,-1)$ of [43.11.01]; it is symmetric negative definite, with eigenvalues
$$
\mu_p = -\frac{4}{h^2}\sin^2\!\Big(\frac{p\pi h}{2}\Big), \qquad 1 \le p \le m,
$$
spreading from $\mu_1 \to -\pi^2$ (the slow mode) down to $\mu_m \to -4/h^2$ (the fast mode) as $h \to 0$. The huge spread $|\mu_m|/|\mu_1| = O(h^{-2})$ is the stiffness of the system in the sense of [43.10.05].

**Full discretisation: the $\theta$-method family.** Apply a one-step time integrator with step $k$ and time levels $t_n = nk$, writing $U^n \approx U(t_n)$. The *$\theta$-method* takes the spatial operator at a convex combination of the new and old levels,
$$
\frac{U^{n+1} - U^n}{k} = A_h\big((1-\theta)U^n + \theta\,U^{n+1}\big), \qquad \theta \in [0,1].
$$
Three choices are standard. With $\theta = 0$ this is **forward Euler**, the *forward-time centred-space* (FTCS) scheme
$$
U^{n+1} = (I + kA_h)\,U^n,
$$
explicit: each new value is an affine combination of known values. With $\theta = 1$ it is **backward Euler**,
$$
(I - kA_h)\,U^{n+1} = U^n,
$$
implicit: one tridiagonal solve per step. With $\theta = \tfrac12$ it is **Crank-Nicolson** (the trapezoidal rule applied to the semidiscrete system),
$$
\Big(I - \tfrac{k}{2}A_h\Big)U^{n+1} = \Big(I + \tfrac{k}{2}A_h\Big)U^n,
$$
also one tridiagonal solve per step. In every case $U^{n+1} = r(kA_h)\,U^n$ for the method's stability function $r$ of [43.10.04]: $r(z) = 1 + z$ (forward Euler), $r(z) = 1/(1 - z)$ (backward Euler), $r(z) = (1 + z/2)/(1 - z/2)$ (Crank-Nicolson), evaluated at the matrix argument $kA_h$.

**Local truncation error.** The *local truncation error* $\tau^n$ is the residual obtained by inserting the exact solution into the scheme and dividing by $k$. For FTCS,
$$
\tau^n_j = \frac{u(x_j,t_{n+1}) - u(x_j,t_n)}{k} - \frac{u(x_{j-1},t_n) - 2u(x_j,t_n) + u(x_{j+1},t_n)}{h^2}.
$$
A scheme is *consistent of order $(p,q)$* if $\|\tau\|_\infty = O(k^p + h^q)$. The discrete maximum norm is $\|V\|_\infty = \max_j |V_j|$, the spatial mesh ratio is $r = k/h^2$, and the symbols $A_h$, $D^2$, $U^n$, $r(z)$, $\mu_p$, $\theta$, $\tau^n$, and $O(k+h^2)$ are recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Semidiscretising is not yet a numerical method.* The method of lines produces an exact ODE system $U'=A_hU$; the approximation in time only enters when an integrator is chosen. Conflating the two hides where each error term â€” $O(h^2)$ from space, $O(k)$ or $O(k^2)$ from time â€” actually comes from.

- *The restriction is $k \le h^2/2$, not $k \le h/2$.* The fast eigenvalue is $\mu_m \approx -4/h^2$, and forward Euler needs $k|\mu_m| \le 2$, giving $k \le h^2/2$. Reading the cap as linear in $h$ underestimates the cost of refinement by a full power of $h$.

- *Unconditional stability is not unconditional accuracy.* Backward Euler and Crank-Nicolson place no cap on $k$ for *stability*, but a large $k$ still destroys *accuracy*; the schemes merely refuse to blow up, they do not promise to resolve the solution.

- *Crank-Nicolson is A-stable but not L-stable.* Its stability function tends to $-1$ as $z \to -\infty$ along the real axis, so the stiffest unresolved spatial modes are damped only marginally and can ring; backward Euler, with $r(\infty) = 0$, annihilates them. This is the [43.10.05] L-stability distinction surfacing in the PDE.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is the **parabolic stability restriction**: the explicit FTCS scheme for the heat equation is stable in the maximum and $\ell^2$ norms exactly when the mesh ratio satisfies $r = k/h^2 \le 1/2$, while the backward-Euler and Crank-Nicolson schemes are stable for every $k$. The mechanism is the [43.10.04] eigen-decoupling: $A_h$ is symmetric, so the one-step operator $r(kA_h)$ is diagonalised by the same sine modes, and stability reduces to forcing each scaled eigenvalue $k\mu_p$ into the method's absolute-stability region [ref: TODO_REF LeVeque, R. J. â€” Finite Difference Methods for Ordinary and Partial Differential Equations: Steady-State and Time-Dependent Problems; ref: TODO_REF Hairer, E. & Wanner, G. â€” Solving Ordinary Differential Equations II: Stiff and Differential-Algebraic Problems (2nd ed.)].

**Theorem (parabolic CFL and unconditional implicit stability).** *Let $A_h = \tfrac1{h^2}\mathrm{tridiag}(1,-2,1)$ with eigenvalues $\mu_p = -\tfrac4{h^2}\sin^2(p\pi h/2)$, and let the time-stepping operator be $U^{n+1} = r(kA_h)U^n$. Then, in the $\ell^2$ norm:*

*(i) The FTCS scheme $r(z) = 1 + z$ satisfies $\|r(kA_h)\|_2 \le 1$ if and only if $k \le h^2/2$.*

*(ii) The backward-Euler scheme $r(z) = 1/(1-z)$ and the Crank-Nicolson scheme $r(z) = (1+z/2)/(1-z/2)$ satisfy $\|r(kA_h)\|_2 \le 1$ for every $k > 0$.*

*Proof.* Since $A_h$ is real symmetric, the spectral theorem gives an orthonormal eigenbasis $v^{(p)}_j = \sqrt{2h}\,\sin(p\pi x_j)$ with $A_h v^{(p)} = \mu_p v^{(p)}$ (the discrete sine modes of [43.11.01]). For any rational $r$ analytic on the spectrum, $r(kA_h)$ has the same eigenvectors with eigenvalues $r(k\mu_p)$, and because the eigenbasis is orthonormal the spectral norm is the largest eigenvalue modulus:
$$
\|r(kA_h)\|_2 = \max_{1\le p\le m} |r(k\mu_p)|.
$$
Thus stability $\|r(kA_h)\|_2 \le 1$ holds exactly when $k\mu_p$ lies in the absolute-stability region $\mathcal{S} = \{z : |r(z)| \le 1\}$ for every $p$.

*(i) Forward Euler.* Here $r(z) = 1 + z$ and $\mathcal{S} = [-2,0]$ on the real axis [43.10.04]. The eigenvalues $k\mu_p = -4r\sin^2(p\pi h/2)$ are real and negative, where $r = k/h^2$; the most negative is at $p = m$, where $\sin^2(m\pi h/2) = \sin^2(\tfrac{\pi}{2} - \tfrac{\pi h}{2}) = \cos^2(\tfrac{\pi h}{2}) \to 1$ as $h \to 0$, so $k\mu_m \to -4r$. The condition $k\mu_p \ge -2$ for all $p$ is $4r\sin^2(p\pi h/2) \le 2$, that is $r\sin^2(p\pi h/2) \le 1/2$; the binding case is the largest $\sin^2$, giving $r \le \tfrac{1}{2\sin^2(m\pi h/2)}$. Since $\sin^2(m\pi h/2) < 1$ for every finite $m$ but tends to $1$, the uniform condition that holds for all $h$ is $r \le 1/2$, i.e. $k \le h^2/2$; this is also sufficient, as then $k\mu_p \in [-2,0]$ for all $p$. Hence $\|r(kA_h)\|_2 \le 1 \iff k \le h^2/2$.

*(ii) Backward Euler and Crank-Nicolson.* For backward Euler $r(z) = 1/(1-z)$; since every $k\mu_p < 0$, the denominator $1 - k\mu_p > 1$, so $|r(k\mu_p)| = 1/(1 - k\mu_p) < 1$ for all $p$ and all $k > 0$. For Crank-Nicolson $r(z) = (1+z/2)/(1-z/2)$; for real $z = k\mu_p \le 0$,
$$
|r(z)|^2 = \frac{(1 + z/2)^2}{(1 - z/2)^2}, \qquad (1-z/2)^2 - (1+z/2)^2 = -2z \ge 0,
$$
so $|r(z)| \le 1$ for every $z \le 0$, with equality only at $z = 0$. Both stability functions are bounded by one on the entire negative real axis where the spectrum lives â€” both methods are A-stable [43.10.05] â€” so $\|r(kA_h)\|_2 \le 1$ holds for every $k > 0$ with no restriction. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem is the foundational reason the parabolic time step splits into two regimes: the explicit scheme inherits the small forward-Euler stability disc, and feeding the discrete Laplacian's largest eigenvalue $\sim -4/h^2$ into that disc is exactly what forces $k \le h^2/2$, while the implicit schemes inherit A-stable regions that swallow the whole negative axis. This is exactly the eigen-decoupling of [43.10.04] carried from a generic linear system to the specific matrix $A_h$ built in [43.11.01], and it builds toward the von Neumann analysis of [43.11.03], where the same eigenvalues reappear as Fourier amplification factors $g(\xi)$ and the criterion $|g| \le 1$ recovers $r \le 1/2$ by a different route. The stability half here is dual to the consistency half computed below â€” one bounds how the scheme amplifies modes, the other bounds the residual the exact solution leaves â€” and putting these together is the central insight that a consistent, stable parabolic scheme converges at its consistency order. The same pattern appears again in the Lax-Richtmyer equivalence theorem [43.11.05], where the power-bounded operator $r(kA_h)^n$ is the abstract object whose uniform boundedness is the stability hypothesis.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (stability function, A-stability, and the parabolic restriction).** The full discretisation is $U^{n+1} = r(kA_h)U^n$ with $r$ the stability function of the time integrator [43.10.04], and because $A_h$ is symmetric the spectral norm is $\|r(kA_h)\|_2 = \max_p |r(k\mu_p)|$. The explicit scheme $r(z) = 1+z$ has stability region $\mathcal{S} = \{|1+z|\le1\}$ meeting the negative real axis in $[-2,0]$, so stability demands $k|\mu_m| \le 2$, the parabolic restriction $k \le h^2/2$ as $|\mu_m| \to 4/h^2$. The implicit schemes are A-stable â€” $\mathcal{S} \supseteq \{\mathrm{Re}\,z \le 0\}$, and the spectrum of $A_h$ lies on the negative real axis â€” so $\|r(kA_h)\|_2 \le 1$ for all $k$; backward Euler is moreover L-stable ($r(\infty) = 0$) while Crank-Nicolson has $r(\infty) = -1$ [ref: TODO_REF Hairer, E. & Wanner, G. â€” Solving Ordinary Differential Equations II: Stiff and Differential-Algebraic Problems (2nd ed.)].

**Theorem 2 (consistency orders of the $\theta$-method).** The $\theta$-method has local truncation error $\tau = (\theta - \tfrac12)k\,u_{tt} + \tfrac{k^2}{\cdots} + \tfrac{h^2}{12}u_{xxxx} + O(k^2 + h^2\cdot k)$, so it is $O(k + h^2)$ for $\theta \ne \tfrac12$ and $O(k^2 + h^2)$ for $\theta = \tfrac12$, the Crank-Nicolson value, where the $k\,u_{tt}$ term cancels. Forward and backward Euler ($\theta = 0, 1$) are first order in time; Crank-Nicolson is second. The space order is $O(h^2)$ throughout, inherited from the second-difference operator $D^2$ of [43.11.01] [ref: TODO_REF LeVeque, R. J. â€” Finite Difference Methods for Ordinary and Partial Differential Equations: Steady-State and Time-Dependent Problems].

**Theorem 3 (convergence by the discrete Duhamel/Lax synthesis).** For $u \in C^{4,2}$ the global error $E^n = U^n - u(\cdot, t_n)$ satisfies the recursion $E^{n+1} = r(kA_h)E^n - k\tau^n$ with $E^0 = 0$, whence $E^n = -k\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} r(kA_h)^{n-1-j}\tau^j$. Power-boundedness $\|r(kA_h)^\ell\|_2 \le 1$ (Theorem 1, in the stable regime) gives $\|E^n\|_2 \le k\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}\|\tau^j\|_2 \le t_n \max_j \|\tau^j\|_2$, so the global error is bounded by $T$ times the truncation error: $O(k + h^2)$ for the Euler schemes and $O(k^2 + h^2)$ for Crank-Nicolson. This is the discrete-Duhamel form of the continuous variation-of-constants representation of [02.13.03], and the abstract version of the Lax-Richtmyer theorem [43.11.05] [ref: TODO_REF ThomĂ©e, V. â€” Galerkin Finite Element Methods for Parabolic Problems (2nd ed.)].

**Theorem 4 (smoothing and the L-stability advantage for rough data).** The continuous heat semigroup $e^{tA_h}$ smooths: $\|A_h e^{tA_h}\|_2 \le C/t$, so high modes are annihilated instantly. A fully discrete scheme inherits a discrete smoothing estimate $\|A_h r(kA_h)^n\|_2 \le C/t_n$ only when $r$ is L-stable, $r(\infty) = 0$. Backward Euler enjoys this and converges at its full rate for non-smooth initial data; Crank-Nicolson, with $r(\infty) = -1$, fails the discrete smoothing estimate and exhibits persistent oscillations from under-resolved high modes unless the data are smooth or the first steps are damped (Rannacher time-stepping: a few backward-Euler steps before switching to Crank-Nicolson) [ref: TODO_REF ThomĂ©e, V. â€” Galerkin Finite Element Methods for Parabolic Problems (2nd ed.)].

**Synthesis.** The method of lines is the device that makes a parabolic PDE into an object the entire numerical-ODE apparatus already controls, and the foundational reason the time step behaves as it does is that the spatial operator $A_h$ of [43.11.01] is symmetric negative definite with spectrum stretched from $-\pi^2$ to $-4/h^2$: this single spectral fact carries the stiffness, the parabolic restriction $k \le h^2/2$, the unconditional stability of the A-stable implicit schemes, and the smoothing that separates L-stable backward Euler from merely A-stable Crank-Nicolson. This is exactly the eigen-decoupling of [43.10.04] specialised to $A_h$ â€” each discrete eigenvalue $\mu_p$ becomes one scalar test equation, and $k\mu_p$ must land in the integrator's stability region â€” and it is dual to the elliptic theory of [43.11.01], where the same matrix's smallest eigenvalue set the conditioning while here its largest sets the time-step budget.

The central insight is that convergence is again the product of a consistency order, computed from a Taylor expansion separating the $O(k^p)$ temporal and $O(h^2)$ spatial errors, with a stability bound, the power-boundedness $\|r(kA_h)^n\|_2 \le 1$; putting these together yields the $O(k+h^2)$ and $O(k^2+h^2)$ rates by a discrete Duhamel sum that generalises the continuous variation-of-constants formula of [02.13.03]. The bridge to the wider FD-PDE theory is that this concrete eigenvalue computation is the special case in which the power-boundedness hypothesis of the Lax-Richtmyer equivalence theorem [43.11.05] is verified by an explicit spectral decomposition, the same hypothesis that the Fourier amplification analysis of [43.11.03] verifies mode by mode for constant-coefficient schemes on the whole line.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (spectral characterisation of $\ell^2$ stability).** *Let $A_h$ be real symmetric with orthonormal eigenbasis $\{v^{(p)}\}$ and eigenvalues $\{\mu_p\}$, and let $r$ be rational and analytic on $\sigma(A_h)$. Then $\|r(kA_h)\|_2 = \max_p |r(k\mu_p)|$, and the scheme $U^{n+1} = r(kA_h)U^n$ satisfies $\|U^n\|_2 \le \|U^0\|_2$ for all $n$ if and only if $k\mu_p \in \mathcal{S} = \{z : |r(z)| \le 1\}$ for every $p$.*

*Proof.* Diagonalise $A_h = Q\Lambda Q^\top$ with $Q$ orthogonal ($Q^\top Q = I$) and $\Lambda = \mathrm{diag}(\mu_p)$. For a rational $r$ analytic on the spectrum, $r(kA_h) = Q\,r(k\Lambda)\,Q^\top$ with $r(k\Lambda) = \mathrm{diag}(r(k\mu_p))$. An orthogonal similarity preserves the spectral norm, so $\|r(kA_h)\|_2 = \|r(k\Lambda)\|_2 = \max_p |r(k\mu_p)|$, the largest diagonal modulus of a diagonal matrix. Iterating, $r(kA_h)^n = Q\,r(k\Lambda)^n Q^\top$, so $\|r(kA_h)^n\|_2 = \max_p |r(k\mu_p)|^n$. This is $\le 1$ for all $n$ iff $\max_p |r(k\mu_p)| \le 1$, i.e. $|r(k\mu_p)| \le 1$ for every $p$, which is $k\mu_p \in \mathcal{S}$ for every $p$; and then $\|U^n\|_2 = \|r(kA_h)^n U^0\|_2 \le \|U^0\|_2$. Conversely if some $k\mu_{p_0} \notin \mathcal{S}$ then $|r(k\mu_{p_0})| > 1$ and the data $U^0 = v^{(p_0)}$ grows as $|r(k\mu_{p_0})|^n \to \infty$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (the explicit restriction is exactly $k \le h^2/2$).** *For FTCS, $r(z) = 1 + z$, applied to the heat semidiscretisation, $\|r(kA_h)\|_2 \le 1$ holds for all admissible $h = 1/(m+1)$ if and only if $k \le h^2/2$.*

*Proof.* By Proposition 1, stability is $|1 + k\mu_p| \le 1$ for all $p$, i.e. $-2 \le k\mu_p \le 0$. Since $\mu_p = -\tfrac4{h^2}\sin^2(p\pi h/2) < 0$, the upper bound $k\mu_p \le 0$ is automatic, and the lower bound is $k\cdot\tfrac4{h^2}\sin^2(p\pi h/2) \le 2$, that is $r\sin^2(p\pi h/2) \le 1/2$ with $r = k/h^2$. The strongest constraint is at the largest $\sin^2$, namely $p = m$, where $\sin^2(m\pi h/2) = \cos^2(\pi h/2) = 1 - \sin^2(\pi h/2)$. For a fixed grid the sharp bound is $r \le \tfrac1{2\cos^2(\pi h/2)}$, but since $\cos^2(\pi h/2) \uparrow 1$ as $h \downarrow 0$, the condition that is sufficient on every grid and necessary in the limit is $r \le 1/2$, i.e. $k \le h^2/2$. With $r \le 1/2$ one has $r\sin^2 \le 1/2$ for all $p$, so every $k\mu_p \in [-2,0]$ and $\|r(kA_h)\|_2 \le 1$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (unconditional stability of the implicit schemes).** *Backward Euler ($r(z) = 1/(1-z)$) and Crank-Nicolson ($r(z) = (1+z/2)/(1-z/2)$) satisfy $\|r(kA_h)\|_2 \le 1$ for every $k > 0$ and every $h$.*

*Proof.* By Proposition 1 it suffices to show $|r(z)| \le 1$ for every $z$ in the spectrum, and $\sigma(kA_h) \subset (-\infty, 0]$ since each $k\mu_p < 0$. Backward Euler: for $z < 0$, $1 - z > 1$, so $|r(z)| = 1/|1-z| = 1/(1-z) < 1$. Crank-Nicolson: for $z \le 0$,
$$
|r(z)|^2 = \frac{(1 + z/2)^2}{(1 - z/2)^2}, \qquad (1 - z/2)^2 - (1 + z/2)^2 = -2z \ge 0,
$$
so the denominator dominates the numerator and $|r(z)| \le 1$, with equality only at $z = 0$. In both cases $|r(z)| \le 1$ throughout the spectrum, so $\max_p|r(k\mu_p)| \le 1$ and $\|r(kA_h)\|_2 \le 1$ for every $k > 0$. The pole of $r$ sits at $z = 1$ (backward Euler) or $z = 2$ (Crank-Nicolson), both off the negative axis, so $r(kA_h)$ is well defined: $I - kA_h$ and $I - \tfrac k2 A_h$ are positive definite, hence invertible, for every $k > 0$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (global error bound: consistency + stability â‡’ convergence).** *Let $u$ be smooth enough that $\|\tau^n\|_2 \le C(k^p + h^2)$ for the $\theta$-method ($p = 1$ for $\theta \ne \tfrac12$, $p = 2$ for $\theta = \tfrac12$), and suppose the scheme is stable, $\|r(kA_h)^\ell\|_2 \le 1$ for $0 \le \ell k \le T$. Then $\max_{0\le t_n\le T}\|U^n - u(\cdot,t_n)\|_2 \le C\,T\,(k^p + h^2)$.*

*Proof.* The exact solution satisfies $u^{n+1} = r(kA_h)u^n + k\tau^n$ by the definition of $\tau^n$ (insert $u$ into the one-step form and collect the residual), while $U^{n+1} = r(kA_h)U^n$. Subtract: the error $E^n = U^n - u^n$ obeys $E^{n+1} = r(kA_h)E^n - k\tau^n$ with $E^0 = 0$. Unrolling the recursion,
$$
E^n = -k\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} r(kA_h)^{\,n-1-j}\,\tau^j .
$$
Take norms and use stability $\|r(kA_h)^{n-1-j}\|_2 \le 1$:
$$
\|E^n\|_2 \le k\sum_{j=0}^{n-1}\|\tau^j\|_2 \le n k\,\max_j\|\tau^j\|_2 = t_n \max_j\|\tau^j\|_2 \le T\,C(k^p + h^2).
$$
Hence the global error is $O(k^p + h^2)$, uniformly for $t_n \le T$, which is the stated convergence rate. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The elliptic finite-difference unit [43.11.01] builds the spatial operator this unit time-steps: the matrix $A_h = \tfrac1{h^2}\mathrm{tridiag}(1,-2,1)$ is the negative of the discrete Laplacian assembled there, its sine-mode eigenvectors and eigenvalues $\mu_p = -\tfrac4{h^2}\sin^2(p\pi h/2)$ are taken directly from that unit's spectral theorem, and where the elliptic unit used the *smallest* eigenvalue ($\to -\pi^2$) to fix the conditioning $\kappa = O(h^{-2})$, this unit uses the *largest* ($\to -4/h^2$) to fix the parabolic time-step restriction. The two units are the steady-state and time-dependent faces of the same matrix.

- The stiffness and A-stability theory of [43.10.05] is the lens through which the semidiscrete system is read: $U' = A_hU$ is the canonical stiff system, its eigenvalue spread $O(h^{-2})$ is the textbook source of stiffness, and the A-stability that makes backward Euler and Crank-Nicolson unconditionally usable here, together with the L-stability that distinguishes them on rough data, are exactly the properties classified in that unit. The parabolic method of lines is the principal PDE application of stiff-ODE stability theory.

- The absolute-stability and stability-region machinery of [43.10.04] supplies the eigen-decoupling that turns matrix stability into a scalar condition: each $k\mu_p$ must lie in the integrator's region $\mathcal{S}$, the forward-Euler interval $[-2,0]$ yielding $k \le h^2/2$ and the A-stable left half-plane yielding no restriction. The stability functions $r(z) = 1+z$, $1/(1-z)$, $(1+z/2)/(1-z/2)$ used throughout this unit are the very objects defined there.

- The continuous heat equation [02.13.03] is the problem being discretised: its heat kernel, its Duhamel/variation-of-constants representation, and its eigenfunction decay $e^{-\pi^2 t}$ are the continuous facts the semidiscrete decay $e^{\mu_p t}$ and the discrete Duhamel error sum of Proposition 4 approximate. The smoothing estimate that separates L-stable from A-stable schemes is the discrete shadow of the parabolic smoothing of the continuous semigroup.

- The von Neumann stability analysis of [43.11.03] is the constant-coefficient Fourier counterpart of the eigenvalue argument here: where this unit diagonalises the finite matrix $A_h$ by sine modes on a bounded interval, the next unit substitutes a Fourier mode $e^{i\xi j h}$ into the constant-coefficient scheme on the whole line and reads stability from the amplification factor $g(\xi)$, recovering the same restriction $r \le 1/2$ from $|g(\xi)| \le 1$. Both feed the Lax-Richtmyer equivalence theorem [43.11.05], whose power-boundedness hypothesis this unit verifies by an explicit spectral decomposition.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The idea of discretising only the space variables of a partial differential equation, leaving a system of ordinary differential equations in time, was used by **Erich Rothe** in 1930 for parabolic problems â€” the *Rothe method* of horizontal lines â€” and the complementary *method of lines* that discretises space and integrates the resulting ODE system in time became a standard computational technique through the 1960s, formalised in the monograph literature and in **W. E. Schiesser**'s later systematic treatment. The explicit forward-time centred-space scheme and its stability limit $k \le h^2/2$ for the heat equation were already implicit in the foundational 1928 analysis of **Richard Courant, Kurt Friedrichs, and Hans Lewy** [ref: TODO_REF Courant, Friedrichs & Lewy 1928], whose work on difference equations of mathematical physics identified the constraint linking the time and space steps that the next unit recasts as the CFL condition.

The unconditionally stable implicit scheme that bears their names was introduced by **John Crank** and **Phyllis Nicolson** in their 1947 paper *A practical method for numerical evaluation of solutions of partial differential equations of the heat-conduction type* (*Proceedings of the Cambridge Philosophical Society* 43, 50â€“67) [ref: TODO_REF Crank & Nicolson 1947], motivated by the need to integrate heat-conduction problems without the punishing explicit step restriction. The systematic stability theory for these schemes â€” eigenvalue decoupling, A-stability, and the smoothing estimates that distinguish backward Euler from Crank-Nicolson on non-smooth data â€” was consolidated in the numerical-analysis literature by **Vidar ThomĂ©e** in *Galerkin Finite Element Methods for Parabolic Problems* (Springer, 1984; 2nd ed. 2006) [ref: TODO_REF ThomĂ©e, V. â€” Galerkin Finite Element Methods for Parabolic Problems (2nd ed.)] and, on the stiff-ODE side, by **Ernst Hairer** and **Gerhard Wanner** [ref: TODO_REF Hairer, E. & Wanner, G. â€” Solving Ordinary Differential Equations II: Stiff and Differential-Algebraic Problems (2nd ed.)].
