## Intuition [Beginner]

When a computer hands you an answer, two different things can have gone wrong. The answer might simply be far from the truth — that is the error you would notice if you compared it to the right value. Or the answer might be exactly right, but right for a question slightly different from the one you asked. These sound similar, but separating them is the single most useful move in all of numerical computing.

Backward error is the second kind. It asks: how much would I have to nudge the original data so that the answer the computer gave is the perfectly correct answer for the nudged data? If a tiny nudge — a change smaller than the rounding you already accepted when you stored the numbers — explains the computed answer, then the algorithm did its job honestly. It gave the exact answer to a question right next door to yours.

An algorithm with this property is called backward stable. The slogan is that a backward-stable method always returns "the right answer to a nearby question". It never promises the answer is close to the truth, because that depends on something the method cannot control: whether the problem itself magnifies small data changes.

That magnification is the condition number from next door. Put the two ideas together and you get the rule that governs the whole field. The actual error in the answer is roughly the condition number times the backward error. A good method keeps the backward error tiny; the problem's conditioning then decides how much of that tininess survives into the answer you read.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture two arrows. The honest arrow goes from your input straight across to the true answer: that is the problem you wanted solved. The computer cannot follow that arrow exactly. Instead it lands on a slightly different output. Backward error asks you to trace a second short arrow backward from that landing spot: how far back along the input side do you have to slide before the honest arrow would have brought you exactly to where the computer landed?

If that slide-back distance is tiny, the method is backward stable. The diagram below contrasts the two error measurements for the same computed result.

| measurement | question it answers | who controls it |
|---|---|---|
| forward error | how far is the computed answer from the true answer? | the problem and the method together |
| backward error | how small a change to the input makes the computed answer exactly correct? | the method alone |
| condition number | how much does the problem magnify input changes? | the problem alone |

## Worked example [Beginner]

Let us measure the backward error of a computed answer for a tiny linear equation. Suppose you want to solve $3x = 6$, so the true answer is $x = 2$. Imagine a method returned the slightly-off value $\tilde{x} = 2.0001$ instead.

Step 1. Measure the forward error. The computed answer $2.0001$ differs from the true $2$ by $0.0001$. As a relative error that is $0.0001 / 2 = 0.00005$, or about five thousandths of a percent. That is the gap you would see if you knew the true answer.

Step 2. Now ask the backward question. Instead of asking how wrong $\tilde{x}$ is, ask: what right-hand side would make $\tilde{x}$ the exact answer? Plug $\tilde{x}$ back in: $3 \times 2.0001 = 6.0003$. So $\tilde{x} = 2.0001$ is the exact solution of the nearby equation $3x = 6.0003$.

Step 3. Measure the data nudge. The right-hand side moved from $6$ to $6.0003$, a change of $0.0003$, or a relative change of $0.0003 / 6 = 0.00005$. So a relative data change of five thousandths of a percent fully explains the computed answer.

Step 4. Read off the verdict. The backward error is about $0.00005$ — the same small size as a single rounding. The method gave the exact answer to a question a hairsbreadth away from the one asked, so it behaved well.

What this tells us: you do not need to know the true answer to certify a method. You feed the computed answer back through the problem, see what data it solves exactly, and check that the data moved only a little. That is backward-error analysis, and it is how every serious numerical method is judged.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $f \colon X \to Y$ be a problem (a map between normed spaces, in the sense of [43.01.02]) and let $\tilde{f} \colon X \to Y$ be an *algorithm* for it: the function actually computed when $f$ is evaluated in floating-point arithmetic, including every rounding the standard model of [43.01.01] introduces. Throughout, $u$ is the unit roundoff and $\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}$ is used interchangeably for it, and $O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$ means bounded by a modest constant (a low-degree polynomial in the problem dimension) times $\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}$.

**Forward error.** The *relative forward error* of $\tilde{f}$ at $x$ is the relative distance between the computed and exact outputs,
$$
\frac{\|\tilde{f}(x) - f(x)\|}{\|f(x)\|}.
$$
This is the error a user feels, and it confounds two distinct causes: the sensitivity of the problem and the quality of the algorithm.

**Backward error.** The *relative backward error* of $\tilde{f}$ at $x$ is the size of the smallest data perturbation that makes the computed answer exactly correct,
$$
\eta(x) = \inf\left\{ \frac{\|\Delta x\|}{\|x\|} \;:\; \tilde{f}(x) = f(x + \Delta x) \right\},
$$
with $\eta(x) = \infty$ when no such $\Delta x$ exists. The defining slogan is that $\tilde{f}(x)$ is the *exact* value of $f$ at the *perturbed* input $x + \Delta x$; the algorithm answers a nearby question exactly.

**Stability.** An algorithm $\tilde{f}$ is *stable* if for each $x$ there is a perturbed input $\tilde{x}$ with $\|\tilde{x} - x\|/\|x\| = O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$ such that
$$
\frac{\|\tilde{f}(x) - f(\tilde{x})\|}{\|f(\tilde{x})\|} = O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}).
$$
In words: a stable algorithm gives *nearly* the right answer to a *nearly* right question. This is *mixed forward-backward stability*, the realistic standard for most algorithms.

**Backward stability.** An algorithm $\tilde{f}$ is *backward stable* if the second clause holds exactly — the computed answer is the *exact* answer to a nearby question:
$$
\tilde{f}(x) = f(\tilde{x}) \quad \text{for some } \tilde{x} \text{ with } \frac{\|\tilde{x} - x\|}{\|x\|} = O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}),
$$
equivalently $\eta(x) = O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$ uniformly in $x$. Backward stability is the stronger and cleaner property; when it holds, the entire accuracy question reduces to the conditioning of $f$ [ref: TODO_REF Trefethen, L. N. & Bau, D. — Numerical Linear Algebra].

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Accuracy is not stability.* An algorithm can be inaccurate on an ill-conditioned problem yet perfectly backward stable: the large forward error is charged to the conditioning, not the method. Conversely a method that happens to be accurate on one input may have no backward-error guarantee at all. The two notions are independent until joined by the master inequality.

- *Backward stability is stronger than stability, not weaker.* Backward stability requires the computed answer to be the *exact* answer to a nearby problem; mere stability allows a *nearly* exact answer to a nearby problem. Every backward-stable algorithm is stable; the converse fails (many norms, square roots, and outputs that are not in the range of $f$ admit only mixed stability).

- *A small residual is not automatically a small backward error.* For a linear system $Ax = b$ the residual $r = b - A\tilde{x}$ must be scaled by $\|A\|\|x\| + \|b\|$ before it reports the backward error; an unscaled residual confuses an absolute quantity with the relative perturbation of the data.

- *Backward error is a property of the algorithm at a point, not of the output value alone.* Two algorithms can produce the same computed number with very different backward errors if one reaches it through a benign computation and the other through a fortuitous cancellation of large intermediate errors.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result joins the two halves of the subject. Conditioning (from [43.01.02]) measures how much error the *problem* forces; backward stability measures how much error the *algorithm* injects; the theorem multiplies them into the forward error a user actually obtains.

**Theorem (fundamental theorem of backward-error analysis).** Let $f$ be differentiable at $x$ with relative condition number $\kappa(x) = \|J(x)\|\,\|x\|/\|f(x)\|$ in the sense of [43.01.02], and let $\tilde{f}$ be a backward-stable algorithm for $f$, so that $\tilde{f}(x) = f(\tilde{x})$ with $\|\tilde{x} - x\|/\|x\| = O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$. Then the relative forward error satisfies
$$
\frac{\|\tilde{f}(x) - f(x)\|}{\|f(x)\|} \le \kappa(x)\,\frac{\|\tilde{x} - x\|}{\|x\|} + O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}^2) = O\!\big(\kappa(x)\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}\big).
$$
That is, *forward error $\le$ condition number $\times$ backward error*, to first order [ref: TODO_REF Trefethen, L. N. & Bau, D. — Numerical Linear Algebra].

**Proof.** Write $\Delta x = \tilde{x} - x$, with $\|\Delta x\|/\|x\| = O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$ by backward stability. Since $\tilde{f}(x) = f(\tilde{x}) = f(x + \Delta x)$, the forward deviation is exactly the deviation of the *exact* problem under the data perturbation:
$$
\tilde{f}(x) - f(x) = f(x + \Delta x) - f(x).
$$
Differentiability at $x$ gives the first-order expansion $f(x + \Delta x) - f(x) = J(x)\,\Delta x + o(\|\Delta x\|)$. Taking norms and using the operator-norm bound $\|J(x)\,\Delta x\| \le \|J(x)\|\,\|\Delta x\|$,
$$
\|\tilde{f}(x) - f(x)\| \le \|J(x)\|\,\|\Delta x\| + o(\|\Delta x\|).
$$
Divide by $\|f(x)\|$ and insert the factor $\|x\|/\|x\|$ to manufacture relative quantities:
$$
\frac{\|\tilde{f}(x) - f(x)\|}{\|f(x)\|} \le \frac{\|J(x)\|\,\|x\|}{\|f(x)\|}\cdot\frac{\|\Delta x\|}{\|x\|} + \frac{o(\|\Delta x\|)}{\|f(x)\|} = \kappa(x)\,\frac{\|\Delta x\|}{\|x\|} + o(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}).
$$
The remainder is $o(\|\Delta x\|) = O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}^2)$ because $\|\Delta x\| = O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}\|x\|)$ and the little-$o$ term is quadratically small in the perturbation. Substituting the backward-stability bound $\|\Delta x\|/\|x\| = O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$ gives the final estimate $O(\kappa(x)\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem is the foundational reason the field is organised around two separate numbers rather than one: it shows that the forward error a user measures factors cleanly into a problem quantity $\kappa(x)$ and an algorithm quantity $\|\Delta x\|/\|x\|$, and this is exactly the master inequality previewed in the summation analysis of [43.01.01] and in the conditioning theory of [43.01.02]. The result builds toward every concrete error guarantee downstream — the $\kappa(A)\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}$ bound for Gaussian elimination, the least-squares accuracy comparison, and the residual estimates for $Ax = b$ — each of which is an instance of condition-number times backward error. It appears again in [43.01.02], where the condition number is the floor a backward-stable algorithm meets but cannot beat. The backward-stability hypothesis generalises the per-operation axiom $\mathrm{fl}(a\,\mathrm{op}\,b) = (a\,\mathrm{op}\,b)(1+\delta)$ of [43.01.01] from one rounding to a whole algorithm, and the central insight is that rounding error is best charged backward to the data: putting these together, conditioning supplies the amplification, backward stability supplies the small perturbation, and the bridge is that their product is the achievable accuracy, with no method able to do better than $\kappa(x)\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}$.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

A backward-stable solver returns $\tilde{x}$ for the scalar problem $5x = 20$ (true $x = 4$) and you find $\tilde{x} = 4.00002$. Compute the relative backward error by determining which right-hand side $\tilde{x}$ solves exactly.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $5\tilde{x}$ to find the perturbed right-hand side, then compare with $20$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$5 \times 4.00002 = 20.0001$, so $\tilde{x}$ is the exact solution of $5x = 20.0001$. The right-hand side moved from $20$ to $20.0001$, a relative change of $0.0001/20 = 5\times 10^{-6}$. So the relative backward error is $5\times 10^{-6}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, true / false).**

Every backward-stable algorithm is stable in the mixed forward-backward sense.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare the two definitions: backward stability gives an *exact* answer to a nearby problem; mixed stability allows a *nearly* exact answer to a nearby problem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.** Backward stability is the special case of stability in which the forward residual term $\|\tilde{f}(x) - f(\tilde{x})\|/\|f(\tilde{x})\|$ is exactly zero (the computed answer equals $f(\tilde{x})$ on the nose). Mixed stability merely requires that residual to be $O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$, so backward stability implies stability but not conversely.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the floating-point sum of two numbers, $\tilde{f}(a, b) = \mathrm{fl}(a + b)$, is a backward-stable algorithm for the problem $f(a, b) = a + b$, by exhibiting the data perturbation explicitly.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the standard model $\mathrm{fl}(a + b) = (a + b)(1 + \delta)$ with $|\delta| \le u$, and distribute the factor $(1 + \delta)$ over the two summands.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the fundamental axiom of [43.01.01], $\tilde{f}(a, b) = \mathrm{fl}(a + b) = (a + b)(1 + \delta)$ with $|\delta| \le u$. Distribute: $(a + b)(1 + \delta) = a(1 + \delta) + b(1 + \delta) = \tilde{a} + \tilde{b}$ with $\tilde{a} = a(1+\delta)$, $\tilde{b} = b(1+\delta)$. So the computed sum is the *exact* sum of the perturbed inputs $\tilde{a}, \tilde{b}$, each within relative distance $|\delta| \le u$ of the originals. The backward error is at most $u = O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$, so floating-point addition is backward stable. The same argument with $\mathrm{op} \in \{-,\times,\div\}$ shows every correctly-rounded operation is backward stable.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

A backward-stable algorithm solves a problem with condition number $\kappa = 2\times 10^{8}$ in binary64 ($u \approx 1.1\times 10^{-16}$). Estimate the relative forward error, and say roughly how many correct significant decimal digits you expect in the answer.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Forward error $\approx \kappa \cdot u$; the number of correct digits is about $-\log_{10}$ of that.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Forward error $\approx \kappa\,u = 2\times 10^{8} \cdot 1.1\times 10^{-16} \approx 2.2\times 10^{-8}$. The number of correct significant decimal digits is about $-\log_{10}(2.2\times 10^{-8}) \approx 7.7$, so roughly seven to eight digits survive. The conditioning has eaten about eight of the sixteen digits double precision started with, exactly $\log_{10}\kappa \approx 8.3$ of them — and a backward-stable method achieves this, the best any method can.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

For the linear system $Ax = b$ with computed solution $\tilde{x}$ and residual $r = b - A\tilde{x}$, show that $\tilde{x}$ is the exact solution of the perturbed system $(A + \Delta A)\tilde{x} = b$ with $\Delta A = r\tilde{x}^{*}/\|\tilde{x}\|_2^2$, and compute $\|\Delta A\|_2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Verify $(A + \Delta A)\tilde{x} = A\tilde{x} + \Delta A\,\tilde{x}$ and use $\tilde{x}^{*}\tilde{x} = \|\tilde{x}\|_2^2$. For the norm, note $\Delta A$ is rank one.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Compute $(A + \Delta A)\tilde{x} = A\tilde{x} + \dfrac{r\tilde{x}^{*}\tilde{x}}{\|\tilde{x}\|_2^2} = A\tilde{x} + r\,\dfrac{\|\tilde{x}\|_2^2}{\|\tilde{x}\|_2^2} = A\tilde{x} + r = A\tilde{x} + (b - A\tilde{x}) = b$. So $\tilde{x}$ exactly solves the system with coefficient matrix $A + \Delta A$. Since $\Delta A = r\tilde{x}^{*}/\|\tilde{x}\|_2^2$ is rank one, $\|\Delta A\|_2 = \|r\|_2\|\tilde{x}\|_2/\|\tilde{x}\|_2^2 = \|r\|_2/\|\tilde{x}\|_2$. Thus a small residual relative to $\|\tilde{x}\|_2$ certifies a small backward error in $A$ — the basis of a-posteriori error checking developed in [43.03.03].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Using the dot-product backward-error bound $\mathrm{fl}\big(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i\big) = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i(1 + \eta_i)$ with $|\eta_i| \le \gamma_n = nu/(1 - nu)$ (from [43.01.01]), show that the computed inner product is backward stable, and write the result as $\hat{p} = (a + \Delta a)^{\!\top} b$ with a bound on $\|\Delta a\|$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Absorb each $(1 + \eta_i)$ into the factor $a_i$, defining $\Delta a_i = a_i\eta_i$, and bound componentwise.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Set $\Delta a_i = a_i\eta_i$, so $a_i(1 + \eta_i) = a_i + \Delta a_i$ and $\hat{p} = \sum_i (a_i + \Delta a_i)b_i = (a + \Delta a)^{\!\top}b$. Each component satisfies $|\Delta a_i| = |a_i|\,|\eta_i| \le \gamma_n|a_i|$, so componentwise $|\Delta a| \le \gamma_n|a|$ and in norm $\|\Delta a\|_2 \le \gamma_n\|a\|_2$. The computed inner product is the *exact* inner product of relatively perturbed data with backward error at most $\gamma_n = O(n\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$, so it is backward stable. The perturbation may be charged entirely to $a$, entirely to $b$, or split between them.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

A matrix-vector product $y = Ax$ is computed column-style as $n$ inner products, one per row of $A \in \mathbb{F}^{m\times n}$. Prove the backward-error bound $\hat{y} = (A + \Delta A)x$ with $|\Delta A| \le \gamma_n|A|$ (entrywise), and deduce the normwise bound $\|\Delta A\|_2 \le \gamma_n\sqrt{mn}\,\max_{ij}|A_{ij}|$ or, more cleanly, $\|\Delta A\|_F \le \gamma_n\|A\|_F$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply Exercise 6 to each row $A_{i,:}$ of $A$ separately; the perturbation lands on the row entries.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The $i$-th computed output is the inner product $\hat{y}_i = \mathrm{fl}\big(\sum_j A_{ij}x_j\big)$, which by Exercise 6 equals $\sum_j A_{ij}(1 + \eta_{ij})x_j$ with $|\eta_{ij}| \le \gamma_n$. Define $\Delta A_{ij} = A_{ij}\eta_{ij}$, so $\hat{y}_i = \sum_j (A_{ij} + \Delta A_{ij})x_j$, i.e. $\hat{y} = (A + \Delta A)x$, and entrywise $|\Delta A_{ij}| = |A_{ij}|\,|\eta_{ij}| \le \gamma_n|A_{ij}|$, the stated $|\Delta A| \le \gamma_n|A|$. For the Frobenius bound, $\|\Delta A\|_F^2 = \sum_{ij}|\Delta A_{ij}|^2 \le \gamma_n^2\sum_{ij}|A_{ij}|^2 = \gamma_n^2\|A\|_F^2$, so $\|\Delta A\|_F \le \gamma_n\|A\|_F$. The product is backward stable with backward error $O(n\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$, and the perturbation respects the sparsity pattern of $A$ — a zero entry stays zero, since $\Delta A_{ij} = 0$ whenever $A_{ij} = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Outer-product accumulation computes a sum $s = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i$ where each $x_i$ is itself a rounded product, so the *backward* error is small but the *forward* error can be large. Exhibit data on which a backward-stable summation has forward error of order $\kappa\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}$ with $\kappa \gg 1$, and identify $\kappa$ as a condition number, citing the master inequality.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take a sum with heavy cancellation, e.g. $x = (1, M, -M)$ for large $M$, and compare $\sum |x_i|$ with $|\sum x_i|$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take $x = (1, M, -M)$ with $M$ large, so the true sum is $\sum_i x_i = 1$ while $\sum_i |x_i| = 1 + 2M$. By the summation theorem of [43.01.01], the computed sum is backward stable: $\hat{S} = \sum_i x_i(1 + \theta_i)$ with $|\theta_i| \le \gamma_{n-1} = O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$. But the relative forward error is bounded by
$$
\frac{|\hat{S} - \sum_i x_i|}{|\sum_i x_i|} \le \gamma_{n-1}\frac{\sum_i|x_i|}{|\sum_i x_i|} = \gamma_{n-1}\cdot(1 + 2M),
$$
and the amplifying factor $\kappa = \sum_i|x_i|/|\sum_i x_i| = 1 + 2M$ is the relative condition number of the summation problem in the sense of [43.01.02]. For $M = 10^{12}$ the forward error reaches order $10^{12}\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}$ even though the backward error stays at $\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}$. This is the fundamental theorem of backward-error analysis in action: forward error $=$ condition number $\times$ backward error, with the backward-stable method delivering the small factor and the ill-conditioned problem supplying the large one.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (backward stability of correctly-rounded operations).** Each of the four arithmetic operations and the square root, computed under the IEEE-754 correct-rounding requirement of [43.01.01], is backward stable. For $\mathrm{op} \in \{+, -, \times, \div\}$ and $a, b \in \mathbb{F}$ with in-range result, $\mathrm{fl}(a\,\mathrm{op}\,b) = (a\,\mathrm{op}\,b)(1 + \delta) = \tilde{a}\,\mathrm{op}\,\tilde{b}$ where the factor $(1+\delta)$, $|\delta| \le u$, is absorbed into the operands, exhibiting the computed result as the exact result on data within relative distance $u$. The composition of backward-stable operations is not automatically backward stable in the strict sense — the perturbations attach to different intermediate quantities — but the accumulated analysis of a straight-line algorithm yields the mixed forward-backward bound with a constant of order $\gamma_n$, which is the operative guarantee for summation, inner products, and matrix-vector products [ref: TODO_REF Higham, N. J. — Accuracy and Stability of Numerical Algorithms (2nd ed.)].

**Theorem 2 (backward stability of back substitution).** Solving an upper-triangular system $Tx = b$ with $T \in \mathbb{F}^{n\times n}$ nonsingular by back substitution in floating-point arithmetic produces a computed $\tilde{x}$ that exactly solves a perturbed triangular system $(T + \Delta T)\tilde{x} = b$ with the componentwise bound $|\Delta T| \le \gamma_n|T|$, hence $\|\Delta T\|_\infty \le \gamma_n\|T\|_\infty$. The perturbation respects the triangular structure: $\Delta T$ is upper-triangular with the same sparsity as $T$. Back substitution is therefore backward stable independently of the condition number of $T$; the accuracy of $\tilde{x}$ is then governed by $\kappa(T)$ through the master inequality, not by the algorithm. This is the cleanest nontrivial componentwise backward-error result and the template for the analysis of triangular solves inside Gaussian elimination [ref: TODO_REF Higham, N. J. — Accuracy and Stability of Numerical Algorithms (2nd ed.)].

**Theorem 3 (backward stability of Householder triangularization).** The QR factorization of $A \in \mathbb{F}^{m\times n}$ ($m \ge n$) computed by Householder reflectors, as proved backward-stable in [01.01.09], satisfies the following. The computed factors $\tilde{Q}$ (the product of the computed reflectors) and $\tilde{R}$ obey
$$
\tilde{Q}\tilde{R} = A + \Delta A, \qquad \|\Delta A\|_2 \le c_{m,n}\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}\,\|A\|_2,
$$
for a modest polynomial constant $c_{m,n}$, and moreover $\tilde{Q}$ is *exactly* orthogonal to working accuracy in the sense that there is a genuinely orthogonal $Q$ with $\|\tilde{Q} - Q\|_2 = O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$. The defining feature, traced to Wilkinson, is that the backward error is independent of $\kappa(A)$ because each reflector is an orthogonal transformation, which has condition number $1$ in the $2$-norm and so neither amplifies nor accumulates error. This is why Householder QR replaced the normal equations and classical Gram-Schmidt for least squares, a comparison assembled in [43.04.01]; the loss-of-orthogonality bound of modified Gram-Schmidt, by contrast, carries a $\sqrt{\kappa(A)}$ factor [ref: TODO_REF Wilkinson, J. H. — The Algebraic Eigenvalue Problem].

**Theorem 4 (Rigal-Gaches: the normwise backward error has a closed form).** For a computed solution $\tilde{x}$ of $Ax = b$ with residual $r = b - A\tilde{x}$, the smallest normwise relative perturbation that makes $\tilde{x}$ exact, measured by
$$
\eta(\tilde{x}) = \min\big\{ \varepsilon : (A + \Delta A)\tilde{x} = b + \Delta b,\ \|\Delta A\|_2 \le \varepsilon\|A\|_2,\ \|\Delta b\|_2 \le \varepsilon\|b\|_2 \big\},
$$
is given exactly by
$$
\eta(\tilde{x}) = \frac{\|r\|_2}{\|A\|_2\,\|\tilde{x}\|_2 + \|b\|_2}.
$$
The optimal perturbation is rank one and explicit. The theorem turns backward stability from an existence statement into a computable certificate: evaluate the scaled residual and read off the backward error, the basis of the a-posteriori analysis and iterative refinement of [43.03.03] [ref: TODO_REF Rigal, J. L. & Gaches, J. — On the compatibility of a given solution with the data of a linear system].

**Synthesis.** Backward stability is the foundational reason numerical analysis can certify a computation without knowing its answer: the question "how wrong is the result?" is replaced by "how small a change to the data makes the result exact?", and the latter is a property of the algorithm alone, checkable from the residual by the Rigal-Gaches identity. This is exactly the Wilkinson paradigm — charge rounding error backward to the data — and the central insight is that it decouples the two causes of inaccuracy that the forward error confounds. The fundamental theorem then multiplies them: forward error equals condition number times backward error to leading order, the master inequality this unit shares with the conditioning theory of [43.01.02] and the floating-point summation bound of [43.01.01]. Putting these together, the orthogonal transformations of Householder QR are backward stable with a $\kappa$-independent constant precisely because their own condition number is $1$, which generalises the observation that correctly-rounded operations are backward stable with constant $u$; back substitution and the inner product inherit the same structure with the constant $\gamma_n$. The bridge to the rest of numerical linear algebra is that every later guarantee — for Gaussian elimination with partial pivoting, for least squares, for the $Ax = b$ residual bound — is this theorem instantiated: prove the algorithm backward stable, read the conditioning off the problem, and the achievable accuracy $O(\kappa\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$ follows, with no algorithm able to do better and a backward-stable one able to do this well.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (correctly-rounded operations are backward stable).** *For $\mathrm{op} \in \{+, -, \times, \div\}$ and $a, b \in \mathbb{F}$ whose exact result is in the normalized range, the computed $\mathrm{fl}(a\,\mathrm{op}\,b)$ is the exact result of $\mathrm{op}$ on data within relative distance $u$ of $(a, b)$.*

*Proof.* The standard model of [43.01.01] gives $\mathrm{fl}(a\,\mathrm{op}\,b) = (a\,\mathrm{op}\,b)(1 + \delta)$ with $|\delta| \le u$. For $\mathrm{op} = \times$: $(a\times b)(1 + \delta) = (a(1+\delta))\times b = \tilde{a}\times b$ with $\tilde{a} = a(1+\delta)$, $|\tilde{a} - a|/|a| = |\delta| \le u$. For $\mathrm{op} = \div$: $(a/b)(1+\delta) = (a(1+\delta))/b = \tilde{a}/b$, same perturbation. For $\mathrm{op} = +$: $(a + b)(1+\delta) = a(1+\delta) + b(1+\delta) = \tilde{a} + \tilde{b}$ with $\tilde{a} = a(1+\delta)$, $\tilde{b} = b(1+\delta)$, each within relative distance $u$; subtraction is identical with $\tilde{b} = b(1+\delta)$. In every case the computed value is the exact operation applied to inputs perturbed by at most $u$ in relative norm, so the backward error is at most $u = O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (back substitution is componentwise backward stable).** *Let $T \in \mathbb{F}^{n\times n}$ be nonsingular upper-triangular and let $\tilde{x}$ be computed from $Tx = b$ by back substitution under the standard model. Then $(T + \Delta T)\tilde{x} = b$ for an upper-triangular $\Delta T$ with $|\Delta T| \le \gamma_n|T|$ entrywise.*

*Proof.* Back substitution computes, for $i = n, n-1, \dots, 1$,
$$
\tilde{x}_i = \mathrm{fl}\!\left(\frac{b_i - \sum_{j > i} T_{ij}\tilde{x}_j}{T_{ii}}\right).
$$
Evaluate the inner sum and the division under the standard model. The accumulated rounding of the $n - i$ products $T_{ij}\tilde{x}_j$, their subtraction from $b_i$, and the final division attaches to each term a factor $(1 + \theta)$ with $|\theta| \le \gamma_{n-i+1} \le \gamma_n$, by the product-of-factors lemma of [43.01.01] applied to the at-most-$n$ rounding factors involved in row $i$. Collecting these factors, there exist $|\Delta T_{ij}| \le \gamma_n|T_{ij}|$ for $j \ge i$ such that the *exact* relation
$$
\sum_{j \ge i}(T_{ij} + \Delta T_{ij})\tilde{x}_j = b_i
$$
holds for every $i$: the rounding factors are absorbed into the matrix entries $T_{ij}$ in the row, leaving $b_i$ unperturbed. Stacking the $n$ rows gives $(T + \Delta T)\tilde{x} = b$ with $\Delta T$ upper-triangular and $|\Delta T| \le \gamma_n|T|$. Since $\gamma_n = O(n\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$, the backward error is $O(n\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$ independently of $\kappa(T)$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Rigal-Gaches normwise backward error).** *For $\tilde{x}$ with residual $r = b - A\tilde{x}$, the minimal $\varepsilon$ admitting $(A + \Delta A)\tilde{x} = b + \Delta b$ with $\|\Delta A\|_2 \le \varepsilon\|A\|_2$, $\|\Delta b\|_2 \le \varepsilon\|b\|_2$ equals $\eta(\tilde{x}) = \|r\|_2/(\|A\|_2\|\tilde{x}\|_2 + \|b\|_2)$.*

*Proof.* Feasibility requires $A\tilde{x} + \Delta A\,\tilde{x} = b + \Delta b$, i.e. $\Delta A\,\tilde{x} - \Delta b = r$. For any feasible pair, the triangle inequality and the norm constraints give $\|r\|_2 = \|\Delta A\,\tilde{x} - \Delta b\|_2 \le \|\Delta A\|_2\|\tilde{x}\|_2 + \|\Delta b\|_2 \le \varepsilon(\|A\|_2\|\tilde{x}\|_2 + \|b\|_2)$, so $\varepsilon \ge \|r\|_2/(\|A\|_2\|\tilde{x}\|_2 + \|b\|_2)$, the lower bound. To attain it, set $\varepsilon^{*} = \|r\|_2/(\|A\|_2\|\tilde{x}\|_2 + \|b\|_2)$ and choose
$$
\Delta A = \frac{\varepsilon^{*}\|A\|_2}{\|r\|_2}\,r\,\frac{\tilde{x}^{*}}{\|\tilde{x}\|_2}, \qquad \Delta b = -\frac{\varepsilon^{*}\|b\|_2}{\|r\|_2}\,r.
$$
These are rank-one and have exactly $\|\Delta A\|_2 = \varepsilon^{*}\|A\|_2$ and $\|\Delta b\|_2 = \varepsilon^{*}\|b\|_2$. Compute $\Delta A\,\tilde{x} - \Delta b = \tfrac{\varepsilon^{*}\|A\|_2}{\|r\|_2}r\tfrac{\tilde{x}^{*}\tilde{x}}{\|\tilde{x}\|_2} + \tfrac{\varepsilon^{*}\|b\|_2}{\|r\|_2}r = \tfrac{\varepsilon^{*}r}{\|r\|_2}\big(\|A\|_2\|\tilde{x}\|_2 + \|b\|_2\big) = \tfrac{\varepsilon^{*}r}{\|r\|_2}\cdot\tfrac{\|r\|_2}{\varepsilon^{*}} = r$, so the pair is feasible and meets the bound. Hence the minimum is $\varepsilon^{*}$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (orthogonal transformations do not amplify backward error).** *Let $Q \in \mathbb{F}^{n\times n}$ be orthogonal and let $\hat{y} = \mathrm{fl}(Qx)$ be a matrix-vector product computed by an algorithm with backward error $\hat{y} = (Q + \Delta Q)x$, $\|\Delta Q\|_2 \le c\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}$. Then the forward error satisfies $\|\hat{y} - Qx\|_2 \le c\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}\|x\|_2 = c\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}\|Qx\|_2$, with no condition-number amplification.*

*Proof.* The forward error is $\hat{y} - Qx = \Delta Q\,x$, so $\|\hat{y} - Qx\|_2 \le \|\Delta Q\|_2\|x\|_2 \le c\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}\|x\|_2$. Because $Q$ is orthogonal, the $2$-norm is preserved: $\|Qx\|_2 = \|x\|_2$, so equivalently $\|\hat{y} - Qx\|_2 \le c\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}\|Qx\|_2$. The relative forward error is therefore $O(\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$ with no $\kappa$ factor, because $\kappa_2(Q) = \sigma_1(Q)/\sigma_n(Q) = 1$ for orthogonal $Q$ (all singular values equal $1$, as established for the condition number in [43.01.02]). This is the precise reason Householder reductions, being products of orthogonal reflectors, are backward stable with a condition-number-independent constant: the master inequality forward error $\le \kappa\cdot$ backward error reduces to forward error $\le$ backward error when $\kappa = 1$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Floating-point arithmetic and the IEEE model [43.01.01]** supplies the per-operation backward-error statement that this unit promotes to whole algorithms: the fundamental axiom $\mathrm{fl}(a\,\mathrm{op}\,b) = (a\,\mathrm{op}\,b)(1+\delta)$ is the backward stability of a single operation, the $\gamma_n$ summation and dot-product bounds are the backward stability of the basic linear-algebra kernels, and the product-of-factors lemma is the engine of the back-substitution proof here. This unit is the conceptual closure of that one: floating point provides the atomic backward errors, and backward-error analysis composes them into algorithm-level guarantees.

- **Conditioning and condition numbers [43.01.02]** is the dual half of the master inequality. That unit owns the factor $\kappa(x)$ — how much error the problem forces; this unit owns the factor $\eta(x)$ — how much error the algorithm adds; and the fundamental theorem proved here multiplies them into the achievable forward error $O(\kappa(x)\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}})$. Neither unit alone explains the accuracy of a computation, and the two together are the conceptual core of numerical analysis: a backward-stable algorithm on an ill-conditioned problem is inaccurate, and the inaccuracy is the conditioning's fault, not the method's.

- **Gaussian elimination, LU, and partial-pivoting stability [43.03.01]** is the first major application: GE with partial pivoting is backward stable in practice with backward error $O(\rho\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}\|A\|)$, where $\rho$ is the growth factor, and this unit supplies the framework — the computed $L$ and $U$ multiply to a perturbed $A + \Delta A$, and the achievable solution accuracy is then $\kappa(A)\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}$ by the master inequality. The growth-factor analysis is precisely the search for a backward-error bound for elimination.

- **Perturbation theory for $Ax = b$ [43.03.03]** takes the Rigal-Gaches backward error proved here and turns it into the a-posteriori error bound $\|\delta x\|/\|x\| \le \kappa(A)\,\|r\|/\|b\|$ and the iterative-refinement loop. The backward error this unit certifies from the residual is the input to that solver-level guarantee; the connection is that residual-based error estimation is backward-error analysis applied after the fact.

- **Least squares: normal equations vs QR vs SVD [43.04.01]** rests on the backward stability of Householder QR established here (Theorem 3) and in [01.01.09]: the $\kappa$-independent backward error of orthogonal triangularization, contrasted with the $\kappa(A)^2$ conditioning of the normal-equations route, is exactly why QR and SVD replaced the normal equations for least squares. This unit furnishes the stability half of that comparison; [43.01.02] furnishes the conditioning half.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The backward-error viewpoint was created by **James H. Wilkinson** in the 1950s and codified in *Rounding Errors in Algebraic Processes* (Prentice-Hall, 1963) and *The Algebraic Eigenvalue Problem* (Oxford, 1965) [ref: TODO_REF Wilkinson, J. H. — The Algebraic Eigenvalue Problem]. Before Wilkinson the dominant approach tracked error forward through a computation, and the resulting bounds for large problems were so pessimistic — von Neumann and Goldstine's 1947 analysis of matrix inversion is the famous instance — that some doubted whether large linear systems could be solved at all. Wilkinson inverted the question: rather than bound how the error grows, he asked what perturbation of the data would account for the computed answer exactly, and showed that for Gaussian elimination with pivoting and for orthogonal reductions that perturbation is of the order of the rounding unit. The decisive consequence is that a well-designed algorithm on a well-conditioned problem is safe; the danger lives in the conditioning of the problem, not in the accumulation of arithmetic.

The closed form of the normwise backward error of a computed linear-system solution is due to **J. L. Rigal and J. Gaches**, *On the compatibility of a given solution with the data of a linear system* (*Journal of the ACM* 14, 1967, 543–548) [ref: TODO_REF Rigal, J. L. & Gaches, J. — On the compatibility of a given solution with the data of a linear system], which made backward stability a computable certificate rather than an existence claim. **Lloyd N. Trefethen and David Bau** gave the framework its modern pedagogical form in *Numerical Linear Algebra* (SIAM, 1997), stating the definitions of stability and backward stability and the forward-error theorem as the organising spine of the subject, and **Nicholas Higham**'s *Accuracy and Stability of Numerical Algorithms* (SIAM, 1996; 2nd ed. 2002) is the comprehensive modern reference, supplying the componentwise back-substitution and Householder analyses cited here and the $\gamma_n$ notation in which they are stated.
